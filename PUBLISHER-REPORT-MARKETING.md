## Back Cover Blurb (200 words)

It started on a beach. A man in board shorts, eating ice cream. His wife beside him in full niqab, sealed in black fabric in thirty-five-degree heat. Same towel. Same God. Same book. An anonymous author who grew up close to Islam -- but inside no faith -- saw that image and could not unsee it.

*Beyond Belief* is a two-year investigation into what four billion Christians, Muslims, and Jews actually believe, and what those beliefs actually do. It begins with genuine curiosity, tracking down original texts, sitting in places of worship, and listening to believers with care and respect. It does not end where it began.

Along the way, the author meets a woman in Memphis who has tithed twenty-six thousand dollars from a salary she can barely live on, waiting for a harvest that will never arrive. A man in Belgium who left his Hasidic community and could not read a street sign in the language of the country where he was born. A social worker in eastern Germany who watches a secular town slowly come apart at the seams without the structures religion once provided.

Fair, unflinching, and ultimately surprising, *Beyond Belief* is a book that takes faith seriously enough to examine it honestly -- and honest enough to admit what we lose when it is gone.

## One-Paragraph Publisher's Pitch (100 words)

An anonymous author with roots in the Islamic world conducts a rigorous, deeply reported investigation of the three Abrahamic religions -- their texts, their institutions, their money, their treatment of women and children, and the human cost of both belief and unbelief. Written in a literary, first-person voice reminiscent of Jon Ronson crossed with Bart Ehrman, *Beyond Belief* is that rare religion book that neither attacks nor defends -- it investigates, with real people, real data, and a narrative arc that moves from curiosity through disillusionment to a hard-won, unsentimental reckoning with what secular life still lacks. 76,000 words, 20 chapters.

## Amazon/Kindle Description (300 words)

**What happens when you take religion seriously enough to look at it honestly?**

An anonymous author -- raised near Islam but belonging to no faith -- set out to understand what four billion Christians, Muslims, and Jews actually believe, and what those beliefs do to the people who hold them. The result is *Beyond Belief*: a deeply reported, unflinching, and surprisingly personal investigation of the world's three largest monotheistic religions.

This is not a polemic. It is not a defence. It is an investigation.

*Beyond Belief* examines the original texts of all three traditions with care and precision. It follows the money -- from a woman in Memphis tithing her way to an empty refrigerator, to Joel Osteen's locked church during Hurricane Harvey, to Saudi Arabia's hundred-billion-dollar export of Wahhabism. It sits with children who were taught to fear hell before they could read, and with adults still carrying the nightmares decades later. It asks what happens to women when sacred texts are used to justify asymmetry -- and listens to the women who push back from inside.

But the book does something else that sets it apart. It goes to the places where religion has already disappeared -- a town in eastern Germany, a kitchen table in suburban England -- and documents, with unsettling honesty, what fills the vacuum. Loneliness. Conspiracy movements. A secular world that has not yet built anything strong enough to replace what it dismantled.

The author speaks with imams, rabbis, evangelicals, ex-believers, social workers, and ordinary people whose lives have been shaped by faith -- for better and for worse. The result is a book that will challenge both believers and atheists, and leave neither comfortable.

**For readers of**: Bart Ehrman, Reza Aslan, Jon Ronson, Johann Hari, and anyone who has wondered whether the decline of religion is entirely good news.

## Chapter 10: The Money Machine

**1.** The sentence "Kenneth Copeland Airport. In Newark, Texas." -- Copeland's airport is located near his property outside **Newark, Texas**, which is correct. However, readers unfamiliar with Texas geography may confuse this with Newark, New Jersey. Consider adding a brief clarifier: "In Newark, Texas, about fifty miles northwest of Dallas."

**2.** The passage on Jim Bakker states he "was convicted of fraud and sentenced to forty-five years in prison" and "served five years." Bakker's original sentence was forty-five years, but it was later reduced to eighteen years on appeal, and he served approximately five years before parole. The jump from forty-five to five without explaining the reduction reads like an error to any reader who looks it up. Suggest: "sentenced to forty-five years in prison, later reduced on appeal. He served five."

**3.** The paragraph beginning "The Vatican Bank, formally the Institute for the Works of Religion" -- the word "formally" should be **"formally known as"** or, more likely, the intended word is **"formally called"**. As written, "formally" is ambiguous -- it could be misread as "formerly." Consider: "The Vatican Bank -- formally known as the Institute for the Works of Religion --"

**4.** The passage about Cardinal Angelo Becciu: "convicted of embezzlement in a London real estate deal." The deal involved a London property, but Becciu was convicted by the **Vatican tribunal**, not a London court, and the charges included embezzlement, abuse of office, and witness tampering -- not embezzlement alone. The phrasing "convicted of embezzlement in a London real estate deal" compresses this in a way that could mislead. Suggest: "convicted by a Vatican tribunal of financial crimes connected to a London real estate deal that lost the Vatican an estimated $150 million."

**5.** The sentence "The pattern is global. The mechanism is local." is a strong line, but it arrives after a long section on Islamic finance and Saudi influence. The transition back to the general argument feels slightly abrupt. Consider adding one bridging sentence before it that explicitly connects the Saudi/Gulf funding discussion back to the opacity theme that unites the chapter.

**6.** The section on Jewish institutional finance is notably brief compared to the treatment of Christian and Islamic finance. The single paragraph beginning "Jewish institutional finance is structurally different" reads almost like a disclaimer appended for balance. The Lakewood, New Jersey fraud example is good, but the section would benefit from one or two additional sentences acknowledging, for instance, the financial scale of major Jewish organizations (the Jewish Federations system raises over $2 billion annually) to give the section proportional weight.

**7.** The final scene in the Atlanta prosperity gospel church is excellent. However, the sentence "I saw the pastor smile" -- standing alone as its own paragraph -- risks reading as melodramatic. It works on the page because of the buildup, but in the context of the chapter's otherwise precise, restrained voice, it borders on editorializing through staging. Consider whether the smile needs its own line, or whether folding it into the surrounding paragraph would maintain the chapter's documentary register.

**8.** The statistic "$135 billion to religious organizations in 2021" is attributed to Giving USA. Confirm the exact figure: Giving USA's 2022 report (covering 2021) reported $135.78 billion. The rounded figure is fine, but since this chapter is data-heavy and its credibility depends on precision, consider adding the year of the Giving USA report in a parenthetical or endnote.

## Chapter 11: The Children

**9.** The opening: "I was eight years old the first time someone told me I might go to hell." This is powerful, but it introduces an autobiographical detail that seems to contradict the Prologue, where the author states: "I wasn't raised in a faith and then lost it. I didn't have a crisis of belief." and "Religion was something other people did." The chapter later clarifies the Sunday school exposure, but the contradiction needs to be addressed more cleanly. A reader paying close attention will wonder: if religion was "something other people did," how was the author attending Sunday school at eight? The Note Before We Begin positions the author as someone who "grew up surrounded by" Islam specifically. The English Sunday school experience needs a sentence of contextual framing -- was this a school-mandated RE session? A one-off visit? The gap between "I was never religious" and "I prayed for forty-five minutes" at age eight is significant and currently unresolved.

**10.** The passage about Kitzmiller v. Dover states the school board vote was "in late 2004" and the case "went to trial in 2005." This is correct. However, the sentence "It cost the school district over $1 million in legal fees" -- the actual figure was approximately $1 million in the plaintiffs' legal fees that the district was ordered to pay. The district's own legal costs were additional. Consider: "It cost the school district over $1 million in legal fees paid to the plaintiffs alone."

**11.** The paragraph beginning "A 2018 study in the American Journal of Epidemiology found that children who attended religious services at least weekly were approximately 16 percent more likely to report high happiness as young adults" -- this study (Chen & VanderWeele, 2018) is widely cited, but its methodology has been debated. The percentages cited here (16%, 29%, 33%) are drawn from adjusted hazard ratios in a single cohort study. Using "approximately" is appropriate, but the chapter should note that this is a single longitudinal study, not a meta-analysis, to avoid overstating the evidence -- especially since the chapter is otherwise careful about qualifying claims.

**12.** The passage about Hasidic yeshivas states "fewer than one percent of students in grades three through eight passed the state standardized tests in English. Fewer than one percent." The repetition of "Fewer than one percent" is clearly intentional for rhetorical emphasis, but the second instance comes across as slightly heavy-handed in a chapter that otherwise trusts the reader. The fact is damning enough without the echo. Consider cutting the second "Fewer than one percent."

**13.** The sentence "In parts of Pakistan, Bangladesh, and West Africa, residential Quranic schools do something structurally similar" -- this transition from the Hasidic yeshiva section to the talibe system is somewhat abrupt. The two systems are being compared (educational deprivation in religious settings), but they differ significantly in kind: the yeshiva system deprives children of secular education while providing substantial (if narrow) religious education; the talibe system, as described, involves forced begging, physical abuse, and malnutrition. The structural parallel is valid, but the sentence "The geography changes. The mechanism doesn't" flattens real differences. Consider a brief acknowledgment that the talibe system involves physical exploitation that goes beyond educational deprivation.

**14.** The passage "I don't have children yet" (near the chapter's end) seems to contradict Chapter 14, where the author describes his daughter asking "Where is Grandma now?" and the Epilogue, where "My daughter is at the water's edge." The chronology of the book is not strictly linear -- the author appears to have written different chapters at different times -- but this creates a noticeable inconsistency for a reader going cover to cover. Either add a temporal marker ("When I first wrote this chapter, I didn't have children yet") or revise to reflect the author's current circumstances.

**15.** The final paragraph: "I have Sarah's voice in my head, asking people not to tell the children. And David's voice, warm with gratitude, talking about Friday nights. And the silence of a boy in Brooklyn who can recite the Talmud but can't read his own lease." The Brooklyn boy has not been introduced as a specific character in this chapter -- the chapter discusses the yeshiva system but the "boy in Brooklyn" is a composite or archival figure, not someone the author met. This is fine, but the sentence places him alongside Sarah and David, who are real, named individuals. The shift from specific real people to an unnamed representative figure weakens the closing. Consider either cutting the Brooklyn reference or naming a specific source (if one exists from the reporting).

## Chapter 14: What Comes Next

**16.** The sentence "In the Netherlands, in 1958, sixty percent of the population attended church regularly. Today that number is under ten percent." -- the 1958 figure should be verified. The commonly cited statistics refer to *church membership* in the Netherlands declining from roughly 75-80% in the 1960s to around 50% by 2000 and below 30% by 2020. Regular *attendance* figures are different and typically lower. If the source is specifically about regular attendance, consider adding the source; if it is about membership or affiliation, the sentence should be adjusted. The claim is directionally correct but the specific number may not withstand scrutiny.

**17.** The passage about Katrin and Herr Brandt is the emotional heart of the chapter and is beautifully written. However, the sentence "She told me about Herr Brandt. Retired machinist. Seventy-four." uses a staccato, fragment-heavy style that appears nowhere else in the chapter. The fragments work emotionally, but they represent a minor voice break -- the rest of the chapter uses flowing, clause-heavy sentences. This is a judgment call: if the voice break is intentional (to mimic Katrin's matter-of-fact delivery), it works. If it is accidental, consider integrating the fragments into fuller sentences.

**18.** The QAnon section featuring Christine is strong, but the sentence "She said that. She actually said that." (after Christine compares QAnon followers to early Christians) is a rare instance of the author breaking the fourth wall in a way that feels slightly condescending -- toward Christine, specifically. The rest of the chapter treats its subjects with empathy even when disagreeing with them. This aside sounds like the author is inviting the reader to laugh at Christine, which undercuts the paragraph that follows, where the author explicitly asks the reader not to dismiss her. Consider cutting "She said that. She actually said that." and letting the quote land on its own.

**19.** The passage about Dale and effective altruism: "Effective altruism collapsed the moment its most prominent evangelist turned out to be running one of the largest frauds in history." This refers to Sam Bankman-Fried. Effective altruism as a movement did not collapse -- it was damaged and underwent significant public scrutiny, but the movement continues, with organizations like GiveWell, 80,000 Hours, and the Centre for Effective Altruism still operating. The sentence overstates the case. Suggest: "Effective altruism was thrown into crisis the moment its most prominent evangelist turned out to be running one of the largest frauds in history."

**20.** The sentence "When people leave religion, they don't stop being religious. They stop having good guardrails." is one of the strongest lines in the chapter, but it is followed by "And the impulse, unguarded, produces monsters." The word "monsters" is too strong for the register of this chapter and for the people described (Christine, Dale). Neither is a monster. The sentence risks undoing the careful empathy the chapter has built. Consider: "And the impulse, unguarded, produces harm" or "produces distortion."

**21.** The "list in my notebook" passage near the end is excellent, but the aside about Margaret in Glasgow -- "Margaret in Glasgow is building these" -- introduces a character who has not appeared in this chapter. She presumably appeared in an earlier chapter, but for a reader arriving at Chapter 14, the reference is unexplained. Add a brief identifier: "Margaret, the humanist celebrant I met in Glasgow" or similar.

**22.** The chapter's final line: "Now I want to go back to where this started." This is a transition to the Epilogue, but as a chapter ending it feels like a stage direction rather than a conclusion. It lacks the resonance of the chapter's other strong moments. Consider whether the chapter could end on the preceding paragraph ("the pills don't provide") and let the Epilogue open on its own terms.

## The Women

**23.** The opening scene in the Talmud class is effective, but the sentence "a copy of the Talmud open on the table before her" -- the Talmud is a multi-volume work. Specify: "a volume of the Talmud" or "a tractate of the Talmud" (the text specifies tractate Niddah two sentences later, so this could simply be: "a volume of tractate Niddah open on the table before her").

**24.** The passage about Genesis and *tzela*: "The word used in Hebrew, *tzela*, can mean rib, but it can also mean side." This is a well-established point in biblical scholarship. However, the chapter then states that "the plain text also says what it says: woman is created second, from man's body, as a *ezer kenegdo* -- a helper corresponding to him." The phrase *ezer kenegdo* is a subject of significant scholarly debate -- *ezer* appears elsewhere in the Hebrew Bible to describe God's own role (e.g., Psalm 121:2), which is hardly subordinate. The chapter is aware of interpretive flexibility ("The phrase is rich enough in Hebrew to sustain an argument") but moves past it quickly. Since this chapter is explicitly about taking women's textual inheritance seriously, a sentence acknowledging that *ezer* is used for God elsewhere would strengthen the passage and demonstrate the author's scholarly rigor.

**25.** The passage about 1 Corinthians 14:34-35 and 1 Timothy 2:12: "First Timothy's authorship is seriously disputed." This is correct (most New Testament scholars consider the Pastoral Epistles pseudepigraphal), but the statement is presented without any qualification of what "seriously disputed" means. A reader unfamiliar with biblical scholarship might read this as a fringe position. Consider: "Most mainstream New Testament scholars regard First Timothy as pseudonymous -- written in Paul's name by a later author."

**26.** The passage on Quran 4:34 and the word *daraba*: the chapter spells it "*darab*" in one instance and gives it as "*daraba*" in another (in Fatima's section). Standardize throughout. The root is d-r-b; the relevant verb form is *daraba* (past tense, third person masculine singular). Use one consistent transliteration.

**27.** The section on Aisha bint Abi Bakr: "She disputed rulings made by the male companions. She taught men who came to learn." This is strong. However, the next sentence -- "After the Prophet's death she commanded an army at the Battle of the Camel in 656 CE" -- presents the Battle of the Camel without noting that it is a deeply contested episode in Islamic history, and that Aisha's role is viewed negatively by Shia Muslims. Since the book aims for fairness across traditions, a brief note acknowledging that this is a sectarian faultline would be appropriate: "...a fact celebrated in Sunni tradition but viewed very differently in Shia Islam, where it remains a source of profound theological disagreement."

**28.** The passage on the eruv: "one of its historical functions was to extend the legal category of 'home,' within which women's movement was less restricted than in public space." This is an interesting but potentially contentious claim. The eruv's primary halachic function is to permit carrying on Shabbat, not specifically to extend women's freedom of movement. The gendered implication is a modern scholarly reading, not a traditional one. The sentence should be qualified: "Some scholars have argued that one of its historical effects..." to distinguish this from mainstream halachic understanding.

**29.** The passage about Fatima (the biochemist who wears niqab): "She was harder to pin down on 4:34." The phrase "harder to pin down" reads as though the author is trying to catch her out rather than understand her. This is a subtle register problem -- the rest of the section treats Fatima with respect, but this phrase positions the author as an interrogator. Consider: "She was more careful when we turned to 4:34" or "She took more time with 4:34."

**30.** The final paragraphs of the chapter shift between the Talmud class framing device and a broader authorial reflection. The last sentence -- "That is not a resolution. But it might be the only honest place to end." -- echoes the pattern used in several other chapters (Chapter 11 ends similarly with "That's all I have"). This is not a flaw in isolation, but across the book it risks becoming a tic. Since this chapter appears after Chapter 11 in the book's order, the reader will have already encountered this closing move. Consider a more distinctive ending for one of the two chapters.
