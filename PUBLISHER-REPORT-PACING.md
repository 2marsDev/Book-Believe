# PUBLISHER REPORT: PACING & READER-EXPERIENCE PASS

**Manuscript:** Beyond Belief
**Chapters reviewed:** Prologue, Ch4, Ch5, Ch6, Ch7, Ch7b (The Inner Life), Ch8, Ch9, Ch9b (The Violence), Ch12, Ch13
**Date:** 11 March 2026
**Reader profile:** Intelligent general reader, curious, non-academic

---

## OVERALL PACING ASSESSMENT

The manuscript has a strong natural rhythm in its first and final movements. The Prologue hooks immediately and carries genuine emotional momentum through the beach scene. Chapters 12 and 13 are among the best writing in the book -- raw, personal, structurally inventive. The problem is the middle. Chapters 4 through 6 form a long prosecutorial stretch (evidence, contradictions, borrowed myths) that is intellectually rigorous but emotionally uniform. The reader is being told "it's not true" for roughly 25,000 words in a row with the same rhetorical posture: here is a claim, here is the evidence against it, here is why the apologetic response fails. This is where you lose the reader who is already sympathetic and bore the reader who isn't.

The book accelerates well at two key points: the transition from Chapter 7 (cognitive science) into 7b (The Inner Life), where the author suddenly becomes vulnerable and uncertain, and the Ch8/Ch9 pairing (Beautiful Answer / Horrifying Answer), which is the strongest structural move in the book. It stalls most noticeably in the middle of Chapter 5 (the Quran section rehashes the same logical structure used for the Bible) and in Chapter 9b (The Violence), which covers ground that Chapter 9 has already primed.

The overall shape is: **gripping start -- long intellectual plateau -- emotional pivot at Ch7/7b -- strong dialectic in Ch8/9 -- potential drag in 9b -- powerful close.** The plateau needs breaking up. One chapter of "the evidence doesn't hold" would be devastating. Three in a row dulls the blade.

## CHAPTER BY CHAPTER NOTES

### Prologue (The Beach)
Opens brilliantly. The beach scene is cinematic and specific -- the ice cream, the board shorts, the niqab in thirty-five-degree heat. The author's voice is established immediately: observational, self-aware, willing to be uncomfortable. The pivot from the beach to the broader catalogue of religious harm (the creationist, the honour killing, the woman in Iran) moves a touch fast -- the accumulation of examples begins to feel like a list before the chapter earns it. Ends on a perfect note: "I couldn't have guessed where it would take me." You turn the page.
**Improvement:** Trim the catalogue of examples from seven to four. The strongest ones (the creationist, the honour killing) carry the point. The others dilute impact.

### Chapter 4 (The Receipt)
Strong conceptual opening -- "every deal has a receipt" is memorable framing. The Exodus section is the best-written passage in the chapter: the detail about spending three weeks with excavation reports makes the narrator credible. The Jesus section is strong but more familiar; anyone who has read Ehrman will recognise the arguments. The Muhammad section is genuinely surprising -- the detail about Muhammad initially fearing possession, taken from the tradition's own sources, is the kind of finding that justifies the book's existence. The philosophical arguments section (Plantinga, Kalam, fine-tuning) feels like a different essay bolted onto the chapter. It shifts register from narrative investigation to analytic philosophy, and the reader has to change gears.
**Improvement:** Move the philosophical arguments (Plantinga, Kalam, fine-tuning) to a shorter standalone interlude or fold them into Chapter 7. They interrupt the archaeological/historical narrative flow.

### Chapter 5 (The Books Don't Agree)
Opens well with a clear, testable standard ("if the Quran is the literal word of God, it should be flawless"). The Genesis contradictions section is compelling, especially the Documentary Hypothesis history -- the detail about Jean Astruc as a devout Catholic trying to explain the seams is excellent. The Gospel contradictions are well-assembled but begin to feel like a catalogue by the time we reach the genealogy problem. The Quran section repeats the same rhetorical structure used for the Bible (claim, error, apologetic, rebuttal) and loses momentum because the reader already knows the pattern. The "sophisticated response" section at the end -- the inspired-but-not-inerrant argument and the "buffet" conclusion -- is the strongest passage in the chapter and should come earlier or be given more room.
**Improvement:** Cut the Quran section by 40%. The backbone-and-ribs example is strong; the Alexander/Dhul-Qarnayn example and the Mary/Aaron example make the same point. Pick two of three.

### Chapter 6 (Older Gods)
This is the chapter where the book's intellectual argument peaks. The Gilgamesh discovery story (George Smith undressing in the British Museum) is irresistible. The Sargon parallel is clean and devastating. The dying-and-rising god section is handled with genuine intellectual honesty -- the author acknowledges that the parallels are not as clean as internet atheists claim, which earns trust. The Justin Martyr "devil did it" section is the most memorable argument in the entire book -- the Ponzi scheme analogy is devastating and original. The chapter's weakness is its length: the accumulated parallels (Enuma Elish, Code of Hammurabi, Amenemope, Psalm 104, Hindu flood, Chinese flood) begin to feel like a lecture. After the third parallel, the reader gets the point.
**Improvement:** After Gilgamesh, Sargon, and the dying-god section, summarise the remaining parallels in a single paragraph rather than giving each its own section. The reader doesn't need six examples when three make the case.

### Chapter 7 (The God-Shaped Hole)
The cognitive science chapter. Opens with a sharp inversion ("What if we have a brain-shaped god?") and the daughter/curtains anecdote is perfect -- specific, human, immediately relatable. HADD is explained with real clarity. The Terror Management Theory section is strong, especially the bail-setting experiment. The chapter is too long. The section on Pascal Boyer (minimally counterintuitive concepts) is interesting but feels like a fifth gear after the argument has already landed. The Justin Barrett rebuttal section is excellent -- the wife/love analogy and its dismantling is one of the best pieces of philosophical reasoning in the book. The church-in-the-rain passage at the end is the emotional heart of the first half of the book. Do not touch it.
**Improvement:** Cut the Boyer section by half. The "cube of pure mathematics" example is memorable enough to keep; the rest is supplementary.

### Chapter 7b (The Inner Life) -- NEW
This is the best new addition to the manuscript. It does something no other chapter does: it makes the author genuinely uncertain. The man in the mosque in the grey cardigan is the book's most memorable recurring image. The tour through contemplative traditions (Teresa, John of the Cross, Rumi, Merton, Weil) is handled with real respect and genuine knowledge. The neuroscience section (Newberg, parietal cortex) is well-integrated. The author's own attempt at sitting still is honest and slightly comic and completely disarming. The chapter's only weakness is that it is almost too restrained -- the author steps back from the edge of his own experience before the reader is ready to leave.
**Improvement:** Expand the author's personal meditation attempt by 300-500 words. The moment when the "snow globe settled" is the most vulnerable passage in the book, and it ends too quickly. Let the reader sit in that silence longer.

### Chapter 8 (The Beautiful Answer)
Strong structural move -- after chapters of demolition, the author rebuilds. David and the soup kitchen, Margaret the chaplain, James in AA, Ellen in Loma Linda: these are the human anchors the book needs. The health data (Harvard study, Putnam) is well-deployed. The AA section is particularly strong -- James's voice is distinctive and his line about "something louder than the voice in my head" is quotable. The friend-who-lost-his-mother passage is devastating in its brevity. The chapter ends perfectly with the unanswered question: "What replaces it?"
**Improvement:** The loneliness-epidemic statistics section reads slightly like a magazine sidebar. Integrate the Surgeon General data into the narrative rather than presenting it as a standalone block.

### Chapter 9 (The Horrifying Answer)
Structurally mirrors Ch8, which is smart. The Magdalene laundry opening is powerful. Marie's story is the most affecting individual testimony in the book. The chapter's main problem is density: it covers hell, women (across all three traditions), children, LGBTQ people, and the Catholic abuse crisis in a single chapter. Each of these subjects deserves -- and could sustain -- its own treatment. As it stands, the chapter reads like a prosecution brief: one charge after another, relentlessly. The reader becomes numbed rather than horrified. The David Kato passage and the Mahsa Amini passage are both individually devastating, but placed within pages of each other, neither gets the space to breathe.
**Improvement:** This chapter needs to be split. The current structure tries to do too much. The theology-as-weapon half (hell, women, LGBTQ, children) and the institution-as-predator half (Catholic abuse, cover-ups) are each strong enough to stand alone.

### Chapter 9b (The Violence) -- NEW
This chapter has excellent individual passages -- the checkpoint photograph framing device is effective, the Goldstein/Rabin section is chilling, and the former Salafi's testimony ("it organised it") is one of the best quotes in the book. The Crusades section and the Inquisition section are well-handled, with genuine nuance (the correct estimate of Inquisition executions, the honest acknowledgment of secular ideology death tolls). The problem: this chapter covers ground that Chapter 9 has already seeded. Chapter 9 discusses theology as a weapon. Chapter 9b discusses theology as a weapon with state power. The distinction is real but may not feel distinct enough to the reader. The chapter also runs long in its historical survey -- the Thirty Years War and Northern Ireland sections feel like obligatory inclusions rather than essential ones.
**Improvement:** Cut the Thirty Years War and Northern Ireland sections (both make points already made by stronger examples). Tighten the Christianity section to focus on the Crusades and the Inquisition's logic of mercy, which is genuinely chilling and original.

### Chapter 12 (What We Lose)
The best chapter in the book. Paul's funeral is devastating. The grandmother's death is devastating. Margaret and her Wednesday Club is devastating. The author's 3am passage -- "a kettle and a screen. It is not enough" -- is the most honest sentence in the manuscript. The Chartres section is beautifully written. The chapter earns its emotional weight because it comes after the long demolition: the reader has watched the author take everything apart and now watches him reckon with what he's lost. The ending -- "we owe them something to do with it" -- is perfect.
**Improvement:** None of substance. This chapter works. Minor note: the secular festivals paragraph (New Year's Eve, Valentine's Day) is slightly glib compared to the rest of the chapter's tone. Soften or cut.

### Chapter 13 (What We Keep)
Margaret Campbell is a wonderful character. The shipyard funeral -- the toast story, the smoke alarm, the crying turning to laughing -- is a masterful set piece. Tom the retired electrician and Arthur watching football on Thursdays is quietly moving. The "grammar of awe" section (grace, mercy, sin, sacred) is the book's intellectual climax and its most original contribution. The AA callback works well. The chapter's weakness: it tries to be both a conclusion and a programme, and the programme sections (keep the ethics, keep the practice, keep the words) read slightly like a TED talk outline. The specific human stories carry the argument better than the prescriptive passages.
**Improvement:** Trim the prescriptive "keep the X, retire the Y" sections and let the stories do more of the work. The reader doesn't need to be told "keep the ethics" when Margaret Campbell's funeral has already shown them.

## THE MIDDLE PROBLEM

Yes, this book sags in the middle, specifically across Chapters 4-6. The problem is not quality -- the writing is consistently strong -- but monotony of posture. All three chapters adopt the same rhetorical stance: the author as investigator, presenting evidence, rebutting apologetics, arriving at the same conclusion (it's not true). By the end of Chapter 6, the reader knows the verdict and is waiting for the author to move on.

**Chapters that feel like work:** Ch5 (The Books Don't Agree) is the most vulnerable. It reads like a catalogue of contradictions, and while each individual example is well-chosen, the accumulation is numbing. The second half of Ch6 (after the Gilgamesh section) also drags.

**Chapters that pull you forward:** Ch4 is strong throughout because the archaeological narrative (spending three weeks with excavation reports) gives it a quest structure. Ch7 pulls hard because the cognitive science reframes the entire book. Ch7b pulls hardest of all because the author suddenly doesn't know the answer.

**Recommendation:** Consider combining Ch4 and Ch5 into a single, tighter chapter. The evidence chapter and the contradictions chapter are doing the same work -- showing that the factual claims don't hold up. A combined chapter of ~8,000 words (down from ~12,000) would be devastating rather than exhausting. This would also solve the pacing problem: the reader gets one chapter of demolition, one chapter of borrowed origins, then the pivot to "why do we believe?"

## REDUNDANCY AUDIT

1. **The "I wanted to find something" refrain.** The author states some version of "I hoped to find evidence and didn't" in Ch4 (para beginning "I had expected, if I'm honest"), Ch5 (para beginning "I wasn't looking for reasons to dismiss"), Ch6 (para beginning "I knew what I hoped to find"), and again in Ch6 (the extended passage about wanting a fingerprint of superhuman knowledge). The sentiment is powerful the first time. By the fourth, it reads as a rhetorical tic. **Cut two of the four instances.** Keep the Ch4 version (it's the first and most effective) and the Ch6 "what would it have looked like" version (it's the most specific).

2. **The apologetics rebuttal pattern.** Chapters 4, 5, and 6 all contain sections where the author presents a sophisticated theological defence, acknowledges its strength, then dismantles it. This is fair and rigorous, but by Chapter 6 the reader recognises the move. Consider consolidating the strongest apologetic responses into a single section rather than repeating the pattern in each chapter.

3. **The "both sides of religion" observation.** The insight that religion is simultaneously beautiful and harmful appears at the end of Ch9 ("The beautiful answer and the horrifying answer are not two different things"), at the end of Ch9b ("the violence is not inevitable... but it is in there"), and is the structural premise of the Ch8/Ch9 pairing. The Ch9 closing statement is the strongest version. The Ch9b version is redundant.

4. **Mahsa Amini.** Her story appears in full in Ch9 (the women's rights section) and is the kind of detail that should appear once, with full force, and not be diluted by repetition elsewhere. Verify she is not also mentioned in the Prologue's catalogue (she is not, but check other unread chapters).

5. **The "I'm not a believer" disclosure.** The author reminds us of his non-belief in the Prologue, Ch4, Ch6, Ch7, Ch7b, and Ch12. The Prologue establishes this clearly. After that, the reader knows. Two or three subsequent reminders are enough; six is over-explaining.

## THE HUMAN STORY PROBLEM

### Memorable characters (and why they work):
- **The man in the mosque in the grey cardigan** (Ch7b): He works because he is observed, not interviewed. The author never learns his name. The restraint makes him universal.
- **David the soup kitchen volunteer** (Ch8): His line "It's not complicated" is perfect characterisation -- a man for whom faith and action are the same thing.
- **Margaret Campbell the humanist celebrant** (Ch13): She is the book's most fully realised character. Her voice is distinctive, her observations are sharp, and the shipyard funeral set piece is the best scene in the manuscript.
- **James the AA member** (Ch8): His voice is raw and specific. "I walked in because I had nowhere else to be warm" is the line the reader will remember.
- **Marie** (Ch9): Her testimony about being "a problem to be managed" at age eleven is harrowing and specific.
- **Margaret the Wednesday Club woman** (Ch12): "This is my week" is heartbreaking in its simplicity.
- **Claire the hospice nurse** (Ch12): "I can manage pain. I can't manage meaninglessness."
- **Tom the retired electrician** (Ch13): Quietly effective. The Thursday routine with Arthur is the book's most hopeful image.

### Characters that feel thin or functional:
- **Tariq the mosque volunteer** (Ch8): He is competent but generic. He serves the argument (Muslim charity works the same as Christian charity) but doesn't linger as a person.
- **Ellen the Adventist** (Ch8): She exists to illustrate the Blue Zone data. She has no voice beyond the data point.
- **Dr. Harmon the psychologist** (Ch9): She is a mouthpiece for clinical findings, not a character. Consider giving her a single specific patient story rather than clinical generalisations.
- **The former Salafi member** (Ch9b): His quotes are excellent but he appears and disappears in a single page. He deserves more room.

### Chapters with no human anchor:
- **Chapter 5 (The Books Don't Agree)** has no human characters at all. It is pure textual analysis. This is the chapter's biggest problem. The Documentary Hypothesis section could benefit enormously from a brief portrait of a scholar -- Wellhausen's reception, or Ehrman's journey from evangelical to sceptic, or a present-day student encountering the contradictions for the first time. One human moment in this chapter would transform it.
- **Chapter 6 (Older Gods)** has George Smith (briefly) but no contemporary human presence after the first page. The author's own emotional response at the end partially compensates, but a conversation with a scholar or a believer encountering these parallels would anchor the chapter.

## CHAPTER TRANSITIONS

- **Prologue to Part One:** Works. "I couldn't have guessed where it would take me" is a clean handoff.
- **Ch4 to Ch5:** Works well. "And then I did something that made it worse. I opened the books themselves." This is a strong cliffhanger transition.
- **Ch5 to Ch6:** Works. The final paragraph of Ch5 ("I pulled the oldest texts I could find... What I found made the contradiction problem look small") is an effective escalation.
- **Ch6 to Ch7:** Works. "Why do we believe them? ... The answer, it turns out, is in our skulls." Clean pivot from historical argument to cognitive science.
- **Ch7 to Ch7b:** This transition needs work. Ch7 ends on a broad question about what religion does in the real world, but Ch7b is about the interior life of prayer -- a narrowing, not a broadening. The reader expects to go outward (into sociology, into institutional analysis) and instead goes inward. This is actually the right move, but the transition should signal it. **Suggestion:** Add a brief bridging passage at the end of Ch7 or the start of Ch7b that says something like: "Before I looked at what religion does to the world, I needed to look at what it does to the person."
- **Ch7b to Ch8:** Needs a transition. Ch7b ends with "I would try again" -- a quiet, personal note. Ch8 opens with an analytical thesis statement. The tonal shift is jarring. Consider adding a brief passage at the start of Ch8 that bridges from the personal to the institutional.
- **Ch8 to Ch9:** Works perfectly. The unanswered question ("What replaces it?") creates tension, and Ch9 answers it with horror rather than hope. The structural inversion is the book's best architectural move.
- **Ch9 to Ch9b:** This is the most problematic transition. Ch9 ends with a powerful synthesis ("The same comfort that helps the widow also terrorizes the child"). Ch9b then opens with another photograph framing device and begins making arguments about violence that feel like an extension of Ch9 rather than a new chapter. **Suggestion:** If Ch9b remains a separate chapter, it needs an opening that clearly signals a shift -- perhaps from institutional harm (Ch9) to theology-sanctioned killing (Ch9b). The current opening (the checkpoint photograph) does this, but the chapter's middle sections blur back into Ch9's territory.
- **Ch12 to Ch13:** Works beautifully. Ch12 ends with "we owe them something to do with it." Ch13 opens with someone who is doing it. The transition is implicit and earned.

## THREE SPECIFIC CUTS

1. **Chapter 5, the feeding-of-the-five-thousand passage** (approx. location: paragraph beginning "Consider the feeding of the five thousand"). This is the weakest of the Gospel contradiction examples. It demonstrates minor discrepancies (grass vs. grass, Philip vs. the disciples) rather than the devastating contradictions shown by the crucifixion last words and the resurrection witnesses. It slows the chapter without adding proportional force. Cut entirely; the chapter is stronger without it.

2. **Chapter 6, the extended "what would it have looked like" passage** (approx. location: the paragraph beginning "What would it have looked like, the thing I hoped to find?"). This runs for two full paragraphs imagining a text that described DNA or the moons of Jupiter. The point is valid but the execution is self-indulgent -- the author is narrating a hypothetical that doesn't exist. The preceding paragraph ("I knew what I hoped to find") makes the same point more concisely. Cut the extended hypothetical; keep the single-sentence version.

3. **Chapter 9b, the Thirty Years War and Northern Ireland paragraphs.** These are competent summaries of well-known events that add nothing the Crusades section and the Goldstein/Rabin section haven't already established. The chapter is too long and these are the weakest sections. Cut both; redirect the reader's attention to the stronger, more original material (the Inquisition's logic of mercy, the former Salafi's testimony).

## THREE SPECIFIC EXPANSIONS

1. **Chapter 7b, the author's meditation attempt.** The passage where the "snow globe settled" and something was "nearly there" is the most vulnerable and original moment in the entire manuscript. It currently occupies about 400 words. It should be 700-800. Let the reader sit in the silence. Describe the quality of the quiet in more sensory detail. The phone ringing is the perfect comic punctuation, but the silence before it is too brief. This is the passage that will be quoted in reviews.

2. **Chapter 9, Marie's story.** Marie appears for two paragraphs. She deserves a full page. What happened after the diocese transferred the priest? How old was she when she first spoke publicly? What was the cost of telling? The reader wants to stay with her longer because her single quoted line ("They used me up and moved on") is the most powerful testimony in the book. A few more sentences of her voice would make this unforgettable rather than merely affecting.

3. **Chapter 9b, the former Salafi member.** He appears, delivers two devastating quotes ("It organised it"), and vanishes. This man's journey -- into the network and back out -- is exactly the kind of human story that Chapter 9b is missing. Even 500 additional words about his experience would transform the chapter from a historical survey into a human narrative. What pulled him in? What was the moment he began to doubt? What did leaving cost him?

## YOUR VERDICT ON READER EXPERIENCE

**On the "puts the book down" to "stays up until 3am" scale: this manuscript sits at "reads in two sittings over a weekend."**

The first sitting takes you from the Prologue through Chapter 7. You put it down not because you're bored but because the middle chapters (4-6) require intellectual stamina and the reader needs a break. The second sitting, starting at Chapter 7b or 8, takes you to the end without stopping. Chapters 8, 9, 12, and 13 are genuinely page-turning. Chapter 12 is the kind of chapter that makes a reader text passages to a friend.

The book's greatest strength is the author's voice: honest, self-doubting, willing to sit with discomfort, funny in small doses, and never smug. This is not a New Atheist polemic. It is a genuine reckoning, and the reader trusts the author because the author keeps admitting what he doesn't know and what he's lost. The moments of personal vulnerability (the church in the rain, the grandmother's death, the Chartres cathedral, the meditation attempt) are what elevate this from a good argument book to a potentially great one.

The book's greatest weakness is the prosecutorial plateau in Chapters 4-6. The reader gets the verdict by the end of Chapter 4. Chapters 5 and 6 are providing additional evidence for a case the reader has already decided. This is where the manuscript loses its "3am" quality.

**The one change that would move it toward 3am:** Combine Chapters 4 and 5 into a single, tighter chapter. This eliminates the longest stretch of uniform argumentation, gets the reader to the cognitive science pivot (Ch7) faster, and preserves the devastating individual passages (the Exodus archaeology, the resurrection contradictions, the backbone-and-ribs) while cutting the catalogue effect. The current structure asks the reader to sit through three consecutive chapters of "the evidence doesn't hold up." One chapter of evidence and one chapter of borrowed myths, followed by the cognitive science turn, would be relentless rather than exhausting.

The book has a real chance at being one of the important religion books of the decade. The closing chapters (12 and 13) are genuinely original -- no other book in this space has reckoned so honestly with what secularism fails to provide. The author's willingness to grieve what he's taken apart, rather than celebrate it, is what separates this manuscript from every other entry in the genre. Protect that. Build toward it faster. Trust that the reader is smart enough to get the evidential case in one chapter rather than three.
