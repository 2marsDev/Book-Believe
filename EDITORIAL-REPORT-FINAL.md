# EDITORIAL REPORT — FINAL PRE-PUBLICATION REVIEW

**Manuscript:** Beyond Belief
**Date:** 11 March 2026
**Scope:** Final review of three new chapters (The Inner Life, The Violence, The Women) plus overall flow assessment

---

## VERDICT ON NEW CHAPTERS

These three chapters are strong. They are among the best writing in the manuscript and they are close to publication-ready. The voice is consistent, the research is serious, and the personal honesty that distinguishes this book from the Dawkins/Hitchens school is fully present. Each chapter has a small number of issues — mostly structural or factual precision points — but none that would delay publication. The fix list below is short because the writing does not need much fixing.

---

## THE INNER LIFE — Review

**Voice:** Consistent. The opening vignette (the man in the grey cardigan) is quintessentially this author — a small, closely observed human moment used to anchor a large intellectual question. The first-person attempt at contemplative practice is brave and well-calibrated: honest without being self-deprecating, modest without false modesty.

**Factual issues:**

- Thomas Merton died in Bangkok in **1968** — correct. He was electrocuted by a faulty fan, not specified here, which is fine. However, the text says he "fell in love with a nurse when he was fifty-one." Merton's relationship with the student nurse known as "M" occurred in 1966, when he was **fifty-one** — this checks out.
- Simone Weil died in **1943 at thirty-four** — correct (born 1909, died August 1943 at age 34).
- Edgar Mitchell walked on the Moon in **1971** as part of Apollo 14 — correct.
- Rumi described as "the best-selling poet in the United States" — this is a widely repeated claim based on a 2014 article, and it is at minimum debatable. The claim has been repeated so often it has become received wisdom, but it has never been rigorously verified. Consider softening to "reportedly the best-selling poet in the United States" or "often described as..."
- The description of the posterior superior parietal cortex and Andrew Newberg's work is accurate in its broad strokes. The term "orientation association area" is Newberg's own terminology, used in his popular writing. This is fair.

**Strongest moment:** The author's own attempt at contemplative practice (lines 79-99). This is the chapter's centre of gravity and the reason it works. Without it, the chapter would be a survey of mysticism. With it, it becomes something personal and earned.

**Weakest moment:** The section on Kabbalah (line 45) is the thinnest of the three mystical traditions surveyed. It reads more like a summary from a reference text than lived engagement. The Sufi and Christian mystical sections have named individuals, specific texts, biographical texture. The Kabbalah section has the Tree of Life and the sefirot but no equivalent human anchor — no individual mystic whose life you can feel.

**Fix list:**

1. **Rumi claim (line 41):** Soften "now paradoxically the best-selling poet in the United States" to "now often called the best-selling poet in the United States" or add "reportedly." The underlying claim is contested.
2. **Kabbalah section needs a human anchor (line 45):** Consider adding a brief mention of a specific Kabbalist — Isaac Luria, Abraham Abulafia, or the Baal Shem Tov (who bridges Kabbalah into Hasidism) — to give the section the same biographical specificity as the Sufi and Christian passages. Two or three sentences would suffice.
3. **William James date (line 69):** The text says "writing in 1902" — this is correct (*The Varieties of Religious Experience* was published in 1902 based on his Gifford Lectures). No fix needed, but note for fact-check confidence.
4. **Minor repetition:** "I watched that man in the mosque too many times" (line 15) and "I don't know his name" (line 7) — the return to the mosque-man motif at the end works well, but the phrase "too many times" in line 15 is slightly odd. You watched him "several different Fridays" (line 7), which is not "too many times." Consider "enough times" or simply "too carefully."
5. **Formatting consistency:** This chapter uses `*     *     *` as section breaks. The Women chapter uses `---`. These should be unified across the manuscript before publication.

## THE VIOLENCE — Review

**Voice:** Consistent. The opening photograph device mirrors the author's established technique (compare the beach in the Prologue, the Magdalene laundry photograph in Chapter 9). The chapter sustains an extraordinarily difficult balance: it takes the theological arguments for violence seriously without endorsing them, and it refuses both the "religion of peace" deflection and the "Islam is uniquely violent" slander. This is the hardest chapter in the book to write well, and it is written well.

**Factual issues:**

- Baruch Goldstein killed **29 people** at the Cave of the Patriarchs on **February 25, 1994** — correct.
- Yigal Amir assassinated Yitzhak Rabin — correct. The text says "a year after the Hebron massacre." Rabin was killed on **November 4, 1995**, which is roughly 20 months after the Hebron massacre (February 1994), not "a year." This should be corrected to "less than two years after" or "twenty months after."
- Pope Urban II's speech at Clermont in **1095** — correct.
- The fall of Jerusalem in **July 1099** — correct.
- Raymond d'Aguilers is correctly identified as a priest and chronicler present at the siege. The blood-up-to-the-bridles quote is accurately attributed, and the author correctly notes that modern historians consider it exaggeration. Good scholarly practice.
- The Spanish Inquisition: "established by Ferdinand and Isabella in 1478" — correct. The execution estimate of "somewhere between three thousand and five thousand" is consistent with the work of Henry Kamen and other serious historians. This is a responsible figure.
- The Thirty Years War: "killed between four and eight million people — perhaps a third of the population of the German territories" — the range is consistent with current scholarship (Peter Wilson's estimate). Correct.
- The Troubles: "approximately 3,500 people" — correct (the commonly cited figure is 3,532).
- The Islamic State described as controlling "territory roughly the size of the United Kingdom" — at its peak in 2014, ISIS controlled approximately 100,000 km2 (some estimates higher). The UK is approximately 242,000 km2. This comparison overstates ISIS territorial control by roughly a factor of two. Consider "roughly the size of Great Britain" (which is still generous) or simply state "an area of roughly 100,000 square kilometres" to avoid the comparison.
- Bin Laden's 1996 fatwa — the text says he declared "the ruling to kill the Americans and their allies — civilians and military — is an individual duty for every Muslim." This specific language is from the **1998** fatwa (the "Declaration of the World Islamic Front"), not the 1996 one. The 1996 declaration was focused on the expulsion of American forces from Saudi Arabia and did not explicitly call for killing civilians. This is a factual error that should be corrected: either change the date to 1998 or adjust the quote attribution.

**Strongest moment:** The former Salafi member's testimony near the end (lines 115-128). "The theology just... organised it. Gave it a direction." This is the chapter's thesis in a single reported sentence, and it arrives at exactly the right moment — after the historical survey, before the conclusion. It shifts the chapter from analysis to something human.

**Weakest moment:** The Christianity section (lines 63-81), while accurate, covers ground that Chapter 9 ("The Horrifying Answer") has already substantially addressed — the Crusades, the Inquisition, institutional violence. The overlap is not ruinous, but a reader moving from Ch9 to this chapter (with only Ch10 between them) may feel they are rereading material. Consider whether the Christianity section here can be shortened and made to emphasise what Ch9 did not cover (e.g., the theological mechanism of "cosmic permission," which is this chapter's distinctive contribution, rather than re-narrating the Crusades).

**Fix list:**

1. **Rabin assassination timing (line 39):** Change "a year after the Hebron massacre" to "less than two years after the Hebron massacre" or "twenty months after." The current phrasing is inaccurate.
2. **Bin Laden fatwa date (line 57):** The quote about killing "Americans and their allies — civilians and military" is from the **1998** fatwa, not the 1996 declaration. Either change the date to 1998 or use a quote from the actual 1996 text.
3. **ISIS territory comparison (line 59):** "Roughly the size of the United Kingdom" overstates the territory by approximately 2x. Consider "roughly the size of South Korea" or give a square-kilometre figure.
4. **Christianity overlap with Chapter 9:** Review lines 63-81 for material that duplicates Ch9's treatment of the Crusades and Inquisition. The Thirty Years War and the Troubles are new ground; the Crusades and Inquisition are not. Consider trimming the Crusades/Inquisition paragraphs here to focus on the *mechanism* (cosmic permission) rather than re-narrating the events.
5. **The opening photograph:** The device works, but note that Chapter 9 also opens with a photograph ("There is a photograph I can't get out of my head"). Two photograph-openings in close proximity is a minor structural echo that some readers will notice. This is not necessarily a problem — the repetition could feel like a motif — but the author should make the choice consciously.

## THE WOMEN — Review

**Voice:** Consistent, with a notable and welcome shift. This chapter is more tentative than the others, more willing to sit with irresolution, and that tentativeness is appropriate to the subject. The author's acknowledgment that he is a man writing about women's experience of religion (line 15) is handled without excessive hand-wringing — stated clearly, once, then moved past. Good.

**Factual issues:**

- Amina Wadud's mixed-gender prayer took place on **March 18, 2005** — correct.
- The Malleus Maleficarum dated to **1487** — correct (published 1486 or 1487 depending on source; 1487 is acceptable).
- Witch trial estimates: "tens of thousands of people executed, perhaps seventy to eighty percent of them women" — the commonly cited range is 40,000-60,000 executed. "Tens of thousands" is accurate. The 70-80% women figure is consistent with most scholarship (Brian Levack and others).
- Aisha commanding an army at the Battle of the Camel in **656 CE** — correct.
- The Women of the Wall have been active since **1988** — correct.
- The Church of England ordained its first women priests in **1994** — correct. Women bishops followed in **2015** — the first woman was consecrated bishop in January 2015. Correct.
- United Methodist Church ordaining women since **1956** — correct (the Methodist Church, which became UMC in 1968, began ordaining women with full clergy rights in 1956).
- 1 Corinthians 14:34-35 and 1 Timothy 2:12 — correctly quoted and the scholarly debate about interpolation and disputed authorship is accurately represented.
- Quran 4:34 — the treatment is careful and accurate. The Arabic terms (*qawwamun*, *qanitat*, *daraba*) are correctly presented. The note about classical versus modern readings is fair.
- Numbers 12 (Miriam) — accurately summarised.

**Strongest moment:** Nadia's response near the end (lines 119-125): "I don't need the curtain to be universally right. I need it to be mine." This is a devastating line, and the author's decision to let it sit without commentary is exactly right. It also encapsulates the chapter's central tension — between universal feminist critique and individual women's lived experience — more precisely than any analytical passage could.

**Weakest moment:** The section headed "What the traditions did" (lines 39-53) tries to cover the historical treatment of women across all three traditions in roughly 15 paragraphs. It is competent but survey-like. The Jewish section in particular (lines 50-53) moves quickly from the eruv to "women have rarely been actually marginal" in a way that feels rushed compared to the nuanced treatment Islam and Christianity receive. The eruv point is interesting but underdeveloped — it deserves either more space or less.

**Fix list:**

1. **Section break formatting:** This chapter uses `---` for section breaks while the other two new chapters use `*     *     *`. Unify across all chapters before publication.
2. **Bold sub-headers:** This chapter uses bold sub-headers ("What the texts say," "What the traditions did," etc.) that do not appear in the other new chapters or the existing manuscript. This is a formatting inconsistency. Either add similar sub-headers to the other new chapters or remove them here. My recommendation: keep them. This chapter's structure benefits from the signposting, and it is long enough to warrant it.
3. **The eruv passage (line 51):** Either develop the gendered dimension of the eruv more fully (it is a genuinely interesting point that deserves its space) or cut it. Currently it sits in an awkward middle — asserted but not substantiated.
4. **Jewish women's section balance (lines 50-53):** The Islamic and Christian "what the traditions did" sections each get substantial treatment with named figures and specific historical detail. The Jewish section is noticeably thinner. Consider adding one or two specific historical examples — Gluckel of Hameln, Dona Gracia Nasi, or the Women of the Wall (who appear later but could be briefly previewed here).
5. **"C of E" abbreviation (line 111):** The text uses "C of E" without prior introduction of the abbreviation. Non-British readers may not recognise it. Write out "Church of England" or at minimum ensure it has been spelled out earlier in the chapter (it has not).
6. **The teacher in the opening scene:** She is described as "compact and fast-talking" (line 3) — consider whether "compact" is the word you want. It can read as a comment on a woman's body in a chapter about how traditions view women's bodies. This may be overthinking it, but in this specific chapter, word choices about women's physical descriptions carry extra weight.

## OVERALL FLOW ASSESSMENT

**Does "The Inner Life" (v2-07b) fit between Chapter 7 and Chapter 8?**

Yes, and it is arguably the manuscript's most important structural addition. Chapter 7 ("The God-Shaped Hole") explains the cognitive science of why we believe. Chapter 8 ("The Beautiful Answer") explains the social utility of religion. Between them, there was a gap: the book had no account of what religious practice actually *feels like* from the inside. The Inner Life fills that gap precisely. It moves from "the brain is wired this way" (Ch7) to "and here is what people experience when they engage that wiring through practice" (7b) to "and here is why it works as a social technology" (Ch8). The sequence is now: mechanism, experience, function. That is the right order.

**Does "The Violence" (v2-09b) fit between Chapter 9 and Chapter 10?**

Yes, with one caveat. Chapter 9 ("The Horrifying Answer") covers institutional harm — Magdalene laundries, abuse, structural damage. The Violence covers theological violence — Crusades, jihad, religiously motivated killing. These are different subjects and the distinction is valid. However, there is overlap in the Crusades/Inquisition material (see fix list above), and placing them back-to-back creates a long stretch of darkness in the manuscript: Ch9 (institutional horror) then Ch9b (theological violence) then Ch10 (the money machine). That is three consecutive chapters of critique before the book turns toward "What We Lose" in Ch12. The manuscript can sustain this — the author's voice is humane enough to prevent it from feeling like a polemic — but the author should be aware that readers may feel battered by this point. A brief moment of breath at the opening of Ch10 might help.

**Does "The Women" (v2-11b) fit between Chapter 11 and Chapter 12?**

Yes. Chapter 11 ("The Children") examines how religion affects those with the least power within it. The Women continues that examination for a different population. The transition to Chapter 12 ("What We Lose") works because The Women ends on an unresolved note — the image of two women arguing with their inheritance — which creates the right emotional readiness for Ch12's question about what disappears when religion disappears. The placement is sound.

## REMAINING MANUSCRIPT ISSUES

These are minor, as the existing chapters have been reviewed twice:

1. **Part structure with new chapters:** The file naming suggests Part Three is "Exposing" (starting at Ch8). Where do the new chapters fall in the Part structure? The Inner Life (7b) sits at the boundary between Part Two and Part Three. The manuscript's table of contents / Part headings may need updating to accommodate the three new chapters.
2. **Chapter numbering:** The new chapters are currently filed as 7b, 9b, and 11b. For publication, they will need proper sequential numbers, which means all subsequent chapters must be renumbered. This is a production task, not an editorial one, but it should be flagged now.
3. **The Prologue opens with a beach scene; Chapter 9 opens with a photograph; The Violence opens with a photograph; The Women opens with a classroom scene.** The author has a strong instinct for opening with observed scenes, which is good. But the two photograph-openings (Ch9 and The Violence) are close together and use very similar syntax ("There is a photograph I can't get out of my head" vs "There is a photograph I keep returning to"). Differentiate one of them.

## WHAT IS WORKING

Protect these — they are what makes this book distinct from every other religion book on the shelf:

1. **The author's refusal to resolve.** Every chapter ends without a neat answer. This is not indecisiveness — it is intellectual honesty, and it is the book's signature move. The final lines of all three new chapters ("I would try again," "It has always been in there," "That is not a resolution. But it might be the only honest place to end.") demonstrate this consistently. Do not let anyone edit these into conclusions.

2. **The human anchors.** The man in the grey cardigan. Nadia behind the curtain. The former Salafi member. Fatima with her doctorate. Hana and the imam. These are not illustrations of arguments — they *are* the arguments, or at least the reason the arguments matter. The book's power comes from the fact that it never loses sight of actual people inside these systems.

3. **The willingness to steelman.** The passage on Baruch Goldstein ("if you believe, without qualification... then Goldstein is not a madman") is an example of intellectual courage that most writers on religion would not risk. The conservative women's voices in The Women chapter are heard without condescension. This is what separates this book from polemic.

4. **The author's proximity to Islam.** The personal stake — "I grew up around gendered Islam" — gives the book an authority that pure outsider critique cannot match. The author is harder on Islam than a Muslim apologist would be and more sympathetic than a Western atheist would be. That exact position is the book's most valuable asset.

5. **The prose.** The sentences are clean, varied in length, and rhythmically controlled. The author knows when to be brief ("But this.") and when to build a long, accretive sentence that earns its length. The snow-globe metaphor in The Inner Life is the kind of image that readers will carry with them. Do not let a copy editor flatten this voice into house style.
