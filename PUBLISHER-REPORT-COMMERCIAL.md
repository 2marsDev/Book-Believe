# Publisher's Commercial Assessment: BEYOND BELIEF
### *What I Found When I Looked at What Four Billion People Believe*
### Anonymous Author | Manuscript sample (Prologue, Chapters 1-3, Epilogue, TOC)

**Assessor:** Senior Acquisitions Editor, Narrative Non-Fiction
**Date:** 11 March 2026

---

## 1. THE PITCH

A self-described outsider -- raised without religion in England, triggered by an image of a woman in a niqab on a Mediterranean beach -- spends years immersing himself in Christianity, Islam, and Judaism, talking to believers and doubters, reading primary texts, and sitting in pews and mosques, only to arrive at a position more nuanced and more commercially interesting than any of the New Atheist books: that the supernatural claims are almost certainly false, but the secular world has been dishonest about what it costs to admit that. This is the anti-God-Delusion -- a book that takes religion apart with genuine respect for the people inside it, and then admits it has nothing equivalent to offer in its place.

---

## 2. COMPARABLE TITLES

**1. *God Is Not Great* -- Christopher Hitchens (2007, Twelve/Atlantic)**
The gold standard for popular atheist polemic. Hitchens was brilliant, furious, and merciless. "Beyond Belief" shares the scope (all three Abrahamic faiths) but is fundamentally different in temperament. Hitchens sneered. This author hesitates. He admits the cost of disbelief. That emotional honesty is what Hitchens never offered, and it is the gap in the market this book could fill.

**2. *The God Delusion* -- Richard Dawkins (2006, Bantam/Houghton Mifflin)**
The bestselling atheist book of the century. Dawkins argued from biology and logic. "Beyond Belief" argues from human stories -- Sarah, Yusuf, Thomas, Miriam. It is a narrative book, not an argument book. Dawkins convinced the already convinced. This book might actually reach believers, which is the thing Dawkins never managed. However, Dawkins had a global platform and a household name. Anonymous authorship means this book starts at zero.

**3. *Educated* -- Tara Westover (2018, Random House)**
Not an atheism book, but the closest structural comparison: a person raised in an extreme belief system, examining it through the lens of education and experience, with gorgeous narrative prose. "Beyond Belief" has the same quality of intimate, literary non-fiction about belief systems. What it lacks is Westover's personal stakes -- she was inside. This author was always outside looking in.

**4. *Strangers Drowning* -- Larissa MacFarquhar (2015, Penguin Press)**
Profile-driven narrative non-fiction about moral extremists. The chapter structure of "Beyond Belief" -- Sarah, Yusuf, Thomas, Miriam -- echoes MacFarquhar's method. Similar audience: literary readers interested in the texture of moral lives. MacFarquhar had a New Yorker platform and a named reputation.

**5. *Why We Believe* -- Agustin Fuentes (2019, Yale University Press)**
An anthropological account of belief as a human universal. More academic, less narrative. "Beyond Belief" does what Fuentes doesn't: make you feel what belief costs and what it gives. But Fuentes offers the scientific framework that this book, based on the sample, may not have.

**6. *Unfathomable* -- Megan Phelps-Roper, *Unfollow* (2019, Riverhead)**
A leaving-faith memoir by a former Westboro Baptist Church member. The Miriam sections of "Beyond Belief" cover similar emotional territory. Phelps-Roper had the advantage of a specific, notorious backstory. This author has no backstory at all -- by design.

## 3. THE AUDIENCE

The primary buyer is a person aged 28-55, university-educated, culturally liberal, probably lapsed or non-religious, living in the UK, US, Canada, Australia, or Western Europe. They have read or are aware of the Hitchens/Dawkins/Harris canon and found it intellectually satisfying but emotionally incomplete. They may have a religious parent or partner. They have experienced the loneliness of secularism without admitting it to their social circle. They are the person who, at a dinner party, says "I'm not religious, but..." and never finishes the sentence.

This is a real audience and it is large. The "spiritual but not religious" demographic is one of the fastest-growing categories in Western survey data. Pew Research (2024) found that roughly 30% of Americans and over 50% of Britons identify as religiously unaffiliated. Within that group, a significant subset reports feeling that something is missing. These are not militant atheists. They are people living in the gap this book describes.

Secondary audience: progressive believers -- Christians, Muslims, and Jews who are comfortable with self-examination and who would read this book the way they read a challenging sermon. The author's genuine respect for faith traditions (the Talmud passages, the Sermon on the Mount section, the adhan in Istanbul) would make this accessible to believers in a way that Hitchens never was.

Tertiary audience: book-club readers. This is a book-club book. Every chapter ends with an unresolved question. The prose is accessible and emotionally resonant. The subject matter is universal. This would perform well in the reading-group market.

Estimated addressable market: 200,000-500,000 copies over the lifetime of the English-language edition, if positioned correctly and if the anonymity problem is solved. Without a named author, cut that number by 75%.

## 4. THE ANONYMITY QUESTION

This is the single largest commercial obstacle to acquisition. Let me be direct: anonymity in non-fiction is almost always a sales killer unless the anonymity itself becomes the story.

**Comparable cases:**

**1. *Primary Colors* -- "Anonymous" (Joe Klein, 1996, Random House)**
The most successful anonymous non-fiction publication of the modern era. It worked because the guessing game -- who wrote this? -- became a media event. The book was a roman a clef about a sitting president. The mystery was the marketing. When Klein was unmasked, sales surged further. Lesson: anonymity works when the identity is itself newsworthy.

**2. *A Warning* -- "Anonymous" (Miles Taylor, 2019, Twelve)**
The anonymous Trump administration official. Sold well initially on the strength of the mystery and the political moment. When the author was revealed as a mid-level DHS official rather than a senior cabinet member, sales and credibility collapsed. Lesson: the reveal must meet or exceed the expectation created by the anonymity.

**3. *Go Ask Alice* -- "Anonymous" (Beatrice Sparks, 1971, Prentice Hall)**
Presented as a real teenager's diary. Later revealed to be substantially or entirely fabricated. Sold millions. But the anonymity was a deception, not a protection. Lesson: anonymous works when the audience believes the anonymity serves the story. It fails when it feels like evasion.

**4. *The Autobiography of Malcolm X* -- as told to Alex Haley (1965)**
Not anonymous, but relevant: a book about Islam and race that put its author at mortal risk. Malcolm X published under his name and was assassinated the same year. The courage of named authorship became inseparable from the book's authority.

**My advice to this author:**

The Author's Note is the strongest case for anonymity I have read. The line "I am not writing from fear of Islam. I am writing from proximity to it" is powerful and credible. The author clearly has genuine ties to Muslim communities and genuine reason to fear reprisal.

But the market does not care about your reasons. The market cares about trust. A reader investing fifteen hours in a book about the most sensitive subject in contemporary culture needs to know who is speaking. Not because anonymity is cowardly -- the author pre-empts this -- but because authority in non-fiction comes from accountability.

Three options, in order of commercial viability:
1. **Publish under a pseudonym with a verified biographical note** -- "The author is a British writer of [Muslim/mixed] heritage who grew up in [region]." Give the reader enough to trust without giving enough to identify. Have the publisher verify the author's identity internally. This is the Elena Ferrante model.
2. **Publish anonymously and make the anonymity the story** -- but this requires a publicity apparatus (interviews in silhouette, voice-altered podcasts, a publisher willing to invest in the mystery). Expensive and risky.
3. **Publish under real name with security precautions** -- the bravest and commercially strongest option, but one only the author can make.

## 5. THE OPENING (First 15 Pages)

**The Author's Note:** Exceptional. This is one of the best author's notes I have read in twenty years of acquisitions. The opening line -- "I am not going to tell you my name" -- is a hook. The tone is honest without being self-pitying, vulnerable without being weak. The passage about compulsion ("I couldn't not write it") is the kind of thing that makes an editor lean forward. This note does two things simultaneously: it establishes the author's credibility and it establishes the emotional register of the entire book. If this were a query letter, I would request the full manuscript based on this note alone.

**The Prologue (The Beach):** This is where the book either wins or loses its audience, and it wins. The beach scene is cinematic, specific, and viscerally effective. "Board shorts and ice cream" as a one-line summation of religious hypocrisy is the kind of phrase that gets quoted in reviews. The author's restraint -- "I want to be careful here. I know the arguments" -- is essential. It signals to the reader: I am not a bigot. I am not ranting. I am noticing something and I am going to follow it honestly. By page 5, I would keep reading.

The transition from the beach scene to the author's secular upbringing is smooth. The line about borrowing "someone else's comfort" at funerals is quietly devastating. The accumulation of examples (the creationist colleague, the honor killing, the hospital in Ireland) risks becoming a catalogue of horrors, but the author pulls back before it tips. The closing -- "I'm not going to tell you the ending. Not yet" -- is effective commercial storytelling. It creates forward momentum.

**By page 15 (into Chapter 1):** Still hooked. The synagogue library scene, the Talmud, the line about "a conversation rendered in architecture" -- this is strong literary non-fiction prose. The author has a genuine gift for making intellectual material sensory and specific. The librarian who says "You could spend a lifetime -- that's rather the point" is a small, perfect moment. The section on Christianity (the cold church, the thin singing, the biscuits and tea) is even better. "The institution had centuries of blood on its hands. The people in the pews had biscuits and tea." That is a line that will be highlighted in thousands of copies.

**What does not work:** The Islam section of Chapter 1 is slightly more careful, slightly more hedged than the other two. The author is visibly nervous. This is understandable but it creates an unevenness. The Christianity and Judaism sections feel like a writer at full stretch; the Islam section feels like a writer checking over his shoulder. If this book is going to work, every section needs to be written at the same temperature.

## 6. WHAT MAKES THIS BOOK DISTINCT

Three things.

**First: the emotional position.** This book is neither for religion nor against it. It is something rarer and harder -- it is honest about the cost of both. The epilogue contains the line: "I believe that the atheist movement, including the books that shaped my own thinking, has been dishonest about the cost of being right. We presented the abandonment of religion as liberation. For some, it was. For many others, it was amputation." No major atheist author has written that sentence. It is the thesis that separates this book from everything else on the shelf.

**Second: the human portraits.** Sarah, Yusuf, Thomas, Miriam. Chapter 3 is the heart of the book and it is superb. The Sarah section alone -- "I believe it in the way you believe the ground is solid. Not because I've tested it. Because if I stop believing it, I'll fall" -- is the kind of moment that makes a reader stop and put the book down and stare at the ceiling. The Thomas section is equally powerful precisely because there is nothing to explain. The Miriam section is devastating. These are not case studies. They are characters rendered with novelistic skill.

**Third: the structural honesty.** The book gives religion its best case before it makes its own. Part One is called "Understanding" (or "The Traditions" per the TOC). The author spends three full chapters -- and presumably significant further material in the chapters I have not seen -- earning the right to criticize by first demonstrating that he has listened. This is the structural decision that makes the book trustworthy. Hitchens started with the prosecution. This author starts with the defense. By the time the criticism comes, the reader trusts the critic.

The prose is also genuinely good. This is not workmanlike non-fiction. It is literary non-fiction with real sentences. "The same church that runs hospitals across the developing world systematically covered up the rape of children across the developed one." That is a sentence that does a lot of work in very few words.

## 7. THE TITLE

"Beyond Belief" is a problem.

It has been used by Michael Shermer (*Science, Religion, and the Search for Truth*, 2006), by V.S. Naipaul (*Among the Converts*, 1998), and by Elaine Pagels (*The Secret Gospel of Thomas*, 2003). The phrase is also a common idiom. It is not distinctive enough for a book that needs to stand out.

The subtitle -- "What I Found When I Looked at What Four Billion People Believe" -- is stronger than the title. It is specific, it is intriguing, it signals scope. But it is too long for a cover.

**Three alternative suggestions:**

1. **The Ground Beneath: What Happens When You Question What Four Billion People Believe**
   Draws from the manuscript's recurring metaphor (Sarah's "ground is solid," Thomas's "foundations," the author standing on "something thinner"). Emotionally resonant. Signals both the investigation and the personal cost.

2. **The Beautiful Lie: Faith, Doubt, and What We Owe the People Who Believe**
   Provocative. Commercially strong. Captures the book's central tension -- the beauty of religion alongside the author's conclusion that its claims are untrue. The subtitle signals that this is not a hit job.

3. **What We Keep: A Secular Investigation of Sacred Things**
   Draws from the chapter title "What We Keep" (Ch13). Quieter, more literary. Signals the book's nuance -- not what we discard, but what survives examination. Works well for the book-club market.

## 8. WHAT WOULD MAKE YOU PASS

I will be direct.

**1. The anonymity, unresolved.** If the author insists on full anonymity with no biographical detail, no verified identity, no publicity strategy, I cannot take this to an acquisitions committee. The book's credibility depends on the reader trusting the author's proximity to these communities. "Trust me, I grew up near this" is not enough when your name is not on the cover. Every review will lead with the anonymity. Every hostile reader will use it as a dismissal. The Ferrante model (pseudonym, verified biography, publisher vouching) is the minimum.

**2. The missing middle.** I have read Chapters 1-3 and the Epilogue. The TOC promises fourteen chapters plus an epilogue. The chapters I have not read -- "The Receipt," "The Books Don't Agree," "Older Gods," "The God-Shaped Hole," "The Beautiful Answer," "The Horrifying Answer," "The Money Machine," "The Children," "The Women," "What We Lose," "What We Keep," "What Comes Next" -- are where the hard work happens. The opening is gorgeous. The epilogue is moving. But books are sold on their openings and reviewed on their middles. If the investigation chapters (Part Two and Three) descend into a catalogue of religious crimes without the same narrative skill and emotional nuance shown in Chapters 1-3, the book will fail. The tone of the opening is a promise. If the middle does not keep it, readers will feel betrayed.

**3. The Islam asymmetry.** The author is visibly more cautious with Islam than with Christianity or Judaism. This is understandable but commercially and editorially dangerous. If the book is perceived as soft on Islam (to avoid controversy) or hard on Islam (because the author clearly has personal stakes there), it will be dismissed by both sides. The book's entire credibility rests on the claim of equal treatment. That claim must be airtight.

**4. No new reporting or scholarship.** Based on the sample, this is a book of observation, reflection, and interviews. There is no original research, no new historical discovery, no data analysis. The prose is strong enough to carry the book without those things, but an acquisitions committee will ask: what does this book know that we don't? The answer needs to be "it sees what we've been unable to see," not just "it's well written."

---

## 9. WHAT WOULD MAKE YOU ACQUIRE IT

**1. A pseudonym with a verified biographical note and a committed publicity strategy.** The author does not need to reveal their name. They need to reveal enough for the reader to trust them, and the publisher needs a plan to make the mystery an asset rather than a liability. Silhouette interviews, podcast appearances with voice alteration, a verified letter from the publisher confirming the author's background -- these are workable. The Elena Ferrante playbook exists. Use it.

**2. The full manuscript, and evidence that the middle keeps the promise of the opening.** If Chapters 4-14 are as good as 1-3, this is a significant book. If they are a notch below -- still strong but less narratively driven -- it is a good book. If they are substantially weaker, it is an opening in search of a book. I need to see the full text.

**3. A clear editorial vision for the Islam chapters.** The author needs to write about Islam at the same temperature as the other two faiths. Not hotter. Not cooler. The same. This may require an editor who can push back on the author's instinct to hedge. I would want to be that editor.

**4. Confidence that the author can sustain public attention without a name.** Can this person do interviews? Can they write op-eds? Can they respond to criticism? Anonymous authors who cannot engage with the public conversation around their book are commercially dead on arrival. Ferrante never gave interviews and it worked, but Ferrante was fiction and had a major Italian publisher behind her. Non-fiction requires engagement.

---

## 10. VERDICT

**Revise and resubmit.**

This is not a pass. I want to be clear about that. The prose is the best I have read in an unsolicited non-fiction submission in at least three years. The opening three chapters and the epilogue demonstrate a writer of genuine literary talent working on a subject of enormous commercial and cultural relevance. The emotional position -- neither pro-religion nor anti-religion, but honestly reckoning with the cost of both -- is the gap in the market that no major book currently fills. The human portraits in Chapter 3 are exceptional. The Author's Note is one of the best pieces of persuasive non-fiction writing I have encountered in this role.

But I cannot acquire a book with no author, no middle, and no publicity plan.

Here is what I need:

1. The full manuscript.
2. A pseudonym strategy with verified biographical details (at minimum: cultural background, approximate age, country of residence, relationship to the faith traditions discussed).
3. A one-page publicity concept: how will this book reach readers without a named author?
4. The author's willingness to work with an editor on the Islam sections to ensure parity of treatment.

If those four things arrive on my desk, I will take this to the acquisitions committee with a strong recommendation. The advance I would argue for, assuming the full manuscript delivers on the promise of the sample, would be in the range of GBP 75,000-150,000 for UK/Commonwealth rights, with a realistic expectation of earning out if the book receives the review attention it deserves and if the anonymity is handled as an asset rather than a deficit.

This book has the potential to be the most important popular book about religion since *God Is Not Great*. It is better written than Hitchens. It is more humane than Dawkins. It is more honest about its own limitations than anything in the New Atheist canon. The line "I believe that 'I don't know' is a braver position than either 'God exists' or 'God doesn't exist.' But I also believe it is the loneliest. I am tired of pretending it isn't" -- that is the thesis statement for a generation of people who have left religion and not yet found what to replace it with. That audience exists. It is large. And no one is speaking to it with this level of craft.

Send me the rest.
