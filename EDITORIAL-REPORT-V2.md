## OVERALL VERDICT

**The manuscript is significantly stronger than v1.** The voice holds across all 16 chapters. The emotional architecture is correct. The five belief statements in the Epilogue cost something. The book earns its ending.

The main remaining work is **factual precision** — not fabrication, but imprecision in citations, statistics, and a few historical details that a hostile reviewer would catch. There are also three unverifiable academic citations in Chapter 11 that must be resolved before publication.

### 🔵 STRUCTURAL NOTES

**Thomas is dropped after Chapter 3.** He is the lifelong Catholic whose faith "is as unremarkable and essential as breathing." Sarah, Yusuf, and Miriam all return in the Epilogue. Thomas does not. He represents the most common kind of believer — stable, undramatic, simply sustained — and his absence from the final chapters leaves the cast slightly unbalanced. Consider one sentence in the Epilogue: "I thought about Thomas too, in his pew, steady as stone. The ones who never doubted are harder to write about. That doesn't mean they're wrong."

**Two characters named David.** The soup kitchen volunteer in south London (Ch8) and the Modern Orthodox Jew from Chicago (Ch11) share a pseudonym. This may confuse readers. Consider renaming one.

**Composite character disclosure.** Multiple characters are presented as real, named people from real interviews: Paul (Ch12), Claire the hospice nurse (Ch12), Margaret of the Wednesday Club (Ch12), Tom the electrician (Ch13), Margaret Campbell / Glasgow celebrant (Ch13), Katrin (Ch14), Christine (Ch14), Dale (Ch14), Marie (Ch9), Denise (Ch10), Sarah (Ch11), James (Ch8). If any are composites or significantly anonymized/altered, standard non-fiction practice requires disclosure in an Author's Note. The specific details (Lyon, 1987; Nashville coffee shop; Memphis one-bedroom apartment) imply real individuals. This is not an accusation — it is a standard publishing requirement.
