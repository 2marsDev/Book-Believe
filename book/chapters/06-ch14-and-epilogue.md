# PART FIVE
# FORWARD

## Chapter 14: What Comes Next

Here is what is actually happening.

In the Netherlands, in 1958, sixty percent of the population attended church regularly. Today that number is under ten percent. In a single lifetime, a country went from majority churchgoing to almost entirely secular. The churches are still there. You can see them in every Dutch city — beautiful old buildings, now converted into bookshops, apartments, climbing gyms. A friend in Amsterdam told me he lives in a former Catholic church. His bedroom is where the altar was. He said it like he was telling me the previous owner had installed new countertops.

Britain crossed a threshold sometime in the last decade. For the first time in its recorded history, the non-religious are now the majority. The Church of England, once the spiritual backbone of an empire, can't fill its pews. The average age of an Anglican churchgoer is sixty-one. They aren't being replaced.

And in the United States — the great outlier, the most religious wealthy nation on Earth for as long as anyone has been measuring — the fastest-growing religious category is "none." Twenty-eight percent of Americans now identify with no religion. Among adults under thirty, it's closer to forty percent. In 1972, when Gallup first asked the question, the number was five percent.

This is not a blip. This is an avalanche.

*     *     *

I spent a week with the Pew Research Center's data, and the picture is clear if you're willing to see it whole.

In the industrialized West, religion is in freefall. Every metric — church attendance, prayer frequency, belief in God, identification with a faith tradition — is declining, and the decline is accelerating. Each generation is less religious than the one before it, and they're not coming back as they age. The old assumption that people get more religious as they get older is no longer holding. Millennials who left religion in their twenties are now in their forties, and they haven't returned. Generation Z is starting from an even lower baseline.

But here is the part that most secular commentators leave out, because it complicates the story they want to tell.

Globally, religion is growing.

Not declining. Growing. The world is becoming more religious, not less. Africa is being won — simultaneously — by Christianity and Islam. Pentecostalism is exploding across sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America, and Southeast Asia. Islam is the fastest-growing religion on Earth by fertility rates alone, even before conversion. By 2050, according to Pew's projections, Muslims will nearly equal Christians in global numbers. By 2100, they may surpass them.

The global south is more religious now than it was fifty years ago. The secularization thesis — the confident twentieth-century prediction that modernization would inevitably kill religion — was not a universal truth. It was a description of what happened in northwestern Europe. And even there, it's not the clean story that secular triumphalists want it to be.

Because here's what declining religiosity actually looks like, up close.

*     *     *

I visited a town in eastern Germany — the former DDR — that is now one of the least religious places on Earth. Fewer than fifteen percent of residents identify with any faith. The churches are empty. Religiosity was systematically suppressed for forty years under communism and never recovered after reunification.

I expected to find liberation. Freethinking. A thriving secular culture.

What I found was loneliness.

I talked to a social worker named Katrin who has spent twenty years working in these communities. She told me something I've been unable to forget.

"The churches used to hold people," she said. "Not just on Sundays. When your mother died, the church held you. When your child was sick, the church came. When you were drinking too much and your wife had left, the pastor knocked on your door. Not because you asked. Because that's what the church did."

She paused.

"Now when your mother dies, you call a funeral company. They handle it. Very professional. And then you go home, and nobody knocks on your door. Nobody. Because nobody knows. Because there is no structure that notices."

I asked her if people were happier now. Without religion. Without the superstition and the guilt and the rules.

She gave me a look that made me feel naive.

"They're freer," she said. "They're also more alone. You tell me which matters more."

*     *     *

The data backs her up.

Across the Western world, the decline of religious participation has coincided with a rise in what sociologists call "social atomization." Loneliness epidemics. Collapsing civic engagement. Declining membership in every kind of voluntary association — not just churches, but clubs, unions, community groups, neighborhood organizations. Robert Putnam documented it in *Bowling Alone* twenty-five years ago. It's gotten worse.

Religion was never just about God. It was about Thursday night Bible study and Sunday potluck and the women's group that brought meals when someone was sick and the men's group that fixed your porch when you couldn't afford to hire someone. It was infrastructure. Social infrastructure at a scale that nothing has replaced.

People who leave religion don't stop needing community. They don't stop needing ritual. They don't stop needing a framework for meaning, a way to mark transitions, a place to go when the worst thing happens.

They just stop having those things.

And into that vacuum, something else moves.

*     *     *

I want to show you something.

QAnon, at its peak, had an estimated thirty million American adherents or sympathizers. Its structure is immediately recognizable to anyone who has studied apocalyptic religion. There is a hidden truth known only to the initiated. There is a great evil — a cabal of powerful elites engaged in unspeakable acts. There is a coming reckoning — "the Storm" — when the evil will be exposed and the righteous vindicated. There is a prophet figure who speaks in coded language that requires interpretation by the faithful. There is a community of believers who reinforce each other's commitment and ostracize doubters.

This is not politics. This is eschatology. End-times theology with different vocabulary.

Or look at the wellness industry. Gwyneth Paltrow's Goop empire doesn't call itself a religion, but its structure is religious in every way that matters. There is a path to purification. There are dietary laws. There are forbidden substances (toxins, chemicals, processed foods) and sacred ones (crystals, essential oils, alkaline water). There is a priestly class of practitioners who mediate between you and the higher truth. There is an eschatological promise: if you follow the path faithfully enough, you will be transformed. You will transcend your current fallen state. You will be healed.

The language is different. The mechanism is identical.

*     *     *

And then there's Silicon Valley.

I spent three days at a conference in San Francisco that was nominally about technology and philanthropy. What I actually attended was a revival meeting.

The theology went like this: Artificial intelligence will achieve superintelligence within our lifetimes. This will represent either the salvation or the destruction of humanity, depending on whether the right people control it. The right people are, conveniently, the people in the room. Through correct alignment of AI systems — and through the philosophical framework of effective altruism — the elect can steer humanity toward a future of unimaginable abundance. Suffering will be eliminated. Death itself will become optional. The faithful who invest in the right companies and support the right research will be, in a meaningful sense, the saviors of the species.

I sat in that room and listened to men worth billions of dollars talk about defeating death with the same absolute conviction I'd heard from pastors in Mississippi talking about the Second Coming.

The structure is the same. The eschatology is the same. The saved and the damned. The great transformation. The promise that right belief leads to transcendence.

Ray Kurzweil's Singularity is the Rapture for people with engineering degrees. Cryonics — freezing your body in liquid nitrogen so that future technology can resurrect you — is bodily resurrection for people who read *Wired* instead of the Bible. The longevity movement's promise that death is a "disease" that can be "cured" is eternal life with a venture capital pitch deck.

These are not metaphors. These are structural equivalences. The human need for a salvation narrative doesn't disappear when you stop believing in God. It puts on a different outfit.

*     *     *

Nationalism fits the same template.

The nation as sacred. Its founding as genesis myth. Its fallen soldiers as martyrs. Its flag as sacred object. Its anthem as hymn. Its enemies as demonic forces. The idea that the nation has a divine — or at least a special, exceptional — destiny.

Watch a political rally. Any country, any party that's found its stride. Watch the crowd. Watch the faces. Watch what happens when the leader speaks and the audience responds in unison, thousands of voices merging into one, the individual dissolving into something larger.

That's worship. It has always been worship. The object has changed. The neurological experience has not.

*     *     *

Here is the problem.

The new religions are worse.

I don't say that lightly, and I don't say it because I'm nostalgic for Christianity. I say it because the traditional religions, whatever their flaws — and this book has documented many of them — had something that their secular replacements lack.

They had millennia of practice at being wrong.

Christianity has had two thousand years to develop theological brakes — internal mechanisms for self-correction, humility, restraint. "Judge not, lest ye be judged." "Let he who is without sin cast the first stone." "The meek shall inherit the earth." These aren't just nice phrases. They're institutional antibodies. They don't always work. They often fail catastrophically. But they exist.

QAnon has no brakes. Nationalism has no brakes. The wellness industry has no Sermon on the Mount. Silicon Valley's effective altruism collapsed the moment its most prominent evangelist turned out to be running one of the largest frauds in history, and nobody within the movement had the theological architecture to ask, before it happened, whether a twenty-nine-year-old who claimed to be optimizing the future of humanity might be worth scrutinizing.

When people leave religion, they don't stop being religious. They stop having good guardrails for the religious impulse. And the impulse, unguarded, produces monsters.

*     *     *

So what do we actually need?

I've spent the last section of this book trying to answer that honestly. Chapter 12 dealt with what we lose. Chapter 13 dealt with what we keep. Now I'm standing in the space between the old world and whatever comes next, and I have to admit something.

I don't know what comes next.

But I know what I think it needs to contain.

It needs community infrastructure at scale. Not online communities. Physical places where people show up on a regular schedule, see the same faces, build relationships across lines of age and class and interest. Churches did this. Nothing else has replicated it. We need secular institutions — not just gyms and co-working spaces — that take seriously the work of binding people together. That do the boring, unglamorous work of checking on the widow and welcoming the newcomer and sitting with the dying.

It needs honest rituals. Rituals for grief that don't require lying about what death is. Rituals for birth that don't require pretending a baby is stained with original sin. Rituals for the transitions of life — adulthood, marriage, parenthood, aging, loss — that mark them as significant without invoking the supernatural. The humanist movement has made progress here. It's not enough. The rituals are still thin. They lack the weight of centuries. They will need time.

It needs a shared moral language that doesn't rely on divine authority. This is harder than it sounds. "Because God says so" is an extraordinarily efficient moral technology. It settles arguments instantly. It provides a foundation that feels absolute. Without it, we have to do the much harder work of building moral consensus from human needs, human suffering, human flourishing. We have to argue. We have to persuade. We have to accept that moral progress is slow and messy and reversible. That's not a bug. That's what intellectual honesty costs.

And it needs institutions that take the long view. Churches think in centuries. They plant trees they won't live to see mature. They maintain buildings for generations. They invest in the formation of young people with a patience that most secular institutions can't match. We need that patience. We need organizations that are not optimized for quarterly returns or election cycles. That think about what kind of civilization we are building for the people who will be here after we're gone.

*     *     *

There is a question I can't answer.

I have two children. They are young. They will grow up in a world that is less religious than the one I grew up in, which was already less religious than my parents' world.

Someday — maybe soon — one of them will ask me: "Is there anything after we die?"

I know what the believer says. They say yes. They say it with conviction and love and total sincerity. And the child who hears it is comforted.

I can't say yes. I don't believe it. I won't lie to my children.

But what do I say instead?

I've turned this over a thousand times. I've read the secular parenting books. I've talked to other non-religious parents. I've considered the philosophical arguments.

And the best I've come up with is this: I don't know.

Not "no." I don't know.

I don't know what happens after we die. Nobody does. The people who say they know — whether they say heaven or nothing — are claiming a certainty they don't have. What I know is that you are here now. That I love you now. That the people who love you will remember you, and that the things you do and say will ripple outward in ways you can't predict. And that the honest answer to the biggest question is: we don't know. And that not knowing is not a failure. It's where real courage begins.

It's not as comforting as heaven.

But it's true. And I have decided — slowly, painfully, over the course of writing this book — that I would rather give my children something true than something beautiful.

Even when the beautiful thing would be easier. Even when the true thing leaves a silence that I don't know how to fill.

*     *     *

I don't know what comes next.

I know what I want it to contain: honesty, community, courage, and the willingness to sit with uncertainty without reaching for easy answers.

That's not a religion. It's not a movement. It's barely a philosophy.

But it's what I have. And standing here — at the end of a long road that started on a beach in southern Europe, watching a woman sweat in black fabric while her husband ate ice cream — it feels like enough.

Not comfortable. Not triumphant.

Enough.

The inquiry is over. What I found is what I found. The question now is not what do I believe. It's how do I live.

That's a different book. Maybe a harder one.

But first, I want to go back to where this started.

# EPILOGUE
## Facing the Unknown

I'm on a beach again.

Not the same one. Different country, different year. But the same sea. The Mediterranean doesn't change. It was here before any of the religions I've spent years studying, and it will be here after the last one is forgotten. The water doesn't care what we believe. It just keeps arriving at the shore.

It's September. The crowds have thinned. The light has that late-season quality — golden, lower, the kind that makes everything look like a painting of itself. My children are at the water's edge, doing what children do. Building something. Destroying it. Building it again.

I'm watching them and I'm thinking about a woman in a niqab.

*     *     *

Years ago, on a beach not far from here, I saw something I couldn't look away from. A woman sealed in black fabric in thirty-five-degree heat. Her husband beside her in board shorts, eating ice cream. Their children running between them. Nobody troubled. Nobody questioning.

That image launched this whole thing. Every chapter. Every sleepless night in a hotel room with a stack of theology books. Every interview. Every argument. Every uncomfortable dinner where I said what I actually thought and watched the temperature in the room drop.

I've been looking at it for years now. Does it look different?

Yes. It does.

I still see the asymmetry. I still see one person's comfort purchased at the cost of another person's freedom. I still can't call that holy.

But I see other things now too. I see a woman who may have a relationship with her faith that is real and deep and sustaining in ways I will never fully understand, because I have never been inside it. I see a tradition that gives her a framework for her entire life — from birth to death, from waking to sleeping, from how she eats to how she grieves — and I know, now, what it costs to live without that framework. I've spent this whole book looking at what it costs to live with it. The cost of living without it is also real.

I still think the asymmetry is wrong. I still think a religion that covers women and frees men is expressing power, not piety.

But I hold it differently now. With less certainty about my own clarity. With more awareness of what I can't see from the outside.

That doesn't make her covering right. It makes my seeing incomplete.

*     *     *

I called Sarah last month.

Sarah — from Chapter 3 — the evangelical woman in Leeds who told me, over tea in her kitchen, that she talks to God every morning and He answers. Not in words. In feelings. In a sense of presence that is as real to her as the chair she's sitting in.

I'd asked her then: "What if it's not real? What if it's just you, talking to yourself?"

And she'd smiled — not offended, not defensive, just calm — and said: "Then I'm talking to myself in a way that makes me kinder, more patient, and less afraid of dying. What's your method doing for you?"

I didn't have a good answer then. I'm not sure I have one now.

She's still in Leeds. Still in the same church. Her daughter got married last year — in the church, of course. Sarah told me the ceremony was beautiful. The hymns, the vows before God, the whole community gathered in a building that had held them through births and deaths and everything in between.

"I know you don't believe any of it," she said on the phone. "That's all right. I pray for you anyway."

She laughed when she said it. I laughed too. There was warmth in it. Real warmth.

I thought about what Katrin, the social worker in eastern Germany, had told me. About the door that nobody knocks on anymore. Sarah's church knocks on doors. It brings meals. It sits with the dying. It does the slow, unglamorous, profoundly human work of showing up.

I haven't found the secular equivalent. Not yet. Maybe not in my lifetime.

That's not an argument for God. It's an argument for paying attention to what we're losing while we celebrate what we're gaining.

*     *     *

Yusuf — the imam from Chapter 3 — has moved. He left Manchester for a position in Toronto. We exchanged emails. I told him I was finishing the book. He asked if he'd come off well. I told him the truth: he'd come off as a thoughtful, serious man whose faith I couldn't share but whose sincerity I couldn't question.

He wrote back one line: "That is enough."

And Miriam — the rabbi in North London who told me, over Friday night dinner, that Judaism's greatest strength was its comfort with argument. "We don't need you to believe," she'd said. "We need you to wrestle."

She's still there. Still wrestling. She sent me an article she'd written about young Jews leaving the faith, and about her refusal to panic about it. "The tradition survived Babylon, Rome, the Inquisition, and the Shoah," she wrote. "It will survive your book."

I hope she's right. Not because I want religion to survive in its current form. But because the impulse behind it — the human need to make meaning, to bind together, to face death with something other than silence — that impulse deserves to survive. It is, in some ways, the best thing about us.

The question is whether we can keep the impulse and lose the lies.

I don't know.

*     *     *

Here is what I believe, after all of it.

I believe that the supernatural claims of Christianity, Islam, and Judaism are almost certainly not true. I believe this not because I want it to be the case, but because the evidence points there and I have decided to follow the evidence. A man did not rise from the dead. An angel did not dictate a book in a cave. A sea did not part for a nation of slaves. These are human stories — beautiful, powerful, civilizationally significant human stories — but human.

I believe that the people who believe these stories are not stupid. They are not weak. They are not broken. They are human beings doing what human beings have always done: using the best tools available to face the terror of existence. Their tools have real costs — I've documented those costs honestly, I hope — but the terror they're facing is real, and dismissing their response to it as mere ignorance is its own kind of stupidity.

I believe that losing religion costs more than most atheists admit. Community. Ritual. A shared moral language. A framework for grief. A place to go when the worst thing happens. The confident secular position — "we don't need any of that" — is wrong. We need all of it. We just need versions of it that don't require us to believe things that aren't true.

I believe that "I don't know" is a braver position than either "God exists" or "God doesn't exist." The honest answer to the ultimate questions — why is there something rather than nothing? what happens after death? is there meaning beyond what we make? — is that we do not know. We may never know. And the willingness to stand in that uncertainty, without reaching for comforting fictions or aggressive certainties, is the closest thing I've found to wisdom.

I believe that the next century will be defined by whether we can build secular institutions that do what religious institutions did — bind people together, mark the passages of life, provide meaning in suffering, take the long view — without the supernatural scaffolding. I don't know if we can. The track record so far is not encouraging. But the alternative — returning to beliefs we know are false because the truth is too cold — is worse.

*     *     *

My daughter is running toward me. She's wet and sandy and holding something — a shell, I think. She wants to show me.

This is what I have. This moment. This beach. This child who will someday ask me questions I can't answer. This light that is fading because the Earth is turning, not because God is dimming the sky. This body that will stop working, and there is probably nothing after that, and I have to be okay with that, and most days I am, and some days I am not.

I think about the woman in the niqab. I think about Sarah in her kitchen, talking to a God I don't believe in, becoming kinder because of it. I think about Yusuf, bowing toward Mecca five times a day, oriented in the world in a way I will never be. I think about Miriam, lighting Shabbat candles on Friday night, connecting herself to a chain of human beings stretching back three thousand years.

I can't join them. I've looked too carefully at the foundations.

But I can see what they have. And I can admit — without shame, without apology — that there is a version of what they have that I want. Not the God part. Not the afterlife part. Not the rules about what to eat and who to love and what women should wear on a beach.

The other part. The part where you are not alone. The part where your suffering means something. The part where someone knocks on your door.

*     *     *

The sun is getting lower. My children are getting cold. It's time to go.

I don't have a conclusion. I have something less satisfying and more honest: a description of where I ended up.

I ended up in a room where all the furniture has been removed. It's the same room — same walls, same windows — but everything that used to fill it is gone. The old certainties. The old comforts. The stories that used to make the dark feel smaller.

There's more space now. And it's colder. And the light comes in differently.

I stand in it. I look around. I notice that the room is still standing. That I am still standing. That outside the window, the world is exactly as beautiful and as terrible as it was when the room was full, and that I can see it more clearly now, even if the clarity comes at a cost.

My daughter hands me the shell. It's not remarkable. Just a shell. Ordinary calcium carbonate, built by an animal that is probably dead, shaped by the same blind forces that shaped everything, including us.

She thinks it's beautiful. She's right. It is.

I close my hand around it. I take her other hand in mine. We walk back up the beach toward the road, toward the car, toward home, toward whatever comes next.

Behind us, the sea keeps doing what it has always done.

It doesn't need us to believe in it.

It just keeps coming in.
