EPILOGUE
## Facing the Unknown

I'm on a beach again.

Not the same one. Different country, different year. But the same sea. The Mediterranean doesn't change. It was here before any of the religions I've spent years studying, and it will be here after the last one is forgotten. The water doesn't care what we believe. It just keeps arriving at the shore.

It's September. The crowds have thinned. The light has that late-season quality — golden, lower, the kind that makes everything look like a painting of itself. My children are at the water's edge, doing what children do. Building something. Destroying it. Building it again.

I'm watching them and I'm thinking about a woman in a niqab.

*     *     *

Years ago, on a beach not far from here, I saw something I couldn't look away from. A woman sealed in black fabric in thirty-five-degree heat. Her husband beside her in board shorts, eating ice cream. Their children running between them. Nobody troubled. Nobody questioning.

That image launched this whole thing. Every chapter. Every sleepless night in a hotel room with a stack of theology books. Every interview. Every argument. Every uncomfortable dinner where I said what I actually thought and watched the temperature in the room drop.

I've been looking at it for years now. Does it look different?

Yes. It does.

I still see the asymmetry. I still see one person's comfort purchased at the cost of another person's freedom. I still can't call that holy.

But I see other things now too. I see a woman who may have a relationship with her faith that is real and deep and sustaining in ways I will never fully understand, because I have never been inside it. I see a tradition that gives her a framework for her entire life — from birth to death, from waking to sleeping, from how she eats to how she grieves — and I know, now, what it costs to live without that framework. I've spent this whole book looking at what it costs to live with it. The cost of living without it is also real.

I still think the asymmetry is wrong. I still think a religion that covers women and frees men is expressing power, not piety.

But I hold it differently now. With less certainty about my own clarity. With more awareness of what I can't see from the outside.

That doesn't make her covering right. It makes my seeing incomplete.

*     *     *

I called Sarah last month.

Sarah — from Chapter 3 — the evangelical woman in Leeds who told me, over tea in her kitchen, that she talks to God every morning and He answers. Not in words. In feelings. In a sense of presence that is as real to her as the chair she's sitting in.

I'd asked her then: "What if it's not real? What if it's just you, talking to yourself?"

And she'd smiled — not offended, not defensive, just calm — and said: "Then I'm talking to myself in a way that makes me kinder, more patient, and less afraid of dying. What's your method doing for you?"

I didn't have a good answer then. I'm not sure I have one now.

She's still in Leeds. Still in the same church. Her daughter got married last year — in the church, of course. Sarah told me the ceremony was beautiful. The hymns, the vows before God, the whole community gathered in a building that had held them through births and deaths and everything in between.

"I know you don't believe any of it," she said on the phone. "That's all right. I pray for you anyway."

She laughed when she said it. I laughed too. There was warmth in it. Real warmth.

I thought about what Katrin, the social worker in eastern Germany, had told me. About the door that nobody knocks on anymore. Sarah's church knocks on doors. It brings meals. It sits with the dying. It does the slow, unglamorous, profoundly human work of showing up.

I haven't found the secular equivalent. Not yet. Maybe not in my lifetime.

That's not an argument for God. It's an argument for paying attention to what we're losing while we celebrate what we're gaining.

*     *     *

Yusuf — the imam from Chapter 3 — has moved. He left Manchester for a position in Toronto. We exchanged emails. I told him I was finishing the book. He asked if he'd come off well. I told him the truth: he'd come off as a thoughtful, serious man whose faith I couldn't share but whose sincerity I couldn't question.

He wrote back one line: "That is enough."

And Miriam — the rabbi in North London who told me, over Friday night dinner, that Judaism's greatest strength was its comfort with argument. "We don't need you to believe," she'd said. "We need you to wrestle."

She's still there. Still wrestling. She sent me an article she'd written about young Jews leaving the faith, and about her refusal to panic about it. "The tradition survived Babylon, Rome, the Inquisition, and the Shoah," she wrote. "It will survive your book."

I hope she's right. Not because I want religion to survive in its current form. But because the impulse behind it — the human need to make meaning, to bind together, to face death with something other than silence — that impulse deserves to survive. It is, in some ways, the best thing about us.

The question is whether we can keep the impulse and lose the lies.

I don't know.

*     *     *

Here is what I believe, after all of it.

I believe that the supernatural claims of Christianity, Islam, and Judaism are almost certainly not true. I believe this not because I want it to be the case, but because the evidence points there and I have decided to follow the evidence. A man did not rise from the dead. An angel did not dictate a book in a cave. A sea did not part for a nation of slaves. These are human stories — beautiful, powerful, civilizationally significant human stories — but human.

I believe that the people who believe these stories are not stupid. They are not weak. They are not broken. They are human beings doing what human beings have always done: using the best tools available to face the terror of existence. Their tools have real costs — I've documented those costs honestly, I hope — but the terror they're facing is real, and dismissing their response to it as mere ignorance is its own kind of stupidity.

I believe that losing religion costs more than most atheists admit. Community. Ritual. A shared moral language. A framework for grief. A place to go when the worst thing happens. The confident secular position — "we don't need any of that" — is wrong. We need all of it. We just need versions of it that don't require us to believe things that aren't true.

I believe that "I don't know" is a braver position than either "God exists" or "God doesn't exist." The honest answer to the ultimate questions — why is there something rather than nothing? what happens after death? is there meaning beyond what we make? — is that we do not know. We may never know. And the willingness to stand in that uncertainty, without reaching for comforting fictions or aggressive certainties, is the closest thing I've found to wisdom.

I believe that the next century will be defined by whether we can build secular institutions that do what religious institutions did — bind people together, mark the passages of life, provide meaning in suffering, take the long view — without the supernatural scaffolding. I don't know if we can. The track record so far is not encouraging. But the alternative — returning to beliefs we know are false because the truth is too cold — is worse.

*     *     *

My daughter is running toward me. She's wet and sandy and holding something — a shell, I think. She wants to show me.

This is what I have. This moment. This beach. This child who will someday ask me questions I can't answer. This light that is fading because the Earth is turning, not because God is dimming the sky. This body that will stop working, and there is probably nothing after that, and I have to be okay with that, and most days I am, and some days I am not.

I think about the woman in the niqab. I think about Sarah in her kitchen, talking to a God I don't believe in, becoming kinder because of it. I think about Yusuf, bowing toward Mecca five times a day, oriented in the world in a way I will never be. I think about Miriam, lighting Shabbat candles on Friday night, connecting herself to a chain of human beings stretching back three thousand years.

I can't join them. I've looked too carefully at the foundations.

But I can see what they have. And I can admit — without shame, without apology — that there is a version of what they have that I want. Not the God part. Not the afterlife part. Not the rules about what to eat and who to love and what women should wear on a beach.

The other part. The part where you are not alone. The part where your suffering means something. The part where someone knocks on your door.

*     *     *

The sun is getting lower. My children are getting cold. It's time to go.

I don't have a conclusion. I have something less satisfying and more honest: a description of where I ended up.

I ended up in a room where all the furniture has been removed. It's the same room — same walls, same windows — but everything that used to fill it is gone. The old certainties. The old comforts. The stories that used to make the dark feel smaller.

There's more space now. And it's colder. And the light comes in differently.

I stand in it. I look around. I notice that the room is still standing. That I am still standing. That outside the window, the world is exactly as beautiful and as terrible as it was when the room was full, and that I can see it more clearly now, even if the clarity comes at a cost.

My daughter hands me the shell. It's not remarkable. Just a shell. Ordinary calcium carbonate, built by an animal that is probably dead, shaped by the same blind forces that shaped everything, including us.

She thinks it's beautiful. She's right. It is.

I close my hand around it. I take her other hand in mine. We walk back up the beach toward the road, toward the car, toward home, toward whatever comes next.

Behind us, the sea keeps doing what it has always done.

It doesn't need us to believe in it.

It just keeps coming in.
