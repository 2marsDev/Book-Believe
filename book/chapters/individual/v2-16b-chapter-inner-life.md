## Chapter: The Inner Life

There is a man I used to watch through the window of a mosque on Fridays.

Not watch in any sinister way — the window faced the street, and in the few minutes before the congregation closed the doors for the khutbah I would sometimes slow my pace to look inside. He was elderly, North African I think, with a small white taqiyah and a grey cardigan that looked like it had survived several decades. He sat in the front row and he prayed before the service began, rocking forward and back in a motion so slight you could almost miss it — just a few centimetres, a breath of movement, a pendulum almost perfectly stilled. His lips moved. His eyes were closed. The room around him was filling up — young men arriving, finding spots, straightening their rows — and he was entirely elsewhere. Not absent in the checked-out way of someone bored. Absent in the way of someone who has arrived somewhere else.

I watched him for perhaps thirty seconds on several different Fridays. I don't know his name. I don't know what he was saying. But I could not look at him and feel nothing. There was something in the quality of his stillness that I couldn't explain away, and the honest thing is to begin there.

*     *     *

This chapter is the hardest one to write. Not because the subject is complicated — though it is — but because it requires me to be honest in a way the others haven't. I can analyse theology from a distance. I can trace the violence, examine the contradictions, hold the historical record up to the light. Those are tasks I'm comfortable with. They ask for rigor and fairness, and I can provide both without being destabilised.

But this.

This is about what happens inside. What people report when they pray, when they meditate, when they submit their attention entirely to something beyond themselves. And here I have a problem: I cannot dismiss it. I've read too much, listened too carefully, watched that man in the mosque too many times. Something happens in genuine religious practice that I don't fully understand. And this book owes an honest account of it.

So let me try.

*     *     *

First, what prayer claims to be.

Most of us who grew up outside religion imagine prayer as a form of asking — petitioning an invisible manager for better outcomes. Please let the biopsy be negative. Please let my flight be on time. Please let the team win. And plenty of prayer is exactly that. Walk through the supplication sections of any tradition and you find human beings doing what human beings do: negotiating with power, hoping for favour, projecting their anxiety into the void.

But the serious theologians of every tradition are quietly embarrassed by this, and they have a different account.

In Christian mystical theology, prayer is not speaking to God so much as being in the presence of God. The Carmelite tradition — rooted in the work of Teresa of Ávila and John of the Cross — describes prayer as an interior journey toward union with the divine. Teresa, the sixteenth-century Spanish nun who reformed her order while also being investigated by the Inquisition, wrote of prayer not as communication in any ordinary sense but as a series of interior rooms. Her book *The Interior Castle*, written in 1577, describes the soul as a castle with seven mansions. Most people live in the outer courts. The work of prayer is to move inward, room by room, until you reach the centre — where, she says, God already lives. God isn't somewhere you travel to. God is the centre you've been keeping yourself from.

This is not a comfortable theology. It asks something much harder than belief. It asks you to be quiet. To sit with yourself until the noise stops. Most people, Teresa observed, never even enter the first mansion. They're too busy to notice they're outside.

In Islam, the word most often translated as prayer — salah — means something closer to connection. The five daily prayers aren't primarily petitionary: they are acts of orientation, five moments in every day when a Muslim turns toward something and performs an act of conscious submission. The Arabic root s-l-y also carries connotations of drawing near, of relationship. And beyond the obligatory prayers there is dhikr — literally "remembrance" — the repetitive recitation of God's names or phrases, sometimes aloud, sometimes silently, sometimes in rhythmic movement with others. In Sufi practice, dhikr becomes something more radical still: a systematic dissolution of the ordinary self.

In Judaism, the concept is kavanah — intention, the directed attention of the heart. Prayer without kavanah is considered technically incomplete. It is the husk without the grain. The Hasidic masters understood prayer as a practice of devekut — cleaving to God, a constant awareness of the divine presence threaded through ordinary moments. The davening body — the swaying, rocking prayer posture associated with Jewish worship — has been linked to Psalm 35:10: *All my bones shall say, Lord, who is like unto thee?* Not just the mind. The whole body praying. Bones included.

What these traditions share is not a theology — they differ enormously in their theologies — but a phenomenology. They agree on what prayer at its most serious level is supposed to feel like, and supposed to do. It quiets the self. It opens something. It produces, at its best, a state that is variously described as peace, love, union, presence, or annihilation, depending on the tradition. It is not primarily cognitive. It is, whatever it is, more like something than like thinking about something.

*     *     *

The mystical traditions are where this gets most extreme, and most interesting.

Sufism is Islam's interior tradition, and its goal is stated with a clarity that most of Western religion has been too nervous to match. The concept is fana — annihilation. The dissolution of the individual self in the divine. Rumi, the thirteenth-century Persian poet who is now paradoxically the best-selling poet in the United States, wrote about this with erotic urgency. His Masnavi is a long argument that the soul's longing for God is the deepest thing in existence — that the self's dissatisfaction with itself is actually evidence of its origins. A reed cut from the reed bed makes music from its wound. We are the reed. The music is our grief. And grief, he says, is the path home.

Dhikr is the technology. The Mevlevi order — the whirling dervishes — developed a form of dhikr involving spinning prayer in which the right hand reaches upward, the left hand down, and the body becomes an axis between heaven and earth, receiving and distributing the divine energy. The whirling is not performance, though it has become spectacle. It is a practice with a specific interior aim: to dislodge the ego from its centrality. When self-consciousness is sufficiently suspended, something else moves into the vacancy. Rumi calls it the Friend. Others call it God. The practitioners call it, when they try to describe it, essentially indescribable.

Kabbalah — Jewish mysticism — developed a different map but walks a similar country. The sefirot are the ten aspects or attributes of the divine, depicted in a structure called the Tree of Life: Keter at the crown (the ineffable, unmediated divine); Chokhmah (wisdom); Binah (understanding); and down through seven more stations to Malkhut, the divine presence as it manifests in the material world. The map is not the territory. But the map suggests something important: God is not a being who lives somewhere else. God is the deep structure of reality, and the mystical work is learning to see it. The Kabbalist doesn't pray to God from outside. The Kabbalist tries to align with the divine flow that is already present in every level of creation.

Christian mysticism has a geography of extremity that is almost unbearable to look at directly. John of the Cross — Teresa's younger contemporary, fellow Carmelite, fellow reformer, fellow suspect of the Inquisition — wrote *Dark Night of the Soul* as an account of something he'd lived through: a period of complete interior desolation in which all consolation was withdrawn, in which prayer felt like speaking into stone, in which God seemed not absent but deliberately, almost cruelly silent. He called this not failure but purgation. The soul is being emptied of its dependence on the feeling of God's presence so that it can come to know God without the mediation of feeling. It is, he wrote, a gift — the hardest possible gift to receive.

Thomas Merton, the Trappist monk who died in Bangkok in 1968 just as he was deepening his encounter with Zen and Sufism, wrote about this geography with a twentieth-century honesty that earlier mystics didn't have access to. He doubted. He was lonely. He fell in love with a nurse when he was fifty-one and kept a secret journal. He found the monastery claustrophobic and couldn't stop thinking about the wider world. And through all of this, he continued to practice contemplative prayer with a seriousness that never seemed performative, and continued to insist — with a specificity that I find hard to dismiss — that something happened in that practice that had no name and required none.

"At the center of our being," he wrote, "is a point of nothingness which is untouched by sin and illusion, a point of pure truth, a point or spark which belongs entirely to God... This little point of nothingness and absolute poverty is the pure glory of God in us."

I read that sentence many times. I do not know what it means. But I am not sure it means nothing.

Then there is Simone Weil — perhaps the most unusual figure in all of modern religious thought. A secular Jew, a philosopher, a factory worker by political choice, a refugee. She never converted to Christianity, refused baptism repeatedly, and yet she described mystical encounters with Christ that were as specific and physically overwhelming as anything in Teresa. "Christ himself came down and took possession of me," she wrote. She resisted making too much of this, insisted on uncertainty, and never stopped asking the hardest questions. Her concept of attention — the disciplined, self-emptying act of attending fully to reality without projection — she described as a form of prayer available to anyone, believer or not. "Attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity."

Weil starved herself to death in 1943 at thirty-four, partly in solidarity with the occupied French she had fled, partly in what looked like a long wager with her own body. I don't hold her up as a model. But I hold her up as evidence that something happened in contemplative attention that even the most rigorous intellectual could not explain away.

*     *     *

The scientists have been paying attention, too, and this is where it gets genuinely strange.

Andrew Newberg, the neuroscientist who coined the term neurotheology, has spent decades scanning the brains of people in prayer and meditation. His subjects have included Franciscan nuns praying, Tibetan Buddhist monks in deep meditation, Pentecostals speaking in tongues, and Sufi practitioners engaged in dhikr. The findings are not simple — they never are — but one pattern recurs: decreased activity in the posterior superior parietal cortex. This region of the brain maintains what researchers call the orientation association area: the neurological sense of where your body ends and the rest of the world begins. It is, in the most literal sense, the hardware of self-hood. When it quiets, the experienced boundary between self and not-self begins to dissolve.

This is what the mystics call union.

There are two ways to respond to this finding. One is to say: so mystical experience is just an artefact of decreased parietal activity. The brain produces the feeling. God is not required. The other is to ask a harder question: why does every serious contemplative tradition across history and culture converge on methods that produce this specific neurological state? Why does it reliably produce experiences that practitioners describe as more real, not less, than ordinary consciousness? Why do people come back from these experiences changed — sometimes permanently — in ways that appear, by any available measure, to be good?

William James, writing in 1902 with a precision that has not been surpassed, described the marks of mystical experience: ineffability (it cannot be adequately communicated), noetic quality (it feels like knowledge, not just feeling), transiency (it does not last), and passivity (it happens to you; you cannot force it). He was not a believer in any orthodox sense. But he was a serious psychologist, and he thought these experiences were among the most significant data in human life. "The further limits of our being," he wrote, "plunge into an altogether other dimension of existence from the sensible and understandable world. Name it the mystical region, or the supernatural region, whichever you choose."

The astronauts who have looked back at Earth from orbit describe something that overlaps, almost point for point, with mystical experience. Edgar Mitchell, who walked on the Moon in 1971, described a moment on the return journey when he looked out the window and felt "an instantaneous global consciousness" — the conviction that the universe was, in some sense, purposeful, that the separation between self and world was a useful fiction and not the final truth. He was not a religious man. He had no framework for it. He spent the rest of his life trying to make sense of it.

I mention this not to steal from religion the authority of its own experience, but to note that the experience itself seems to be available by more routes than any one tradition controls. What religion has done — what it has done better than anything else in human history — is develop systematic methods for arriving there, and communities of practice to support the journey, and languages rich enough to describe what happens and what it might mean.

That is not nothing.

*     *     *

I tried to sit quietly and pray.

I want to be honest about this. I'm not a meditator. I have never sustained a contemplative practice of any kind. My relationship to silence is adversarial — I fill it with podcasts, with movement, with the small frantic manoeuvres of a mind that would rather be anywhere than still. So what I did was not prayer in any serious sense. It was an experiment conducted by someone who doesn't know what he's doing, and the results should be weighted accordingly.

I sat in my study, which was as quiet as I could make it at ten o'clock on a Tuesday evening. I put my phone in another room. I sat in a chair rather than lying down — something I'd read was important, to prevent the body interpreting stillness as sleep. I had no technique, no tradition, no teacher. I was borrowing nothing but the basic form: sit still, close your eyes, try to be present.

The first ten minutes were, I'll be honest, absurd. My mind produced a continuous ticker-tape of trivia: things I'd forgotten to do, an email I should have sent, a conversation from three days ago that I replayed and improved in my favour, a vague anxiety about a noise the boiler had been making. I had not known, before sitting still, how loud my own thoughts were. They were a crowd.

I tried to follow a piece of advice I'd read about in contemplative practice: don't fight the thoughts, just notice them and let them pass. This is easy to understand and, it turns out, quite hard to do. What you notice is that the noticing is itself a thought, which produces a meta-noticing, which produces — you can see where this goes. The mind is very good at watching itself.

But somewhere around twenty minutes, something small happened.

I don't want to overclaim this. It was not a vision. Nobody appeared. No voice came from anywhere. What happened was closer to a reduction in the noise — a slight settling, the way a snow globe clarifies after you stop shaking it. The ticker-tape slowed. I became more aware of my breath. I became, in a way that surprised me, more aware of the room — not the objects in it, but the space. There was a quality to the quiet that I don't have a good word for. It wasn't absence. It was more like a faint presence that I kept nearly registering and then losing when I tried to look at it directly.

I stayed there for perhaps five minutes.

Then my phone rang in the other room, which I'd forgotten to silence, and that was the end of that.

But I noticed, getting up, that I felt different than I had when I sat down. Slower. Slightly more centred. A word I don't usually use, but here I'll use it: a little more here. It wasn't transformative. It wasn't a vision. It was a temperature shift, not a lightning bolt.

I thought about the man in the mosque in his grey cardigan, and I understood, a little, why he came back every week.

*     *     *

The honest question, then.

If contemplative practice reliably produces certain states of consciousness — if these states are reported as meaningful and transformative across cultures and centuries and traditions that share no other common ground — if practitioners consistently describe them as more real than ordinary experience rather than less — what does that mean?

The reductive answer is: the brain produces the experience, the tradition provides the interpretation, and the meaning is a story we tell ourselves. This is not wrong. But I am not sure it is complete.

The brain also produces music. The brain produces love. The brain produces the sense of mathematical truth when a proof clicks shut, and the sense of moral horror when you witness cruelty. We do not usually say that these experiences are "just" brain chemistry and leave it there. We take seriously the idea that the brain can be a receiver as well as a generator — that experience can track something real even when we cannot fully specify what.

The mystics across every tradition report, with a consistency that should give any empiricist pause, that what happens in the deepest states of contemplative practice is not the production of a pleasant feeling but an encounter with what they variously call presence, truth, ground of being, the Friend, the ein sof, the living God. They report that the experience carries information — noetic quality, in James's term. They report that it changes them. They report that it is more real than what they thought was real before.

I do not know what to do with this. I am not a believer. I do not think these experiences constitute proof of God's existence. The machinery of the brain is there, and the machinery is doing real work, and I don't want to pretend otherwise.

But I am also not willing to say: the experience is a production of grey matter and that settles it. That settles nothing. It tells you about the instrument but nothing about whether the instrument is receiving a signal.

Teresa of Ávila, who thought harder about this than almost anyone, was remarkably untroubled by the question of proof. She didn't need proof. She had experience — specific, recurring, datable experience. What she needed was a framework to understand it and a community to practise it within. The church provided both. Whether the church was right about everything else — about heretics, about Jews, about the bodies of women — is a different question and one she, constrained by her time and her survival, could not fully ask. But her account of what happened in the innermost rooms of her interior castle was not designed to persuade anyone. It was a map drawn from territory she had walked.

John of the Cross, writing in a prison cell after being kidnapped by his own order, described the dark night not as failure but as something closer to surgery. The comfort of God is removed so that you can learn to want God rather than comfort. The frame is theological and not available to me in that form. But the underlying insight — that the easiest thing to confuse with spiritual development is just the pleasant feeling of being developed — seems to me straightforwardly true.

*     *     *

Here is where I have to be careful not to end on a note that wraps this up.

Because it doesn't wrap up.

I can read the history of Sufism and hold in my mind simultaneously the ecstasy of Rumi and the political exploitation of dervish orders by Ottoman and Persian rulers who found spiritual hierarchy a useful tool of social control. Both are true. I can read Merton and know that the Trappist tradition he lived within had its own dark shadows — institutional cruelty, silencing, obedience demanded to the point of self-destruction — and still not be able to dismiss what he reported happening in his own contemplative practice. I can acknowledge that mystical experience has been used to manipulate: that charismatic leaders have deployed "spiritual experiences" to control followers, that altered states are available to be harvested by predators, that the dissolution of the self-boundary is also the dissolution of protective scepticism, and that genuine traditions have been weaponised by people who understood this.

All of that is true and none of it is the end of the matter.

What I cannot do — what I refuse to do — is look at that man in his grey cardigan and say: that means nothing. I have spent months with the worst of what religion does in the world. The blood, the manipulation, the small and large cruelties done in the name of the divine. I have looked at all of it. And at the end of it I find myself still unable to account for what I see in the face of someone who has, whatever else is happening, clearly arrived somewhere I have not been.

The interior life of religious practice is not an argument for God. But it is evidence that something matters, something works, something produces in human beings a quality of attention and presence and — I will use the word carefully — love that the rest of human life does not consistently deliver. The architecture for it exists in every tradition, built and refined over centuries by people who took it with a seriousness I can only begin to approximate.

I sat still in my study for twenty-five minutes on a Tuesday evening. The snow globe settled. Something was there, or I almost thought it was, or I thought I nearly thought it. Then the phone rang.

I would try again.
