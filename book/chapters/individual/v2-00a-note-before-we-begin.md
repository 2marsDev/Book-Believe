# A Note Before We Begin

I am not going to tell you my name.

That's not cowardice, though I understand if it reads that way. It's a calculation I've made after a long time sitting with this book, this subject, and the specific shape of the world we live in now. Writing critically about religion — any religion — is a different thing when the words exist in print, attached to a name, findable by anyone, forever. Writing critically about Islam in particular is a different thing again. There are communities in which this book would not be read as a philosophical inquiry. It would be read as a betrayal. And there are people I love inside those communities.

So I chose to protect myself. And in doing that, I know I've given some readers a reason to dismiss me. An anonymous author is easier to discard. I've accepted that.

But I want to be clear about something, because I think you deserve it before we go any further.

This is not an Islamophobic book. I know that word gets used to end conversations rather than begin them, and I know that a man writing anonymously about the problems of Islam is not in a strong position to claim otherwise. But I grew up surrounded by this tradition. I know what it looks like from the inside. I know what the prayer sounds like at five in the morning when the neighbourhood is still asleep. I know what it feels like to be part of something that old and that serious. I am not writing from fear of Islam. I am writing from proximity to it.

The chapters about Islam in this book are the same chapters as the ones about Christianity and Judaism. Same questions. Same standards. Same refusal to look away. If anything, those chapters cost me more than the others. Because the damage we examine most honestly is usually the damage we've been closest to.

---

I also need to tell you something I can't fully explain.

I did not choose to write this book in the way that you choose a project or a hobby or even a career. I didn't sit down one day and decide the world needed another book about religion. Something else happened. A feeling I can only describe as an insistence. A pressure that didn't go away when I ignored it. A sense that this had to be written, and that I was the one who had to write it, and that not writing it was not an option I was being offered.

I'm aware of how that sounds. I'm a person who spent two years questioning the claims people make about divine compulsion, about being called, about hearing something that others can't hear. And here I am, describing my relationship with this book in almost exactly those terms.

I don't think God told me to write it. I don't think anyone told me to write it.

But I couldn't not write it. And the distinction between those two things — between a calling and a compulsion — is smaller than I expected when I started.

Maybe that's the most honest thing this book can offer. Not an answer, but a window into the question itself. The same force that drives a person to pray at five in the morning in a cold house, when no one is watching and nothing requires it — I don't know what that force is. But I recognize it. I felt something like it every morning I sat down to write.

---

I want to be honest about one more thing. I don't know what this book will do. I can't see around that corner. I've tried to write something fair and I genuinely believe it is, but I know that fairness is not always how it arrives. People are carrying things I can't see. This subject touches something older and deeper than argument. Words about faith don't land the way words about other things land — they land in the place where people keep their dead, their fear, their sense of who they are. I know that. I haven't forgotten it.

I've written the most careful version of this I know how to write. And I still cannot tell you with certainty that it won't hurt someone. That uncertainty didn't stop me. But I want you to know I felt it. I still feel it.

---

What follows is a fair investigation. I've tried to make it that. I've tried to give each tradition its due, to show the people who live inside these beliefs with the same honesty I've applied to the institutions. I've tried not to sneer. I know I've sometimes failed at that.

But the investigation is real. The questions are real.

And whatever drove me to ask them — I'm still not sure what to call it.

Maybe you'll have a name for it by the end.

---

*The Author*
