Chapter 6: Older Gods
The first time I read the Epic of Gilgamesh, I had to put it down and walk around the room.
Gilgamesh is a Sumerian poem. Parts of it date to around 2100 BCE — a thousand years before the earliest parts of the Bible were written. It is one of the oldest surviving works of literature in human history.
And it contains a flood story.
A man is warned by a god that a great flood is coming. He builds a boat. He loads it with animals. The flood destroys the world. The waters recede. The man sends out birds to find dry land. He makes a sacrifice. The god smells the offering and is pleased.
If you grew up in any Abrahamic tradition, you recognize this story. You know it as Noah’s Ark. You learned it in Sunday school or heard it from your parents or saw it on a felt board in a church basement. God tells Noah to build an ark. He loads it with animals, two by two. The flood comes. The dove returns with an olive branch.
Same story. Different name. Written a thousand years earlier.
I sat there staring at my screen and thinking: how is this possible? How is it possible that the “word of God” tells a story that someone else told first — in a different language, in a different culture, about a different god — a millennium before Moses supposedly wrote it down?
And then I found another one.
And another.
And another.
*     *     *
I’m going to show you what I found. But I’m going to do it slowly, because the implications are enormous and I want you to feel them the way I felt them — not as a list of academic footnotes, but as a series of doors opening onto a room you didn’t know was there.
The flood story was just the beginning.
Because it turns out that many of the “sacred” stories at the heart of the Abrahamic tradition aren’t original.
They’re inherited.
From civilizations that came before. From gods that are now dead. From stories that were already ancient when Abraham was supposedly born.
And once you see this — really see it — you can’t unsee it.
Turn the page.
Chapter 6: Older Gods
The first time I came across the Epic of Gilgamesh, I had to put it down and walk around the room.
Gilgamesh is a Sumerian poem. Parts of it were scratched into clay tablets around 2100 BCE — a full thousand years before the earliest parts of the Bible were written. It is one of the oldest surviving works of literature in human history. It was buried under the sands of Mesopotamia for millennia before Victorian archaeologists dug it up in the nineteenth century and started translating it.
And it contains a flood story.
A man named Utnapishtim is warned by a god that a great flood is coming to destroy the world. He builds a boat. He loads it with animals and his family. The flood comes. The waters cover everything. When the waters begin to recede, Utnapishtim sends out a dove. The dove returns — no dry land. He sends a swallow. It returns. He sends a raven. It doesn’t come back. He knows the waters have receded. He makes a sacrifice. The gods smell the offering and are pleased.
Read that again.
Dove. Raven. Sacrifice. Divine satisfaction.
If you grew up in any Abrahamic tradition, you know this story. You know it as Noah’s Ark. God tells Noah to build a boat. Two of every animal. The flood comes. A dove returns with an olive branch. Noah makes a sacrifice.
Same story. Same structure. Same details. Different name. Written a thousand years earlier.
*     *     *
I spent the next several weeks chasing this thread. And the further I pulled, the more unraveled.
The flood wasn’t the only borrowed story. It wasn’t even the most important one.
Take the story of Moses as an infant. His mother, fearing for his life, places him in a basket of rushes, waterproofs it with pitch, and sets it in a river. He’s found by royalty and raised as a prince.
Now take the legend of Sargon of Akkad. Sargon was a Mesopotamian king who lived around 2300 BCE — roughly seven hundred years before Moses. According to the legend inscribed on a tablet now in the British Museum, Sargon’s mother “set me in a basket of rushes, with bitumen she sealed my lid. She cast me into the river.” He was found by a water-drawer, raised, and became a great king.
Basket. River. Rushes. Sealed with pitch. Found by someone of higher status. Two stories. Same structure. Same specific details. One predates the other by seven centuries.
Coincidence? Possibly. Once.
But this kept happening.
*     *     *
The creation accounts in Genesis bear striking resemblances to the Enuma Elish, the Babylonian creation epic dated to around 1100 BCE. Both begin with primordial waters. Both describe a separation of the waters to form sky and earth. Both describe the creation of humans from divine material to serve the gods. Both end with the creator resting.
The Ten Commandments — the moral bedrock of the Judeo-Christian tradition — share substantial overlap with the Code of Hammurabi, a Babylonian legal code carved into a black stone pillar around 1754 BCE. Don’t murder. Don’t steal. Don’t bear false witness. The Code of Hammurabi predates the Torah by centuries.
The concept of a dying and rising god — the narrative template at the heart of Christianity — existed across the ancient Mediterranean long before Jesus was born. Osiris in Egypt: killed by his brother, dismembered, partially reassembled by his wife Isis, transformed into ruler of the underworld. Tammuz in Mesopotamia: dies and descends to the underworld, mourned by his lover Ishtar. Adonis in Greece: killed, mourned, and associated with annual renewal. Attis in Phrygia: dies and is associated with resurrection and the return of spring. Dionysus: in one tradition, torn apart as a child, reconstituted, reborn through a mortal woman.
Now, I need to be fair here. The parallels are not as clean as some internet atheists claim. These myths differ enormously in their details. Osiris doesn’t physically rise from the dead the way Jesus supposedly does — he becomes king of the underworld, which is a different concept. Mithras is “born from a rock,” which is not exactly a virgin birth in any meaningful sense. And several of these dying-god traditions only appear in written form after Christianity, which means influence could have gone the other direction.
But here’s what’s undeniable: the idea of a divine or semi-divine being who dies and returns to life was a well-established category in the ancient Mediterranean world centuries before Jesus lived. The template existed. The cultural expectation existed. The story pattern was in the air.
And the early Christians knew it.
*     *     *
This is the part that really got me.
The early Church Fathers — the theologians who built Christianity in its first few centuries — were aware of these parallels. They couldn’t ignore them. The similarities between Jesus and earlier dying-rising gods were obvious enough that pagan critics like Celsus threw them in Christians’ faces.
So how did the Church Fathers respond?
Justin Martyr, writing in the second century, addressed it head-on. And his explanation was extraordinary. He said that when Christians claim Jesus was born of a virgin, was crucified, died, and rose again, “we propound nothing new or different from what you believe regarding those whom you call Sons of Zeus.” He listed the parallels himself — Dionysus, Hercules, Asclepius.
And then he explained why: the devil did it.
I’m not paraphrasing. That was the actual theological argument. Satan, knowing that Jesus would come, planted counterfeit versions of the story in advance to confuse people. The earlier myths were preemptive fakes. The devil’s disinformation campaign.
Tertullian made the same argument. The similarity isn’t because the Christians copied from the pagans. It’s because the devil copied from the future and planted the evidence in the past.
Think about what that claim actually is. Think about the intellectual architecture. Your story looks exactly like older stories — and your explanation is that the devil traveled backward in time to plant decoys. This is not theology. This is conspiracy thinking. The same structure as every conspiracy theory in history: the evidence against my belief is itself proof of the conspiracy that makes my belief necessary.
If someone told you that their business plan looked like a Ponzi scheme because criminals had preemptively created Ponzi schemes to discredit their legitimate business, you’d back away slowly. But when a Church Father says the devil planted earlier dying-god myths to discredit Jesus, we call it theology and study it in universities.
*     *     *
I want to be careful about what I’m claiming and what I’m not.
I am not claiming that Christianity was directly, mechanically copied from any single pagan myth. The evidence doesn’t support that simple a narrative. Jesus’s story has deep roots in Jewish messianic expectation, in Old Testament prophecy, in the specific political and cultural context of first-century Palestine. It’s not a photocopy of Osiris.
What I am claiming is this: the building blocks of the Jesus story — virgin birth, miraculous powers, death and resurrection, divine parentage — were not invented by Christianity. They were pre-existing cultural categories, available in the narrative toolbox of the ancient Mediterranean world. When the early Christians built the story of Jesus, they used tools that were already lying around. Consciously or unconsciously, deliberately or organically, the story of Jesus was assembled from parts that other cultures had already been using for centuries.
This doesn’t mean Jesus didn’t exist. He almost certainly did. But it means that the supernatural story built around the historical man follows patterns that humans had been applying to their heroes and their gods for a very long time. The virgin birth wasn’t new. The dying and rising wasn’t new. The divine son wasn’t new.
What was new was the community that formed around this particular version of the story — and the empire that eventually adopted it as state religion, giving it the political power to outlast every competitor.
Christianity didn’t win because its story was true. It won because its story was familiar enough to assimilate and its institution was powerful enough to eliminate the competition.
*     *     *
There’s a word for what I was experiencing at this point in my research, and it’s not one I expected.
Grief.
Not for my own faith — I’d never had one. But for the idea that this might all be true. For the door that was closing. I had started this process genuinely hoping to find something extraordinary. Some piece of evidence, some uncanny detail, some quality of these texts that would make me say: this is different from every other human story. This really could be divine.
Instead I found borrowed flood myths, recycled hero patterns, creation stories echoed from Babylon, and Church Fathers who explained away the evidence by blaming it on the devil.
Human stories. All the way down.
Beautiful ones. Powerful ones. Stories that built civilizations and comforted billions. But human.
And if the stories are human, then the next question is the most important one in this entire book:
Why do we believe them?
Not why should we. Why do we. What is it about the human mind that makes us so ready, so eager, so neurologically predisposed to believe in gods that we’ve done it in every culture, on every continent, for fifty thousand years?
The answer, it turns out, is in our skulls.
