# Chapter 10: The Money Machine

In 2017, Hurricane Harvey dumped fifty-one inches of rain on Houston, Texas. People were drowning in their living rooms. Shelters filled up. The convention center ran out of space. And Joel Osteen's Lakewood Church — a 16,800-seat former NBA arena sitting on prime Houston real estate — locked its doors.

The building was dry. The parking garage had minor flooding on its lowest level. The arena itself was fine.

Osteen didn't open the doors until the internet shamed him into it. Until the photographs of locked gates went viral. Until other churches, smaller churches, churches without book deals and television contracts, had already been sheltering hundreds.

When he finally opened Lakewood, he said the church had been inaccessible due to flooding. Aerial photographs showed otherwise.

I keep coming back to that image. Sixteen thousand empty seats in a dry building. People sleeping in water two miles away.

Because it tells you something about what the money machine has become.

*     *     *

Let me give you a number. Actually, let me give you a range, because nobody knows the real number.

The total assets held by religious organizations in the United States are estimated at somewhere between $1.2 trillion and $4.8 trillion.

That range should bother you. The gap between the low estimate and the high estimate is $3.6 trillion. We don't know where in that range the truth falls, because religious organizations in America are not required to file the financial disclosures that every other nonprofit must file.

Every secular charity — the Red Cross, Doctors Without Borders, your local food bank — files a Form 990 with the IRS. This form details revenue, expenses, executive compensation, assets, and liabilities. It's public. Anyone can look it up.

Churches don't file Form 990s.

They don't have to. Under Section 508(c)(1)(A) of the Internal Revenue Code, churches are automatically exempt from federal income tax without even applying. They don't pay property tax. They don't pay sales tax in many states. They aren't required to disclose what they earn, what they spend, or what they pay their leaders.

The reasoning is the First Amendment. Government shouldn't entangle itself with religion. Requiring financial reporting, the argument goes, is a form of government oversight that infringes on religious liberty.

I understand the argument. I even respect it in principle.

But here's what it looks like in practice.

*     *     *

Kenneth Copeland is worth an estimated $300 million. He lives in a lakefront mansion valued at roughly $7 million, which is classified as a parsonage — a church-provided home — making it tax-exempt. He owns a fleet of private jets. He has his own airport. Kenneth Copeland Airport. In Newark, Texas. A man of God with his own runway.

In 2019, a reporter from Inside Edition caught Copeland outside and asked him about the jets. Why does a preacher need a private jet?

Copeland's answer, delivered with a smile that didn't reach his eyes: "If I flew commercial, I'd have to stop 65 percent of what I'm doing." In an earlier conversation with fellow prosperity preacher Jesse Duplantis, Copeland had been more candid. He said he couldn't fly commercial because he'd be stuck "in a long tube with a bunch of demons."

The demons, apparently, are you and me.

Creflo Dollar — and yes, that's his real name — stood in front of his congregation in 2015 and asked them to help him buy a $65 million Gulfstream G650 jet. He needed 200,000 people to give $300 each. He framed it as an act of faith. Giving to your pastor, he explained, is giving to God.

Dollar eventually got his jet.

Joel Osteen's net worth is estimated between $50 million and $100 million. His books have sold millions of copies. His television broadcast reaches over 100 countries. Lakewood Church takes in an estimated $90 million per year in donations.

None of these figures can be independently verified, because Lakewood doesn't have to tell anyone what it does with the money.

*     *     *

The prosperity gospel is the most financially efficient theology ever invented. Its core claim is elegant: God wants you to be rich. If you give generously to His church — and by church, we mean this specific pastor's ministry — God will multiply your investment. It's divine returns on divine capital.

The people who give are overwhelmingly not rich.

Sociological research consistently shows that prosperity gospel congregations skew lower-income. These are people who can't afford to tithe 10 percent of their income. They do it anyway, because they've been told that financial struggle is a symptom of insufficient faith, and that giving more will unlock God's abundance.

A 2016 study in the Journal for the Scientific Study of Religion found that members of prosperity-oriented churches reported higher rates of financial stress than members of non-prosperity churches, even when controlling for income. They gave more. They worried more. They had less.

The theology is unfalsifiable. If you give and get rich, God rewarded your faith. If you give and stay poor, your faith wasn't strong enough. Give more.

It's a closed loop with one exit: the pastor's bank account.

*     *     *

This isn't new. The money has always been there. What changes is the scale and the shamelessness.

Oral Roberts built a university, a hospital, and a television empire starting in the 1950s. In 1987, he went on air and told his viewers that God would "call him home" — kill him — if they didn't send $8 million by March. They sent $9.1 million. Roberts lived. God, apparently, was satisfied.

Jim Bakker and his wife Tammy Faye built the PTL Club into a broadcasting powerhouse. They had a satellite network, a theme park called Heritage USA, and an air-conditioned doghouse. In 1989, Bakker was convicted of fraud and sentenced to forty-five years in prison. He'd oversold "lifetime partnerships" at Heritage USA — basically timeshares — by 60,000 units. He took in $158 million and spent most of it on operations, personal luxury, and hush money to Jessica Hahn, a church secretary he'd sexually assaulted.

Bakker served five years. He's back on television now, selling survival food buckets.

Jimmy Swaggart was the most-watched televangelist in the world in the mid-1980s. His ministry brought in $150 million a year. In 1988, he was photographed with a prostitute at a motel in New Orleans. He gave his famous crying confession on live television — "I have sinned against you, my Lord" — and his viewership briefly dropped. Then he was caught with another prostitute in 1991. This time he told his congregation, "The Lord told me it's flat none of your business."

He kept preaching.

The pattern is always the same. Build the audience. Build the revenue. When scandal hits, cry, repent, and rebuild. The audience forgives because forgiveness is the product they've been buying all along.

*     *     *

But the prosperity preachers are the loud, obvious cases. The real money is quieter.

The Catholic Church is the largest non-governmental landowner on Earth. The Vatican's wealth is estimated at somewhere between $10 billion and $15 billion — and that's the conservative, liquid-assets estimate. It doesn't include the art.

The Vatican Museums contain works by Michelangelo, Raphael, Caravaggio, and Bernini. The Sistine Chapel alone is effectively priceless. The total value of the Vatican's art collection has been estimated in the hundreds of billions of dollars, though these estimates are speculative because the collection has never been independently appraised and never will be.

Then there's the Vatican Bank, formally called the Institute for the Works of Religion — the IOR. Its history reads like a crime novel because, in parts, it is one.

In 1982, Roberto Calvi, the chairman of Banco Ambrosiano — a bank closely tied to the Vatican — was found hanging under Blackfriars Bridge in London. His pockets were filled with bricks and cash. Banco Ambrosiano had collapsed with $1.3 billion in debt, much of it connected to loans funneled through the IOR. The Vatican denied responsibility but eventually made a $244 million payment it described as a "voluntary contribution" rather than an admission of guilt.

Calvi's death was initially ruled a suicide. It was later reclassified as murder. No one has been convicted.

The P2 Masonic lodge, which had members in the Italian government, military, and intelligence services, was deeply intertwined with both Banco Ambrosiano and the IOR. Licio Gelli, the lodge's grandmaster, was convicted of fraud in connection with the collapse. The full extent of the Vatican's involvement has never been established, in part because the IOR is not subject to Italian banking regulations.

More recently, in 2020, the Vatican put a cardinal on trial for the first time in modern history. Cardinal Angelo Becciu was charged with embezzlement, abuse of office, and witness tampering in connection with a London real estate deal that lost the Vatican an estimated $400 million. The money had come from Peter's Pence — donations from ordinary Catholics around the world who thought they were giving to charity.

Becciu was convicted in 2023 and sentenced to five and a half years.

He was one man. The system that allowed him to operate — opaque, unregulated, accountable to no external authority — remains intact.

*     *     *

Islamic finance operates on different principles but generates its own questions.

The waqf system — the Islamic endowment — is one of the oldest charitable structures in the world. A waqf is property donated for religious or charitable purposes in perpetuity. Mosques, schools, hospitals, and vast tracts of land have been held as waqf for centuries. In many Muslim-majority countries, the total waqf holdings are enormous. Turkey's Directorate General of Foundations manages assets accumulated over five hundred years. Egypt, India, and Malaysia hold waqf properties worth billions.

The system works well when it works. Waqf properties fund genuine charitable work — education, healthcare, community infrastructure.

But then there's the petrodollar pipeline.

Since the oil boom of the 1970s, Saudi Arabia has spent an estimated $100 billion exporting Wahhabism — its ultraconservative interpretation of Sunni Islam — around the world. The money has built mosques, funded madrasas, trained imams, and distributed literature on every continent.

This is not a conspiracy theory. It's documented Saudi government policy. The kingdom's own figures acknowledge spending billions on Islamic outreach. A 2006 Freedom House report examined literature distributed by the Saudi government to mosques in the United States and found material promoting hatred of Jews and Christians, rejection of democracy, and endorsement of violence against those who leave Islam.

The radicalization pipeline doesn't start with a bomb. It starts with a building. A new mosque. A free school. A charismatic imam whose salary is paid from thousands of miles away, whose theology was not chosen by the community he serves but by the state that signs his checks.

Most mosques funded by Saudi money are not breeding grounds for terrorism. That claim would be absurd. But the influence operation is real, and the money is staggering, and it has shifted the global character of Sunni Islam in a more conservative direction over fifty years.

*     *     *

Jewish institutional finance is different in structure, though not immune to questions.

Jewish organizations in America tend to operate with more financial transparency than churches, in part because most are structured as secular nonprofits that do file Form 990s. Synagogues operate on membership dues rather than tithes, and the amounts are generally disclosed.

The major Jewish philanthropic organizations — the Jewish Federations of North America, Hadassah, the American Jewish Joint Distribution Committee — are among the most financially transparent large nonprofits in the country. Their budgets are audited. Their reports are public.

But wealth exists. The real estate holdings of major Orthodox institutions in New York City alone are worth hundreds of millions. And where there is concentrated wealth in religious institutions, there are questions about accountability, about who decides how community money is spent, and about what happens when leadership is insular.

In 2019, a federal investigation into a decades-long tax fraud scheme led to charges against members of a Lakewood, New Jersey, Orthodox community who had systematically underreported income to qualify for government benefits while receiving substantial communal support. The fraud involved millions of dollars.

It was not representative. It was specific. But it was enabled by a community structure that resists outside scrutiny on religious grounds.

*     *     *

Now zoom out. Forget the scandals. Look at the basic mechanism.

Tithing. Ten percent.

The concept comes from the Hebrew Bible. Abraham gave a tenth of his spoils to Melchizedek, the priest-king of Salem. Leviticus 27:30 declares that "a tithe of everything from the land, whether grain from the soil or fruit from the trees, belongs to the Lord."

Modern churches, particularly evangelical Protestant churches, have turned this into a weekly expectation. Some churches teach that tithing is mandatory for salvation. Others frame it as an act of obedience. Either way, the social pressure is enormous. In many congregations, giving is tracked. Envelopes are labeled. Some churches publish donor lists.

Americans gave an estimated $135 billion to religious organizations in 2022, according to Giving USA. That's more than they gave to education, human services, and health organizations combined.

Where does it go?

Some of it goes to genuine charitable work — food pantries, homeless shelters, disaster relief, community programs. Religious organizations do an enormous amount of social good, and denying that would be dishonest.

But studies suggest that the majority of church spending goes to operational costs — buildings, salaries, utilities, and internal programs for existing members. A 2016 analysis by the Evangelical Council for Financial Accountability found that member churches spent an average of 82 percent of their budgets on internal operations and 18 percent on outreach and missions. Of that 18 percent, much of the "missions" spending was evangelism — not charity but recruitment.

So when someone puts $100 in the offering plate, roughly $82 goes to keeping the building open and the pastor paid. Maybe $10 goes to community service. And some portion of the rest goes to convincing more people to put money in the offering plate.

This is not fraud. It's how organizations work. Buildings cost money. Staff costs money.

But the donor thinks she's giving to God. She's giving to overhead.

*     *     *

The tax exemption question is, at its core, a math problem.

If religious organizations in the United States paid taxes at the same rate as other nonprofits — or, more dramatically, at the rate of for-profit businesses — what would the government collect?

A widely cited 2012 study by University of Tampa professor Ryan Cragun estimated that the total subsidy to religion through tax exemptions is approximately $71 billion per year. This includes property tax exemptions ($26.2 billion), the tax-deductibility of donations ($12.5 billion), and various income, investment, and parsonage exemptions.

Seventy-one billion dollars a year. That's more than the annual budget of the Department of Homeland Security.

Now, defenders of religious tax exemption make several arguments, and some of them are strong.

First, the First Amendment. Government has no business entangling itself with religious institutions. Taxation is a form of control. If you can tax a church, you can shut it down by taxing it to death. The founders, having fled religious persecution, had good reason to build a wall.

I take this seriously. I do.

Second, social services. Religious organizations provide an estimated $20 billion or more in social services annually — food banks, addiction programs, shelters, disaster relief. Remove the tax exemption, and some of that goes away.

Third, small churches. The vast majority of religious congregations in America are small. The median church has seventy-five regular attendees. The median budget is under $200,000. These aren't corporations. They're community organizations run by someone who probably has a second job.

All of this is true. And none of it addresses the problem.

The problem is that the same exemption that keeps a small Baptist church in rural Alabama solvent also shields Lakewood Church's $90 million annual revenue from any public scrutiny. The same rule that protects a modest synagogue in Brooklyn protects Kenneth Copeland's airport. The system makes no distinction between a church that spends everything on its community and a ministry that makes its leader a billionaire.

Because to make that distinction, you'd need oversight. And oversight means entanglement. And entanglement means the First Amendment argument kicks in.

It's a perfect shield. The smallest church and the largest televangelist hide behind the same constitutional principle.

*     *     *

I want to be clear about something, because it matters.

Most of the people who work in religious institutions are not getting rich. Most pastors in America earn modest salaries. Most imams are underpaid. Most rabbis balance spiritual leadership with the mundane reality of keeping a building from falling down.

The woman who runs the food pantry out of her church basement is not the problem. The imam who teaches Arabic classes after evening prayers is not the problem. The rabbi who spends three hours every week visiting elderly congregants in the hospital is not the problem.

The problem is the industry that has grown up alongside them. The television empires. The mega-churches with coffee bars and gift shops. The Vatican's investment portfolio. The Saudi export machine. The political lobbying firms that ensure the tax exemption never gets examined too closely.

The problem is that this industry — one of the largest economic systems on the planet — operates in the name of God and reports to no one.

Not to its donors. Not to the government. Not to the people who fill the seats every week and believe their money is being used for sacred purposes.

Trillions of dollars. Zero mandatory transparency.

In any other sector, we'd call that a scandal. In religion, we call it freedom.

*     *     *

I sat in a prosperity gospel church once, in a suburb of Atlanta. The building was enormous. The sound system was professional grade. The screens were high-definition. The parking lot had a traffic management team.

The pastor wore a suit that cost more than my first car. He preached about abundance. He preached about the windows of heaven opening. He preached about planting a seed — that was the word, "seed" — and watching God multiply it.

Then the offering came. The ushers passed the buckets. But first, the pastor asked everyone who wanted to give $1,000 or more to stand up.

A few people stood. The congregation applauded.

Then $500. More people stood. More applause.

Then $100. Most of the room.

I looked at the faces. I saw a woman in her sixties who had come in a car with a cracked windshield. I saw a young couple who didn't look like they had $100 to give. I saw them put money in the bucket.

I saw the pastor smile.

And I thought: this is the oldest con in the world. It's just wearing a nicer suit.
