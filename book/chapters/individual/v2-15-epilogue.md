EPILOGUE
## Facing the Unknown

I'm on a beach again.

Not the same one. Different country, different year. But the same sea. The Mediterranean doesn't change. It was here before any of the religions I've spent years studying, and it will be here after the last one is forgotten. The water doesn't care what we believe. It just keeps arriving at the shore.

It's September. The crowds have thinned. The light has that late-season quality — golden, lower, the kind that makes everything look like it's being remembered even as it happens. My daughter is at the water's edge, doing what children do. Building something. Destroying it. Building it again.

I'm watching her and I'm thinking about a woman in a niqab.

*     *     *

Years ago, on a beach not far from here, I saw something I couldn't look away from. A woman sealed in black fabric in thirty-five-degree heat. Her husband beside her in board shorts, eating ice cream. Their children running between them.

That image launched this whole thing. Every chapter. Every sleepless night with a stack of theology books. Every interview. Every uncomfortable dinner where I said what I actually thought and watched the temperature in the room drop.

I've been looking at it for years now. Does it look different?

Yes.

I still see the asymmetry. I still see one person's comfort purchased at the cost of another person's freedom. I still can't call that holy.

But I see other things now. I see a woman who may have a relationship with her faith that is real and sustaining in ways I will never fully understand, because I have never been inside it. I see a tradition that gives her a framework for her entire life, and I know now what it costs to live without that framework.

I still think the asymmetry is wrong.

But I hold it differently. With less certainty about my own clarity.

That doesn't make her covering right. It makes my seeing incomplete.

*     *     *

I called Sarah last month. Sarah from Leeds — the evangelical woman who told me, over tea in her kitchen, that she talks to God every morning and He answers. Not in words. In feelings. In a sense of presence as real to her as the chair she's sitting in.

I'd asked her then: "What if it's not real? What if it's just you, talking to yourself?"

And she'd said: "Then I'm talking to myself in a way that makes me kinder, more patient, and less afraid of dying. What's your method doing for you?"

I have thought about that sentence more than almost anything else anyone has said to me in the course of writing this book.

She's still in Leeds. Still in the same church. Her daughter got married last year — in the church, of course. Sarah told me the ceremony was beautiful. Then she said something that stopped me.

"My neighbour died in January. Edith. Eighty-three. Lived alone. We'd been bringing her meals on Tuesdays for two years. The church, I mean. When she died, there were fourteen people at her funeral. All from the church. She had no family left."

She paused.

"Fourteen people who wouldn't have known she existed if we hadn't knocked on her door."

I thought about Herr Brandt. Eleven days.

"I know you don't believe any of it," Sarah said. "That's all right. I pray for you anyway."

She laughed when she said it. I laughed too. There was warmth in it. Real warmth. The kind you can't manufacture by being right.

*     *     *

Yusuf — the imam from Manchester — has moved to Toronto. We exchanged emails. I told him I was finishing the book. He asked if he'd come off well.

I told him the truth: he'd come off as a thoughtful, serious man whose faith I couldn't share but whose sincerity I couldn't question.

He wrote back: "I have been thinking about what you said — that the evidence doesn't support our claims. I think you are wrong. But I also think that if you are right, it doesn't change what I owe. The obligation to feed the hungry, to shelter the orphan, to stand in prayer — if God is there, these are worship. If God is not there, they are still the right way to live. You and I end up in the same place. We just get there by different roads."

I read that email three times. He'd done something I hadn't managed in three hundred pages. He'd made the God question irrelevant to the moral question. He'd separated the two things I'd spent years trying to separate, and he'd done it in a paragraph.

*     *     *

And Miriam.

Miriam — the rabbi in North London who told me, over Friday night dinner, that Judaism's greatest strength was its comfort with argument. "We don't need you to believe," she'd said. "We need you to wrestle."

I'd spent an evening at her table. Her husband had made chicken. Her two teenagers had argued about politics through dessert. She'd led the blessings over the wine and the bread with the casual ease of someone who has done it a thousand times — not performing faith but inhabiting it the way you inhabit your own house.

After dinner, walking me to the door, she'd said: "You know, the best rabbis were always the doubters. Abraham argued with God. Jacob wrestled an angel. The tradition doesn't ask you to be certain. It asks you to show up."

She sent me an article last month. She'd written it for a Jewish publication — about young Jews leaving the faith, and about her refusal to panic.

"The tradition survived Babylon, Rome, the Inquisition, and the Shoah," she wrote. "It will survive your book."

Then, at the bottom of the email, a line that made me set down my coffee.

"I should tell you — since you'll probably find out anyway — I nearly left the rabbinate two years ago. Six months after we had dinner. I didn't tell you because I didn't know you well enough. I had a period of real doubt. Not intellectual doubt — I've always had that. The kind where you stand in front of the congregation on Shabbat morning and the words feel hollow in your mouth. Where you wonder if you're performing something you no longer believe."

She stayed. She told me she stayed not because the doubt resolved but because the community needed her, and being needed turned out to be enough.

"I don't know if I believe in God the way I did ten years ago," she wrote. "I believe in Friday night. I believe in the argument. I believe in the chain. Maybe that's enough. Maybe that's all it ever was."

I read that and I felt something I didn't expect. Not triumph — not "she's coming to my side." Something closer to grief. Because Miriam had found a way to hold doubt and practice together, and I hadn't. She was standing in the uncertainty, the same uncertainty I'd been writing about for years, and she was doing it while also showing up — while lighting candles and blessing bread and sitting with the dying and leading a community through its worst moments. I was doing it alone, at a desk, with a manuscript.

*     *     *

And Thomas.

I haven't spoken to Thomas since we met. I thought about calling him several times while writing this book and never did. I'm not sure what I'd say. He was the one I couldn't argue with — not because his reasons were better, but because he didn't seem to need reasons. His faith was like his accent: something he'd grown up inside and never thought to question, not because he was incurious but because it fit. It held him without pinching. He went to Mass the way he went to work — not with ecstasy, not with crisis, just with the quiet assumption that this was what a life contained.

I have a photograph from that day. Thomas outside the church after Mass, squinting in the sun, holding a paper cup of tea. He's smiling at someone off-camera. He looks exactly like what he is: a man at ease in a world that makes sense to him.

I thought about Thomas more than I expected. He troubled me — not because his faith was wrong, but because it was working. Quietly, without drama, without the agonies I'd been documenting for three hundred pages. He wasn't harmed by religion. He wasn't deluded by religion. He was held by it, the way a house is held by its foundations — invisibly, without effort, so completely that the question of whether the foundations are made of the right material never arises because the house is warm and the roof doesn't leak.

I don't feel pity for Thomas. I don't feel envy, exactly. I feel something closer to recognition. He has what the data says he should have — community, purpose, structure, peace. He is the person this book can't quite account for. The one who doesn't need rescuing from his beliefs because his beliefs aren't a cage. They're a home.

I couldn't live in that home. I looked too carefully at the foundations. But I can see the light in the windows, and I know what it means to stand outside in the cold, and I am done pretending that the cold is freedom enough.

*     *     *

Here is what I believe, after all of it.

I believe that the supernatural claims at the heart of Christianity, Islam, and Judaism are almost certainly not true. A man did not rise from the dead. An angel did not dictate a book in a cave. A sea did not part. These are human stories — powerful, civilizationally significant — but human.

I believe the people who believe those stories are, on average, living more connected lives than the people who don't. That sentence costs me something to write. The data is clear: religious people report higher well-being, stronger social ties, more community involvement, and lower rates of despair. This is not because their beliefs are true. It is because their institutions work. And ours — the secular alternative — do not yet work as well. Possibly they never will. I believe that and it frightens me.

I believe that the atheist movement, including the books that shaped my own thinking, has been dishonest about the cost of being right. We presented the abandonment of religion as liberation. For some, it was. For many others, it was amputation. We were so focused on the false limb that we didn't ask what the limb had been holding up.

I believe I have been, at times in this book, unfair. Not in the facts. In the emphasis. I spent more time on the harm than the help. I spent more time with the damaged than the sustained. That's the bias of the project — you investigate what's wrong, not what's working. But the woman in the pew who has been held by her faith through a cancer diagnosis and a dead child and thirty years of ordinary Tuesdays — she deserved more of my attention than I gave her. I owe her that admission.

I believe that "I don't know" is a braver position than either "God exists" or "God doesn't exist." But I also believe it is the loneliest. I am tired of pretending it isn't.

*     *     *

My daughter is running toward me. She's wet and sandy and holding something — a shell, I think. She wants to show me.

This is what I have. This moment. This beach. This child who has already asked me where Grandma went and will ask harder questions. This light that is fading because the Earth is turning. This body that will stop working, and there is probably nothing after that, and I have to be okay with that, and most days I am, and some days I am not, and the days I am not are more frequent than I expected when I started writing this book.

I think about the woman in the niqab. I think about Sarah in her kitchen, becoming kinder by talking to a God I don't believe in. I think about Yusuf, who arrived at the same moral place by a different road. I think about Miriam, lighting candles she's no longer sure she believes in, staying because being needed was enough.

I can't join them. I've looked too carefully at the foundations.

But I can see what they have. And I can admit — without shame — that there is a version of what they have that I want. Not the God part. Not the afterlife part. Not the rules about what to eat and who to love and what women should wear on a beach.

The other part. The part where you are not alone. The part where someone knocks on your door. The part where Edith gets fourteen people at her funeral instead of none.

*     *     *

The sun is getting lower. My daughter is getting cold. It's time to go.

She hands me the shell. It's not remarkable. Ordinary calcium carbonate, built by an animal that is probably dead, shaped by the same blind forces that shaped everything, including us.

She thinks it's beautiful. She's right.

I close my hand around it. I take her hand in mine. We walk back up the beach toward the road.

The old certainties are gone. I'm not going to pretend that doesn't cost something. It does. It costs something every day.

But there is more room now. For honesty. For the questions that faith couldn't hold.

Behind us, the sea keeps doing what it has always done.

It doesn't need us to believe in it.

It just keeps coming in.
