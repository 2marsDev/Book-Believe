Chapter 6: Older Gods

The first time I came across the Epic of Gilgamesh, I had to put it down and walk around the room.

I was in a reading room at the university library — one of those rooms with high windows and long oak tables, the kind where the silence has a weight to it. I'd requested a translation from the reference desk, a Penguin Classics edition with an introduction by Andrew George. The cover was unpromising — a small image of a cuneiform tablet, scholarly typeface, the kind of book that doesn't try to sell itself. I opened it expecting something dry. A curiosity. An ancient text that would feel as remote from the Bible as a shopping list from a love letter.

It didn't feel remote at all.

Gilgamesh is a Sumerian poem. The oldest Gilgamesh poems date to around 2100 BCE, though the flood narrative in the form we know it was recorded in later centuries — still a full thousand years before the earliest parts of the Bible were written. It is one of the oldest surviving works of literature in human history. It was buried under the sands of Mesopotamia for millennia before Victorian archaeologists dug it up in the nineteenth century and started translating it.

And it contains a flood story.

A man named Utnapishtim is warned by a god that a great flood is coming to destroy the world. He builds a boat. He loads it with animals and his family. The flood comes. The waters cover everything. When the waters begin to recede, Utnapishtim sends out a dove. The dove returns — no dry land. He sends a swallow. It returns. He sends a raven. It doesn't come back. He knows the waters have receded. He makes a sacrifice. The gods smell the offering and are pleased.

Read that again.

Dove. Raven. Sacrifice. Divine satisfaction.

If you grew up in any Abrahamic tradition, you know this story. You know it as Noah's Ark. God tells Noah to build a boat. Two of every animal. The flood comes. A dove returns with an olive branch. Noah makes a sacrifice.

Same story. Same structure. Same details. Different name. Written a thousand years earlier.

The story of this discovery is itself extraordinary. In 1872, a self-taught Assyriologist named George Smith was working in the basement of the British Museum, sorting through thousands of clay tablet fragments that had been shipped back from the ruins of Nineveh — the ancient Assyrian capital, in what is now northern Iraq. Austen Henry Layard and Hormuzd Rassam had excavated the library of King Ashurbanipal decades earlier, sending crate after crate of broken tablets to London, where they sat largely untranslated for years. Smith, who had taught himself cuneiform while working as an engraver's apprentice, was methodically reading through the fragments.

When he reached Tablet XI of what we now call the Standard Babylonian Version of the Epic of Gilgamesh, he found the flood narrative. He translated it. And then, according to the accounts of those who were present, he became so excited that he began undressing — pulling off his clothes and running around the room. The story may be apocryphal, but the reaction, if not the stripping, was understandable. Smith had just found a flood story that predated the Bible by over a millennium, with parallels so specific that coincidence was essentially impossible.

He presented his findings to the Society of Biblical Archaeology on 3 December 1872. The *Daily Telegraph* offered to fund an expedition to Nineveh to find the missing portions of the tablet. Smith went. He found them. The Victorian world had to reckon with the fact that one of the Bible's most famous stories was not original.

The poem itself is remarkable to read. It is not a crude precursor to biblical narrative. It is sophisticated, psychologically complex, and at times breathtakingly beautiful. When Gilgamesh mourns his friend Enkidu, the language has a rawness that feels contemporary:

"Hear me, O elders, and give ear to me! It is for Enkidu, my friend, that I weep, moaning bitterly like a wailing woman."

And later: "How can I rest, how can I be at peace? Despair is in my heart. What my brother is now, that shall I be when I am dead."

This is not primitive literature. This is a human being confronting death with the same terror and incomprehension that we bring to it today, four thousand years later. And embedded in this profoundly human poem is the same flood narrative that billions of people believe God revealed exclusively to Noah.

*     *     *

I spent the next several weeks chasing this thread. And the further I pulled, the more unravelled.

The flood wasn't the only borrowed story. It wasn't even the most important one.

Take the story of Moses as an infant. His mother, fearing for his life, places him in a basket of rushes, waterproofs it with pitch, and sets it in a river. He's found by royalty and raised as a prince.

Now take the legend of Sargon of Akkad. Sargon was a Mesopotamian king who lived around 2300 BCE — roughly a thousand years before Moses. According to the legend inscribed on a tablet now in the British Museum, Sargon's mother "set me in a basket of rushes, with bitumen she sealed my lid. She cast me into the river." He was found by a water-drawer, raised, and became a great king.

Basket. River. Rushes. Sealed with pitch. Found by someone of higher status. Two stories. Same structure. Same specific details. One predates the other by roughly a millennium.

Coincidence? Possibly. Once.

But this kept happening.

*     *     *

The creation accounts in Genesis bear striking resemblances to the Enuma Elish, the Babylonian creation epic dated to around 1100 BCE. Both begin with primordial waters. Both describe a separation of the waters to form sky and earth. Both describe the creation of humans from divine material to serve the gods. Both end with the creator resting.

The Ten Commandments — the moral bedrock of the Judeo-Christian tradition — share substantial overlap with the Code of Hammurabi, a Babylonian legal code carved into a black stone pillar around 1754 BCE. Don't murder. Don't steal. Don't bear false witness. The Code of Hammurabi predates the Torah by centuries.

And there are parallels that are even more specific, even harder to explain away. The biblical book of Proverbs, chapters 22 to 24, contains a collection of wisdom sayings that scholars have long recognised as strikingly similar to an Egyptian text called the Instruction of Amenemope, composed sometime between 1300 and 1000 BCE. The parallels are not vague thematic echoes. They are structural and sequential. Both texts are organised into thirty sections. Both open with nearly identical exhortations to listen and learn. Proverbs 22:17-18 reads: "Incline your ear and hear the words of the wise, and apply your heart to my knowledge; for it will be pleasant if you keep them within you." Amenemope's opening: "Give your ears, hear the sayings, give your heart to understand them; it is profitable to put them in your heart." The same structure. The same sequence of ideas. The same number of sections. Most biblical scholars now accept that the author of Proverbs either knew the Egyptian text directly or was drawing on a shared tradition in which the Egyptian version came first.

The Psalms, too, have parallels in older traditions. Psalm 104 — one of the great hymns of creation in the Hebrew Bible — bears remarkable similarities to the Great Hymn to the Aten, composed during the reign of the Egyptian pharaoh Akhenaten in the fourteenth century BCE. Both hymns celebrate a single creator deity who brings light, sustains all living things, and causes the earth to flourish. Both describe animals emerging at dawn to find the food their creator has provided. Both praise the god for the gift of water and the cycle of day and night. The parallels are close enough that some scholars have suggested direct literary dependence, though others argue for a shared ancient Near Eastern tradition of solar hymns. Either way, the Psalm is not the original. It is the inheritor.

And the flood itself isn't just a Mesopotamian story. It appears in traditions that had no known contact with the ancient Near East. In Hindu scripture, Manu is warned by a fish about a coming deluge and builds a boat to survive. In Chinese mythology, the hero Gun-Yu tames a catastrophic flood that covers the earth. Mesoamerican traditions from the Maya to the Aztecs describe previous worlds destroyed by water. The pattern doesn't just predate the Bible. It appears everywhere. On every continent. Among people who never read a cuneiform tablet.

*     *     *

The concept of a dying and rising god — the narrative template at the heart of Christianity — existed across the ancient Mediterranean long before Jesus was born. Osiris in Egypt: killed by his brother Set, dismembered into fourteen pieces, partially reassembled by his wife Isis, who conceived their son Horus from his reconstituted body, and transformed into ruler of the underworld. Tammuz in Mesopotamia: dies and descends to the underworld, mourned by his lover Ishtar, whose grief disrupts the natural world until his partial return. Adonis in Greece: born from a tree, killed by a boar, mourned by Aphrodite, and associated with annual cycles of renewal. Attis in Phrygia: dies — by self-castration in some versions, killed by a boar in others — and is associated with resurrection and the return of spring. Dionysus: in one tradition, torn apart as a child by the Titans, reconstituted by Rhea or Zeus, reborn through a mortal woman.

Now, I need to be fair here. The parallels are not as clean as some internet atheists claim. These myths differ enormously in their details. Osiris doesn't physically rise from the dead the way Jesus supposedly does — he becomes king of the underworld, which is a different concept. Mithras is "born from a rock," which is not exactly a virgin birth in any meaningful sense. And several of these dying-god traditions only appear in written form after Christianity, which means influence could have gone the other direction. The scholar Jonathan Z. Smith has rightly criticised sloppy parallelomania — the tendency to see connections everywhere regardless of context. I take that criticism seriously.

But here's what's undeniable: the idea of a divine or semi-divine being who dies and returns to life was a well-established category in the ancient Mediterranean world centuries before Jesus lived. The template existed. The cultural expectation existed. The story pattern was in the air. A first-century person hearing the claim that a divine figure had died and been restored to life would not have found the concept alien. They would have found it familiar — and that familiarity is precisely what made the claim persuasive. It didn't sound like something new. It sounded like something they already understood, applied to a new figure.

What the early Christians knew and didn't know about these parallels is itself revealing. Paul, writing in the 50s CE — the earliest Christian author whose work survives — never mentions a virgin birth. He never describes the empty tomb. He says Jesus "was raised on the third day in accordance with the scriptures," but the scriptures he means are the Jewish scriptures, not the Gospels, which hadn't been written yet. Paul's Christ is a cosmic, spiritual figure — the firstborn of a new creation, the one through whom God reconciles the world. The more earthly, narrative details — the manger in Bethlehem, the star, the shepherds, the empty tomb with its angels — came later, in Gospels written decades after Paul. And those later authors were living in a cultural world saturated with dying-and-rising god narratives. Whether they consciously drew on those narratives or simply breathed them in like the air of their civilisation is a question that cannot be answered. But the parallels are there, and they are not coincidental.

And the early Christians knew it.

*     *     *

This is the part that really got me.

The early Church Fathers — the theologians who built Christianity in its first few centuries — were aware of these parallels. They couldn't ignore them. The similarities between Jesus and earlier dying-rising gods were obvious enough that pagan critics threw them in Christians' faces.

The most formidable of these critics was Celsus, a second-century Greek philosopher who wrote a systematic attack on Christianity called *The True Word* (or *Alethes Logos*), around 175 CE. We don't have the original text — it was eventually destroyed — but we can reconstruct most of it because the Church Father Origen quoted it extensively in his rebuttal, *Contra Celsum*, written about seventy years later. Origen was so thorough in his quotations that he preserved the very arguments he was trying to destroy.

Celsus was not a fool. He was well-read, sharp, and merciless. He pointed out that the stories Christians told about Jesus — the miraculous birth, the divine parentage, the resurrection — were structurally identical to stories that already existed about Hercules, Asclepius, Dionysus, and others. He asked why Christians accepted their version of these stories while dismissing the identical claims made about earlier figures. He compared the resurrection of Jesus to the stories told about Zamolxis, a Thracian deity, and about Rhampsinitus, an Egyptian king whom Herodotus described as descending to the underworld and returning.

His challenge was direct: you Christians are not offering anything new. You are retelling stories that pagans told first, and then claiming yours are the only true versions. On what grounds? What makes your dying-and-rising god more credible than the ones who came before?

Celsus went further. He attacked the resurrection specifically, pointing out that the witnesses were, by the Christians' own admission, a handful of grief-stricken followers — "a hysterical female," as he put it, referring to Mary Magdalene — and that their testimony would not be accepted in any court. He compared the Christian claim to the stories told by followers of other cults about their own deceased leaders. He noted that the Christians' insistence on the bodily resurrection of Jesus was, in the context of the ancient world, not a unique claim but a familiar one — and that the Christians had no better evidence for their version than the followers of Zamolxis had for theirs.

Origen's response, written decades later, is itself fascinating — not because it refutes Celsus convincingly, but because it reveals how seriously the early Church took the challenge. Origen devoted eight books to answering Celsus, point by point, in a work that runs to hundreds of pages. You don't write eight books to answer an argument you find trivial. You write eight books to answer an argument you find dangerous.

It was a devastating question. And the broader Christian response to it is one of the most revealing moments in the history of theology.

Justin Martyr, writing in the second century, addressed it head-on. And his explanation was extraordinary. He said that when Christians claim Jesus was born of a virgin, was crucified, died, and rose again, "we propound nothing new or different from what you believe regarding those whom you call Sons of Zeus." He listed the parallels himself — Dionysus, Hercules, Asclepius.

And then he explained why: the devil did it.

I'm not paraphrasing. That was the actual theological argument. Satan, knowing that Jesus would come, planted counterfeit versions of the story in advance to confuse people. The earlier myths were preemptive fakes. The devil's disinformation campaign.

Tertullian made the same argument. The similarity isn't because the Christians copied from the pagans. It's because the devil had read the Old Testament prophecies and planted counterfeit versions in advance, to confuse people when the real thing arrived.

Think about what that claim actually is. Think about the intellectual architecture. Your story looks exactly like older stories — and your explanation is that the devil read the prophecies and planted preemptive counterfeits across multiple civilisations. This is not theology. This is conspiracy thinking. The same structure as every conspiracy theory in history: the evidence against my belief is itself proof of the conspiracy that makes my belief necessary.

If someone told you that their business plan looked like a Ponzi scheme because criminals had preemptively created Ponzi schemes to discredit their legitimate business, you'd back away slowly. You'd recognise the structure immediately: the evidence against the claim is being reframed as evidence for the claim. Every disconfirming fact becomes a confirming one. The system is hermetically sealed. It cannot be tested. It cannot be falsified. And an unfalsifiable claim is not a strong claim — it is a claim that has immunised itself against reality. It is not the architecture of truth. It is the architecture of self-deception.

The Ponzi scheme analogy is worth dwelling on, because the structural parallel is exact. In a Ponzi scheme, a promoter takes money from investors, pays early investors with money from later ones, and claims the returns are generated by a legitimate business. When someone points out that the returns are suspiciously similar to the structure of known frauds, the promoter doesn't say "you're right, let me show you the underlying business." The promoter says: "the fraudsters modelled their schemes on my legitimate operation." The evidence of fraud becomes evidence of legitimacy. The argument is circular. It explains everything and predicts nothing. And it works — until it doesn't.

Justin Martyr's argument works the same way. The earlier myths aren't evidence that the Christian story was built from pre-existing templates. They're evidence that the devil anticipated the truth and planted decoys. The more parallels you find, the more active the devil must have been — which means the more important the truth must be that he was trying to obscure. Every piece of evidence against the claim strengthens the claim. Every counter-example confirms the conspiracy. It is an argument that cannot be wrong, which is precisely why it cannot be right.

But when a Church Father says the devil planted earlier dying-god myths to discredit Jesus, we call it theology and study it in universities. The argument has survived for nearly two thousand years, not because it's convincing, but because it offers believers a way to acknowledge the parallels without accepting what the parallels imply. It is a pressure valve, not a solution.

*     *     *

There is a more sophisticated version of this defence, and it deserves a hearing.

Some theologians argue that God deliberately chose to express divine truth through existing cultural forms. That the parallels aren't embarrassing — they're intentional. God spoke to humanity in the narrative language humanity already had. Dying-and-rising gods were already meaningful to people in the ancient world, so God used that template to communicate something real.

It's a more elegant argument than the devil theory. But follow it to its conclusion. If God used pre-existing story patterns to deliver the Christian message, then the stories were not revealed from outside human culture. They were borrowed from within it and adapted. The dying-and-rising template wasn't a divine disclosure. It was a human narrative convention that one particular tradition claimed as uniquely true. That's a very different claim than "God revealed this to us." It's closer to: "We took a story humans were already telling and said ours was the real one."

*     *     *

I want to be careful about what I'm claiming and what I'm not.

I am not claiming that Christianity was directly, mechanically copied from any single pagan myth. The evidence doesn't support that simple a narrative. Jesus's story has deep roots in Jewish messianic expectation, in Old Testament prophecy, in the specific political and cultural context of first-century Palestine. It's not a photocopy of Osiris.

What I am claiming is this: the building blocks of the Jesus story — virgin birth, miraculous powers, death and resurrection, divine parentage — were not invented by Christianity. They were pre-existing cultural categories, available in the narrative toolbox of the ancient Mediterranean world. When the early Christians built the story of Jesus, they used tools that were already lying around. Consciously or unconsciously, deliberately or organically, the story of Jesus was assembled from parts that other cultures had already been using for centuries.

This doesn't mean Jesus didn't exist. He almost certainly did. But it means that the supernatural story built around the historical man follows patterns that humans had been applying to their heroes and their gods for a very long time. The virgin birth wasn't new. The dying and rising wasn't new. The divine son wasn't new.

What was new was the community that formed around this particular version of the story — and what that community became.

Christianity spread, first, because it offered something genuinely radical: dignity and hope to the poor, the enslaved, the marginalised. In a Roman world stratified by class and power, the message that God loved the lowest members of society was revolutionary. That message found an audience because it deserved one.

But message alone doesn't build a world religion. Paul of Tarsus took a small Jewish sect and gave it organisational structure, theological coherence, and a strategy for expansion beyond its ethnic origins. He planted churches across the Mediterranean, wrote letters that became scripture, and created a network that could survive the death of any individual leader. That was institutional genius, and it mattered as much as any miracle claim.

Then came Constantine. In 312 CE, the Roman emperor adopted Christianity — not necessarily out of spiritual conviction, but because he recognised its organisational power. With imperial backing, Christianity went from a persecuted minority to the official religion of the largest empire on earth. Competing traditions — the mystery cults of Mithras, Isis, Dionysus — were suppressed, their temples closed or converted, their texts destroyed. Christianity absorbed what it could use from local customs, feast days, and sacred sites, and eliminated what it couldn't.

By the fifth century, the competition was gone. Not because the Christian story was truer than the stories of Osiris or Tammuz or Dionysus. But because the institution behind the Christian story had the power of an empire, and the others didn't.

Christianity didn't win because its story was the only one. It won because its story was familiar enough to assimilate, its message was compelling enough to spread, and its institution became powerful enough to eliminate every alternative.

*     *     *

There's a word for what I was experiencing at this point in my research, and it's not one I expected.

Grief.

Not for my own faith — I'd never had one. But for the idea that this might all be true. For the door that was closing. I had started this process genuinely hoping to find something extraordinary. Some piece of evidence, some uncanny detail, some quality of these texts that would make me say: this is different from every other human story. This really could be divine.

I knew what I hoped to find, and I can be specific about it, because the specificity matters. I hoped to find a single detail in these ancient texts that could not be explained by the knowledge available to their authors at the time they wrote. A piece of astronomy that predated telescopes. A medical insight that predated anatomy. A historical fact that the author could not have known. Something — anything — that would constitute a fingerprint of genuinely superhuman knowledge. Not beauty. Not moral wisdom. Not the feeling of transcendence that comes from reading great literature. I can get all of those from Shakespeare, and no one claims he was taking dictation from God.

I hoped to find something that Shakespeare couldn't have produced. Something that required access to information no human in that century had.

I didn't find it. What I found instead was the opposite: texts that consistently reflected the knowledge and limitations of the time and place in which they were written. The cosmology of Genesis matches what a Mesopotamian observer would have concluded from looking at the sky. The medicine of the Quran matches what a seventh-century Arab would have learned from Galenic tradition. The history of Exodus matches what a later Judean court would have constructed to legitimise a national origin story. Everything in these texts is explicable without recourse to the supernatural. Everything in them is human.

Instead I found borrowed flood myths, recycled hero patterns, creation stories echoed from Babylon, and Church Fathers who explained away the evidence by blaming it on the devil.

Human stories. All the way down.

Every one of them human. Every one of them borrowed. Every one of them passed down with total conviction by people who didn't know any of this.

And the grief was not for a belief I had lost but for a belief I had wanted to gain. I had wanted to be persuaded. I had wanted to sit down with these texts and find myself outmatched — to encounter a mind so far beyond my own that the only honest response was awe. Instead I found minds like mine. Brilliant, sometimes. Profound, often. But human. Limited in the ways humans are limited. Wrong about the things humans are wrong about.

What would it have looked like, the thing I hoped to find? I can tell you, because I spent a long time imagining it. It would have looked like a text that knew something its author couldn't have known. A passage in Genesis that described the structure of DNA. A verse in the Quran that named the moons of Jupiter. A prophecy in Isaiah that gave the date and location of an event centuries in the future with a specificity that excluded lucky guessing. Not metaphorical resonance. Not after-the-fact reinterpretation. Actual knowledge, demonstrably beyond the reach of the person holding the pen.

That would have been different. That would have stopped me in my tracks. That would have been the fingerprint of something genuinely other — a mind that was not limited by the century it appeared in. And I would have said so, clearly and without hedging, because that is what the evidence would have demanded.

Instead, every text I examined was perfectly consistent with human authorship. The knowledge in them was the knowledge available at the time. The errors in them were the errors common at the time. The stories in them were the stories circulating at the time. There was nothing left over. Nothing unexplained. Nothing that required a supernatural author to account for.

And that is a loss, even for someone who never believed. It is the loss of a possibility. The closing of a door you never walked through but always liked knowing was there.

And if the stories are human, then the next question is the most important one in this entire book:

Why do we believe them?

Not why should we. Why do we. What is it about the human mind that makes us so ready, so eager, so neurologically predisposed to believe in gods that we've done it in every culture, on every continent, for fifty thousand years?

The answer, it turns out, is in our skulls.
