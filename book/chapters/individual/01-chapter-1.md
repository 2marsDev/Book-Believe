Chapter 1: One God, Three Stories
There are over four thousand religions in recorded human history.
Most of them are dead.
The ancient Greeks prayed to Zeus. Nobody does anymore. The Egyptians built the pyramids for Ra — his temples are tourist attractions now. The Norse worshipped Odin, the Romans sacrificed to Jupiter, the Aztecs cut out human hearts for Quetzalcoatl. All of them believed with total conviction. All of them are gone. We study them the same way we study dinosaurs — with curiosity, with fascination, and with the comfortable certainty that they have nothing to do with us.
But three survived.
Three religions, born from the same patch of Middle Eastern desert, from the same ancestor — a man named Abraham who supposedly made a deal with God around four thousand years ago — grew until they swallowed more than half the world. Christianity: two point four billion. Islam: one point nine billion. Judaism: sixteen million. Together, over four billion people. More than half of every human being alive.
They share a root. They share prophets. They share, in theory, the same God.
They do not share much else.
*     *     *
I started with Judaism, because it came first.
And because, of the three, it surprised me the most.
I expected rules. I found arguments. The Talmud — the vast body of Jewish commentary and law — isn’t a rulebook. It’s a record of disagreement. Rabbis debating across centuries, challenging each other, preserving the losing argument alongside the winning one. There’s a concept: machloket l’shem shamayim. A disagreement for the sake of heaven. The idea that arguing about truth is itself a form of worship.
That stopped me. Because in my experience, religion is the place where questions go to die. “Have faith.” “Don’t question God.” “It’s a mystery.” But here was a tradition that had built questioning into its operating system. Not as rebellion. As devotion.
Judaism also doesn’t obsess over hell the way Christianity and Islam do. The concept barely exists in traditional Jewish thought. And it doesn’t demand the whole world convert. The Talmud says righteous people of all nations share in the world to come. For a religion, that’s remarkably uninterested in forcing itself on others.
I found myself thinking: if all religions worked like this, I probably wouldn’t be writing this book.
But they don’t.
*     *     *
Christianity is the largest religion on Earth, and also the most internally contradictory institution I’ve ever encountered.
The same tradition that produced the Sermon on the Mount — “blessed are the peacemakers,” “love your enemies,” “turn the other cheek” — also produced the Crusades, the Inquisition, the transatlantic slave trade, and a network of residential schools where indigenous children were beaten for speaking their own language. The same church that runs hospitals across the developing world systematically covered up the rape of children across the developed one.
How do you hold both of those in your head at the same time?
Believers manage it, somehow. Because what Christianity offers — at its best, in its purest form — is extraordinary. It offers forgiveness. Absolute, unconditional, no-matter-what-you’ve-done forgiveness. You’re broken? Come as you are. You’ve failed? God loves you anyway. In a world drowning in shame and self-hatred, that message has pulled people back from ledges. Literally.
It offers hope in the face of death. Not philosophical comfort. A specific, personal promise: you will see your mother again. Your suffering is not meaningless. This isn’t all there is.
If you’ve ever watched someone you love die, you understand why people want that to be true. You don’t have to believe it to see why it matters.
I wrote all of this down. And I meant it. I mean it now.
But I also wrote down a question that wouldn’t leave me alone:
What if it’s beautiful and not true?
We’ll come back to that.
*     *     *
Then there’s Islam.
And here I have to be honest about something. In the modern West, the moment you say anything about Islam that isn’t flattering, a wall goes up. You get sorted into a box labeled Islamophobe and the conversation is over. I’m going to say things about Islam in this book that will make some people uncomfortable. I’m also going to say things about Christianity and Judaism that will make different people uncomfortable. That’s the deal. Everyone gets the same treatment. No one gets a shield.
So here’s what I found when I looked at Islam with open eyes.
The theology is clean. One God, indivisible, no mystery you have to accept without understanding. No Trinity, no divine son, no incarnation. Just God. And a clear set of instructions: declare your faith, pray five times a day, give to the poor, fast during Ramadan, make the pilgrimage if you can. It’s elegant. If you’re designing a religion from scratch, you’d be hard pressed to make the core architecture simpler.
And the history — the early history — is genuinely revolutionary. Muhammad’s first wife, Khadijah, was his employer. A successful businesswoman who proposed to him, not the other way around. Early Islamic law gave women the right to own property, inherit wealth, and initiate divorce. In the seventh century. Thirteen hundred years before most Western women had any of those rights. The Quran says, explicitly, “there is no compulsion in religion.”
I wrote all of that down too. Because it’s true.
But then I started looking at what happened next. At what Islam became, versus what it started as. At who holds the power now, and how they use it. At what happens to women, to doubters, to people who try to leave.
And that’s where the story gets darker.
Much darker.
*     *     *
Three religions. One God. Four billion people.
I went in knowing almost nothing. I came out knowing more than I ever expected to — about the faiths themselves, about the people who follow them, and about something I hadn’t anticipated at all:
About myself. About what I think truth is. About what I think we owe each other. About whether comfort and honesty can live in the same room, or whether at some point you have to choose.
I chose.
This book is what happened after that.
