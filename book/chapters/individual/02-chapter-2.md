Chapter 2: What They Promise
Every religion makes a deal.
It doesn’t usually look like a deal. It looks like love, or truth, or salvation. But underneath the poetry and the ritual, the architecture is always the same: give us your belief, your obedience, your time, your money, and your children’s minds — and in return, we’ll give you answers to the questions that keep you awake at three in the morning.
Why do I suffer?
What happens when I die?
Does any of this matter?
These are the most important questions a human being can ask. And for most of history, religion was the only institution that even attempted to answer them. Philosophy tried. Art tried. But religion was the only one that looked you in the eye and said: I know. For certain. Here’s the answer. And it’s good news.
The answers are different depending on which door you walk through. But the confidence is always the same.
*     *     *
In Christianity, the deal goes like this:
You are broken. Not because of anything you did — you were born that way. There’s a term for it: original sin. Adam and Eve disobeyed God in the Garden of Eden, and their disobedience infected the entire human species like a genetic disease. Every person ever born inherits this stain. You’re guilty before you take your first breath.
But there’s a cure. God loved humanity so much that he sent his own son — Jesus Christ, who is also God, who is also the Holy Spirit, three persons in one being, a concept called the Trinity that has confused theologians for two thousand years — to be tortured and killed. His death paid the debt that human sin created. If you believe in him, accept him as your savior, and repent of your sins, you’re forgiven. Completely. Eternally. The slate is wiped clean.
When you die, you go to heaven. Eternal communion with God. You’ll see everyone you’ve loved and lost. No more suffering. No more death. Forever.
If you don’t believe? Hell. Eternal separation from God. In some versions, literal fire. In others, just an infinite absence of everything good. Either way: forever.
That’s the deal. Believe and live forever in paradise. Don’t believe and suffer forever in darkness.
I want you to sit with that for a moment. Not to argue with it. Just to feel the weight of it. If you genuinely, truly, in your bones believed this was real — that the people you love will either spend eternity in bliss or eternity in agony, and that the deciding factor is whether they accept Jesus Christ — how would you live? What wouldn’t you do to save them?
That’s the power of the promise. And that’s why two and a half billion people organize their lives around it.
*     *     *
Islam’s deal is different in the details but identical in the architecture.
You are not born broken. Islam rejects original sin. Every child is born pure — in a state called fitrah, an innate orientation toward God. You don’t inherit Adam’s guilt. You start clean. What you do with that clean start is up to you.
The rules are clear. Five pillars: declare that there is no god but God and Muhammad is his messenger. Pray five times a day, facing Mecca. Give 2.5% of your wealth to the poor. Fast from dawn to sunset during Ramadan. Make the pilgrimage to Mecca once in your life if you’re able. Do these things, live a righteous life, and on the Day of Judgment your deeds will be weighed on a scale. If the good outweighs the bad, you enter Jannah — paradise. Gardens, rivers of honey and milk, companionship, peace, and the nearness of God.
If the bad outweighs the good? Jahannam. Hellfire. The Quran describes it in vivid, physical detail — scalding water, boiling skin, chains, trees with fruit like the heads of devils. Some scholars say it’s not eternal for Muslims who sinned but still believed. Others say it is. Either way, the imagery is designed to terrify. And it does.
There’s one sin that is always unforgivable: shirk. Associating partners with God. Saying God has a son, for instance. Or worshipping anything other than the one true God. This is the reason Islam considers Christianity misguided — the Trinity, in Islamic theology, is the ultimate error. A well-intentioned but catastrophic corruption of the pure monotheism that all prophets, from Adam to Muhammad, were sent to teach.
The scale. The weighing. The fire or the garden. Your life as a test with eternal consequences. That’s the deal. And one point nine billion people live inside it every day.
*     *     *
Judaism’s deal is the most unusual of the three.
Because, in a sense, Judaism doesn’t make the same deal at all.
There’s no detailed heaven to earn. There’s no vivid hell to avoid. The afterlife in Jewish theology is remarkably vague — a concept called Olam Ha-Ba, the World to Come, which is affirmed but barely described. There’s Gehinnom, sometimes compared to purgatory, but it’s temporary — a purification process lasting no more than twelve months. Not eternal torment. A car wash for the soul.
The focus isn’t on what happens after you die. It’s on what you do while you’re alive.
Judaism has 613 commandments — mitzvot — covering everything from how you eat to how you treat your employees to how you mourn the dead. The deal isn’t “believe the right things and you’ll be saved.” The deal is: “God chose your people for a special responsibility. You carry the burden of 613 rules. You serve as a light to the nations. In return, God maintains his covenant with your people.”
It’s not about individual salvation. It’s about collective obligation. You don’t ask “am I saved?” You ask “am I living rightly?”
Of the three deals, this one made the most sense to me. Not because I believe it’s true — I don’t — but because it’s the least manipulative. It doesn’t threaten you with eternal fire. It doesn’t bribe you with eternal paradise. It says: here are the rules for a good life. Try to follow them. The rest is between you and God, and frankly, God hasn’t given us the details.
There’s a kind of honesty in that. An admission that we don’t know what’s coming.
But it’s still a deal. Still a covenant you didn’t sign. Still a set of rules handed down from a being whose existence cannot be proven, enforced by a community you were born into and didn’t choose.
Still a story you’re asked to believe on faith.
*     *     *
I studied these promises for weeks. Months, eventually. I read the primary texts. I read the theologians. I read the critics and the defenders. I watched debates, listened to sermons, sat in the back of churches and mosques and synagogues.
And something started to bother me.
Not the promises themselves. The promises are brilliant. If I were designing a system to capture the human mind, I couldn’t do better than this. The combination of cosmic significance (“you matter to the creator of the universe”), existential comfort (“death is not the end”), moral clarity (“here is exactly how to live”), and community (“you belong with us”) is the most psychologically powerful package ever assembled. It hits every need. Every fear. Every hope.
What bothered me was the delivery mechanism.
Because every single one of these promises is contingent on a single condition: you have to believe something you cannot verify. You have to accept that a specific set of stories, written by specific people in specific historical circumstances, are not just wise or meaningful or culturally important — but literally true. Dictated or inspired by the creator of the universe. And you have to accept this on the say-so of people who benefit enormously from your acceptance.
That’s not a gift. That’s a transaction.
And I started to wonder what happens when you look at the receipt.
