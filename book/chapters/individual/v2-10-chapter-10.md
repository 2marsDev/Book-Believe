# Chapter 10: The Money Machine

In 2017, Hurricane Harvey dumped fifty-one inches of rain on Houston, Texas. People were drowning in their living rooms. Shelters filled up. The convention center ran out of space. And Joel Osteen's Lakewood Church — a 16,800-seat former NBA arena sitting on prime Houston real estate — locked its doors.

The building was dry. The parking garage had minor flooding on its lowest level. The arena itself was fine.

Osteen didn't open the doors until the internet shamed him into it. Until the photographs of locked gates went viral. Until other churches, smaller churches, churches without book deals and television contracts, had already been sheltering hundreds.

When he finally opened Lakewood, he said the church had been inaccessible due to flooding. Aerial photographs showed otherwise.

I keep coming back to that image. Sixteen thousand empty seats in a dry building. People sleeping in water two miles away.

Because it tells you something about what the money machine has become.

* * *

Let me give you a number. Actually, let me give you a range, because nobody knows the real number.

The total assets held by religious organizations in the United States are estimated at somewhere between $1.2 trillion and $4.8 trillion.

That range should bother you. The gap between the low estimate and the high estimate is $3.6 trillion. We don't know where in that range the truth falls, because religious organizations in America are not required to file the financial disclosures that every other nonprofit must file.

Every secular charity — the Red Cross, Doctors Without Borders, your local food bank — files a Form 990 with the IRS. This form details revenue, expenses, executive compensation, assets, and liabilities. It's public. Anyone can look it up.

Churches don't file Form 990s.

They don't have to. Under Section 508(c)(1)(A) of the Internal Revenue Code, churches are automatically exempt from federal income tax without even applying. They don't pay property tax. They don't pay sales tax in many states. They aren't required to disclose what they earn, what they spend, or what they pay their leaders.

The reasoning is the First Amendment. Government shouldn't entangle itself with religion. Requiring financial reporting, the argument goes, is a form of government oversight that infringes on religious liberty.

I understand the argument. I even respect it in principle.

But here's what it looks like in practice.

* * *

Kenneth Copeland is worth an estimated $300 million. He lives in a lakefront mansion valued at roughly $7 million, which is classified as a parsonage — a church-provided home — making it tax-exempt. He owns a fleet of private jets. He has his own airport. Kenneth Copeland Airport. In Newark, Texas. A man of God with his own runway.

In 2019, a reporter from Inside Edition caught Copeland outside and asked him about the jets. Why does a preacher need a private jet?

Copeland's answer, delivered with a smile that didn't reach his eyes: "If I flew commercial, I'd have to stop 65 percent of what I'm doing." In an earlier conversation with fellow prosperity preacher Jesse Duplantis, Copeland had been more candid. He said he couldn't fly commercial because he'd be stuck "in a long tube with a bunch of demons."

The demons, apparently, are you and me.

Creflo Dollar — and yes, that's his real name — stood in front of his congregation in 2015 and asked them to help him buy a $65 million Gulfstream G650 jet. He needed 200,000 people to give $300 each. He framed it as an act of faith. Giving to your pastor, he explained, is giving to God.

Dollar eventually got his jet.

Joel Osteen's net worth is estimated between $50 million and $100 million. His books have sold millions of copies. His television broadcast reaches over 100 countries. Lakewood Church takes in an estimated $90 million per year in donations.

None of these figures can be independently verified, because Lakewood doesn't have to tell anyone what it does with the money.

* * *

The prosperity gospel is the most financially efficient theology ever invented. Its core claim is elegant: God wants you to be rich. If you give generously to His church — and by church, we mean this specific pastor's ministry — God will multiply your investment. It's divine returns on divine capital.

The people who give are overwhelmingly not rich.

Sociological research consistently shows that prosperity gospel congregations skew lower-income. These are people who can't afford to tithe 10 percent of their income. They do it anyway, because they've been told that financial struggle is a symptom of insufficient faith, and that giving more will unlock God's abundance.

A 2016 study in the Journal for the Scientific Study of Religion found that members of prosperity-oriented churches reported higher rates of financial stress than members of non-prosperity churches, even when controlling for income. They gave more. They worried more. They had less.

The theology is unfalsifiable. If you give and get rich, God rewarded your faith. If you give and stay poor, your faith wasn't strong enough. Give more.

It's a closed loop with one exit: the pastor's bank account.

* * *

I wanted to understand what this looks like from the inside. Not the pastor's side. The giver's side.

In Memphis, I sat with a woman named Denise in her one-bedroom apartment on the third floor of a building where the elevator hadn't worked in months. She was fifty-three. She worked as a home health aide, earning about $1,800 a month after taxes. She tithed $200 of it. Every month. Without fail. Sometimes more, when the pastor called for a special offering.

Her apartment was clean and nearly empty. A couch with a blanket folded on one arm. A small television. A framed print of the Last Supper above the kitchen table. The refrigerator held a half-gallon of milk, a loaf of white bread, eggs, and a bottle of hot sauce.

She'd been tithing for eleven years. She told me this with pride.

"Pastor says the harvest is coming," she said. "You plant a seed, God multiplies it. You can't out-give God."

I asked her what the harvest would look like.

"A house," she said. Without hesitating. "A house of my own. God told me it's coming. I just have to keep planting."

She'd planted over $26,000 in eleven years. She didn't own a car. She took two buses to work. She'd never been on a plane. The last time she'd been to a dentist was four years ago, and she'd paid out of pocket because she didn't have insurance.

I need to say something about Denise. She was not stupid. That's the word people reach for, and it's wrong. She was sharp. She noticed when I'd left my recorder off and reminded me. She asked me pointed questions about what I was writing and who would read it. She made a joke about the elevator that was genuinely funny.

The seed-faith theology worked on her not because she lacked intelligence but because it mapped perfectly onto what she already knew. She'd grown up watching her mother put money in the collection plate. She'd grown up hearing that God rewards the faithful. And the biggest truth the prosperity gospel tells — the one that makes it stick — is that your suffering has meaning. That it isn't random. That the money leaving your hand is going somewhere, and that God sees it, and that something is coming.

When your life is hard enough, the idea that your hardship is an investment rather than just a loss — that is not a small comfort. That is the difference between endurance and despair.

I asked her if she'd ever thought about what would happen if the harvest didn't come.

She looked at me the way you'd look at someone who asked if the sun might not rise tomorrow.

"It's coming," she said. "He promised."

She didn't mean the pastor. She meant God. But the pastor was the one who'd made the promise, in God's name, and was cashing the checks, in God's name, and the distance between those two things — the promise and the cashing — was the entire con, laid bare in a one-bedroom apartment with an empty refrigerator and a woman whose faith was real even if the promise behind it wasn't.

I sat there for another twenty minutes. She offered me water. I took it.

When I left, I stood in the hallway for a long time. The elevator was broken. I took the stairs.

* * *

This isn't new. The money has always been there. What changes is the scale and the shamelessness.

Oral Roberts built a university, a hospital, and a television empire starting in the 1950s. In 1987, he went on air and told his viewers that God would "call him home" — kill him — if they didn't send $8 million by March. They sent $9.1 million. Roberts lived. God, apparently, was satisfied.

Jim Bakker and his wife Tammy Faye built the PTL Club into a broadcasting powerhouse. They had a satellite network, a theme park called Heritage USA, and an air-conditioned doghouse. In 1989, Bakker was convicted of fraud and sentenced to forty-five years in prison. He'd oversold "lifetime partnerships" — basically timeshares — by 60,000 units. He took in $158 million and spent most of it on operations, personal luxury, and hush money to Jessica Hahn, a church secretary he'd sexually assaulted.

Bakker served five years. He's back on television now, selling survival food buckets.

Jimmy Swaggart was the most-watched televangelist in the world in the mid-1980s. His ministry brought in $150 million a year. He was caught with a prostitute in 1988 and gave his famous crying confession on live television. Caught again in 1991, he told his congregation, "The Lord told me it's flat none of your business."

He kept preaching.

The pattern is always the same. Build the audience. Build the revenue. When scandal hits, cry, repent, and rebuild. The audience forgives because forgiveness is the product they've been buying all along.

* * *

The Catholic Church is the largest non-governmental landowner on Earth. The Vatican's liquid assets are estimated at $10 billion to $15 billion, not including an art collection — Michelangelo, Raphael, Caravaggio, Bernini — that has never been independently appraised and is effectively priceless.

The Vatican Bank, formally the Institute for the Works of Religion, has a long history of opacity. The details could fill their own book — shell companies, a banker found hanging under a London bridge with bricks in his pockets, ties to a secret Masonic lodge, decades of scandal that reads like a thriller nobody would believe if it were fiction. What matters here is the pattern: when an institution claims divine authority and answers to no earthly regulator, money disappears. In 2023, Cardinal Angelo Becciu was convicted of embezzlement in a London real estate deal that lost the Vatican an estimated $400 million, money that had come from Peter's Pence — donations from ordinary Catholics who thought they were giving to charity. He was one man. The system that allowed him to operate remains intact.

* * *

Islamic finance operates on different principles but generates its own questions.

The waqf system — the Islamic endowment — is one of the oldest charitable structures in the world. Mosques, schools, hospitals, and vast tracts of land have been held as waqf for centuries, and the system works well when it works.

But then there's the petrodollar pipeline.

Since the oil boom of the 1970s, Saudi Arabia has spent an estimated $100 billion exporting Wahhabism — its ultraconservative interpretation of Sunni Islam — around the world. The money has built mosques, funded madrasas, trained imams, and distributed literature on every continent.

This is not a conspiracy theory. It's documented Saudi government policy. A 2006 Freedom House report examined literature distributed by the Saudi government to mosques in the United States and found material promoting hatred of Jews and Christians, rejection of democracy, and endorsement of violence against those who leave Islam.

The radicalization pipeline doesn't start with a bomb. It starts with a building. A new mosque. A free school. A charismatic imam whose salary is paid from thousands of miles away, whose theology was not chosen by the community he serves but by the state that signs his checks.

Most mosques funded by Saudi money are not breeding grounds for terrorism. That claim would be absurd. But the influence operation is real, and the money is staggering, and it has shifted the global character of Sunni Islam in a more conservative direction over fifty years.

* * *

Jewish institutional finance is structurally different. Most Jewish organizations in America operate as secular nonprofits that file Form 990s, and synagogue budgets are generally disclosed. The transparency is genuinely better. But concentrated religious wealth is never immune to abuse: in 2019, a federal investigation into a Lakewood, New Jersey, Orthodox community uncovered a systematic tax fraud scheme involving millions of dollars, enabled by a community structure that resists outside scrutiny on religious grounds. Not representative. But not unthinkable either.

* * *

Now zoom out. Forget the scandals. Look at the basic mechanism.

Tithing. Ten percent.

The concept comes from the Hebrew Bible. Modern churches, particularly evangelical Protestant churches, have turned it into a weekly expectation. Some teach that tithing is mandatory for salvation. Others frame it as obedience. Either way, the social pressure is enormous. In many congregations, giving is tracked. Envelopes are labeled. Some churches publish donor lists.

Americans gave an estimated $135 billion to religious organizations in 2022, according to Giving USA. That's more than they gave to education, human services, and health organizations combined.

Where does it go?

Some goes to genuine charitable work — food pantries, shelters, disaster relief, community programs. Religious organizations do an enormous amount of social good, and denying that would be dishonest.

But studies suggest that the majority of church spending goes to operational costs. A 2016 analysis by the Evangelical Council for Financial Accountability found that member churches spent an average of 82 percent of their budgets on internal operations and 18 percent on outreach and missions. Of that 18 percent, much of the "missions" spending was evangelism — not charity but recruitment.

So when someone puts $100 in the offering plate, roughly $82 goes to keeping the building open and the pastor paid. Maybe $10 goes to community service. And some portion of the rest goes to convincing more people to put money in the offering plate.

This is not fraud. It's how organizations work. Buildings cost money. Staff costs money.

But the donor thinks she's giving to God. She's giving to overhead.

* * *

The tax exemption question is, at its core, a math problem.

A widely cited 2012 study by University of Tampa professor Ryan Cragun estimated that the total subsidy to religion through tax exemptions is approximately $71 billion per year. That's more than the annual budget of the Department of Homeland Security.

Defenders of religious tax exemption make several arguments, and some of them are strong. The First Amendment — if you can tax a church, you can shut it down. The social services — an estimated $20 billion or more annually. The small churches — the median congregation has seventy-five regular attendees and a budget under $200,000. These are community organizations, not corporations.

All of this is true. And none of it addresses the problem.

The problem is that the same exemption that keeps a small Baptist church in rural Alabama solvent also shields Lakewood Church's $90 million annual revenue from any public scrutiny. The system makes no distinction between a church that spends everything on its community and a ministry that makes its leader a billionaire.

Because to make that distinction, you'd need oversight. And oversight means entanglement. And entanglement means the First Amendment argument kicks in.

It's a perfect shield. The smallest church and the largest televangelist hide behind the same constitutional principle.

* * *

I want to be clear about something, because it matters.

Most of the people who work in religious institutions are not getting rich. Most pastors earn modest salaries. Most imams are underpaid. Most rabbis balance spiritual leadership with the reality of keeping a building from falling down.

The woman who runs the food pantry out of her church basement is not the problem. The imam who teaches Arabic classes after evening prayers is not the problem. The seventy-five-member congregation in rural Alabama that keeps its doors open with bake sales and a pastor who drives a ten-year-old Camry — they are not the problem.

And here's the part I can't leave out: some of the money does real good. After Hurricane Katrina, churches organized faster than FEMA. After the 2010 earthquake in Haiti, Catholic Relief Services provided shelter, clean water, and medical care to hundreds of thousands. When fires swept through Maui in 2023, it was local churches that opened their doors first. Every week, in thousands of communities across the country, religious organizations feed people, house people, counsel people, bury people. Quietly. Without cameras. Without book deals.

That matters. I've sat in enough church basements and community halls to know it matters.

The problem is the industry that has grown up alongside these efforts. The television empires. The mega-churches with coffee bars and gift shops. The Vatican's investment portfolio. The Saudi export machine. The political lobbying firms that ensure the tax exemption never gets examined too closely.

The problem is that this industry — one of the largest economic systems on the planet — operates in the name of God and reports to no one.

Trillions of dollars. Zero mandatory transparency.

In any other sector, we'd call that a scandal. In religion, we call it freedom.

* * *

I sat in a prosperity gospel church once, in a suburb of Atlanta. The building was enormous. The sound system was professional grade. The screens were high-definition. The parking lot had a traffic management team.

The pastor wore a suit that cost more than my first car. He preached about abundance. He preached about the windows of heaven opening. He preached about planting a seed — that was the word, "seed" — and watching God multiply it.

Then the offering came. The ushers passed the buckets. But first, the pastor asked everyone who wanted to give $1,000 or more to stand up.

A few people stood. The congregation applauded.

Then $500. More people stood. More applause.

Then $100. Most of the room.

I looked at the faces. I saw a woman in her sixties who had come in a car with a cracked windshield. She stood when they called $100. She stood slowly, the way you stand when your knees hurt, and she held her envelope up and the people around her clapped and she closed her eyes. I watched her face. She wasn't performing. Something was happening to her. Something that looked, from where I sat, like relief. Like the moment you hand over a burden you've been carrying. She believed — I could see it — that this money was leaving her hand and entering God's. That the exchange was real. That she was planting something that would bloom.

I saw a young couple who didn't look like they had $100 to give. They stood too. They put money in the bucket. The woman squeezed the man's hand.

I saw the pastor smile.

And I thought about Denise in Memphis. Her empty refrigerator. Her eleven years of seeds. Her harvest that was always coming and never arriving. And I looked at this room full of people being applauded for giving money they couldn't afford, and the pastor collecting it in a suit that cost more than their rent, and the God who was supposedly watching all of this and blessing it, and I couldn't speak. I just sat there.

The woman with the cracked windshield sat back down. She folded her hands in her lap. She looked peaceful. That was the worst part.

The applause kept going.
