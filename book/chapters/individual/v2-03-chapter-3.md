## Chapter 3: The People Inside

Before I start questioning the stories, I need to talk about the people who live inside them.

Because it's easy — too easy — to write about religion as if it's just a set of ideas. A system of propositions you can analyze and debunk the way you'd debunk a bad business plan. But religion is not an idea. Religion is a woman kneeling beside her daughter's hospital bed at two in the morning, whispering to someone she believes can hear her. Religion is a man washing his father's body after death according to rituals his grandfather taught him. Religion is a teenager in a small town who has no friends, no status, no confidence — until she walks into a youth group and someone says "Jesus loves you exactly as you are" and for the first time in her life she feels seen.

If I was going to take these faiths apart — and I was beginning to sense that the evidence would lead me there — I owed it to the people inside them to see them first. Not as data points. Not as case studies. As people. People whose lives worked, whose days had shape, whose suffering had meaning, because of the thing I was about to examine.

I needed to understand those people before I could honestly examine what they believe.

*     *     *

I met a woman — I'll call her Sarah — at a community center in London. A church-adjacent building in Lewisham, one of those postwar brick halls with fluorescent lighting and stackable chairs and a kitchen that smelled permanently of instant coffee. There was a noticeboard by the entrance covered in flyers for grief support groups, AA meetings, English language classes. A place where people came when they needed something and didn't know where else to go.

Sarah was in her late fifties, retired teacher, grew up in a secular household in Kent. She had short grey hair, reading glasses pushed up on her forehead, and the kind of direct eye contact that made you feel she was really listening to you rather than waiting for her turn to speak. We sat at a table near the window. Outside, a man was hosing down the car park for no obvious reason. Sarah held her tea with both hands and talked about her son.

What happened at forty-two? Her son died. Nineteen years old. Motorcycle accident. The kind of senseless, random, irreversible thing that makes you want to crawl out of your own skin because the pain has nowhere to go. His name was Daniel. She told me his name early, and she used it throughout, as if to keep him present in the room. Daniel liked motorcycles, Daniel studied music, Daniel had his father's laugh.

"I tried therapy," she told me. "I tried medication. I tried drinking. I tried pretending I was fine. None of it worked. I was drowning. I would wake up in the morning and for about three seconds everything was normal, and then I'd remember, and it was like being hit by a car. Every single morning. For months."

A neighbor invited her to a church service. She went because she had nothing left to try. She didn't believe in God. Didn't believe in heaven. Didn't believe in anything except the fact that her son was gone and she couldn't breathe.

"The first Sunday, I just sat there and cried. Nobody told me to stop. Nobody tried to explain why it happened. Nobody said 'God has a plan' or 'he's in a better place' — all the things people say that make you want to scream. A woman I'd never met put her hand on my arm and just sat with me. Didn't say a word. Just sat there. And when the service was over she said, 'Same time next week?' and I said yes."

"The next Sunday, I went back. And the one after that. And the one after that. I went because it was the only hour of the week where I wasn't pretending to be okay. And eventually I started to believe. Not because someone proved God to me. Not because of an argument or a book. Because I needed it to be true. I needed my son to still exist somewhere. And these people — they made me feel like he did."

I asked her: "Do you actually believe? That there's a heaven? That he's there?"

She looked at me for a long time. Not with anger. Not with defensiveness. With something closer to patience — the patience of someone who has been asked the question before and knows that no answer will satisfy the asker.

"I believe it in the way you believe the ground is solid," she said. "Not because I've tested it. Because if I stop believing it, I'll fall."

I didn't have a response to that. I still don't.

What I had was a recognition — sudden, uncomfortable — that her faith wasn't naivety. It wasn't ignorance. It wasn't a failure of critical thinking. It was something I didn't have a word for. The closest I could get was structural. Her belief was structural. It held things up. Remove it and other things would collapse — not just her theology but her ability to get out of bed, to eat, to teach, to function in a world that had taken her son. The belief was load-bearing. And I was sitting across from her with a notebook full of questions about whether the load-bearing wall was built on solid ground.

I thanked her. I meant it. I walked out into the Lewisham afternoon — drizzle, traffic, a woman pushing a pram past a betting shop — and I thought about what it would mean to tell Sarah that her wall was built on sand. What it would cost her. What right I had.

I didn't have an answer. I kept walking.

*     *     *

I spoke with a man in Amsterdam — I'll call him Yusuf — who converted to Islam in his early twenties. Dutch-born, non-religious parents, studied engineering at Delft. Met a Moroccan girl at university, fell in love, started learning about her faith.

We met at a cafe near Vondelpark. He was tall, fair-haired, with a neatly trimmed beard — not the sort of person you'd immediately identify as Muslim, which, he told me with a slight smile, was something he dealt with constantly. People assumed he was joking when he said he was headed to Friday prayers.

"She never asked me to convert," he said. "Her parents did. Not directly. But it was clear: if I wanted to marry her, I needed to be Muslim. At first I thought I'd just go through the motions. Say the shahada, tick the box, marry the girl. But then I actually started reading the Quran. And something happened."

He paused here, and I could see him searching for the right words. Not performing. Searching.

"It was like finding the manual for something I'd been trying to operate without instructions. My whole life I'd felt like I was making it up as I went along. And here was this book that said: no. There's a way to do this. There's a right way to wake up, to eat, to speak to people, to handle your anger, to grieve, to be grateful. I didn't have to invent it all from scratch."

He described the structure of Islamic daily life — the five prayers, the fasting, the discipline — as something that rescued him from what he called "the chaos of having no rules."

His day, he told me, begins before dawn. The alarm goes off for Fajr, the first prayer. He washes — wudu, the ritual ablution, hands, face, arms, feet — and prays in the dark of his living room while his wife and children sleep. Then he goes back to bed for an hour or reads Quran. The second prayer, Dhuhr, comes at midday. He prays at his office — there's a small room set aside for it, which his employer arranged after he asked. Asr in the afternoon. Maghrib at sunset. Isha at night. Five anchors in the day. Five moments when everything else stops and there is only God.

"When you're young and secular and Dutch, everything is permitted and nothing matters. You can do anything, but nothing has weight. Islam gave me weight. It gave me a schedule, a purpose, a framework. I knew when to wake up, when to eat, when to pray, when to stop. For the first time in my life, my days had shape."

He said this with a kind of quiet gratitude that was hard to dismiss. He wasn't performing. He wasn't trying to recruit me. He was describing something that had changed his life, and he knew it, the way you know your own name.

I asked him about the parts of Islam that troubled me. The treatment of women. The apostasy laws. The blasphemy rules. He was thoughtful, not defensive.

"Those things bother me too," he said. "But I separate the faith from the culture. The Quran says one thing. What some countries do with it is another. Islam gave me a better life. I can't throw away the whole thing because some people misuse it."

I nodded. I understood the logic. But a question formed in my head that I didn't ask him, because it would have been rude:

If your wife had been Buddhist, would you have found the same peace in Buddhism? If she had been Hindu, would the Bhagavad Gita have given you the same structure? Was it Islam that saved you — or was it the having-of-something, the belonging-to-anything, that did the work?

I sat with that question longer than I'd expected to. It kept circling back. Because if Yusuf's transformation was really about Islam — about the specific truth claims of the Quran, about the specific reality of Allah — then it mattered enormously which religion he'd found. It was the right one. The true one. And the peace he felt was evidence of its truth.

But if his transformation was about structure — about the human need for rhythm, discipline, community, and meaning, which any sufficiently comprehensive system could provide — then the specific content of Islam was almost incidental. What saved him was the shape, not the substance. The container, not the contents.

Both possibilities were real. Both were sitting in the same man, in the same cafe, drinking the same coffee. And I couldn't tell which was true. I don't think Yusuf could either, and I don't think the question had ever occurred to him, because from inside the faith there's no reason to ask it. The container and the contents are one. You don't separate the water from the glass.

I kept that question to myself. It didn't feel like mine to ask. Not yet.

*     *     *

Through a mutual contact, I was introduced to a man I'll call Thomas. He's sixty-three, lives in a small city in the south of England, and has been Catholic his entire life.

We met at his home — a terraced house with a small front garden, a statue of the Virgin Mary in a little alcove by the door, and a hall that smelled of furniture polish and something baking. His wife made tea and then left us in the sitting room, which had a crucifix on the wall above the fireplace, a shelf of family photographs, and a well-worn leather armchair that was clearly his.

His parents were Catholic. Their parents were Catholic. The faith in his family went back as far as anyone could trace — generations of English Catholics who had held on through the centuries when Catholicism was illegal in England, when priests were hidden in secret rooms and Mass was said behind locked doors. Thomas knew this history. He carried it lightly, but he carried it.

He was baptized as an infant, did his First Communion at seven, was confirmed at thirteen. He went to Catholic school. He married in a Catholic church. His three children were baptized in the same font he was.

I asked him if he'd ever had a moment of doubt. A crisis. A dark night of the soul.

He thought about it for a while. Not quickly, the way you'd answer a question you'd rehearsed, but slowly, as if actually checking, rummaging through decades of interior life for something that matched what I was asking.

Then he shrugged.

"Not really," he said. "I mean, there are things I don't understand. Things the Church does that I disagree with. The abuse scandals — those were terrible. I struggled with those. I was angry. But doubt? About whether God exists? No. That's like asking if I've ever doubted that I have parents. They were just there. He was just there."

Thomas goes to Mass every Sunday. Has done since he was old enough to sit still. I asked him what it meant to him — not theologically, but experientially. What happens when he's there.

He was quiet for a moment.

"It's the one hour of the week where I'm not thinking about anything else," he said. "Not work, not bills, not the news. When the bread is consecrated — when it becomes the body of Christ — I know that sounds mad to you. I know it does. But I believe it. And in that moment, I'm as close to God as I'll ever be in this life. Everything else falls away. It's just me and him."

He said this without embarrassment. Without apology. Without the faintest suggestion that he needed me to understand, let alone agree.

He prays before meals, not performatively, just a quiet moment with his eyes closed. He volunteers at a food bank run by his parish. When his wife was diagnosed with cancer three years ago, the congregation organized meals for six months. Someone drove her to every chemotherapy appointment. He told me this matter-of-factly, as if it were obvious, as if of course that's what people do.

"People ask me why I believe," he said. "And I never know what to say. It's not a decision I made. It's just the way the world is. The sky is blue. Water is wet. God is real. I don't mean that to sound simple. I know it sounds simple. But it is what it is."

He wasn't defensive. He wasn't evangelical. He wasn't even particularly interested in convincing me. He was just a man who had been born into a faith the way you're born into a language, and the faith had been good to him, and he had no reason to question it any more than he'd question his own accent.

I found Thomas more difficult to reckon with than Sarah or Yusuf. They had both come to faith from outside — pulled in by crisis or by love. You could trace the mechanism. You could see the need that faith had filled. With Sarah, it was grief. With Yusuf, it was chaos. In both cases, there was a before and an after, and the faith was the hinge between them.

Thomas had no such story. No crisis. No conversion. No dramatic turning point. Just a life lived inside a tradition that fit him the way water fits a glass — completely, naturally, without strain. There was no before. There was only ever inside.

And that made him the hardest to write about, because there was nothing to explain. Sarah believed because her son died. Yusuf believed because a woman led him to a structure he needed. Thomas believed because he believed. He'd always believed. The faith wasn't a response to anything. It was the ground he stood on, and he'd never needed to examine the ground because the ground had never shifted.

He was, I realized, the most common kind of believer. Not the one who found God in a foxhole. Not the one who discovered faith through a lover. The one who was simply born into it and stayed. The quiet majority. The ones who never make the news, never write memoirs, never show up in debates about religion. They just live. They just believe. And their belief is so deeply woven into their identity that the question "why?" barely makes sense to them. You might as well ask why they speak English. Why they're right-handed. Why their eyes are brown.

I kept trying to find the seam in Thomas. The place where the faith was stitched onto the person, where you could see the join. I couldn't find it. There was no join. It was all one piece. And that bothered me — not because there was anything wrong with Thomas, but because my entire project depended on the assumption that belief is something you can examine separately from the person who holds it. That you can hold it up to the light and see through it. Thomas suggested otherwise. Thomas suggested that for some people, the belief and the person are the same thing, and you can't remove one without destroying the other.

I left his house and sat in my car for ten minutes before starting the engine. I wrote in my notebook: "Thomas is the chapter's challenge. Not its counterpoint. Its challenge."

*     *     *

I spent time with a young woman in Brooklyn — I'll call her Miriam — who had recently left the Hasidic community she was raised in.

She'd grown up in Williamsburg. Spoke Yiddish before English. Married at eighteen to a man she'd met twice. Had three children by twenty-three. She'd never watched a movie, never used the internet unsupervised, never worn pants. She'd never eaten in a restaurant that wasn't kosher. She'd never been alone in a room with a man who wasn't her husband or a close relative. The boundaries of her world were absolute and total, and for the first twenty-five years of her life she didn't know they were boundaries. They were just the world.

"The community was my entire world," she said. "Every person I knew, every place I went, every meal I ate, every word I spoke was inside that world. Leaving wasn't like changing your religion. It was like leaving your planet."

She left when she was twenty-six. The process took over a year. It started with doubt — a question she couldn't answer, a contradiction she couldn't resolve — and progressed through secret library visits, hidden internet searches on a phone she kept in her locker at a gym she'd joined under a false pretence. She read books she'd been told were dangerous and found that they weren't dangerous at all. They were just books. And slowly, invisibly, the structure she'd lived inside her whole life began to look different. Not like a home. Like a box.

Her husband got custody of the children — the religious court, the beit din, ruled in his favor. She had no secular legal representation. She had no money, no work experience, no qualifications recognised outside the community. The court — which was not a court in any legal sense she would later come to understand, but which had total authority within the world she was leaving — decided that the children's religious upbringing must be preserved, and that a mother who had abandoned the faith could not be trusted to provide it.

Her parents sat shiva for her. Shiva. The mourning ritual for the dead. They covered the mirrors in their house. They sat on low chairs for seven days. They received visitors who came to comfort them for the loss of their daughter — their living, breathing daughter, who was at that moment sitting in a one-bedroom flat in Crown Heights, trying to figure out how to open a bank account.

They declared their living daughter dead because she stopped believing.

When I met her, she was thirty-one. Five years out. She saw her children every other weekend, supervised, in a neutral location — a community center in Midtown, fluorescent lights, plastic chairs, a box of toys in the corner that her children were too old for. She told me about those visits with a steadiness that I found almost more painful than tears. The children were polite. They called her Mama. They told her about school, about friends, about things she couldn't share and a life she wasn't part of. And then the two hours were up and they went back to Williamsburg and she took the subway home alone.

She was working as a barista and taking community college classes at night. She was learning to live in a world she'd never been prepared for — a world where nobody tells you what to wear, what to eat, when to pray, or what to think.

"People think freedom is easy," she said. "It's not. When you've been told what to do every second of your life, freedom feels like falling. You don't know how to choose. You don't know who you are when nobody's telling you who to be. I remember standing in a supermarket for the first time, in the regular supermarket, not the kosher one, and I just stood there staring at the shelves. There was so much. So many options. And nobody was telling me which one was allowed. I left without buying anything. I went home and cried."

I asked her if she regretted leaving.

She didn't hesitate.

"No. But I grieve every day. Not for the beliefs. For the people. For my mother. For Friday night dinners. For the singing. For the feeling that someone was holding the whole world together and all I had to do was follow the rules."

She paused. Turned her coffee cup in her hands.

"Nobody tells you the worst part of losing your faith. It's not that you stop believing in God. It's that you start having to figure out everything for yourself. And some mornings that's the loneliest feeling in the world."

She said this looking out the window, not at me, and I had the sense that she was saying it to herself as much as to anyone. That it was something she told herself on those mornings, a kind of anti-prayer — an acknowledgement addressed to no one, because there was no one to address it to.

*     *     *

We sat in silence for a while after that. The coffee shop was busy around us. Someone was laughing at a table near the window. A barista called out a name and nobody claimed it. The espresso machine hissed. Outside, the Williamsburg Bridge was visible between two buildings, its cables strung against a grey sky like the strings of some enormous instrument, and I thought about how close we were, geographically, to the community she'd left. A few blocks. A few turns. A different planet.

I thought about the preparations that would be happening over there right now. It was late Friday afternoon. The bakeries would be busy — challah for Shabbat, the braided bread that Miriam would have baked herself every week for eight years of marriage. The women would be cleaning, cooking, setting tables with white cloths. The men would be coming home from the mikveh, the ritual bath, skin still damp, dressed in their Shabbat best. Soon the candles would be lit — two at minimum, one for zachor (remember) and one for shamor (observe), the two versions of the fourth commandment. The woman of the house would cover her eyes and draw the light toward her three times and speak the blessing, and for a few seconds, in kitchens and dining rooms across Williamsburg, time would stop. The work week would end. Shabbat would begin. And for twenty-five hours, the community would rest, and eat, and sing, and pray, and be together in a way that the modern world has largely forgotten how to be.

And Miriam was out here. On this side of the line. In a coffee shop in Brooklyn, talking to a stranger with a notebook, drinking coffee that might or might not be kosher — she'd stopped keeping track — while a few streets away, a world she'd been exiled from was preparing to do the most beautiful thing it did.

I asked her one more question. I asked if she ever walked through Williamsburg on a Friday evening. If she ever went to look.

She smiled. It was the saddest smile I'd seen in a long time.

"Sometimes," she said. "I walk past. I hear the singing through the windows. I smell the food. And for about thirty seconds I want to go back more than I want anything in the world. And then I remember why I left. And I keep walking."

*     *     *

Four people. Four different relationships with faith.

Sarah needed God because her son died and nothing else could carry the weight. Yusuf found God because a woman he loved led him to a structure his life was missing. Thomas never needed to find God because God had always been there, part of the furniture of his life, as unremarkable and essential as breathing. Miriam lost God because the structure became a prison, and the price of freedom was everything she'd ever known.

Each of them was telling the truth.

And that's what makes this so hard. Because the part of me that cares about evidence wants to say: Sarah, the comfort you feel doesn't make heaven real. Yusuf, the structure you found exists in a hundred non-religious forms. Thomas, the fact that you've never questioned something doesn't make it true. Miriam, the community you lost was also the cage that held you.

All of that might be right.

But so is this: Sarah sleeps at night because of her faith. Yusuf is a kinder, more disciplined person because of his. Thomas moves through the world with a steadiness that most people never find. And Miriam is braver and more honest than all of them because she gave hers up.

I kept thinking about that for a long time afterward. About the gap between what's useful and what's true. About whether it's possible for something to work beautifully and still be built on a story that doesn't hold up. About what you owe people — what I owed Sarah, what I owed Thomas — if you start pulling at the threads of the thing that holds their world together.

I thought about Thomas most of all. Because with Sarah and Yusuf, I could at least imagine the conversation. I could imagine saying: the comfort is real, but the source might not be what you think. They would resist, but they would understand the question. They'd been on the other side themselves. They knew what it was like to not believe.

Thomas had never been on the other side. There was no other side for Thomas. And so the question — is it true? — would land differently. Not as a challenge to his beliefs, but as a challenge to his identity. To the ground he stood on. To every Sunday morning of his sixty-three years. I wasn't sure I had the right to ask it. I wasn't sure anyone did.

But I was starting to think that the question didn't care about rights. The question was just there, like gravity, and it was pulling me toward something I couldn't yet see.

I didn't have answers. I had a notebook full of conversations, a head full of questions, and the sense that I was standing at the edge of something I couldn't yet see clearly.

The coffee had gone cold. Miriam had left twenty minutes ago — she had a shift starting at four. I paid the bill and walked out into the Brooklyn afternoon. The air was sharp, the kind of cold that catches in your throat. The bridge cables caught the light, copper and gold against the grey. A few blocks south, behind the buildings and the traffic and the ordinary Friday noise of a city going about its business, a community was preparing for Shabbat — setting tables, lighting candles, singing songs that had been sung for a thousand years. Mothers were blessing children. Fathers were pouring wine. Doors were closing against the night.

And Miriam was out here, on this side of the line, figuring it out alone. Putting on her apron. Making someone's coffee. Starting her shift.

I turned up my collar against the wind and kept walking.
