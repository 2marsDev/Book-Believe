Chapter 9: The Horrifying Answer
There is a photograph I can't get out of my head.

It was taken in Ireland, sometime in the 1960s. A group of women in a Magdalene laundry. They're standing in a row, wearing identical grey smocks, holding sheets. Their heads are down. They look like prisoners.

They were prisoners.

The Magdalene laundries were institutions run by Catholic religious orders in Ireland from the eighteenth century until the last one closed in 1996. Not 1896. 1996. Women and girls were sent there for the crime of being "fallen" — which could mean pregnant out of wedlock, sexually abused, orphaned, too pretty, too flirtatious, or simply inconvenient. Once inside, they were unpaid laborers. They washed sheets for commercial clients. Hotels. Hospitals. The Irish military. The profits went to the nuns.

An estimated thirty thousand women passed through the Magdalene laundries. Many stayed for years. Some stayed for life. They were given new names. They were forbidden to speak without permission. They were beaten for infractions. When they died, some were buried in unmarked graves on the grounds.

The Irish state knew. The Irish public knew. Everyone knew. And everyone looked the other way, because the Church was the moral authority, and if the Church said these women needed saving, then who were you to argue?

This is the other answer. The one that isn't beautiful at all.

*     *     *

In Chapter 8, I showed you what religion does when it works. Community. Comfort. Meaning. Recovery. Belonging.

Now I'm going to show you what religion does when it has power.

Not fringe cases. Not lone extremists. Not the "bad apples" that every apologist points to when confronted with institutional harm. I'm talking about structural, systematic, documented patterns of damage — built into the institutions, protected by the theology, and repeated across centuries in all three Abrahamic traditions.

I'm not going to be generous in this chapter. I was generous in the last one. This one gets the truth without softening.

*     *     *

Start with control.

Every religion needs a way to keep people inside. Recruitment is only half the equation. The other half is retention. And the most effective retention mechanism ever invented is not love, or community, or meaning. It's fear.

Christianity built the most effective fear machine in human history. It's called hell.

Think about what hell actually is, as a concept. You don't just die. You don't just cease to exist. You suffer. Forever. Not for a thousand years. Not for a million. Forever. An infinity of conscious torment, inflicted by an all-loving God on people whose only crime was not believing the right things.

Jonathan Edwards, the eighteenth-century Puritan preacher, described it in his most famous sermon: "The God that holds you over the pit of hell, much as one holds a spider, or some loathsome insect over the fire, abhors you... he looks upon you as worthy of nothing else but to be cast into the fire."

This wasn't a fringe figure. This was one of the most influential theologians in American history. His sermon, "Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God," was preached to congregations that included children.

Children.

We'll come back to that.

Islam has its own version. Jahannam. The Quran describes it in clinical detail. Surah 4:56: "Those who disbelieve in Our signs — We will drive them into a fire. Every time their skins are roasted through, We will replace them with other skins so they may taste the punishment." The torture doesn't end when the nerve endings are destroyed. God regenerates them so the pain can continue.

This is in the text. The holy text. The text that 1.9 billion people believe was dictated by the most merciful, most compassionate being in existence.

Judaism, to its credit, mostly opted out of this. As I mentioned earlier, the Jewish concept of the afterlife is vague, and Gehinnom — the closest thing to hell — is temporary. Twelve months, maximum. Judaism's control mechanisms operate differently: through community, identity, obligation, and — in ultra-Orthodox contexts — the threat of expulsion, which amounts to social death.

But the broader point holds. Religious institutions maintain power through a system of rewards and punishments that operates beyond the reach of any earthly authority. You can appeal a prison sentence. You can sue a government. You cannot appeal hell. There is no court. No oversight. No parole. The punishment is infinite and the judge is invisible.

Excommunication. Shunning. Disfellowshipping. Cherem. Takfir. Every tradition has a mechanism for expelling members — and the psychological effects are devastating. The Jehovah's Witnesses practice "disfellowshipping," in which members who violate doctrine are cut off from all social contact with the congregation, including family members. Parents stop speaking to their children. Siblings become strangers. A 2021 study published in the Journal of Religion and Health found that former Jehovah's Witnesses who experienced shunning had significantly elevated rates of depression, anxiety, PTSD, and suicidal ideation.

In Islam, apostasy — leaving the faith — is punishable by death under traditional Islamic jurisprudence. This is not a historical relic. As of 2024, apostasy carries the death penalty in at least thirteen countries, including Saudi Arabia, Iran, Afghanistan, and Somalia. Even in countries where the law doesn't mandate execution, social consequences can be devastating. Families disown their children. Communities turn their backs. In some contexts, the apostate simply disappears.

This is the architecture of control. The threat of eternal suffering for disobedience. The threat of social death for doubt. The promise that the all-seeing judge is always watching, always recording, and that the punishment for stepping out of line is infinite.

And if you think this is just historical, consider this: a 2023 Pew Research survey found that 73 percent of American Christians believe in hell. Not metaphorical hell. Actual hell. A real place where real people really suffer for eternity.

Seventy-three percent.

Now let's talk about women.

I am going to describe what all three Abrahamic religions do to women, structurally and institutionally. Not what individual men do. What the systems do.

In Orthodox Judaism, women cannot serve as rabbis or cantors. They cannot count toward a minyan — the quorum of ten required for communal prayer. They sit behind a mechitza, a physical barrier separating them from men in the synagogue. In matters of divorce, a woman requires a get — a document of release that only the husband can grant. If he refuses, she becomes an agunah — a "chained woman," unable to remarry, trapped in a marriage that's over in every way except the one that counts. Estimates suggest there are hundreds of agunot in the United States alone, and thousands worldwide.

In Christianity, the Catholic Church does not ordain women. Not because of a policy that could change, but as a matter of doctrine. Pope John Paul II declared in 1994 that the Church "has no authority whatsoever to confer priestly ordination on women" and that "this judgment is to be definitively held by all the Church's faithful." Definitively. The door is not open. It is not ajar. It is welded shut. A woman can be a saint. She can be the mother of God. She cannot hand out communion.

In evangelical Protestantism, complementarianism — the doctrine that men and women have different but "complementary" roles — assigns women to submission within marriage and bars them from church leadership. The Southern Baptist Convention, the largest Protestant denomination in the United States, formally adopted a resolution in 1998 stating that a wife should "submit herself graciously" to her husband's leadership. In 2023, the SBC voted to expel churches that had women pastors.

In Islam, the structural inequality is codified in scripture. Surah 4:34 of the Quran states that "men are in charge of women" and prescribes a three-step process for dealing with a wife from whom a husband fears disobedience: admonish her, forsake her in bed, and strike her. The Arabic word is idribuhunna. Scholars have debated its precise meaning for centuries — some say it means "strike lightly," some say it means "separate from." But the verse is there. In the text. In the perfect, unchanged word of God.

Women's testimony in Islamic courts is worth half that of a man's in financial matters, per Surah 2:282. A daughter inherits half what a son does, per Surah 4:11. In Saudi Arabia, women were not permitted to drive until 2018. In Iran, a woman who appears in public without a hijab can be arrested, imprisoned, and beaten — as the world saw in September 2022 when Mahsa Amini, a twenty-two-year-old woman, died in the custody of Iran's morality police after being detained for "improper hijab."

None of this is fringe. None of this is extreme interpretation. This is institutional policy. Defended by mainstream theologians. Practiced by mainstream communities. Right now.

*     *     *

I want to talk about children. Not the full education chapter — that comes later. Here I want to talk about one specific thing: what it does to a child's mind to be told, at an age when they cannot evaluate the claim, that if they think the wrong things, they will burn forever.

I spoke with a psychologist in London — I'll call her Dr. Harmon — who specializes in what she calls "religious trauma syndrome." It's not an official DSM diagnosis, but a growing body of clinical literature supports its recognition. She sees patients — adults, mostly — who are still dealing with the psychological effects of religious upbringing.

"The most common presentation is anxiety," she told me. "Specifically, existential anxiety related to hell. I have patients in their forties and fifties who intellectually know they don't believe in hell anymore, but who still wake up at night in a cold sweat, terrified they're going to burn. The fear was installed in them at age four, five, six — before their critical faculties developed. You can't just think your way out of that. It's in the nervous system."

Marlene Winell, the psychologist who coined the term "religious trauma syndrome," has documented cases of chronic anxiety, depression, difficulties with decision-making, and sexual dysfunction in adults raised in high-control religious environments. Her research describes a pattern: children raised with vivid hell-fear develop hypervigilance about their own thoughts, because the religion teaches that wrong thoughts are sinful, and sin leads to damnation. The child learns to police their own mind. To fear their own curiosity. To suppress doubt the way other children learn to suppress the urge to touch a hot stove.

Except the stove is real. And hell is not.

The cognitive damage is measurable. A 2012 study published in the journal Child Abuse & Neglect found that exposure to religious fear and shaming in childhood was associated with increased rates of anxiety disorders, depression, and PTSD symptoms in adulthood — even after controlling for other forms of childhood adversity.

Let me put this plainly. We are telling children — children who cannot reason about abstract claims, who cannot evaluate evidence, who trust adults the way flowers trust the sun — that an invisible being is watching their every thought, and that if they think the wrong thing, they will be tortured forever. And we call this moral education.

If a stranger on the street told a five-year-old that he would burn them alive if they didn't obey him, we would arrest that stranger. When a Sunday school teacher says the same thing but attributes it to God, we call it faith formation.

The mechanism is identical. The only difference is the costume.

LGBTQ people.

I am going to state some facts.

In 2023, the Williams Institute at UCLA estimated that over forty-six thousand LGBTQ youth in the United States had been subjected to conversion therapy — the practice of attempting to change a person's sexual orientation or gender identity through psychological or spiritual intervention. The practice has been condemned by the American Psychological Association, the American Medical Association, and every major mental health organization in the world. A 2020 study published in JAMA Pediatrics found that LGBTQ youth who had undergone conversion therapy were more than twice as likely to attempt suicide as those who had not.

Conversion therapy is overwhelmingly driven by religious conviction. The largest providers have historically been religious organizations. Exodus International, which operated for thirty-seven years as the world's largest conversion therapy network, was an explicitly Christian ministry. When it shut down in 2013, its president, Alan Chambers, publicly apologized for the harm it had caused. "I am sorry for the pain and hurt that many of you have experienced," he said. "I am sorry that some of you spent years working through the shame and guilt you felt when your orientation didn't change."

Years. Working through shame. Because their church told them God made them wrong.

In Uganda, the Anti-Homosexuality Act of 2023 imposes penalties up to life imprisonment for homosexual acts and the death penalty for "aggravated homosexuality." The law's development was directly influenced by American evangelical missionaries. In 2009, three American evangelicals — Scott Lively, Don Schmierer, and Caleb Lee Brundidge — held a conference in Kampala titled "Exposing the Homosexual Agenda." A month later, the first version of the bill was introduced. Scott Lively described homosexuality as more dangerous than terrorism.

American Christians exported their bigotry to a country that didn't have the institutional safeguards to resist it. And people died.

In territories controlled by the Islamic State, gay men were thrown from the roofs of buildings. This was filmed. Published. Celebrated. Crowds gathered below, not to catch the falling men, but to throw stones at any who survived the fall. ISIS justified this with reference to hadiths about the punishment of the people of Lot.

In Iran, homosexuality is punishable by death. In Saudi Arabia, it can be punished by flogging, imprisonment, or execution. In Brunei, a sharia penal code introduced in 2019 prescribes death by stoning for gay sex.

This is not extremism. This is law. Enacted by governments. Justified by scripture. Imposed on people whose only offense is existing.

A 2018 study published in the American Journal of Preventive Medicine analyzed data from over twenty-one thousand LGBTQ youth and found that family rejection based on sexual orientation or gender identity was one of the strongest predictors of suicide attempts. Religious belief was the most commonly cited reason for family rejection.

The church tells parents their child is an abomination. The parents reject their child. The child kills themselves.

That is a causal chain. And religion is the first link.

I need to talk about the Catholic abuse crisis. Not briefly. Not with the euphemisms that institutional PR has trained us to use. Not "inappropriate contact" or "boundary violations." What happened was this: Catholic priests raped children. Tens of thousands of children. Over decades. Across continents. And the institution — the Catholic Church — knew. And chose to protect the priests instead of the children.

In the United States, the John Jay Report found that more than four thousand Catholic priests had been accused of sexually abusing minors between 1950 and 2002. More than ten thousand children were identified as victims. The actual number is almost certainly higher — many victims never come forward.

The financial settlements alone exceed four billion dollars. Four billion. That's how much it cost to settle the cases where victims had enough evidence, enough courage, and enough access to the legal system to come forward.

In Germany, a 2018 study commissioned by the German Bishops' Conference found that at least 3,677 minors had been abused by Catholic clergy between 1946 and 2014. A subsequent investigation by a Munich law firm in 2022 implicated Pope Benedict XVI — then Joseph Ratzinger — in the mishandling of abuse cases during his tenure as Archbishop of Munich. He initially denied any involvement, then acknowledged he had attended a meeting where an abusive priest was discussed — a meeting he had previously said he did not attend.

In France, an independent commission reported in 2021 that an estimated 330,000 children had been victims of sexual abuse within the Catholic Church since 1950. Three hundred and thirty thousand. In one country.

In Australia, a Royal Commission found that 7 percent of Catholic priests who served between 1950 and 2010 had been accused of child sexual abuse. Seven percent. In some religious orders, the figure was higher than 20 percent.

In Ireland, the Ryan Report documented decades of endemic physical and sexual abuse in Catholic-run industrial schools and orphanages. Children were beaten, starved, raped, and forced into labor. The Church and the Irish state shared responsibility. The state had outsourced the care of vulnerable children to the Church — and the Church used those children.

I need you to understand something about what these numbers represent. These are not isolated incidents. This is not a case of a few predators infiltrating an otherwise healthy institution. This is a pattern. Global. Decades long. Spanning every continent where the Catholic Church operates.

And the pattern isn't just the abuse. The pattern is the cover-up.

The institutional response, documented in report after report, in country after country, was the same: move the priest. Send him to a new parish, where no one knows what he did, where he has access to a new group of children. Do not tell the police. Do not tell the new parish. Protect the institution. Silence the victims. Let the priest continue.

This happened in Boston. In Dublin. In Sydney. In Munich. In Santiago. In Manila. The same playbook. The same institutional logic. The same calculation: the reputation of the Church is more important than the safety of children.

Cardinal Bernard Law of Boston — documented in the Boston Globe's Spotlight investigation — personally reassigned priests he knew to be abusers. When the scandal broke, he was not defrocked. He was not arrested. He was transferred to Rome, where he was given a position at one of the city's most prestigious basilicas. He lived there until his death in 2017, on a Vatican salary, in a Vatican apartment.

The priest was protected. The children were not. And the institution that calls itself the moral authority on Earth made that choice deliberately, repeatedly, systematically, for decades.

I want to be clear about what I'm arguing and what I'm not.

I am not arguing that all religious people are abusers, or bigots, or misogynists. Most aren't. Most are people like David in the soup kitchen, like Sarah in the church pew, like Yusuf with his prayer mat — people trying to live decent lives according to the best framework they have.

But that's not the point.

The point is this: when you give an institution absolute moral authority — when you tell people that its leaders speak for God, that its rules are divine law, that questioning it is sin — you remove every mechanism of accountability that protects human beings from the abuse of power.

A government that abuses its citizens can be voted out. A corporation that harms its customers can be sued. A school that mistreats its students can be shut down.

But a church that claims to represent the creator of the universe? Who checks that? Who audits God's representatives? Who tells the Pope he's wrong when the Pope's authority is defined as coming directly from Christ?

Nobody. That's the answer. Nobody does. Because the system is designed so that nobody can.

The horror of the Catholic abuse crisis is not that bad people became priests. Bad people are everywhere. The horror is that the institution — the structure, the hierarchy, the theology — was built in a way that made the abuse possible, made the cover-up logical, and made accountability impossible. The system worked exactly as designed. It protected itself.

And this is not unique to Catholicism. Every religious institution that claims divine authority, that insulates its leaders from external oversight, that defines criticism as blasphemy or sin, has the same structural vulnerability. The mechanism is identical across traditions. Only the details differ.

When you cannot question the authority, the authority will eventually abuse you. Not because religious leaders are worse than other leaders. Because all leaders who are freed from accountability eventually abuse their power. That's not a theory about religion. That's a law of human nature.

Religion doesn't create bad people. It creates unaccountable institutions. And unaccountable institutions create suffering at scale.

I finished this research on a Saturday afternoon. I closed my laptop and sat in silence for a long time.

I thought about David and his soup kitchen. I thought about Margaret and her hospital visits. I thought about Sarah and her dead son and the belief that kept her alive.

And then I thought about the women in the Magdalene laundries. The children in the Catholic schools. The gay men falling from rooftops. The women in Iran, beaten for showing their hair. The children lying in bed at night, terrified that God will burn them forever for thinking the wrong thought.

Both of these are religion. Both of them. At the same time.

The beautiful answer and the horrifying answer are not two different things. They are two sides of the same mechanism. The same authority that builds the soup kitchen also protects the abuser. The same community that catches you when you fall also shuns you when you doubt. The same comfort that helps the widow also terrorizes the child. The same institution that says "you are loved" also says "obey or burn."

This is not what bad people do while pretending to be religious. This is what institutions do when they are given absolute moral authority, freedom from external accountability, and the ability to define the rules of reality.

The horror is not accidental. It is structural. It is built into the design. And no amount of soup can wash it out.
