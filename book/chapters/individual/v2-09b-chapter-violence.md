## Chapter: The Violence

There is a photograph I keep returning to, though I didn't take it and wasn't there when it was taken.

It shows a checkpoint in the West Bank. The year is unclear — it could be any year from the 1990s to the present, because this particular checkpoint has not changed much in thirty years. A young Israeli soldier, probably nineteen or twenty, stands with his rifle. Across from him, on the other side of the concrete barrier, a Palestinian man is waiting. The Palestinian man is older — perhaps sixty. He is holding papers. His hands are raised slightly, not in surrender exactly, but in that particular posture of people who have learned that sudden movements are dangerous. The soldier is looking at the papers. His face is neutral.

Nothing is happening. There is no violence in this photograph.

And yet, if you know what you are looking at, you can feel the force field of theology running through it. The soldier did not wake up that morning believing he was doing something secular. He is Israeli — some of those soldiers, not all, but some, believe they are standing on land covenanted by God to their people. The Palestinian man has his own theological frame: this is his land, occupied by people who arrived with a claim from a book. The checkpoint exists because of a conflict that is political and historical and economic and territorial, yes — but the foundation stone, the thing underneath all of it, is religious. God gave this land. God promised it. The disagreement, at its root, is about what God said.

The violence in this photograph is invisible. That's the kind I want to start with.

*     *     *

I should say what this chapter is not.

It is not an argument that religion causes violence. That case is simpler than the truth warrants. Human beings were killing each other enthusiastically long before any of the three Abrahamic faiths existed, and the most lethal ideologies of the twentieth century — Nazism, Stalinism, Mao's Cultural Revolution — were explicitly secular or anti-religious. The Soviet Union liquidated priests. The Khmer Rouge abolished religion entirely. Between them, these secular ideologies killed somewhere in the vicinity of a hundred million people in roughly a century. Religion does not have a monopoly on organised murder.

What religion does have, in some configurations, is a particular kind of fuel. Not the only fuel. Not even the hottest fuel. But a specific kind — one that secular ideologies have often tried to imitate, because it works. It provides cosmic permission. It promises that the violence is not just permitted but ordained. It locates the enemy outside the category of people whose lives fully matter. And it forecloses doubt, because the command comes not from a flawed human leader who might be wrong, but from the creator of the universe, who cannot be.

That is the mechanism I want to understand. How does theological certainty — the conviction that you have the one true God and the one true path — become the permission slip for killing? Not always. Not inevitably. But often enough that the pattern demands examination.

*     *     *

Begin with what the texts actually say. Because one of the dishonest moves in these conversations is to skip past the theology — to treat the violence as a perversion of an essentially peaceful tradition, or to treat the tradition as nothing but its violent passages. Neither is honest.

Judaism does not have a well-developed theology of holy war in the modern sense — the tradition has rarely had state power for the past two thousand years, and much of its legal code assumes a powerless diaspora community. But the Hebrew Bible is not squeamish. The book of Deuteronomy commands the complete destruction of the Canaanite peoples: "You shall utterly destroy them; you shall make no covenant with them and show no mercy to them." The book of Joshua records a campaign of conquest in which entire populations are put to the sword. The word used is *herem* — a kind of sacred annihilation, the dedication of enemy lives to God through their destruction. This is not metaphor. It is presented as divine command.

Jewish tradition has spent two thousand years wrestling with these passages. The dominant conclusion, reached through extensive rabbinic commentary, is that the nations in question no longer exist, the commands were specific to a particular historical moment, and the general ethical thrust of Jewish law points in a different direction. The argument is serious. It is not just apologetics. But the passages are there. They were there when Baruch Goldstein woke up on the morning of February 25, 1994, and drove to the Cave of the Patriarchs in Hebron — a site sacred to both Jews and Muslims because tradition holds that Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob are buried there. Goldstein, a doctor and a settler, walked into the mosque section of the building while the worshippers were at prayer during Ramadan. He was carrying an assault rifle. He killed twenty-nine people and wounded more than a hundred before he was beaten to death. He had worn his Israeli army uniform. He had come prepared.

Some religious nationalists in Israel celebrated him. His grave in Kiryat Arba, a Jewish settlement near Hebron, became a pilgrimage site. A plaque was erected. It read, in part, that he had "given his life for the Jewish people, the Torah, and the Land of Israel." The plaque was eventually ordered removed by the Israeli government, but the worship continued. People still come.

The case for Goldstein, as it was articulated by those who saw him as a hero, was theological. The Palestinians were Amalek — the ancient enemy that God had commanded Israel to destroy. The presence of Muslims in the land of Israel was an affront to the covenant. He was not a murderer. He was a soldier of God.

This is the steel-manned version: the theology, taken seriously, on its own terms. And it is chilling precisely because it is coherent. If you believe, without qualification, that God gave this land to this people, and that the enemies of Israel are literally the enemies of God, and that the texts of the Torah are not historical documents but living commands — then Goldstein is not a madman. He is a man who took the logic all the way to its end.

Most Jews, of course, do not take it to that end. The overwhelming majority of Israeli and diaspora Jews reacted to Goldstein with horror. Rabin himself called it a loathsome criminal act. The tradition has enormous internal resources for pulling back from this edge — the same Torah that contains *herem* also contains "do not murder," also contains the Talmudic principle that whoever saves a single life, it is as if they have saved an entire world. The tradition is not a monolith.

But less than two years after the Hebron massacre, Yigal Amir shot Yitzhak Rabin twice in the back in a public square in Tel Aviv. Rabin, who had signed the Oslo Accords, who was pursuing the path toward a Palestinian state, who was the Prime Minister of Israel — Amir killed him because a rabbinical ruling had been issued declaring that Rabin was a *rodef*, a pursuer, someone whose actions endangered Jewish lives. Under certain interpretations of Jewish law, killing a *rodef* is not murder. It is permitted. Some rabbis — a minority, but rabbis — had issued this ruling.

Two events. Two killers, both motivated by religious legal reasoning, both in the same decade. This is not fringe noise. This is theology with consequences.

*     *     *

Islam's relationship with violence is the most discussed and least honestly examined of the three traditions. Discussed because contemporary jihadism has forced the conversation into public life in the most brutal way imaginable. Dishonestly examined because both sides of the usual debate are wrong.

The apologist position — that Islam is a religion of peace and jihadist violence is a complete distortion with no grounding in the texts — is simply not supportable. The Quran contains passages that authorise and even command violent struggle against non-believers. Surah 9:5, sometimes called the "verse of the sword," reads: "When the sacred months have passed, kill the polytheists wherever you find them, and capture them and besiege them and sit in wait for them at every place of ambush." Surah 9:29: "Fight those who do not believe in Allah or in the Last Day and who do not consider unlawful what Allah and His messenger have declared unlawful, and who do not adopt the religion of truth from those who were given the Scripture — fight until they give the *jizya* willingly while they are humbled." These verses exist. They were not added by extremists. They have been in the text for fourteen centuries.

Classical Islamic jurisprudence developed a framework around them: the distinction between *dar al-Islam* (the house of Islam, territory under Muslim rule) and *dar al-harb* (the house of war, territory not yet under Muslim rule). The implication was that the natural state of relations between Muslim and non-Muslim polities was one of war — not necessarily active war, but a structural enmity that could be suspended through truces but not permanently resolved until the house of war was absorbed. This was not radical fringe theology. It was the working legal framework of the great Islamic empires and is the subject of serious academic scholarship.

The counter-position — that Islam uniquely mandates violence and that all Muslims are therefore potentially violent — is worse than dishonest. It is empirically false. The world's 1.9 billion Muslims are, in the overwhelming majority, peaceful, ordinary people who interpret their faith in entirely non-violent terms. The tradition has enormous textual and intellectual resources for peace — the same Quran that contains the verse of the sword also contains "there is no compulsion in religion" (2:256) and dozens of injunctions toward mercy, forgiveness, and peaceful coexistence. The tradition of Islamic scholarship has spent centuries developing contextual readings that limit the violent passages to specific historical circumstances. Many Muslim-majority societies live in long-term peace with their neighbours. The equation of Islam with violence is not analysis. It is bigotry dressed as concern.

But between those two dishonest positions is a real question: does something in the structure of classical Islamic theology make it more susceptible to being weaponised for violence than the other Abrahamic traditions? And the honest answer is: in certain configurations, possibly.

The early Islamic conquests — from the death of Muhammad in 632 CE to the expansion that within a century stretched from Spain to Central Asia — were explicitly framed as religious enterprise. This is not a retrospective Western interpretation. The Arabic sources describe it that way themselves. The expansion was *jihad*. Populations were offered three choices: conversion, subjugation under *dhimmi* status (a protected but subordinate category for Jews and Christians), or death. This was not unique cruelty by the standards of seventh-century warfare. But it was conquest in the name of God, and the theological framework that made it possible — the division of the world into the house of Islam and the house of war, the belief that the expansion of God's rule on earth is a religious obligation — did not disappear when the conquests ended. It was systematised, codified, preserved.

It re-emerged. Osama bin Laden's fatwas — and they are fatwas, formal religious legal opinions, not just political manifestos — are shot through with specific Quranic citations and classical jurisprudential reasoning. When he declared in 1998 that "the ruling to kill the Americans and their allies — civilians and military — is an individual duty for every Muslim who can do it," he was not making up the framework. He was drawing on a real tradition, however selectively and however perverted his application of it. The nineteen men who carried out the attacks on September 11, 2001, were not confused about their theology. They had spent months preparing, studying, praying. They believed they were doing God's work. They were wrong about that — most Muslim scholars said so immediately and clearly. But the belief was theological, not political, in its deepest structure.

At its peak, the Islamic State controlled territory roughly the size of Jordan — some 88,000 square kilometres of Syria and Iraq. Its recruitment materials were not primarily nationalist or ethnic — they were theological. Join the caliphate. Live under God's law. The final battle is coming. Martyrdom awaits. Thousands of young Muslims from Europe, from North America, from Asia, left comfortable lives for the Syrian desert because they had been convinced that this was what God required of them. Not all of them were mentally ill. Not all of them were desperate or impoverished. Some of them were educated, successful, by ordinary measures fine. The theology was the thing. The theology was enough.

*     *     *

Christianity is the tradition that has held state power the longest, over the largest territories, and the violence it has committed in the name of God is consequently the most documented and in some ways the most spectacular.

In 1095, Pope Urban II stood at Clermont and delivered a speech. The full text has been lost — we have several versions, written from memory afterward — but the gist is preserved. He told the assembled crowd that the Holy Land was in the hands of an accursed race, that the time had come to take it back, that those who died in the attempt would be granted remission of their sins, and that the path to Jerusalem was the path to heaven. The crowd, by multiple accounts, responded with a single cry: "Deus vult." God wills it.

Four years later, in July 1099, the Crusaders took Jerusalem. The massacre that followed has been described in detail by chroniclers on both sides. Raymond d'Aguilers, a priest who was present, wrote with what appears to be satisfaction: "In the temple and porch of Solomon, men rode in blood up to their knees and bridle reins." Modern historians treat this as considerable exaggeration — the physical circumstances don't support blood running ankle-deep through streets. But the massacre was real. The Muslim and Jewish populations of the city were killed in large numbers. Jews who had taken refuge in a synagogue were burned inside it. The man who ordered the assault prayed afterward.

This is one of those moments I find it necessary to sit with rather than summarise. What happens to a man — to thousands of men — when they become convinced that God wants this? That the killing is not just permitted but holy? Raymond d'Aguilers was a priest. He celebrated Mass. He probably prayed for his sins. He also, apparently, felt no contradiction between his faith and his satisfaction at the bloodshed, because in his theological framework there was none. The enemies were not fully human in the relevant sense. They were the enemies of God. Their deaths were an offering.

The Spanish Inquisition, established by Ferdinand and Isabella in 1478, is routinely invoked as the symbol of religious violence, and routinely misrepresented. The popular image — millions tortured and burned — is not the historical record. Serious historians estimate the number of executions over the Inquisition's history at somewhere between three thousand and five thousand. This figure matters, not to minimise it — three thousand people burned at the stake is not a minor footnote — but because the inflation of the number does the opposite of what people intend. It makes the real Inquisition seem like a medieval fantasy rather than a documented historical institution. The real Inquisition is disturbing enough.

What makes it theologically interesting is the logic of mercy. The burning was not presented as punishment. It was presented as salvation. The Inquisitors believed — genuinely, in most cases — that the soul of the heretic was in danger of eternal damnation unless the heresy was rooted out. Torture was permitted to extract confession. Confession led to reconciliation. Reconciliation saved the soul. If the heretic refused to recant, or relapsed, death was the outcome — but the death of the body was a lesser harm than the death of the soul. This is not just rationalisation. It is a coherent position within a theological framework that treats eternal suffering as the worst possible outcome. Once you accept that framework, a great deal follows from it. The Inquisitors were not irrational. They were rational within their premises. The premises are the problem.

What do Christian apologists say? Several things, and not all of them are wrong. They say — correctly — that the teachings of Jesus himself contain nothing that authorises this. The Sermon on the Mount is not a manifesto for conquest. "Love your enemies" does not mean "kill your enemies." There is a genuine discontinuity between the teaching and the historical record, and theologians have spent centuries trying to account for it. They say — also correctly — that Just War theory, developed by Augustine in the fourth century and refined by Aquinas in the thirteenth, was intended as a constraint on violence, not an expansion of it. Just War doctrine requires that war be waged for a just cause, declared by legitimate authority, fought with right intention, undertaken as a last resort, and conducted with proportionate force. By those criteria, most of the Crusades were unjust wars. Most Christian theologians today would agree with that. The tradition has the internal tools to critique itself.

What the apologists sometimes struggle to account for is why the tradition kept producing this violence if the teaching is so clearly against it. The honest answer — the uncomfortable one — is that the institutional church, for most of its history, was also a political power structure, and political power structures do what political power structures do. They expand. They eliminate rivals. They protect their own. The theology was available as justification when needed and set aside when inconvenient. This is not unique to Christianity. It is true of every tradition that has ever acquired state power. But it happened, and it happened in the name of the God of love, and that is a fact that cannot be explained away.

*     *     *

I want to come back to the mechanism, because I don't think the violence is explained simply by bad people who misused good texts.

There is something in the structure of monotheism itself — one God, one truth, one correct path — that tends toward a particular kind of problem. Not violence necessarily. But exclusivity. The others are wrong. And once the others are wrong in a cosmic sense, once their wrongness is not a matter of taste or tradition but a matter of eternal truth — things can follow from that.

Polytheistic systems, by and large, were theologically accommodating. The Roman Empire was remarkably tolerant of religious diversity because Roman theology allowed for local gods. If you conquered a territory, you could add their gods to the pantheon. The gods were local, provincial, manageable. You didn't need to kill anyone for believing in the wrong one, because there was no wrong one — the divine was distributed, plural, negotiable. The Jews, and then the Christians, and then the Muslims, introduced something different: a God who was not local but universal, not one among many but the only one, and whose commands were therefore not negotiable.

This is, in one sense, a profound moral achievement. Universalism is a precondition for universal human rights. If all people are made in the image of the same God, you have the foundation for a moral framework that applies to everyone. The most powerful arguments against slavery in the eighteenth century were made on Christian grounds. The civil rights movement was, at its core, a religious movement. The universalism of the Abrahamic traditions has done enormous good.

But universalism has another edge. If the truth is universal, then the people who reject it are rejecting something they have no right to reject. If God has given a clear command, then to fail to follow it is not just a personal choice but a cosmic error with eternal consequences. And if you have the power to prevent that error — to save someone from eternal damnation, to purify the land, to establish God's kingdom — does that power become an obligation?

The Inquisitors thought so. The Crusaders thought so. The jihadists think so. The religious settler shooting at Palestinian farmers thinks so. The theological structure is the same across traditions. The application is different. The logic is the same.

This is not an argument that monotheism leads inevitably to violence. Most monotheists, throughout most of history, have led peaceful lives and practised their faith without hurting anyone. The disposition of the tradition depends enormously on historical circumstance, political power, economic context, leadership, and a hundred other variables. But the mechanism is there. The fuel is there. When the wrong circumstances combine with the theological permission structure, the result tends to be recognisable across centuries and traditions.

Secular ideologies have tried to replicate this. Nationalism, communism, fascism — all of them have a claim to ultimate truth, a division between in-group and out-group, a cosmic mandate, a willingness to sacrifice the individual for the total. The killing they produced vastly exceeded anything done in God's name. But there is something that religion provides that secular ideologies have always struggled to fully imitate: the promise that the dead approve. When the Crusader died at Antioch, his death was not just strategic sacrifice — it was guaranteed paradise. His comrades were not grieving a waste. They were celebrating a promotion. The willingness to die for a cause, and the willingness to kill for a cause, are both considerably easier when death is not the end but the beginning.

*     *     *

Is any of this reformable? Has it been reformed?

Yes, actually. This is worth saying clearly, because the narrative of unreformed religious violence is as dishonest as the narrative of religious peace.

Catholic teaching on Just War has developed. Vatican II, in the 1960s, made explicit statements against religious coercion that would have been inconceivable to the Inquisitors. The Catholic Church's record on violence is still genuinely terrible in many respects — but the Church of 2025 is not the Church of 1099, and the difference is not just circumstantial. Theological development has happened. Protestant traditions, across most of their spectrum, have moved to a robust theology of pluralism and religious freedom that was not available to Luther or Calvin. The Troubles ended. The ecumenical movement exists.

In Islam, the debate is active, serious, and — from the outside — looks genuinely unresolved. Scholars like Abdullahi Ahmed An-Na'im have argued for decades that classical Islamic jurisprudence, including its approach to non-Muslims and *dar al-harb*, needs to be fundamentally rethought in light of modern concepts of human rights and international law. These arguments have influence. They have also faced violent resistance. The jihadist movements are not in retreat in any simple sense — the ideology persists, finds new adherents, adapts. What is clear is that the vast majority of Muslim scholars, Muslim-majority governments, and ordinary Muslims have consistently rejected jihadist violence, including in the immediate aftermath of 9/11 and every major attack since. Whether that rejection has the structural depth to fully defang the theological framework that enables the violence is a question I cannot answer, and I don't think anyone honestly can.

In Judaism, the situation with religious nationalism is not improving. The settler movement has grown. Religious parties in Israel have moved closer to the mainstream and in some cases into government. The theology that Goldstein and Amir acted on is not confined to a fringe. The assassination of Rabin, specifically, represented something alarming: a case where religious legal reasoning was used to authorise the killing of a legitimate elected leader pursuing a democratic peace process, and some rabbis provided that reasoning. That is not ancient history. That is within living memory, and the conditions that produced it have not substantially changed.

*     *     *

There is a man I met once, briefly, at a conference in London on counter-extremism. He was in his early thirties, well-spoken, wearing a suit that looked recently bought — the kind of careful presentation of someone who is conscious of being watched and assessed. He was a former member of a Salafi network in the UK — not active, not violent, but on the edge of the kind of community that produces people who travel to conflict zones. He had left. He was working now with the organisation hosting the conference, doing counter-extremism education in schools.

After his panel, I asked if he'd talk to me. We sat in a corridor with bad coffee, and I asked him what the pull had been. What the theology offered that nothing else did.

He didn't start with theology. He started with loneliness. He'd grown up in east London, second-generation Pakistani, state school, no particular trouble but no particular direction either. His parents worked long hours. His older brother had gone to university and moved away. He described his late teens as a period of "just floating" — going to college without much purpose, spending evenings online, feeling invisible in the way that young men in large cities often feel invisible. Not desperate. Not radicalised by poverty or trauma. Just unmoored.

The network found him through a study circle at a local mosque — not the main congregation, but a smaller group that met on weekday evenings. "They were the first people who acted like I mattered," he said. "Like I had something to contribute. They remembered my name. They asked me where I'd been if I missed a week." The theology came later, almost incidentally. First came the belonging. The brotherhood. The sense of being part of something structured and purposeful in a life that had neither structure nor purpose. "It was like joining a family that had rules," he said. "And I wanted rules. I didn't know that about myself until someone offered them."

The radicalisation was gradual. The study materials became more specific. The geopolitical framing hardened — the West was waging war on Islam, the ummah was under attack, the obligation to respond was religious, not political. He never crossed into operational activity. He never travelled. But he was in rooms where people discussed it as a real possibility, and he did not leave those rooms. "I didn't agree with everything," he said. "But I didn't disagree out loud. Because disagreeing meant losing the only community I had."

I asked him what changed. What made him begin to doubt.

He was quiet for a long time. "Someone I knew went to Syria," he said. "And I saw what happened to his family. His mother. His younger sister. They came to the mosque looking for answers and nobody could look at them. And I thought — this is what we're producing. This is what the study circles lead to. Not for everyone. But for enough people." He described the next year as a slow, painful process of disentangling — not from belief, he said, but from belonging. "Leaving the theology was easy, actually. You read other scholars, you see the holes. Leaving the people was the hard part. I lost every friend I had. Every single one. I'd go to the mosque and they'd turn away. It was like I'd died."

He rebuilt his life from almost nothing, he said. New contacts through the counter-extremism organisation. A degree completed late. Work that felt meaningful precisely because he knew what he was working against.

He thought about my original question for a while. "It made everything make sense," he said. "There's an enemy and there's a cause and there's a God who sees all of it and who's on your side. You're not just angry and confused in your bedroom. You're a soldier. You have a mission."

He paused. "The anger was real," he said. "The cause felt real. The theology just... organised it. Gave it a direction."

Organised it. I keep returning to that word.

The violence that theology enables is rarely violence from nowhere. It draws on real grievances, real humiliations, real historical injustices. The Crusaders were not invented from nothing — there was a genuine geopolitical crisis in the Christian kingdoms of the East, genuine Muslim expansion into previously Christian territory, genuine calls for help from Byzantine emperors. The Palestinian farmer at the checkpoint has a real grievance. The young Muslim man in a European suburb who feels invisible and demeaned has a real experience. The settler who believes that God promised his people this land is working, however distortedly, with a real text and a real history of persecution.

What theology does — in the dangerous cases — is take that real material and give it infinite stakes. Your grievance is not just your grievance. It is God's grievance. Your enemy is not just your enemy. They are God's enemy. Your willingness to die is not just sacrifice. It is salvation.

Once you have that architecture in place, almost anything is possible.

*     *     *

I come back to the photograph. The checkpoint. The soldier. The older man with his papers.

Neither of them is a monster. I have to believe that, and I do believe it, and I hold it at the same time as knowing what the photograph represents. The violence in it is structural, theological, historical — it does not live in either of their bodies. It lives in the four thousand years of competing claims behind them, in the texts that both communities carry, in the God that both communities, in their different ways, invoke.

The soldier will go home tonight. The older man will wait, and eventually cross, or not cross. The checkpoint will remain. And somewhere in the architecture of the beliefs that built it — the covenant, the promise, the chosen people, the holy land, the God who cares about this particular patch of ground more than anywhere else on earth — the violence is latent, waiting for the next person who is angry enough and certain enough to draw the logic all the way out.

I don't know how you defuse that. I don't think anyone does, not fully. The reform movements exist. The moderates are real. The tradition, in all three cases, has resources for peace that are as genuine as its resources for war. But the mechanism exists. It has been there from the beginning. And the question of whether the good theology can permanently outrun the dangerous theology — whether the love-your-enemies tradition can permanently contain the kill-the-polytheists tradition, whether the mercy of God can be kept permanently louder than his commands — is a question that the next century will answer, as the last hundred centuries have answered it, in blood and fire and people waiting at checkpoints with their papers raised.

I find I cannot end this chapter with anything that resolves. The evidence doesn't resolve. The history doesn't resolve. Three traditions, thousands of years, a mechanism that persists. The best I can say — the most honest thing I can say — is that the violence is not inevitable, and not universal, and not the only thing these traditions contain.

But it is in there.

It has always been in there.

And pretending otherwise is not honesty. It is its own kind of faith.
