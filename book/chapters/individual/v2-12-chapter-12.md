# REPLACING

Chapter 12: What We Lose

My friend Paul died on a Wednesday in November. Bowel cancer. He was forty-one.

I went to the funeral. It was at a crematorium outside Coventry, a low brick building next to a retail park. There was a Tesco Extra visible through the window of the waiting room. Inside, the chapel was clean and neutral and smelled of nothing. The seats were upholstered in something wipeable.

The registrar welcomed us. She mispronounced his surname. She read a short biography that his wife had written, competently, without feeling, the way you'd read a cover letter for someone else's job application. Someone played a Coldplay song through a speaker mounted on the wall. Paul's brother stood up and read a Mary Oliver poem he'd found that morning on his phone. He cried through most of it. Nobody knew what to do. There was a curtain. The coffin slid behind it. The curtain closed. It took nineteen minutes.

Afterward, in the car park, Paul's mother stood with her coat pulled tight and said, to no one in particular: "Is that it, then?"

Nobody answered her. We didn't have an answer. We'd given her the truth -- no God, no heaven, no reunion -- and the truth had given her nineteen minutes in a room that smelled of disinfectant and a curtain that closed on her son.

I drove home and sat in my car for a long time and thought: I have nothing. If this were me in that box, the people I love would have nothing. Not nothing to believe. Nothing to *do*. No script. No container. No shape for the grief to move through. Just a car park and a Tesco and the question: is that it, then?

That was the day this chapter started writing itself. Not as an argument. As a feeling. The feeling of having pulled something out and not having anything to put in the hole.

* * *

I need to say this clearly, because I've spent eleven chapters not saying it.

Walking away from religion cost me something. Personally. Not theoretically. Not as an abstract philosophical position. It cost me something I can feel, most acutely at the moments when feeling is all there is.

I don't pray. I've never prayed, not really, not since I was old enough to understand what I was doing. But there have been nights -- more than I'd like to admit -- when I wanted to. Three in the morning. The kind of darkness that isn't just the absence of light but feels like the presence of something. Anxiety about someone I love. Fear about my own body. The ordinary terrors that visit everyone, believer and unbeliever alike, in the small hours when the rational mind has clocked off.

The believers I know have something to do with that fear. They pray. They hand it over. They address it to someone. The prayer may go nowhere. The someone may not exist. But the act of it -- the formalized surrender, the ancient words, the posture of kneeling or clasping hands -- gives the fear a direction. It moves it from inside to outside. From the self to the Other.

I have nothing to do with the fear. I lie there. I breathe. I wait for it to pass. Sometimes I get up and make tea and read something on my phone until the sun comes up. That's my three-in-the-morning ritual. A kettle and a screen. It is not enough.

I'm not saying this to argue that God exists. I'm saying it because if I don't admit this -- if I write the chapter about what we lose as if the losses are all other people's losses, felt at a distance, observed with intellectual sympathy -- then I'm lying. And this book is supposed to be honest.

So: I feel it. The absence. Not of God. Of the infrastructure that belief provides. The thing you can do when there is nothing to be done.

* * *

My grandmother was Catholic. Properly Catholic. Mass every Sunday, rosary beads in her handbag, a small framed picture of the Sacred Heart above her bed that had been there so long the wallpaper around it had faded to a different shade.

When she was dying -- slowly, over weeks, in a hospital bed that had been set up in what used to be the dining room -- a priest came. Father Brennan. He'd known her for thirty years. He came in and he didn't bustle or perform. He sat on the edge of the bed and held her hand and spoke quietly. He anointed her forehead with oil. He said words that had been said over the dying for a thousand years. He told her she was going home.

She was barely conscious by then. But her face changed. I watched it change. Something that had been tight around her eyes and mouth softened. Her breathing slowed. She died six hours later.

Was any of it true? The home she was going to, the God who was waiting? I don't think so. I didn't think so then and I don't think so now.

But the ritual was true. The accumulated weight of it was real. A thousand years of people dying with those same words in their ears, and she was one of them, and it meant she wasn't doing this unprecedented thing alone. She wasn't the first person to die. She wasn't falling into nothing. The thing she was walking into had a name and a shape and a direction, and a man who had rehearsed this with hundreds of others was holding her hand and telling her the route.

I stood in the corner of that room and I had nothing. No words. No ritual. No role. The believers had something to do -- they prayed, they crossed themselves, they participated in a script that had been written for exactly this moment. I stood there with my hands at my sides and watched.

I'm still standing there, in a sense. I'm still the one in the corner with nothing to do.

* * *

A hospice nurse in Birmingham told me something I haven't been able to forget. Her name was Claire. She'd been doing palliative care for seventeen years -- long enough, she said, to have seen the pattern clearly.

"The religious patients are easier," she said. "Not because they're less scared. They're just as scared. But they have a script. They know what to do with their hands. They know what to say. They know what happens next -- or they believe they know, which at that point is the same thing."

She paused.

"The secular patients -- the ones with no faith and no ritual -- they look at me and they want me to tell them what this is. What's happening. What it means. And I don't have an answer. I'm a nurse. I can manage pain. I can't manage meaninglessness."

I asked her if she was religious herself.

"I was," she said. "I'm not sure what I am now. Seventeen years of watching people die does something to your theology. But I'll tell you this -- I've never once been with a dying person and thought, *I'm glad they don't have God.* Not once. Whatever it is, however wrong the cosmology is, it works. It works in the room. At the end. When nothing else does."

In Judaism, when someone dies, the community sits shiva. For seven days, the mourners don't leave the house. People come to them. They bring food. They sit. They don't try to fix anything or explain anything. They just show up and sit. The mirrors are covered. The mourners sit on low chairs. The entire physical environment says: something has been torn. We will sit with you in the tearing.

In Islam, the body is washed by family members. Gently, ritually, with specific prayers for each part of the body. Then it's wrapped in white cloth -- the same cloth for everyone, rich or poor. No embalming. No cosmetics. No pretending. The funeral happens quickly, within twenty-four hours if possible. The body is placed in the ground facing Mecca. The community prays.

These are not decorations. They are technologies. Grief technologies, refined over centuries, designed to do something specific: to give the living a way through. To say, with the body and not just the mind: this has happened before. Others have survived it. Here is how.

We are dismantling those technologies and replacing them with a pamphlet and a twenty-minute crematorium slot.

* * *

But death is not the only loss, and it is not the one that haunts me most.

The loss that haunts me most happens on a Wednesday afternoon, in a church hall that smells of instant coffee and digestive biscuits, and nobody notices it happening at all.

I visited a church in Sunderland last year. Not for a service. On a Wednesday. The vicar -- a tired woman in her fifties with a fleece over her clerical collar -- was running what she called the Wednesday Club. It met at two o'clock. Fifteen to twenty people, mostly women, mostly over seventy. They drank tea from mismatched mugs. They ate biscuits from a tin. They talked.

That's it. They talked.

I sat with a woman named Margaret. She was seventy-eight. Her husband had died three years earlier. Her son lived in Dubai. She had a cat and a television and a next-door neighbour who waved but never came in.

"This is my week," she said. "Wednesday. I get up on Wednesday morning and I think, today I'll see people. The rest of the week I manage. But Wednesday is the day I have."

She said it without self-pity. She said it as a fact. The way you'd say the shops close at five.

I asked the vicar what would happen if the church closed. The diocese had been making noises. Attendance at Sunday services was down to about thirty, most of them the same people who came on Wednesday.

"The Sunday congregation would find another church," she said. "They'd drive to the next parish. They're committed. But the Wednesday lot -- they can't drive. Some of them can barely walk to the bus stop. They'd just... stop coming out."

She looked at the room -- the stained carpet, the strip lighting, the urn that took twenty minutes to boil.

"This room is the only reason some of these women leave the house. I know how that sounds. I know it's not glamorous. Nobody's going to write about it. But if this goes, Margaret goes home and she watches television until she dies. That's what happens. I'm not being dramatic. That's what happens."

The church in Sunderland hasn't closed yet. But four thousand others in England have, in the last two decades. And when a church closes, the building becomes flats or a coffee shop or a ruin. The Sunday congregation disperses. But the Wednesday Club doesn't relocate. The food bank doesn't find a new venue. The bereavement support group doesn't set up somewhere else. They simply end.

Nobody holds a vigil for the Wednesday Club.

But someone should. Because the Wednesday Club was the entire social infrastructure for people whom the secular state has not built an alternative for. The state built an app. The app requires a smartphone, an internet connection, and the ability to navigate a user interface designed by twenty-six-year-olds in Shoreditch. Margaret doesn't have a smartphone. She had the church.

Now she has her television.

This is what I mean when I say the losses are real. Not the loss of stained glass and organ music, though those matter too. The loss of Margaret's Wednesday. The loss of the only thing standing between a seventy-eight-year-old widow and complete isolation. Repeated in every town, in every parish, across a country that decided it had outgrown the institution without checking whether it had built a replacement.

It hadn't.

* * *

A Jewish friend of mine -- secular, atheist, keeps the holidays anyway -- said something to me over dinner that I wrote on a napkin and still have.

"Without them," he said, meaning the festivals, "one Tuesday is the same as the next. The year has no architecture."

Architecture. That's the word.

Religion gives the year architecture. It tells you: this week is different. This day matters. Stop what you're doing and pay attention to something larger than your inbox.

If you're Muslim, Ramadan transforms daily life for a month -- the fasting, the iftar meals at sunset, the communal discipline, the celebration of Eid. If you're Jewish, the calendar *is* the religion -- Shabbat every week, then the great arc from Rosh Hashanah through Yom Kippur, the building and dismantling of the sukkah, the candles of Hanukkah in the dark of winter, the noise and joy of Purim, the seder at Pesach. Each one carrying its own foods, its own songs, its own obligations, its own memories.

Secular culture has bank holidays. We have New Year's Eve, which is mostly about drinking. We have birthdays, which are about the self. We have Valentine's Day, which is about commerce pretending to be love.

None of these carry moral weight. None of them ask you to reflect. None of them connect you to anything beyond your own appetites.

You can feel this if you've lived a completely secular life with no festivals, no markers, no rhythm. I have lived that life. The months blur. The year is flat. You manufacture your own meaning on a weekly basis, and mostly you don't -- you scroll through another Sunday that feels identical to the last one and will feel identical to the next.

* * *

And then the art.

I stood in Chartres Cathedral once, on a Tuesday afternoon, when it was nearly empty. The light came through the twelfth-century windows -- that blue, that impossible blue they've never been able to reproduce -- and hit the stone floor, and I felt something.

Not God. I don't think it was God.

But something. A sense of standing in the presence of something larger than myself. Something that wasn't about me, or my problems, or my opinions. Something that made me feel small in a way that was, paradoxically, a relief. As if the smallness itself was the gift.

I wanted to stay. I sat in a pew and I wanted to stay there, in that light, in that silence, in that building that had been designed over centuries to produce exactly this feeling in exactly this kind of person -- the doubter, the visitor, the one who doesn't belong but can't quite leave.

And then I remembered. I don't believe any of it. The story in the windows. The God in the stone. The meaning the building was built to house. I'm a tourist in someone else's transcendence. I can feel the feeling but I can't keep it, because keeping it would require believing what produced it, and I can't do that. So I stood up and walked out into the square and the feeling drained out of me like water out of a cracked cup, and I was just a man on a Tuesday afternoon in France with nowhere in particular to go.

Religion built Chartres. It commissioned the Sistine Chapel. It produced Bach's Mass in B Minor -- twenty-five years of a man's life poured into music so structurally perfect that listening to it feels like being dismantled and reassembled. It produced the Psalms -- "Out of the depths I cry to you" -- three thousand years old and still the best language anyone has found for desperation, abandonment, and hope. It produced the geometric patterns of the Alhambra, the tilework of the Shah Mosque in Isfahan, the entirety of Dante.

Can secular civilization produce art of this weight? I think it can. Beethoven. Guernica. Toni Morrison. It has been done.

But the sheer density of what religion produced -- the cathedrals, the requiems, the icons, the illuminated manuscripts -- represents something that secularism hasn't figured out how to match. Because religion gave artists the most extreme emotional material imaginable: sin, redemption, death, resurrection, the silence of God, the hope of mercy. And we replaced it with the market.

* * *

I want to be clear about what I'm not saying.

I'm not saying we should go back. You can't go back. You can't un-know what you know. You can't pretend the evidence doesn't exist because the alternative is uncomfortable. Believing a beautiful lie is still believing a lie, and I'd rather be honestly cold than dishonestly warm.

But these losses are real. Not metaphorical. Not theoretical. Real.

The grandmother with the priest and the grandson with nothing. The hospice nurse who can manage pain but not meaninglessness. Margaret and her Wednesday. The year without architecture. The cathedral you have to leave.

I know the secular response. I've made the secular response, in this book and out of it. The truth matters. The evidence matters. You can't sustain a civilization on stories that aren't true.

Fine. I believe that.

But go and tell Margaret. Go and stand in that church hall in Sunderland and explain to a seventy-eight-year-old woman who hasn't spoken to another person since last Wednesday that the truth matters. Tell her about the evidence. Watch her face.

She doesn't need the truth about the origins of the universe. She needs someone to hand her a cup of tea and ask about her cat.

Religion did that. For centuries. Imperfectly, conditionally, sometimes with terrible strings attached -- but it did it. And we haven't replaced it. We've written books and given talks and built apps and congratulated ourselves on our rationality, and Margaret is sitting in her living room with the television on and the sound turned low, waiting for Wednesday.

I don't have an answer to that. Not yet. I have the next chapter, which is my attempt at one. But I wanted to sit here for a moment, in the loss, without rushing past it. Because the loss is real, and if you don't feel it -- if you treat it as someone else's problem, as a quaint nostalgia, as the growing pains of a species outgrowing its myths -- then you haven't been paying attention.

I've been paying attention. I stood in that crematorium car park and I had nothing. I stood in that cathedral and I had to leave. I sat with Margaret and she told me Wednesday was her week, and I drove home and I thought: we owe these people more than the truth.

We owe them something to do with it.
