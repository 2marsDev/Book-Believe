# PROLOGUE
The Beach

The first time I really saw it, I was on a beach in southern Europe.

It was one of those perfect Mediterranean days — thirty-five degrees, not a cloud, the kind of heat that makes you feel like the air itself is leaning on you. The beach was packed. Families, couples, kids building things in the sand. Everyone in swimsuits, bikinis, shorts. Skin everywhere. The universal human uniform for a hot day near water.

And then I saw them.

A couple, maybe mid-thirties. He was in board shorts and nothing else. Tanned, relaxed, eating ice cream. She was sitting next to him in full black covering — head to toe. Long sleeves. Gloves. A niqab that left only her eyes visible. In thirty-five degree heat. On a beach. Next to a man who was practically naked.

They were together. Same towel. Same bag. Same children running between them.

I stared. I know I shouldn't have. But I couldn't look away.

Not because of her. Because of him.

Because he was comfortable. Completely comfortable. Licking his ice cream in the sun, watching his kids, not a care in the world — while the woman beside him, the woman he supposedly loved, sat sealed in black fabric in the blazing heat. And neither of them seemed to think anything was wrong.

*     *     *

I want to be careful here. I know the arguments. I've heard them.

She chose it. It's her faith. It's her culture. It's between her and God. Who am I to judge?

And maybe she did choose it. Maybe she wakes up every morning and genuinely, freely, without any pressure from her husband, her family, her community, or the country she grew up in, decides to cover every inch of her body while the man who shares her bed wears whatever he wants. Maybe.

But here's the thing. Their religion — the same religion, the same book, the same God — says both men and women should dress modestly. The Quran says it. Verse 24:30 tells men to "lower their gaze and guard their modesty." It comes before the verse about women. Men first. Guard your modesty.

Board shorts and ice cream.

I looked at him and I looked at her and I thought: that's not modesty. That's not faith. That's not God.

That's power. One person's comfort mattering more than another's. One person's body being free while the other's is a problem to be solved. Dressed up in the language of the sacred so that nobody's allowed to call it what it is.

*     *     *

I was never religious.

I should tell you that now, because it matters for everything that comes after. I wasn't raised in a faith and then lost it. I didn't have a crisis of belief. I didn't leave a church or a mosque or a synagogue. There was nothing to leave. My parents weren't atheists — they weren't anything. Religion was something other people did. Like cricket. I could see it mattered to them. It just wasn't mine.

For most of my life, I didn't think about it much. I had no opinion. It was background noise — a thing that existed for others, like golf or opera, and I had no reason to examine it.

But that day on the beach, something shifted. Not dramatically. There was no lightning bolt, no voice from the sky. Just a quiet thought that wouldn't leave:

How does this work?

How does a man sit half-naked next to a woman sealed in black and neither of them question it? What is the mechanism that makes this feel normal? What story were they told — and who told it to them, and when, and why — that made this arrangement seem not just acceptable but holy?

*     *     *

And it wasn't just them. Once I started noticing, I couldn't stop.

A friend's wedding where half the family wouldn't come because the couple were the wrong kind of Christian. A colleague — smart, educated, good at his job — who told me, in complete sincerity, that the Earth is six thousand years old. That dinosaur bones were placed there by God to test our faith. I remember looking at his face for a sign that he was joking. He wasn't.

Then there were the news stories. A girl in rural Pakistan, fourteen years old, who'd been seen talking to a boy from another family. Her brother shot her. An honor killing, they called it. Honor. The local imam told reporters that the family had a right to protect their reputation. That the girl's behavior — talking to a boy — had brought shame on the household.

Between the creationist and the murder, a whole spectrum of smaller things I'd never paid attention to. A school board in Texas voting to replace evolution with "intelligent design" in the biology curriculum. A woman in Iran arrested for posting a video of herself dancing without a headscarf. A Catholic hospital in Ireland letting a woman die of sepsis rather than terminating her nonviable pregnancy, because the fetus still had a heartbeat. Each one its own story. Each one connected by something I couldn't quite name yet.

I started collecting these moments the way you notice cracks spreading across a ceiling — each one small, each one easy to explain away, but together forming a pattern that points to something structural. And the pattern led to a question I couldn't avoid anymore:

What exactly do four billion people believe? And why?

*     *     *

So I started looking.

I didn't start with criticism. I started with curiosity. I wanted to understand, genuinely, what Christians, Muslims, and Jews believe and why they believe it. Not the cartoon version. Not the headlines. The real theology, the real history, the real beauty of it. I went looking. I tracked down the original texts. I sat in places of worship. I talked to believers — not to argue, but to listen. I gave each faith the most generous reading I could.

And I found things that surprised me. Things I admired. Things I didn't expect.

This book is the story of what I found.

I'm not going to tell you the ending. Not yet. I want you to walk the path the way I walked it — starting with respect, starting with wonder, and arriving wherever the road leads.

I couldn't have guessed where it would take me.
