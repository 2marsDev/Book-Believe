# PROLOGUE
The Beach

The first time I really saw it, I was on a beach in southern Europe.

It was one of those perfect Mediterranean days — thirty-five degrees, not a cloud, the kind of heat that makes you feel like the air itself is leaning on you. The sky was a flat, punishing blue, the sort that looks almost white near the horizon where it meets the sea. The sand was too hot to stand on barefoot. You could see the heat shimmer rising off the car park behind the dunes, and the air smelled of sunscreen and salt and the faintly chemical tang of cheap inflatable toys. Every few minutes a breeze would come in off the water, warm and useless, carrying the sound of children screaming in the shallows.

The beach was packed. Families, couples, kids building things in the sand. Everyone in swimsuits, bikinis, shorts. Skin everywhere. The universal human uniform for a hot day near water. Towels laid out in neat rectangles, cool boxes open, paperbacks face-down in the sand. A man a few metres away was sleeping with his mouth open, turning pink. Two teenage girls were taking photographs of each other. An elderly couple sat under a parasol, sharing a bottle of water, their legs the colour of teak. It was the kind of scene you could find on any coastline in the world where the water is warm and the living is easy. Nothing remarkable about it. Nothing you'd remember.

And then I saw them.

A couple, maybe mid-thirties. He was in board shorts and nothing else. Tanned, relaxed, eating ice cream. She was sitting next to him in full black covering — head to toe. Long sleeves. Gloves. A niqab that left only her eyes visible. In thirty-five degree heat. On a beach. Next to a man who was practically naked.

They were together. Same towel. Same bag. Same children running between them.

I stared. I know I shouldn't have. But I couldn't look away.

Not because of her. Because of him.

Because he was comfortable. Completely comfortable. Licking his ice cream in the sun, watching his kids, not a care in the world — while the woman beside him, the woman he supposedly loved, sat sealed in black fabric in the blazing heat. And neither of them seemed to think anything was wrong. He wasn't cruel. That was the thing. He wasn't ignoring her out of malice. He passed her a water bottle at one point, touched her shoulder gently. He was a man being kind to his wife on a family day out. The kindness and the absurdity existed in the same gesture, and neither of them could see it.

*     *     *

I want to be careful here. I know the arguments. I've heard them.

She chose it. It's her faith. It's her culture. It's between her and God. Who am I to judge?

And maybe she did choose it. Maybe she wakes up every morning and genuinely, freely, without any pressure from her husband, her family, her community, or the country she grew up in, decides to cover every inch of her body while the man who shares her bed wears whatever he wants. Maybe.

But here's the thing. Their religion — the same religion, the same book, the same God — says both men and women should dress modestly. The Quran says it. Verse 24:30 tells men to "lower their gaze and guard their modesty." It comes before the verse about women. Men first. Guard your modesty.

Board shorts and ice cream.

I looked at him and I looked at her and I thought: that's not modesty. That's not faith. That's not God.

That's power. One person's comfort mattering more than another's. One person's body being free while the other's is a problem to be solved. Dressed up in the language of the sacred so that nobody's allowed to call it what it is.

*     *     *

I was never religious.

I should tell you that now, because it matters for everything that comes after. I wasn't raised in a faith and then lost it. I didn't have a crisis of belief. I didn't leave a church or a mosque or a synagogue. There was nothing to leave. My parents weren't atheists — they weren't anything. Religion was something other people did. Like cricket. I could see it mattered to them. It just wasn't mine.

Growing up without religion in England is not unusual, but it's not quite invisible either. You notice it in small ways. At school, my friends who went to church or mosque had something I didn't — not just a building to go to on the weekend, but a kind of certainty, a framework that held them. They knew what happened when you died. They knew why bad things happened. They had answers to questions I hadn't even learned to ask yet. During RE lessons I'd listen to them talk about God with a casual intimacy, the way you'd talk about a relative who lived abroad — someone real, someone who mattered, someone you just hadn't seen in a while. I didn't envy them exactly, but I was aware of the gap. They had a floor under their feet. I was standing on something thinner.

At Christmas we did presents and a tree, but there was no nativity, no midnight mass, no story underneath it. Easter was chocolate eggs. When someone died — and people did die, a grandfather, a school friend's mother — there was a funeral, and the vicar said reassuring things, and I stood there knowing none of us in the family believed a word of it. We were borrowing someone else's comfort. It worked well enough. But it wasn't ours.

For most of my life, I didn't think about it much. I had no opinion. It was background noise — a thing that existed for others, like golf or opera, and I had no reason to examine it.

But that day on the beach, something shifted. Not dramatically. There was no lightning bolt, no voice from the sky. Just a quiet thought that wouldn't leave:

How does this work?

How does a man sit half-naked next to a woman sealed in black and neither of them question it? What is the mechanism that makes this feel normal? What story were they told — and who told it to them, and when, and why — that made this arrangement seem not just acceptable but holy?

I sat with that question for the rest of the afternoon. I watched them leave — he carried the cool box, she carried the youngest child, the older ones trailing behind with sandy feet and wet hair. They loaded everything into a minivan and drove away. An ordinary family at the end of an ordinary day. And I sat on my towel in the fading light and felt something I hadn't expected. Not anger. Not contempt. Something more like vertigo. The dizzying sense that I had been looking at the world without seeing one of its largest features — a force that shaped the lives of billions of people and that I had never bothered to examine because it didn't belong to me.

*     *     *

And it wasn't just them. Once I started noticing, I couldn't stop.

A friend's wedding where half the family wouldn't come because the couple were the wrong kind of Christian. A colleague — smart, educated, good at his job — who told me, in complete sincerity, that the Earth is six thousand years old. That dinosaur bones were placed there by God to test our faith. I remember looking at his face for a sign that he was joking. He wasn't. He had a master's degree in mechanical engineering. He could design load-bearing structures that would stand for a century. And he believed the planet was younger than some bristlecone pines.

Then there were the news stories. A girl in rural Pakistan, fourteen years old, who'd been seen talking to a boy from another family. Her brother shot her. An honor killing, they called it. Honor. The local imam told reporters that the family had a right to protect their reputation. That the girl's behavior — talking to a boy — had brought shame on the household. She was fourteen. She talked to someone. And the people who loved her most killed her for it, and believed God approved.

Between the creationist and the murder, a whole spectrum of smaller things I'd never paid attention to. A school board in Texas trying to introduce intelligent design into their biology curriculum — not as philosophy, but as science, with a straight face and legal counsel. A woman in Iran arrested for posting a video of herself dancing without a headscarf — dancing, in her own home, in a country where the morality police had the authority to beat her for the crime of visible hair. A family in London I read about in the Sunday papers, whose teenage son had come out as gay; his evangelical church arranged a programme of conversion therapy, and his parents, who loved him and believed they were saving him, signed the forms. A hospital in Ireland, in a country where the law still reflected the Catholic Church's teaching on the sanctity of fetal life, letting a woman die of sepsis rather than terminating her nonviable pregnancy. Each one its own story. Each one connected by something I couldn't quite name yet.

I started collecting these moments the way you notice cracks spreading across a ceiling — each one small, each one easy to explain away, but together forming a pattern that points to something structural. And the pattern led to a question I couldn't avoid anymore:

What exactly do four billion people believe? And why?

*     *     *

I didn't answer that question straight away. I circled it for weeks. I read a few articles, put them down, picked them up again. I'd find myself in the middle of something else entirely — cooking, walking, half-watching television — and the question would surface again, patient and insistent, like a stone in a shoe. I wasn't sure I wanted to follow it. I wasn't sure it was my question to ask. I had no training in theology, no academic credentials, no particular standing. I was just a man who had seen something on a beach and couldn't unsee it.

But the question wouldn't leave. And eventually I stopped trying to make it.

So I started looking.

I didn't start with criticism. I started with curiosity. I wanted to understand, genuinely, what Christians, Muslims, and Jews believe and why they believe it. Not the cartoon version. Not the headlines. The real theology, the real history, the real beauty of it. I went looking. I tracked down the original texts. I sat in places of worship. I talked to believers — not to argue, but to listen. I gave each faith the most generous reading I could.

And I found things that surprised me. Things I admired. Things I didn't expect.

This book is the story of what I found.

I'm not going to tell you the ending. Not yet. I want you to walk the path the way I walked it — starting with respect, starting with wonder, and arriving wherever the road leads.

I couldn't have guessed where it would take me.
