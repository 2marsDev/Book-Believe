Chapter 4: The Receipt

Every deal has a receipt.

Religion offers you the most extraordinary package of promises in human history: eternal life, cosmic purpose, absolute moral clarity, unconditional love, and a community that will catch you when you fall. In exchange, it asks for one thing: your belief. Just believe. Don't ask for proof. Don't demand evidence. Trust us. Have faith.

Faith. That word does a lot of heavy lifting. In every other area of life, we have a different word for believing something without evidence. We call it gullibility. We call it credulity. We call it wishful thinking. But when it comes to religion, we invented a special word that makes the absence of evidence sound noble.

For three chapters, I've been respectful. I've shown you the beauty, the people, the real human needs these traditions meet. I hope I've done that honestly. Now I'm going to look at the receipt. I'm going to test the claims against the only standard that matters in every other domain of human knowledge: evidence.

If the claims hold up, I'll say so. If they don't, I'll say that too.

*     *     *

But before I get to the evidence, I need you to understand something about how evidence works. Because the religious world has developed a set of remarkably effective strategies for making the absence of evidence look like evidence of something deeper. "Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence," they'll say. "God cannot be measured by human instruments." "Science can't test the supernatural."

These sound reasonable. They're not.

If someone tells you there's an invisible dragon in their garage, and you point out that you can't see it, hear it, touch it, or detect it with any instrument — and they respond that the dragon is invisible, silent, intangible, and undetectable by design — at what point are you allowed to say the dragon isn't there?

You're allowed to say it now.

The philosopher Carl Sagan coined that analogy, and it has never been satisfactorily answered. Not because it's a trick. Because it identifies a genuine logical problem. A claim that cannot in principle be tested is not a claim that deserves special protection. It is a claim that has placed itself beyond the reach of knowledge entirely. And a claim beyond the reach of knowledge is, functionally, no claim at all.

Let me show you the garage.

*     *     *

Let's start with the simplest question. Did the events described in these holy books actually happen?

Not the teachings. Not the moral lessons. The events. The factual claims about things that either happened or didn't happen in physical reality. Things that leave traces.

So I went looking for traces.

*     *     *

I started where everyone starts: with the Exodus.

The Exodus is the founding story of Judaism — and by extension, of Christianity and Islam, which both inherit it. God's people, enslaved in Egypt for four hundred years. Ten plagues rain down on their oppressors. Moses parts the Red Sea. Six hundred thousand men, plus women and children — call it two and a half million people — walk through the desert for forty years before arriving in the Promised Land.

It is one of the most detailed, specific, geographically anchored narratives in the Bible. It names places. It names rulers. It gives numbers. It describes physical events on an enormous scale — plagues that devastated an empire, a sea that split in two, an entire nation wandering a desert for four decades.

If this happened, there should be evidence. Two and a half million people living in a desert for forty years would leave a trail that archaeologists could find in their sleep. Pottery, bones, tools, fire pits, latrines, graves. Forty years of campsites for a population the size of modern-day Chicago. You can't hide that.

I spent three weeks reading excavation reports. Not polemics, not atheist blogs — the actual published findings of the archaeologists who have spent decades sifting through the Sinai Peninsula with trowels and brushes, looking for exactly this evidence. Many of them Israeli. Many of them hoping, personally and professionally, to find confirmation of their nation's founding story.

They found nothing.

No pottery. No bones. No campsites. No graves. No chariot wheels at the bottom of the Red Sea. No inscriptions marking the route. The Sinai is not a small desert, but it is a well-studied one, and the kinds of remains that two and a half million people would leave behind — fire pits, animal bones, broken tools, human waste deposits — are precisely the kinds of things that survive in arid conditions for millennia. Archaeologists have found campsites from the third millennium BCE with populations of a few dozen. Forty years of habitation by millions would be unmissable.

The Egyptian records are equally silent — and the Egyptians were obsessive record-keepers. They documented military campaigns, diplomatic exchanges, trade agreements, border crossings. They noted the arrival of a few dozen nomads seeking grain during a famine. They recorded the escape of two runaway slaves. But two and a half million people walking out of their country, ten plagues that should have crippled their civilisation, their entire army drowning in a sea? Not a word.

Israel Finkelstein, professor of archaeology at Tel Aviv University, has spent a career excavating in the region. His conclusion, published in *The Bible Unearthed*, is unequivocal: the Exodus as described in the Bible simply did not happen. Not in the way the text describes. Not on the scale the text claims. His colleague Ze'ev Herzog, also of Tel Aviv University, wrote in *Ha'aretz* in 1999 that "the Israelites were never in Egypt, did not wander in the desert, did not conquer the land in a military campaign, and did not pass it on to the twelve tribes of Israel." William Dever, an American archaeologist who has spent decades in the Near East and who is emphatically not an atheist polemicist — he has criticised both biblical minimalists and maximalists — reached a similar conclusion: there is "remarkably little of proven or provable historical worth in the biblical Exodus narrative."

Maybe we haven't looked in the right place. Maybe desert conditions destroyed the remains. Maybe the Egyptians erased the records because the events were humiliating. These are possible responses. I considered each of them. But the Sinai has been surveyed extensively — Finkelstein's own surveys covered the entire peninsula. The desert conditions that supposedly destroyed the evidence are the same conditions that preserved far older and far smaller settlements elsewhere in the region. And the idea that the Egyptians erased the records requires a conspiracy of silence that left no seams — no mismatched entries, no redacted passages, no archaeological evidence of the destruction of records. It is possible. It is not probable.

Most scholars allow that the story probably has a historical core — meaning some smaller group of Semitic people probably did leave Egypt at some point. There is some evidence that Semitic-speaking peoples lived in the eastern Nile Delta during the second millennium BCE, and the names Moses and Aaron may have Egyptian origins. But the story as told — the millions, the plagues, the sea parting, the forty years — is not supported by anything found in the ground.

The foundational story of three religions. And we can't find it.

I remember sitting in a library in London, surrounded by volumes of excavation reports, thinking about what that meant. Not as a gotcha. Not as a triumph. Just as a fact. The story that billions of people believe is the literal history of God's intervention in the world — the story that anchors the Passover seder, the Easter liturgy, and the Quranic narrative alike — appears to be, at best, a cultural memory inflated beyond all recognition. At worst, a foundational myth. Powerful. Meaningful. But not history.

*     *     *

What about Jesus?

Here the evidence is better. Significantly better. Virtually all historians — including secular scholars with no Christian loyalty — accept that a Jewish preacher named Jesus of Nazareth existed in first-century Palestine. He was baptised by John the Baptist. He gathered followers. He was crucified under Pontius Pilate around 30 CE. These are among the most well-attested facts in ancient history. The Roman historian Tacitus mentions him in the *Annals*, referring to "Christus" who "suffered the extreme penalty during the reign of Tiberius at the hands of one of our procurators, Pontius Pilatus." The Jewish historian Josephus mentions him twice in the *Antiquities of the Jews* — one passage almost certainly doctored by later Christian scribes, but the other, a brief reference to "the brother of Jesus, who was called Christ, whose name was James," is widely accepted as genuine. Even scholars who doubt everything else about the Gospels accept this much.

Jesus was real.

But that's not what Christianity claims. Christianity doesn't ask you to believe that a Jewish preacher existed. It asks you to believe he was the Son of God, born of a virgin, who performed miracles, died for the sins of humanity, and rose physically from the dead three days later.

And here the distinction between evidence for the man and evidence for the miracles becomes critical — because the two are constantly conflated, sometimes deliberately. Apologists will say: "Even secular historians accept that Jesus existed!" as though this somehow supports the resurrection. It doesn't. The existence of a first-century Jewish preacher is one claim. The claim that he walked on water, raised Lazarus from the dead, and returned to life after crucifixion is an entirely different order of claim, requiring an entirely different standard of evidence.

For the existence of the man: strong evidence.

For the miracles, the virgin birth, the resurrection: nothing that would count as evidence in any other field of inquiry.

I want to be precise about what I mean. I don't mean "controversial evidence" or "disputed evidence." I mean there is no physical, archaeological, medical, or independently verified evidence that any miracle described in any Gospel ever occurred. No Roman record notes anything unusual happening in Jerusalem that weekend. No contemporary observer — and there were literate observers in first-century Palestine — wrote down that the dead were rising, that the temple curtain tore, that darkness covered the land for three hours. Matthew's Gospel claims that at the moment of Jesus's death, "the tombs broke open. The bodies of many holy people who had died were raised to life." Dead saints walking through Jerusalem. And no one else noticed. Not Philo of Alexandria, who was writing at the time. Not the Roman administrators who kept detailed records. Not a single non-Christian source.

To understand why the miracle claims should be treated with scepticism, it helps to understand the world they emerged from. First-century Palestine was a pressure cooker. The Jewish population lived under Roman military occupation — a brutal, grinding reality of taxation, political humiliation, and periodic violence. The temple in Jerusalem was the centre of Jewish religious life, but it was also entangled with Roman power; the high priest served at the pleasure of the Roman governor. The result was a culture saturated with apocalyptic expectation. The world was broken. God would intervene. A messiah would come.

And messianic claimants were everywhere. Jesus was not the only one. Josephus describes several — Judas the Galilean, Theudas, an unnamed Egyptian prophet who led followers into the wilderness with the promise that God would bring down the walls of Jerusalem. Each of them attracted followers. Each of them was destroyed by Rome. What distinguished Jesus from the others was not the nature of his claims during his lifetime, but what happened after his death: his followers didn't scatter. They regrouped and built a movement.

In this cultural context, miracle claims were not unusual. They were the standard currency of religious authority. Apollonius of Tyana, a near-contemporary of Jesus, was credited with healings, exorcisms, and raising the dead. The Emperor Vespasian was said to have healed a blind man by spitting in his eyes. Asclepius, the Greek god of medicine, had healing temples across the Mediterranean where the sick reported miraculous cures. Claiming that your leader performed miracles was how you said "this person matters" in the ancient world. It was a literary and cultural convention, not a factual report. It didn't mean you were lying. It meant you were speaking the language your culture understood.

The only sources for Jesus's miracles are the Gospels themselves, written decades after the events they describe, by people who were not eyewitnesses, in a language Jesus didn't speak, in a culture where miracle stories were the standard way of saying "this person was important."

Alexander the Great was said to be the son of Zeus. Augustus Caesar was called "Son of God." The pharaohs were literal gods. In the ancient Mediterranean, claiming divine parentage and miraculous powers for your hero was like putting a gold badge on a product. It didn't mean it was true. It meant the people telling the story wanted you to take it seriously.

Is it possible that Jesus actually rose from the dead? In the strictest logical sense, yes. Anything is possible. But the question isn't what's possible. The question is what's probable. And a man rising from the dead after three days is, by any standard of evidence we apply to any other claim in any other context, extraordinarily improbable. Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence. That's not an anti-religious principle. It's just how knowledge works. And the evidence here — anonymous accounts written decades later, disagreeing with each other on basic details — does not rise to the occasion.

*     *     *

Muhammad presents a different kind of problem.

His historical existence is not in serious doubt. He was born in Mecca around 570 CE. He married Khadijah, a successful businesswoman. Around the age of forty, he began receiving what he described as revelations from God through the angel Gabriel in a cave near Mecca. Over twenty-three years, these revelations were compiled into the Quran. He built a community, migrated to Medina, fought battles, established a state, and died in 632 CE.

Much of this is well-documented by multiple near-contemporary sources. Muhammad was a real person. That's not the question.

The question is whether an angel actually appeared to him in a cave and dictated the literal word of God.

And here we arrive at the same wall. The claim is supernatural. An invisible being communicated with one man, alone, in a cave on Mount Hira, with no witnesses. The only evidence that this happened is Muhammad's own testimony and the text itself. One man's report of a private experience.

The Islamic tradition itself preserves a striking detail about the nature of this experience. According to the hadith literature — the recorded sayings and traditions of Muhammad — when the revelations first began, Muhammad was terrified. He returned home shaking and asked his wife Khadijah to cover him with a cloak. He feared he was possessed — that a jinn, one of the spirit-beings of Arabian folklore, had seized him. It was Khadijah who reassured him, and her cousin Waraqah ibn Nawfal, a man versed in Jewish and Christian scripture, who told Muhammad that what he had experienced was the same divine messenger who had come to Moses. Without Khadijah's reassurance and Waraqah's theological interpretation, Muhammad might have dismissed his own experience as madness or possession.

This is not a hostile reading. This is what the tradition itself records. The man who received the revelation initially doubted it. His first interpretation was not "God has spoken to me" but "something is wrong with me." It took other people — people with existing theological frameworks — to tell him what the experience meant.

And the doubts did not end there. The early Islamic sources record periods called *fatrah* — gaps in the revelation — during which Muhammad received nothing and reportedly became so distressed that, according to some traditions recorded by al-Bukhari, he contemplated throwing himself from a mountaintop. These are not details invented by hostile outsiders. They are preserved in the most authoritative hadith collections in Sunni Islam.

In every other context, we know how to evaluate this kind of claim. People report private revelations constantly — voices that speak to them, visions that reveal cosmic truths, messages from beings no one else can see. We have clinical frameworks for understanding these experiences. We have neurological explanations. We have the simple, uncomfortable observation that throughout history, thousands of people have sincerely believed they were receiving divine messages, and we dismiss all of them except the ones that happen to have founded our own tradition. Joseph Smith claimed the angel Moroni gave him golden plates. Ellen White reported prophetic visions. The followers of the Bab claimed divine revelation. We evaluate each of these claims with the same basic question: is there any evidence beyond the claimant's own testimony?

The only thing that separates Muhammad's revelation from the thousands of others is that his succeeded. Success is not evidence of truth. It is evidence of persuasion.

Muslims will say: but look at the Quran itself. Its beauty, its complexity, its literary perfection — these prove it could not have been written by a human being. This is the argument from inimitability — *i'jaz*. The Quran challenges anyone to produce a chapter like it, and Muslims believe no one ever has.

But this is a subjective argument, not an evidentiary one. I find Shakespeare's sonnets stunning. I find Beethoven's Ninth Symphony beyond what I thought humans were capable of. Neither of these proves divine authorship. Human beings are capable of extraordinary things. The assumption that beauty requires a god is not a fact. It's a failure of imagination about what humans can do.

*     *     *

I can hear the objection. I've been hearing it since I started writing this chapter.

You're applying the wrong standard. You're asking for empirical, material evidence for claims that are, by definition, outside the material domain. You're demanding that God pass a science test. You're trying to weigh the infinite on a bathroom scale. That's like demanding love show up on an MRI.

This is the strongest counter-argument theology has, and I want to take it seriously.

But here's the distinction. Love does show up — not on an MRI, but in the world. We can verify love exists independently of the person who feels it. We see it in behaviour. We measure it in hormones and neural activity. We observe its effects: the sacrifices people make, the bonds they maintain, the grief they suffer when it's gone. Love is a subjective experience, but it produces objective, observable, independently verifiable consequences. If you told me you loved someone, and I could see no evidence of it in how you acted, what you sacrificed, or how your life was shaped by it, I would be reasonable to doubt you.

God, as described by these religions, is supposed to act in the world. He parts seas. He sends plagues. He raises the dead. He dictates books. These are not metaphysical abstractions. These are physical interventions in material reality — the kind that would leave evidence. When theology says God is beyond empirical testing, it is retreating from the very claims its own scriptures make. The Bible doesn't describe a God who exists only in the hearts of believers. It describes a God who drowns armies, burns cities, and sends his son to walk among us in flesh and blood.

If God acts in the material world, then material evidence is exactly the right standard. And if God doesn't act in the material world — if he is truly beyond all detection and verification — then he is, for all practical purposes, indistinguishable from a God who isn't there.

That's not a gotcha. It's a genuine problem. And I spent a long time looking for a theological response that resolves it.

*     *     *

I read Alvin Plantinga. I read William Lane Craig. I read the Kalam cosmological argument and the fine-tuning argument and the argument from religious experience. I want to give each of them its due, because some of them are genuinely sophisticated, and dismissing them too quickly would be dishonest.

Plantinga's argument is perhaps the most philosophically rigorous. He doesn't try to prove God exists through evidence. Instead, he argues that belief in God can be "properly basic" — a foundational belief that doesn't require evidence any more than my belief that I had breakfast this morning requires evidence. Just as I trust my memory and my senses without being able to prove from scratch that they're reliable, a believer can trust their experience of God without external verification. Belief in God, on this view, is like belief in other minds — something we're rationally entitled to hold even though we can't strictly prove it.

It's a clever move. And it has a real insight at its core: all reasoning starts from assumptions that can't themselves be proven. But the analogy breaks down in a crucial place. My belief that I had breakfast is consistent with everything else I observe — the dishes in the sink, the sensation in my stomach, the testimony of anyone who was in the kitchen. Belief in God is consistent with some things people observe — the feeling of transcendence, the sense of being loved — but wildly inconsistent with others: the distribution of suffering, the silence of prayer studies, the geographical distribution of belief. Properly basic beliefs are supposed to be the kind that everyone with functioning faculties would arrive at. But billions of people with functioning faculties don't believe in God, or believe in a completely different God. That's not how properly basic beliefs work.

The Kalam cosmological argument, championed by William Lane Craig, runs like this: everything that begins to exist has a cause. The universe began to exist. Therefore, the universe has a cause. And that cause, Craig argues, must be personal, timeless, immaterial, and enormously powerful — in other words, God.

The argument is formally valid. The first premise seems obviously true. The second has support from Big Bang cosmology. But the conclusion doesn't follow as cleanly as it appears. Even granting that the universe has a cause, nothing in the argument tells you that the cause is personal, conscious, or interested in human affairs. It could be an impersonal quantum event. It could be something so alien to human categories that calling it "God" is as misleading as calling a black hole a "drain." The Kalam argument gets you to a first cause. It does not get you to a being who parts seas and answers prayers. The gap between "something caused the universe" and "that something is the God of Abraham" is immense, and the argument provides no bridge.

The fine-tuning argument observes that the physical constants of our universe — the strength of gravity, the mass of the electron, the cosmological constant — are set to values that permit the existence of complex life. Change any of them by a tiny fraction, and stars don't form, chemistry doesn't happen, life is impossible. The probability of this arrangement occurring by chance, the argument goes, is so vanishingly small that it points to a designer.

This is, I think, the most genuinely interesting of the three. The fine-tuning is real. The numbers are extraordinary. But the argument has a problem that I've never seen adequately resolved. If the constants were different, we wouldn't be here to notice. We can only observe a universe compatible with our existence — this is the anthropic principle, and it isn't a dodge. It's a logical necessity. You can't observe a universe in which you don't exist. Furthermore, if there are vast numbers of universes with different constants — the multiverse hypothesis, which some interpretations of quantum mechanics and inflationary cosmology suggest — then the fact that we find ourselves in one that supports life is no more surprising than the fact that we find ourselves on a planet with breathable air, even though most planets don't have it. We're not on most planets. We're on this one. And we're not in most universes. We're in this one.

Some of these arguments are clever. None of them produce evidence. They produce reasons why you might consider belief reasonable in the absence of evidence. That's a different thing entirely. A reason to think something might be true is not the same as a reason to think it is true. And the gap between "might" and "is" matters — because on the other side of that gap, people are building lives, raising children, making laws, and sometimes killing each other on the basis of claims that remain, after everything, unproven.

*     *     *

So here's where we stand.

The major historical figures at the centre of these religions — Jesus and Muhammad — almost certainly existed. Moses probably did, though the evidence is much thinner. The places are real. Some of the political and cultural events described in these texts have archaeological support.

But the supernatural claims — the miracles, the revelations, the parting seas and rising dead and angelic dictations — have no evidence outside the texts that make the claims. In every other area of human life, we know what to call a claim supported only by the testimony of the people who already believe it.

We call it unproven.

I sat with that for a long time. It didn't feel like a victory. It felt like something collapsing. Not my faith — I never had one. But my assumption that surely, with four billion people and three thousand years, there must be something. Some piece of evidence I hadn't found. Some artefact, some inscription, some independently verified account that would make me say: well, maybe.

I had expected, if I'm honest, to arrive at a place of respectful uncertainty. I thought the evidence would be ambiguous — strong enough in places to prevent confident dismissal, weak enough in others to prevent confident belief. I imagined ending this chapter by saying: the evidence is mixed, and reasonable people can disagree.

That's not what happened. The evidence wasn't mixed. It was absent. Not hidden, not contested, not awaiting further excavation. Absent. The Exodus left no trace. The miracles of Jesus were recorded by no one outside the movement that proclaimed them. Muhammad's revelation happened in a cave with no witnesses. And the philosophical arguments, however elegant, were bridges that started on solid ground and ended in mid-air.

What collapsed was not a belief but an expectation. The expectation that somewhere in the vast library of human religious experience, there would be a single piece of evidence that couldn't be explained by natural means. One verified miracle. One prophecy that named something specific before it happened. One archaeological discovery that confirmed the supernatural. One prayer study that showed an effect.

There wasn't.

And then I did something that made it worse.

I opened the books themselves.
