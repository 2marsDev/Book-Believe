# Chapter 11: The Children

I was eight years old the first time someone told me I might go to hell.

I don't remember the exact words. I remember the feeling. A Sunday school teacher describing a lake of fire. A place where you burned and burned and never died. A place God sent people who didn't believe the right things.

I was eight. I went home and couldn't sleep.

That's where this chapter starts. Not with theology. Not with doctrine. With the face of a child who has just been told that an all-powerful being might torture them forever.

* * *

In 2006, Richard Dawkins published The God Delusion and made a claim that provoked outrage. He said that labeling children with their parents' religion — calling them "Catholic children" or "Muslim children" — was a form of abuse. He argued that the systematic indoctrination of children into religious belief, particularly when accompanied by threats of eternal punishment, could cause lasting psychological harm comparable to other forms of childhood trauma.

The backlash was fierce. Critics called Dawkins arrogant, insensitive. Religious leaders said he was trivializing actual child abuse.

Was Dawkins right?

He was not entirely right. Calling all religious upbringing "abuse" is too broad and too dismissive of the billions of people who were raised in faith and emerged as healthy, grateful adults. It flattens an enormous range of experience into a single inflammatory label.

But he was not entirely wrong either. And the evidence has only grown since he made the claim.

* * *

Let me tell you about hell.

Not the theological concept. The psychological reality. What it does to a developing brain.

Research published in the Journal of Anxiety Disorders examined the relationship between religious beliefs and anxiety in children and adolescents. Children who held strong beliefs in divine punishment — specifically, in hell as a real place of eternal torment — showed significantly higher levels of generalized anxiety than those who did not. The effect was independent of general religiosity. It wasn't belief in God that correlated with anxiety. It was belief in hell.

Research in Mental Health, Religion & Culture found that adults who had been raised with a vivid, literal doctrine of hell were more likely to report symptoms consistent with post-traumatic stress, including intrusive thoughts, hypervigilance, and avoidance behaviors. The hell exposure was an independent predictor, even after controlling for other childhood adversity.

Dr. Marlene Winell, a psychologist who has spent decades treating people recovering from authoritarian religious upbringings, coined the term "Religious Trauma Syndrome" — anxiety, depression, difficulty with decision-making, problems with self-worth, and a persistent fear response triggered by religious stimuli. She's careful to note that not all religious upbringing produces this. But certain combinations — an angry God, literal hell, rigid rules, social isolation from the non-religious world — reliably produce psychological harm.

"The fear was installed before the critical faculties developed," she told me. "You can't just reason your way out of it. The body remembers what the mind has rejected."

* * *

I spoke with a woman named Sarah, who grew up in a fundamentalist Baptist church in rural Tennessee. She asked me not to use her last name because her parents still don't know she's left the faith.

We met in a coffee shop in Nashville. She was small, sharp-eyed, quick to laugh at herself. She worked in hospital administration. She had a cat named Dolly and a dark sense of humor about her childhood. She was not fragile. I want to be clear about that, because people hear "religious trauma" and picture someone barely holding together. Sarah was holding together fine. She just wasn't sleeping through the night.

"I was five," she told me. "My Sunday school teacher had this flannel board with felt figures. She put a figure on the board and said, 'This is a boy who didn't accept Jesus.' Then she moved the figure down to the bottom where she'd drawn red and orange flames. She said, 'This is where he goes forever.'"

Sarah paused. Took a sip of her coffee.

"I had nightmares for years. I would wake up in the middle of the night and pray, just pray and pray, because I was terrified I hadn't believed hard enough. That maybe I'd said the salvation prayer wrong. That I'd burn."

I asked how often.

"Every night? At least once a week until I was maybe twelve. Then it got less frequent but worse. More vivid. I'd dream about my skin peeling off. About screaming and no one hearing me. And the God in the dream wasn't angry. He was just... watching. That was the worst part. He wasn't even mad. It was just how things were."

She's thirty-six now. She left the church at twenty-two. She still has the nightmares sometimes.

"Fourteen years later," she said. "I know it's not real. My brain knows. But my body doesn't. I'll wake up at three in the morning and my heart is pounding and my sheets are soaked and for about ten seconds I'm absolutely certain I'm going to hell. Then the thinking brain comes online and I remember I'm a thirty-six-year-old woman who doesn't believe in hell, and I go get a glass of water and sit in the kitchen until my hands stop shaking."

She looked at me.

"That's what they did to me. When I was five. And I can't get it out."

* * *

This is not about one woman. This is about a mechanism.

When you teach a child that an omniscient being is watching their every thought, you install a surveillance system inside their mind. When you teach them that wrong thoughts or wrong beliefs will result in infinite torture, you attach the most extreme possible consequence to the act of thinking freely.

A child cannot evaluate this claim. A child trusts the adults in their life. When those adults say hell is real, hell becomes real.

And here's what I find most disturbing: the adults often believe they're helping. The Sunday school teacher with the flannel board isn't trying to traumatize a five-year-old. She's trying to save a soul. The motivation is love — or what has been shaped to look like love by the same system that shaped her.

The harm is structural. It doesn't require bad people. It just requires a bad idea taught to someone too young to reject it.

* * *

Now let's talk about education.

In late 2004, the school board of Dover, Pennsylvania, voted to require that a statement about "intelligent design" be read to students in ninth-grade biology class. The case went to trial in 2005 — Kitzmiller v. Dover. The judge, a Republican-appointed churchgoing Lutheran, ruled that intelligent design was religious doctrine dressed in scientific language. It cost the school district over $1 million in legal fees.

But Dover was the case that made it to court. In thousands of school districts across America, the same pressure operates quietly. Teachers self-censor. They skip the evolution unit. They teach it quickly, afraid of complaints. A 2020 survey in Evolution: Education and Outreach found that only about two-thirds of U.S. public high school biology teachers present evolution as the scientific consensus without qualification.

One-third of biology teachers either hedge on evolution, skip it, or present creationism alongside it. In biology class. In the twenty-first century.

This matters because it teaches children something beyond bad science. It teaches them that authority figures will override evidence when evidence conflicts with belief. That knowledge is negotiable. That the scientific method is just one opinion among many.

* * *

In Brooklyn, New York, there are Hasidic yeshivas where boys spend the majority of their school day studying Talmud in Yiddish. Secular subjects — English, math, science, history — receive minimal attention, sometimes as little as ninety minutes a day, sometimes nothing at all.

This has been an open secret for decades.

In 2015, a group called Young Advocates for Fair Education, or YAFFED, filed a complaint with the New York City Department of Education. The complaint alleged that dozens of Hasidic yeshivas were failing to provide the "substantially equivalent" secular education required by state law. The organization was founded by Naftuli Moster, himself a product of the yeshiva system, who has described graduating unable to write a complete sentence in English.

The city investigated. Slowly. Over the course of years.

In 2022, the New York Times published a major investigative report that examined Hasidic schools receiving more than $1 billion in government funding over the previous four years. The investigation found that in many of these schools, boys could not read English at a functional level. They could not do basic arithmetic. They had never been taught science. In the schools the Times examined, fewer than one percent of students in grades three through eight passed the state standardized tests in English. Fewer than one percent.

Graduates described leaving the yeshiva system unable to fill out a job application in English. Unable to read a lease. Unable to navigate a world they had been deliberately kept separate from.

The city and state knew. Politicians knew. The Hasidic community represents a powerful voting bloc in New York — roughly 200,000 people who vote in high proportions and largely follow the endorsements of their rebbes. Elected officials who challenged the yeshiva system risked losing an entire community's support.

So they didn't challenge it. For years. While children graduated without the tools to survive outside their community.

And that's the point. You don't need to keep someone in a room to trap them. You just need to make sure they can't function in any room but yours.

In parts of Pakistan, Bangladesh, and West Africa, residential Quranic schools do something structurally similar — children memorise a sacred text in a language they don't speak, for years, emerging unable to do multiplication. In Senegal, the talibe system sends boys as young as five to live with Quranic teachers called marabouts. A 2019 Human Rights Watch report documented cases of boys forced to beg in the streets to meet daily quotas, beaten if they failed, sleeping on concrete floors, malnourished and unschooled in anything but Quranic recitation. The Senegalese government has tried repeatedly to regulate the system. It has largely failed, because the marabouts' religious authority insulates them from state oversight.

The geography changes. The mechanism doesn't. In every case, the structure is the same: a religious authority claims jurisdiction over a child's education. The state defers, because challenging religious authority is politically costly. And the child — who did not choose any of this — emerges into adulthood without the tools that every other child in that society takes for granted.

The defenders of these systems always frame the argument as one of religious freedom. And religious freedom is real, and it matters, and I have spent enough of this book defending it to be taken seriously when I say: the freedom of a parent to educate their child in their faith does not include the freedom to render that child helpless. A child is not a vessel for their parents' beliefs. A child is a future adult, and that future adult has rights that exist independently of what their parents believe about God.

* * *

I keep returning to a concept that has no satisfying name: the right to leave.

Every major religion allows conversion — in theory. In practice, the cost of leaving is often so high that the exit door might as well be welded shut.

In fundamentalist Christian communities, leaving the faith means losing your family. Not metaphorically. Literally. Parents stop speaking to adult children who deconvert. Siblings cut contact. The entire social network collapses.

In Islam, apostasy is a capital offense in at least twelve countries. Even where it's not legally punishable, the social consequences are severe.

Among Jehovah's Witnesses, leaving is met with "disfellowshipping" — a formal process of shunning. Your family members who remain in the faith are instructed not to speak to you. Not to eat with you. Not to acknowledge you.

In ultra-Orthodox Jewish communities, leaving — going "off the derech" — means losing your family, your community, and often your children in custody disputes where religious courts carry enormous weight.

Now imagine you were born into one of these systems.

You didn't choose it. You were an infant. The beliefs were installed before you had language to question them. The community was all you knew. Your education was controlled by the community. Your sense of identity was constructed by the community.

And then someone tells you that you're free to leave.

Are you?

Freedom requires options. Options require knowledge, skills, and social connections outside the system. If the system has made sure you don't have those things, the freedom to leave is theoretical. It exists on paper. It doesn't exist in the life of a twenty-year-old who has never been taught to read in the language of the country they live in.

I met a man I'll call Jakob in a cafe in Antwerp. He was twenty-seven. He'd left a Haredi community in Belgium at twenty-three. He spoke Yiddish fluently and could read Talmud with his eyes closed. He could not read a Dutch newspaper. Not slowly. Not at all.

He described his first year outside as a kind of second infancy. He had to learn to read Dutch from children's books. He couldn't fill out a form at the employment office. He didn't know how a bank account worked. He didn't know what a CV was. He had never used the internet unsupervised. He was a grown man with a beard and a brilliant mind, and he was functionally illiterate in the language of the country where he had been born and lived his entire life.

"The community says you can leave," he told me. "That's technically true. But they've made sure that if you leave, you can't survive. You have no language. No qualifications. No references. No friends outside. Your family sits shiva for you — you're dead to them. Your wife takes the children. You walk out of the only world you've ever known, and you're standing on a street you can't even read the signs on."

He paused. He'd been stirring his coffee without drinking it.

"They don't lock the door. They just make sure there's nothing on the other side of it."

Jakob was not angry. He was precise. He'd taught himself Dutch, then English, then basic accounting. He worked in a warehouse. He saw his children every other weekend, under supervision, because the community's rabbinical court had deemed him an unfit influence. He was rebuilding an entire life from materials that should have been given to him as a child, and the system that had withheld them was still, technically, within its rights.

That's the sharpest edge of this argument. The right to leave means nothing if the right to be equipped to leave was never granted. And the communities that most loudly proclaim freedom of conscience are often the ones that have most carefully ensured that conscience has nowhere to go.

This is not, I want to be clear, an argument against all religious education. It is an argument against educational deprivation disguised as religious education. There is a bright line between teaching a child your faith and teaching a child only your faith. Between giving a child roots and giving a child chains. The test is simple: when this child is eighteen, will they have the skills to survive outside this community if they choose to leave? If the answer is yes, the education is legitimate. If the answer is no, then what you have is not education. It is captivity with a curriculum.

* * *

I need to say something that complicates everything I've just written.

Many children raised in religious households turn out wonderfully. They are kind, disciplined, community-oriented, and morally serious. The research on this is real and I won't pretend it doesn't exist.

A 2018 study in the American Journal of Epidemiology found that children who attended religious services at least weekly were approximately 16 percent more likely to report high happiness as young adults. They were 29 percent more likely to volunteer. They were 33 percent less likely to use illicit drugs.

Religious communities provide structure, mentorship, intergenerational connection, and a framework for navigating suffering. For children in unstable environments, a church, mosque, or synagogue may be the most stable institution in their lives.

A religious upbringing can be a profound gift. I have met people for whom it was.

I spoke with a man named David, raised in a Modern Orthodox Jewish home in Chicago. He described Shabbat dinners with extended family every Friday night. He described a community that showed up when his father was sick — meals at the door every day for six weeks. He described learning Hebrew, learning to read Torah, feeling connected to a tradition that stretched back thousands of years.

"It gave me roots," he said. "In a world that feels rootless, I always knew where I came from."

David still practices. He sends his children to Jewish day school — a school, I should note, that teaches math and science and English alongside Torah. His children will have the tools to leave, if they choose to. He told me he wants them to have what he had.

I believe him. I believe it was good.

* * *

So how do you hold both truths?

The child who thrives in a loving religious home and the child who lies awake at night, certain she's going to burn. The boy who learns Torah alongside math and science and becomes a thoughtful adult, and the boy who can recite every word of a holy text but can't read a street sign in the city where he lives.

You hold them by recognising that "religion" is not one thing. The experience of a child in a progressive Reform synagogue and the experience of a child in a fundamentalist compound share almost nothing except the word "religious." The spectrum is enormous, and most religious childhoods fall somewhere in the middle — imperfect, complicated, neither idyllic nor abusive, a mixture of warmth and confusion and hand-me-down assumptions that the child will spend decades sorting through. That's normal. That's what growing up in any tradition looks like. The problem is not religious upbringing in general. The problem is the specific practices — hell-fear, educational deprivation, social isolation, the systematic removal of the tools needed to leave — that move from the territory of imperfect parenting into the territory of measurable harm.

But here's what I can't accept: the idea that we shouldn't ask the question because the answer is complicated.

The question is simple. Does a child's mind belong to their parents' tradition before it belongs to the child?

Every religious tradition answers yes. That's what baptism is. That's what circumcision is. That's what the shahada whispered into a newborn's ear is. The claim is made before the child can speak.

Teach your children your faith. But also teach them that other people believe different things, and that those people are not evil.

Teach them your scripture. But also teach them to read, to reason, to question, and to doubt. Including to doubt what you've taught them.

Teach them your tradition. But give them enough education to survive outside it. Give them math, science, language, history. Give them the tools to leave — even if you pray they never will.

* * *

I don't have children yet. I think about this more than you'd expect from someone who doesn't.

I think about it because of Sarah, and the flannel board, and the nightmares that followed her out of childhood and into her thirties. I think about it because of David, and the warmth in his voice when he talked about Friday nights, and the fact that warmth was real and I have nothing in my life that replicates it. I think about it because someday I might have a child, and I will have to decide what to do with their open, trusting, defenseless mind.

I know what I won't do. I won't tell them about hell. I won't install a surveillance system in their head and call it God's love. I won't make my love conditional on their belief.

But I don't know yet what I will do. I don't know how to give a child what David had — the roots, the belonging, the chain stretching back thousands of years — without the parts that broke Sarah. I don't know if you can separate them cleanly. Maybe you can't. Maybe the belonging and the coercion grow from the same root, and you can't have one without risking the other.

I think about my eight-year-old self. Lying in bed in the dark, staring at the ceiling, trying to believe hard enough that God wouldn't burn me. I didn't know the word "theology." I didn't know the word "doctrine." I knew that the adults I trusted had told me something terrifying, and I had no way to know if it was true, and the penalty for getting it wrong was everything.

I remember a specific Sunday. The teacher — a kind woman, grey-haired, glasses on a chain — had been talking about the parable of the sheep and the goats. She explained that at the end of the world, God would separate people. The sheep go to heaven. The goats go to the fire. She said it matter-of-factly, the way you'd explain a bus timetable. Then she asked: "Does anyone here want to make sure they're a sheep?"

Every hand went up. Mine too. I was terrified not to raise it.

I went home and sat in the garden. I remember the feeling precisely: I was trying to check whether I really believed or was only pretending, and I couldn't tell the difference, and the inability to tell the difference felt like proof that I was a goat. I cried. Not loudly. Quietly, so my parents wouldn't hear, because I was ashamed of being afraid and ashamed of not believing hard enough and ashamed that I couldn't make the fear stop. I was eight. I sat in the garden for an hour, testing my own thoughts for sincerity, like a child putting his hand over a flame to see if he could stand it.

That night I prayed for forty-five minutes. I know because I watched the clock. I thought if I prayed long enough, God would know I meant it. The bargain was clear, even at eight: perform belief convincingly enough, and the torture won't happen. It was the logic of a hostage. And I carried it with me for years — the low-grade dread, the nightly negotiations with an invisible judge, the sense that my own mind was the enemy because doubt lived in it and doubt was the thing that could damn me.

I'm not angry at those adults anymore. Most of them believed what they were teaching. Most of them were scared too — scared for me, scared for themselves, passing along the same fear that had been installed in them when they were small. That's how it works. That's the mechanism. Fear taught to the young becomes love, becomes duty, becomes the thing you do to your own children because you can't imagine not doing it, because the alternative — letting them face the universe without the story — feels like abandonment.

I understand that. I do. And I still think it's wrong.

Not the teaching. Not the tradition. Not the community or the candles or the songs. The fear. Specifically the fear. The idea that a five-year-old needs to be told about eternal fire in order to be saved. The idea that terror is a gift.

That's what was done to me. And I'm old enough now to call it what it was.

* * *

I keep thinking about Sarah, the woman from Tennessee. Thirty-six years old. Fourteen years out of the church. Sitting in a Nashville coffee shop, laughing at her own dark jokes, holding down a good job, living a full life. Still waking up at three in the morning with her heart pounding, absolutely certain, for ten terrible seconds, that she's going to burn forever.

She told me one more thing before I turned off the recorder. I'd asked her if she had any message for the people still in the church she'd left.

She thought about it for a long time.

"Don't tell the children," she said. "Tell them whatever you want about God. Tell them God loves them. Tell them the stories. But don't tell the children about hell. They can't carry it. I couldn't carry it." She paused. "I still can't."

We sat there for a while. The coffee shop was loud around us — the hiss of the espresso machine, someone's laptop playing music too loud through tinny speakers. Normal sounds. The sounds of a Tuesday.

I turned off the recorder and put it in my bag. She asked me if I wanted another coffee. I said yes. We talked about something else for a while. I don't remember what. I remember she laughed.

I think about that laugh sometimes. Not the nightmares. The laugh. The fact that she could still do it, after everything. That she'd built a life on the other side of what was done to her, and it had a cat in it, and bad jokes, and Tuesday coffee with a stranger.

And then I think about all the children who are hearing it right now. Tonight. In Sunday school classrooms and church basements and bedrooms where a well-meaning parent is reading from a book they believe is the word of God. Children who don't have the language to push back. Who will carry it for decades. Who may never fully put it down.

I don't know how to end this chapter. I don't have a closing argument that ties it all together. I have Sarah's voice in my head, asking people not to tell the children. And David's voice, warm with gratitude, talking about Friday nights. And the silence of a boy in Brooklyn who can recite the Talmud but can't read his own lease. And my own voice, eight years old, whispering prayers into a dark ceiling, trying to believe hard enough to be safe.

That's all I have. The voices. And the question that won't stop: what do we owe the children who can't yet speak for themselves?
