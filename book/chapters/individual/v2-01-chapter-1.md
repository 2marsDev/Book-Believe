## Chapter 1: One God, Three Stories

There are over four thousand religions in recorded human history.

Most of them are dead.

The ancient Greeks prayed to Zeus. Nobody does anymore. The Egyptians built the pyramids for Ra — his temples are tourist attractions now. The Norse worshipped Odin, the Romans sacrificed to Jupiter, the Aztecs cut out human hearts for Quetzalcoatl. All of them believed with total conviction. All of them are gone. We study them the way we study dead languages or collapsed empires — with curiosity, sometimes with admiration, and always with the comfortable certainty that their time has passed.

But three survived.

Three religions, born from the same patch of Middle Eastern desert, from the same ancestor — a man named Abraham who supposedly made a covenant with God around four thousand years ago — grew until they swallowed more than half the world. Christianity: two point four billion. Islam: one point nine billion. Judaism: sixteen million. Together, over four billion people. More than half of every human being alive.

They share a root. They share prophets. They share, in theory, the same God.

They do not share much else.

*     *     *

I started with Judaism, because it came first.

And because, of the three, it surprised me the most.

I began the way most people begin when they want to understand something — I bought books. A stack of them: introductions to Jewish theology, a popular history of the Israelites, a translation of selected Talmudic passages, and a paperback of the Torah with facing-page commentary that I found in a second-hand bookshop in Charing Cross Road. I read them in the evenings, in no particular order, with a notebook beside me. Most of what I knew about Judaism at that point could have fit on the back of an envelope: there was a covenant, there were commandments, there was a long history of persecution, and the food had rules. I knew almost nothing. I had the comfortable ignorance of someone who had never needed to know.

What I found was nothing like what I expected.

I expected rules. I found arguments. The Talmud — the vast body of Jewish commentary and law — isn't a rulebook. It's a record of disagreement. Rabbis debating across centuries, challenging each other, preserving the losing argument alongside the winning one. There's a concept: machloket l'shem shamayim. A disagreement for the sake of heaven. The idea that arguing about truth is itself a form of worship.

I read a passage one evening that stopped me cold. Two schools of rabbinical thought — the House of Hillel and the House of Shammai — had been arguing about a point of law for over two years. The Talmud records that a voice came from heaven and declared that both opinions were "the words of the living God," but the law would follow Hillel. Both were right. Both were the voice of God. But only one could be the practical rule. What struck me wasn't the ruling. It was the preservation. They wrote down the losing side. They kept it. Because the argument itself was sacred — not just the answer, but the wrestling. I'd never encountered a tradition that treated disagreement as something to honour rather than suppress.

That stopped me. Because in my experience, religion is the place where questions go to die. "Have faith." "Don't question God." "It's a mystery." But here was a tradition that had built questioning into its operating system. Not as rebellion. As devotion.

I spent an afternoon in a synagogue library in north London, pulling volumes of the Talmud off shelves. The books were enormous, dense, typeset in a way that looked like a conversation rendered in architecture — a central text surrounded by layers of commentary, each generation adding its voice to the page, arguing with the dead and the living simultaneously. The librarian, a soft-spoken man with a greying beard, watched me for a while and then asked if I needed help. I told him I was trying to understand how the whole thing worked. He smiled. "You could spend a lifetime," he said. "That's rather the point."

Judaism also doesn't obsess over hell the way Christianity and Islam do. The concept barely exists in traditional Jewish thought. There's no lake of fire, no scalding water, no elaborate architecture of eternal punishment. The afterlife is vague and deliberately so — a place called Olam Ha-Ba, the World to Come, which is affirmed but not described in any detail. The emphasis is on this life, this world, this set of obligations. And Judaism doesn't demand the whole world convert. The Talmud says righteous people of all nations share in the world to come. For a religion, that's remarkably uninterested in forcing itself on others.

There's another side to this, though. Judaism carries a concept of chosenness — the belief that God selected the Jewish people for a unique covenant and a unique responsibility. It's not supremacy, exactly. Rabbis have spent centuries clarifying that chosen means obligated, not privileged. The idea, as I came to understand it, is that being chosen is less like winning a prize and more like being handed a heavier bag to carry. You have more rules. More obligations. A stricter set of expectations. You are chosen not for reward but for service.

And yet. The idea that one group of people has a special relationship with the creator of the universe — that is a claim worth sitting with. It creates a tension that runs through the whole tradition: a universalist strand that says God cares about all humanity, sitting alongside a particularist strand that says one people, one bloodline, one covenant. Jewish thinkers have wrestled with that tension for millennia. They haven't resolved it. Perhaps the wrestling is the point.

And Judaism is unusually hard to join. Traditionally, a rabbi is supposed to turn away a potential convert three times. Three times you come to the door, three times you're told to go home. Only if you keep coming back do they begin to take you seriously. There's a reason for this — Judaism sees conversion as a serious, life-altering commitment, not a casual decision. But it also means the faith has an ethnic and tribal dimension that Christianity and Islam don't share. You can become Jewish, but the path is deliberately narrow. Most Jews are born Jewish. The community is a family as much as a congregation, and families are harder to join than clubs.

None of this is a criticism. Not yet. I was genuinely fascinated. A religion that argues with itself, doesn't threaten outsiders with hell, actively discourages people from joining, and has somehow survived three thousand years of persecution, exile, and attempted annihilation. The Temple destroyed — twice. The people scattered across every continent. The pogroms. The ghettos. The Holocaust. Six million murdered for the crime of existing. And still here. Still arguing. Still lighting candles on Friday night. Still reading from the same scroll in the same ancient language, unbroken.

That's not just a faith. That's an achievement. And I hadn't even begun to understand it properly. But I knew enough to know that my assumptions — that religion was simple, that faith was for people who didn't think — were wrong. At least here. At least in this tradition. Whatever Judaism was, it wasn't simple.

*     *     *

Christianity is the largest religion on Earth, and also the most internally contradictory institution I've ever encountered.

I sat in a church for the first time — properly sat, not at a wedding or a funeral but for a regular Sunday service — in a small Anglican parish in the Midlands. It was a cold morning. The building was old, stone, with that particular smell that old English churches have — a mixture of damp, wood polish, old paper, and something faintly sweet that might have been incense from centuries ago, still clinging to the walls. The pews were hard. The kneelers were thin. The congregation was mostly elderly, maybe forty people in a space built for two hundred. There was a draught from somewhere, and I sat in the back row with my coat on, feeling conspicuous and trying not to.

The vicar was a woman, which surprised me for reasons I hadn't examined. She read from the Gospel of Matthew. The Sermon on the Mount. I'd heard of it, of course — everyone has — but I'd never actually listened to it read aloud in a place where people believed it was the word of God.

"Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven. Blessed are those who mourn, for they shall be comforted. Blessed are the meek, for they shall inherit the earth."

The words hung in the cold air. I let them.

There was something in the rhythm of it, in the repetition of "blessed are," that worked on you whether you believed it or not. Blessed are the merciful. Blessed are the peacemakers. Blessed are those who are persecuted for righteousness' sake. It was a manifesto for the powerless. A declaration that the losers — the poor, the grieving, the gentle, the persecuted — were the ones God loved most. In a world that rewards strength and punishes weakness, here was a text that turned the entire hierarchy upside down.

And then: "Love your enemies. Bless those who curse you. Do good to those who hate you." I wrote that line down in my notebook. I underlined it. I still think about it. If you could actually do it — actually love the people who were trying to destroy you — you would be something close to superhuman. Nobody can do it. Almost nobody even tries. But as an aspiration, as a direction to face, it's extraordinary. Two thousand years later, nobody has improved on it.

I sat in that church for the full service. The hymns were old and the singing was thin — forty voices where there should have been hundreds — but there was something in the collective effort of it, the willingness to sing badly together, that moved me. The vicar gave a short sermon about forgiveness. A woman read a prayer for the sick. Names were listed — people I'd never met, never would meet — and the congregation bowed their heads and held them in silence. I didn't pray. I didn't know how. But I sat in that silence and felt something I hadn't expected: inclusion. Nobody asked if I believed. Nobody checked my credentials. They just made room.

After the service, a man in a tweed jacket shook my hand and asked if I was new to the area. A woman offered me a biscuit and a cup of tea. They were kind, and their kindness was uncomplicated, and I drove home thinking about the gap between the institution and the people inside it. The institution had centuries of blood on its hands. The people in the pews had biscuits and tea.

That gap is the story of Christianity. It's the thing you can't resolve, the contradiction that sits at the centre of the whole enterprise. Because the same tradition that produced the Sermon on the Mount also produced the Crusades. Armies marching under the banner of Christ, slaughtering Muslims and Jews in Jerusalem until the blood, according to one chronicler, ran ankle-deep through the streets. The Inquisition, where men of God tortured confessions out of suspected heretics and burned them alive in public squares, and called it an act of mercy because it saved the soul even as it destroyed the body. The transatlantic slave trade, blessed by popes and bishops who found in scripture a convenient justification for treating human beings as property. And a network of residential schools across Canada, Australia, and the United States where indigenous children were beaten for speaking their own language — their culture systematically erased in the name of Christian civilisation. The same church that runs hospitals across the developing world systematically covered up the rape of children across the developed one.

How do you hold both of those in your head at the same time?

Believers manage it, somehow. Because what Christianity offers — at its best, in its purest form — is extraordinary. It offers forgiveness. Absolute, unconditional, no-matter-what-you've-done forgiveness. You're broken? Come as you are. You've failed? God loves you anyway. In a world drowning in shame and self-hatred, that message has pulled people back from ledges. Literally.

It offers hope in the face of death. Not philosophical comfort. A specific, personal promise: you will see your mother again. Your suffering is not meaningless. This isn't all there is.

If you've ever watched someone you love die, you understand why people want that to be true. You don't have to believe it to see why it matters.

And then there's the story at the center. A God who doesn't sit on a distant throne issuing commands. A God who comes down. Who becomes human. Who suffers. Who is betrayed by a friend, abandoned by his followers, tortured by the state, and dies in public agony — not because he has to, but because he loves the species that's killing him. Whatever you think about the theology, as a narrative, it's devastating. Two thousand years later, it still moves people to tears.

I found myself sitting in the back of churches — not just that first one, but others, over the weeks that followed. A cathedral in York where the organ filled the nave with a sound so deep I could feel it in my chest. A Baptist chapel in south London where the singing was extraordinary, full-throated, joyful, the kind of music that makes your body want to move whether your mind agrees or not. A tiny Methodist church in a Welsh village where the congregation was nine people and a dog, and the minister preached to them as if they were thousands.

In every one, I felt the same thing: the pull. Not belief. Not even the desire to believe. Just the recognition that something was happening in those rooms that I didn't have access to. A frequency I couldn't tune into. The collective hush of people reaching for the same invisible thing, and some of them — you could see it in their faces, in the way their eyes closed during prayer, in the way they held their hands — actually finding it. There is something there. Something real, even if its source isn't what believers think it is.

What if it's beautiful and not true?

That question followed me everywhere. I didn't try to answer it yet.

*     *     *

Then there's Islam.

And here I have to be honest about something. In the modern West, the moment you say anything about Islam that isn't flattering, a wall goes up. You get sorted into a box labeled Islamophobe and the conversation is over. I'm going to say things about Islam in this book that will make some people uncomfortable. I'm also going to say things about Christianity and Judaism that will make different people uncomfortable. That's the arrangement. Everyone gets the same treatment. No one gets a shield.

So here's what I found when I looked at Islam with open eyes.

The theology is clean. One God, indivisible, no mystery you have to accept without understanding. No Trinity, no divine son, no incarnation. Just God. Alone, complete, beyond comparison. The Arabic word is tawhid — the absolute oneness of God. It's the foundation of everything in Islam, and it has a clarity that Christianity's theology, with its three-persons-in-one-being formulation, simply doesn't. You don't have to tie yourself in philosophical knots to grasp it. God is one. Full stop. There's an elegance to that, a kind of theological minimalism that appeals to the same part of you that admires a clean equation.

And from that single principle, a complete system unfolds. A clear set of instructions: declare your faith, pray five times a day, give to the poor, fast during Ramadan, make the pilgrimage if you can. It's elegant. If you're designing a religion from scratch, you'd be hard pressed to make the core architecture simpler.

And the history — the early history — is genuinely revolutionary. Muhammad's first wife, Khadijah, was his employer. A successful businesswoman who proposed to him, not the other way around. Early Islamic law gave women the right to own property, inherit wealth, and initiate divorce. In the seventh century. Thirteen hundred years before most Western women had any of those rights. The Quran says, explicitly, "there is no compulsion in religion."

The call to prayer is one of the most beautiful sounds I've ever heard. I stood in a courtyard in Istanbul once, late afternoon, the light going gold on the stone, and listened to the adhan echoing off the walls of the Blue Mosque. It started from a single minaret — a human voice, unaccompanied, rising into the air with a purity that made you hold your breath. Then a second voice joined from another mosque across the water, slightly delayed, slightly different in pitch, and then a third and a fourth, until the whole city was wrapped in overlapping waves of sound, each muezzin calling in his own voice but speaking the same words: God is the greatest. I bear witness that there is no god but God. Come to prayer. Come to success.

I'm not a believer. I stood there knowing that. And I still felt the hair rise on my arms. There was something in the call that went past the intellect and reached something older — some part of the brain that responds to a human voice reaching into empty space and declaring that meaning exists. I understood — in a way I hadn't before — why people build their days around this. Five times a day, whatever you're doing, you stop. You wash. You face Mecca. You bow. There's a rhythm to it that secular life doesn't offer. A pause button that forces you to remember something bigger than your inbox.

And there's the concept of ummah — the global community of believers. When a Muslim prays, they face Mecca. Every Muslim, everywhere, five times a day, turning toward the same point on the Earth's surface. A farmer in Indonesia, a banker in London, a student in Lagos, a grandmother in Sarajevo — all facing the same direction, all saying the same words, all part of one body. One point nine billion people connected by a shared orientation, a shared language of prayer, a shared sense of belonging that cuts across every border, every language, every class distinction. I'd never encountered anything in secular life that came close to that scale of solidarity. Nations don't achieve it. Political movements don't achieve it. The ummah is, in principle, the largest community of shared practice on Earth. That's not nothing.

I found all of this genuinely impressive. A faith that conquered half the known world not just by sword — the popular narrative — but by offering something the existing religions didn't: radical simplicity, social mobility, and a direct line to God with no priesthood standing in between.

I wanted to understand what happened between that beginning and the present. How a religion founded by a woman's employee, built on a verse that says there shall be no compulsion, became something else in so many places. I wasn't ready to answer that question. But I knew I wanted to follow it.

I walked away from Istanbul with something I hadn't expected: admiration. Not belief — never belief — but real, honest admiration for a tradition that had taken a single, clean idea and built from it a civilisation. The mathematics, the architecture, the poetry, the philosophy — the Islamic golden age, when Baghdad was the intellectual capital of the world and Christian Europe was still burning people for reading the wrong books. All of it flowing from that single principle: there is no god but God. One idea. One direction. One prayer, spoken a billion times a day across every continent, connecting strangers to strangers in a web of shared devotion that no nation, no empire, no political project has ever matched.

Whatever had gone wrong — and something had gone wrong, that much was obvious — had gone wrong despite that foundation, not because of it. Or at least that was how it looked to me then. I would revise that thought later. But not yet.

*     *     *

Three religions. One God. Four billion people.

I went in knowing almost nothing. I came out with more questions than answers — about the faiths themselves, about the people who follow them, and about something I hadn't anticipated at all.

About what belief does to a person. About how deeply it reaches into the machinery of a life. About what it gives, and what it asks in return.

I kept looking.
