# Beyond Belief — Bibliography and Source Notes

This file provides sources for readers who wish to verify the factual claims made in the book. It is organised by chapter. Where claims are drawn from widely reported events, that is noted rather than a single URL cited. Where scholarly works are referenced, full publication details are given.

## Prologue

**Savita Halappanavar**
Savita Halappanavar died at University Hospital Galway in October 2012 after being denied a termination during a miscarriage. Her death was a catalyst in the debate over Ireland's Eighth Amendment to the Constitution, which effectively banned abortion. The case was covered extensively by the *Irish Times* and *The Guardian*, among others. Ireland repealed the Eighth Amendment by referendum in 2018.

**Intelligent Design in Texas**
The case *Kitzmiller v. Dover Area School District* (US District Court, Middle District of Pennsylvania, 2004–2005) ruled that teaching intelligent design in public school science classes violates the Establishment Clause of the First Amendment. In Texas, the State Board of Education debated "strengths and weaknesses" language regarding evolution in 2009, a phrase critics argued was designed to introduce creationist doubt into science curricula.

## Chapter 4: The Receipt

**Archaeological evidence and the Hebrew Bible**
- Israel Finkelstein and Neil Asher Silberman, *The Bible Unearthed: Archaeology's New Vision of Ancient Israel and the Origin of Its Sacred Texts* (Free Press, 2001)
- William Dever, *Who Were the Early Israelites and Where Did They Come From?* (Eerdmans, 2003)
- Ze'ev Herzog, "Deconstructing the Walls of Jericho," *Ha'aretz*, 29 October 1999 — a summary by an Israeli archaeologist of the scholarly consensus that the Exodus narrative lacks archaeological support

**Roman sources on Jesus and early Christians**
- Tacitus, *Annals* 15.44 — written c. 116 CE, refers to "Christus" being executed under Pontius Pilate during the reign of Tiberius
- Josephus, *Antiquities of the Jews* 18.3.3 — the *Testimonium Flavianum*, debated by scholars; most consider the current text a partial interpolation but believe an original reference existed

**Philosophical defences of religious belief**
- Alvin Plantinga, *Warranted Christian Belief* (Oxford University Press, 2000) — develops the concept of "proper basicality" as a justification for belief without classical evidence
- William Lane Craig, the Kalam Cosmological Argument — widely published in academic philosophy of religion; see Craig, *The Kalam Cosmological Argument* (Macmillan, 1979)

## Chapter 5: The Books Don't Agree

**Documentary Hypothesis**
The theory that the Torah was compiled from four distinct source texts (J, E, D, P) was systematically developed by Julius Wellhausen in *Prolegomena to the History of Israel* (1878). It remains the dominant scholarly framework for understanding the composition of the Pentateuch, though details continue to be debated.

**New Testament manuscript transmission**
- Bart Ehrman, *Misquoting Jesus: The Story Behind Who Changed the Bible and Why* (HarperOne, 2005) — accessible account of textual variants in New Testament manuscripts, written by a former evangelical who became a leading New Testament scholar
- The earliest substantially complete New Testament manuscript is Codex Sinaiticus, dated to approximately 330–360 CE, now held at the British Library
- Mark 16:9–20 (the "longer ending" of Mark) is absent from the earliest and most reliable manuscripts, including Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus; most modern Bible translations note this in a footnote

## Chapter 6: Older Gods

**The Epic of Gilgamesh**
The Gilgamesh flood narrative was identified by George Smith at the British Museum in 1872. Smith's discovery — that a pre-biblical flood story existed in ancient Mesopotamian literature — caused a sensation when announced to the Society of Biblical Archaeology. The standard modern translation is: Andrew George (trans.), *The Epic of Gilgamesh* (Penguin Classics, 2003). The oldest Gilgamesh poems date to approximately 2100 BCE.

**Sargon of Akkad**
The birth narrative of Sargon of Akkad (c. 2300 BCE) describes the king being placed in a reed basket and set adrift on a river by his mother — a narrative structure with close parallels to the Moses story in Exodus. The text is held in the British Museum and the Vorderasiatisches Museum, Berlin.

**The Instruction of Amenemope and Proverbs 22**
The Instruction of Amenemope is an ancient Egyptian wisdom text, likely composed between 1300 and 1000 BCE. E.A. Wallis Budge of the British Museum published it in 1923. Scholars have noted close parallels between sections of Amenemope and Proverbs 22:17–24:22 in the Hebrew Bible, suggesting literary borrowing or a shared source tradition.

**Early Christian awareness of parallel narratives**
- Justin Martyr, *First Apology*, c. 155 CE — Justin acknowledges pagan parallels to the Jesus story (virgin births, dying-and-rising gods) and argues they were demonic imitations planted in advance
- Celsus, *On the True Doctrine*, c. 177 CE — a Greek philosopher's critique of Christianity, now known only through Origen's rebuttal in *Contra Celsum* (c. 248 CE), which quotes Celsus extensively

## Chapter 7: The God-Shaped Hole

**Cognitive science of religion**
- Pascal Boyer, *Religion Explained: The Evolutionary Origins of Religious Thought* (Basic Books, 2001)
- Justin Barrett, *Why Would Anyone Believe in God?* (AltaMira Press, 2004)
- Barrett's concept of the Hyperactive Agency Detection Device (HADD) was introduced in his 2000 paper "Exploring the Natural Foundations of Religion," *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* 4(1). The argument is that human brains evolved to over-attribute agency in the environment, and this tendency underlies the universal human intuition that events have intentional causes — including divine ones.

**Terror Management Theory**
Developed by Jeff Greenberg, Sheldon Solomon, and Tom Pyszczynski, drawing on Ernest Becker's *The Denial of Death* (Free Press, 1973). The core claim is that awareness of mortality is a primary driver of cultural and religious behaviour, and that belief systems which promise transcendence or meaning reduce death anxiety. The theory has generated extensive empirical research since the 1980s.

## The Inner Life

**Primary sources on mystical experience**
- William James, *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (Longmans, Green & Co., 1902) — the foundational text of the psychology of religion; James argued that mystical experiences are psychologically genuine and practically transformative, whatever their metaphysical status
- Teresa of Ávila, *The Interior Castle* (1577) — her systematic account of the stages of contemplative prayer
- John of the Cross, *Dark Night of the Soul* (c. 1578–1579) — his account of the spiritual desolation that can accompany deep contemplative practice
- Thomas Merton, *The Seven Storey Mountain* (Harcourt, 1948) — memoir of his conversion and entry into Trappist monastic life
- Simone Weil, *Waiting for God* (Gallimard, 1951; English translation, Putnam, 1951) — essays and letters on her experience of God, written outside any institutional religious affiliation
- Rumi (Jalal ad-Din Muhammad Rumi), *Masnavi* (13th century) — the major Sufi devotional poem; many English translations available, including by Coleman Barks and by Jawid Mojaddedi (Oxford World's Classics)

**Neuroscience of religious experience**
Andrew Newberg, Eugene d'Aquili, and Vince Rause, *Why God Won't Go Away: Brain Science and the Biology of Belief* (Ballantine Books, 2001) — reports on neuroimaging studies of meditating Tibetan Buddhist practitioners and praying Franciscan nuns, documenting consistent patterns of brain activity during reported experiences of unity or transcendence. Newberg's work founded the field of neurotheology.

## Chapter 8: The Beautiful Answer

**Religion and health outcomes**
- Dan Buettner, *The Blue Zones: Lessons for Living Longer from the People Who've Lived the Longest* (National Geographic, 2008). The Loma Linda, California community of Seventh-day Adventists is among Buettner's five "Blue Zones" — areas of unusual longevity. Adventist health studies have been conducted by Loma Linda University since the 1950s.
- STEP Study (Study of the Therapeutic Effects of Intercessory Prayer): Herbert Benson et al., "Study of the Therapeutic Effects of Intercessory Prayer (STEP) in Cardiac Bypass Patients," *American Heart Journal* 151(4), April 2006. The study found that intercessory prayer had no significant effect on recovery, and that patients who knew they were being prayed for had a slightly higher rate of complications.

**Alcoholics Anonymous**
Alcoholics Anonymous was co-founded by Bill Wilson and Dr. Robert Smith ("Dr. Bob") in Akron, Ohio in 1935. The organisation's Twelve Steps incorporate a spiritual framework centred on surrender to a "Higher Power." It remains one of the most widespread recovery networks in the world.

## Chapter 9: The Horrifying Answer

**Religion and attitudes to the afterlife**
Pew Research Center, *Religious Landscape Study* (2023–24): approximately 72% of Americans reported belief in hell. Pew Research surveys are publicly available at pewresearch.org.

**Sexual abuse in the Catholic Church**
The Independent Commission on Sexual Abuse in the Church (CIASE) in France published its report in October 2021. It estimated that approximately 330,000 people had been victims of sexual abuse within the French Catholic Church since 1950, including abuse by lay staff and members of religious organisations associated with the Church. The report was commissioned by the French Bishops' Conference.

**Mahsa Amini and the 2022 Iran protests**
Mahsa (Jina) Amini, a 22-year-old Kurdish-Iranian woman, died on 16 September 2022 in Tehran, following her arrest by the Guidance Patrol (morality police) for allegedly wearing her hijab improperly. Her death triggered nationwide protests under the slogan "Woman, Life, Freedom." The Human Rights Activists News Agency (HRANA) documented approximately 551 protesters killed and around 68 children among the dead. The figures are contested by the Iranian government; independent verification is difficult given restrictions on journalists.

**David Kato**
David Kato, a Ugandan LGBTQ rights activist and teacher, was murdered at his home on 26 January 2011. His name had appeared on the front page of a Ugandan tabloid, *Rolling Stone*, which published photographs of gay Ugandans under the headline "Hang Them." He had successfully sued the paper. His murder drew international condemnation.

**Apostasy and the death penalty**
As of 2024, apostasy (leaving Islam) is punishable by death under the laws of at least 12 countries, including Iran, Saudi Arabia, Afghanistan, and Qatar. Sudan removed its apostasy law in 2020 as part of post-Bashir legal reforms. Data is maintained by organisations including the US Commission on International Religious Freedom (USCIRF) and Humanists International.

## The Violence

**The First Crusade**
Pope Urban II called for the First Crusade at the Council of Clermont in November 1095. Jerusalem was taken by Crusader forces in July 1099. Primary and secondary sources are extensive; a standard scholarly account is Thomas Asbridge, *The Crusades: The Authoritative History of the War for the Holy Land* (Ecco, 2010).

**The Spanish Inquisition**
The Spanish Inquisition was established by papal bull in 1478 under Ferdinand II of Aragon and Isabella I of Castile. The most thorough historical account remains Henry Charles Lea, *A History of the Inquisition of Spain* (4 vols., Macmillan, 1906–1907). Modern scholars estimate between 3,000 and 5,000 executions over the Inquisition's history; earlier popular figures in the tens of thousands are not supported by archival evidence.

**The Thirty Years War**
The Thirty Years War (1618–1648) was fought primarily in the Holy Roman Empire and drew in most of the major European powers. It began as a conflict between Catholic and Protestant states. Estimates of total deaths — from combat, famine, and plague — vary widely but commonly reach 8 million or more. A standard recent history is Peter Wilson, *The Thirty Years War: Europe's Tragedy* (Harvard University Press, 2009).

**The Troubles**
The Troubles in Northern Ireland lasted from approximately 1968 to 1998. Approximately 3,500 people were killed. Data is maintained by the Conflict Archive on the INternet (CAIN) project at Ulster University, available at cain.ulster.ac.uk.

**Baruch Goldstein**
On 25 February 1994, Baruch Goldstein, an Israeli-American physician and settler, opened fire on Palestinian Muslim worshippers at the Cave of the Patriarchs (Ibrahimi Mosque) in Hebron during Ramadan, killing 29 people and wounding more than 100. He was killed by survivors.

**Yigal Amir and the assassination of Yitzhak Rabin**
Yitzhak Rabin, Prime Minister of Israel, was assassinated on 4 November 1995 in Tel Aviv. His killer, Yigal Amir, was a Jewish extremist who believed the Oslo Accords were a betrayal of the Jewish people. Amir cited a rabbinical ruling of *din rodef* (the law of the pursuer) as justification. Rabbi Dov Lior of Kiryat Arba was among those associated with rulings that influenced this reasoning; the matter remains contested.

**Osama bin Laden and al-Qaeda**
Osama bin Laden issued two major public declarations against the United States: a 1996 "Declaration of War Against the Americans Occupying the Land of the Two Holy Places" and a 1998 fatwa (issued jointly with Ayman al-Zawahiri and others) calling for attacks on Americans and Jews worldwide. Both documents are available in translation through the Combating Terrorism Center at West Point.

**ISIS**
The Islamic State (ISIS/ISIL/Daesh) proclaimed a caliphate in June 2014 under Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi. A detailed analysis of the group's ideological foundations is Cole Bunzel, "From Paper State to Caliphate: The Ideology of the Islamic State," The Brookings Institution, March 2015.

**Dar al-harb / Dar al-Islam**
The classical Islamic jurisprudential division of the world into the "house of Islam" (dar al-Islam, territory under Muslim rule) and the "house of war" (dar al-harb, territory not yet under Muslim rule) is discussed in Ibn Khaldun, *Muqaddimah* (14th century). Modern Islamic scholars debate the applicability and authority of this framework; it does not appear in the Quran or Hadith directly.

## Chapter 10: The Money Machine

**Religious giving in the United States**
Giving USA 2022 Report (published by the Giving USA Foundation, researched and written by the Indiana University Lilly Family School of Philanthropy): Americans gave approximately $135 billion to religious organisations in 2021.

**Vatican finances**
The Vatican's London property investment scandal involves a property in Chelsea purchased through the Vatican's Secretariat of State. Cardinal Angelo Becciu was convicted of embezzlement related to these investments in 2023. The *Financial Times* reported extensively on the affair from 2020 to 2022, estimating losses of approximately $150 million.

**Prosperity gospel ministers**
The reported net worth of figures such as Creflo Dollar (World Changers Church International) and Kenneth Copeland (Kenneth Copeland Ministries, reported at $300 million or more) has been covered by *Forbes*, the *Washington Post*, and multiple investigative outlets. Exact figures are disputed and difficult to verify given the opacity of church finances, but the lifestyle wealth of these ministers is not in dispute.

**Tax status of religious organisations**
The Johnson Amendment (1954) prohibits tax-exempt organisations, including churches, from endorsing political candidates. Religious organisations are exempt from federal income tax under Section 501(c)(3) of the US tax code. The constitutional basis for and limits on this exemption are defined by the Establishment Clause of the First Amendment.

## Chapter 11: The Children

**Kitzmiller v. Dover**
*Kitzmiller et al. v. Dover Area School District* (US District Court, Middle District of Pennsylvania, Case No. 04cv2688, decided 20 December 2005). Judge John E. Jones III ruled that teaching intelligent design as an alternative to evolution in public school science classes is unconstitutional under the Establishment Clause. The full ruling is publicly available.

**Religion and child wellbeing**
Tyler J. VanderWeele and Ying Chen, "Associations of Religious Upbringing with Subsequent Health and Well-Being from Adolescence to Early Adulthood," *American Journal of Epidemiology* 187(11), 2018. The study found that religious upbringing was associated with higher life satisfaction, positive affect, volunteering, and lower rates of smoking and drug use in adulthood — alongside higher rates of conservative sexual attitudes.

**Conversion therapy**
The Williams Institute at the UCLA School of Law has published estimates of the number of LGBTQ adults in the United States who have undergone conversion therapy. Reports are available at williamsinstitute.law.ucla.edu.

Caitlin Ryan et al., "Family Rejection as a Predictor of Negative Health Outcomes in White and Latino Lesbian, Gay, and Bisexual Young Adults," *Pediatrics* 123(1), January 2009 — found that family rejection based on sexual orientation or gender identity was associated with significantly higher rates of depression, suicidal ideation, and risk behaviours.

**The Talibé system**
Human Rights Watch, "Senegal: Children Forced to Beg — Abuses Against Talibés in Dakar" (2019). The report documents conditions in some Quranic boarding schools (daaras) where children called talibés are required to beg on the streets to meet daily quotas set by their teachers (marabouts).

## The Women

**Amina Wadud and mixed-gender prayer**
On 18 March 2005, Amina Wadud led a mixed-gender Friday prayer at Synod House, Cathedral of St. John the Divine, New York City — one of the most publicly prominent examples of a woman leading a Muslim congregation in prayer. The event was reported by the BBC, *The Guardian*, and international press.

**Women of the Wall**
Women of the Wall is a pluralist Jewish women's organisation founded in 1988, which campaigns for the right of women to pray aloud, wear prayer shawls, and read from the Torah at the Western Wall in Jerusalem. The group has been the subject of multiple legal proceedings in the Supreme Court of Israel. The situation remains unresolved as of the time of writing.

**Women's ordination in Christian denominations**
- The Church of England ordained its first women priests in 1994; women bishops followed in 2015.
- The United Methodist Church has ordained women since 1956.
- The Roman Catholic Church's position against women's ordination was restated definitively in Pope John Paul II, *Ordinatio Sacerdotalis* (Apostolic Letter, 1994), which declared the matter "closed to all debate."

**Women in Islamic scholarship and history**
Ruth Roded, *Women in Islamic Biographical Collections: From Ibn Sa'd to Who's Who* (Lynne Rienner Publishers, 1994) — examines the historical record of women in Islamic scholarly tradition.

Aisha bint Abi Bakr, wife of the Prophet Muhammad and one of the most prominent transmitters of hadith, led an armed force against Ali ibn Abi Talib at the Battle of the Camel in 656 CE — one of the earliest and most significant instances of a woman exercising direct political and military leadership in Islamic history.

## Chapter 14: What Comes Next

**Sam Bankman-Fried**
Sam Bankman-Fried (born 5 March 1992), founder of the cryptocurrency exchange FTX, was arrested on 12 December 2022 in Nassau, the Bahamas, and extradited to the United States. FTX filed for bankruptcy in November 2022. Bankman-Fried had been a prominent advocate of effective altruism, a secular philanthropic philosophy. He was convicted of fraud in 2023.

**Secularisation in Britain**
British Social Attitudes Survey 2023: approximately 53% of people in Great Britain described themselves as having no religion. The survey is conducted annually by NatCen Social Research and is publicly available at natcen.ac.uk.

**QAnon and conspiratorial belief**
The Public Religion Research Institute (PRRI) has published polling on QAnon belief in the United States. Reports are available at prri.org.

**Sunday Assembly**
Sunday Assembly — a secular "congregation" offering community, music, and reflection without religious content — was founded by comedians Sanderson Jones and Pippa Evans in London in January 2013. It has since expanded to cities across the UK, US, and Australia.
