01

The Nature of God

All three faiths affirm one God — yet their understanding of God's nature represents the single most significant theological divergence among the traditions.

✝

Christianity — The Trinity

One God existing in three co-equal, co-eternal persons: Father, Son (Jesus), and Holy Spirit. Emphatically not polytheism — one divine essence (ousia) shared by three persons (hypostases).

Formalized

Council of Nicaea (325 CE) affirmed Christ's divinity. Full Trinitarian formula completed at Constantinople (381 CE) under the Cappadocian Fathers.

Key Scripture

Matthew 28:19 — "Baptizing them in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit."

☽

Islam — Tawhid

Absolute, uncompromising oneness of God (Allah). Indivisible, without partner, without son, utterly transcendent. The 99 Names describe facets of one indivisible divine reality.

Gravest Sin

Shirk — associating partners with God. The only sin Allah may not forgive if one dies unrepentant (Quran 4:48).

Key Scripture

Surah 112: "He is Allah, the One. Allah, the Absolute. He neither begets nor was He begotten."

✡

Judaism — YHWH is One

Absolute oneness expressed in the Shema: "Hear O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is One" (Deuteronomy 6:4). God is incorporeal, indivisible, without physical form.

Relationship

Covenantal partnership — God chose Israel; Israel accepted the commandments. Focus on communal responsibility and ethical conduct.

Gravest Sin

Avodah zarah — idolatry, worship of anything other than God.

Denominations & Diversity

None of these faiths is monolithic. Each has fractured into diverse branches — through theology, politics, or both.

⛪

Christianity

Roman Catholicism (~1.3B)

Papal authority, sacraments, Marian devotion. Largest single denomination.

Protestantism (~900M)

Born from 16th-century Reformation. Lutherans, Baptists, Methodists, Pentecostals, and hundreds more.

Eastern Orthodoxy (~220M)

Autocephalous churches (Greek, Russian, Serbian). Rich liturgical tradition, icons.

Oriental Orthodoxy (~60M)

Coptic, Ethiopian, Armenian, Syriac. Split after Council of Chalcedon (451 CE).

🕌

Islam

Sunni (~85–90%)

Follows Quran, Hadith, community consensus. Four legal schools: Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali.

Shia (~10–13%)

Leadership through Ali's line. Subdivisions: Twelvers (Iran/Iraq), Ismailis, Zaidis.

Sufism

Mystical dimension (Sunni or Shia). Inner purification, love of God, dhikr, poetry.

Ibadi (~0.5%)

Neither Sunni nor Shia. Predominant in Oman. Known for moderate theology.

🕍

Judaism

Orthodox

Strict halakha. Modern Orthodox (engaged with world) and Haredi/Ultra-Orthodox (more insular).

Reform / Liberal

Ethics over ritual law. Ordains women and LGBTQ+ rabbis. Largest US movement.

Conservative / Masorti

Middle ground: respects halakha but allows historical evolution. Strong in North America.

Reconstructionist & Renewal

Smaller. Judaism as evolving civilization. Integrates mysticism and meditation.

Historical Relations

Centuries of coexistence, conflict, alliance, and mutual influence — the three faiths have shaped each other profoundly.

c. 2000 BCE

Abraham's Covenant

God establishes a covenant with Abraham — the shared root of all three faiths.

c. 1300 BCE

Moses & the Torah

Torah received at Sinai — foundational law of Judaism. The Exodus becomes the defining narrative.

c. 4 BCE – 30 CE

Jesus of Nazareth

Ministry, crucifixion, and (per Christian belief) resurrection give rise to Christianity.

610 CE

Revelation to Muhammad

First Quranic revelation in the cave of Hira — the beginning of Islam.

711–1492 CE

Al-Andalus — Golden Age

Muslim-ruled Spain: high point of interfaith intellectual exchange. Maimonides flourished under Muslim rule.

1096–1291 CE

The Crusades

Two centuries of religious warfare. Entrenched mutual hostility; Jewish communities suffered pogroms along Crusader routes.

1939–1945

The Holocaust

Six million Jews murdered. Centuries of Christian anti-Judaism created cultural preconditions for genocide.

1965

Nostra Aetate

Vatican II declaration repudiated collective Jewish guilt for the crucifixion. Transformed Catholic-Jewish relations.

Ottoman Millet System (14th–20th Century)▼
The Ottoman Empire organized religious minorities into semi-autonomous "millets" with their own courts, schools, and leaders. Jews expelled from Spain in 1492 were welcomed by Sultan Bayezid II. Non-Muslims paid the jizya tax but had a framework for pluralistic governance remarkable for its era. Istanbul, Thessaloniki, and other Ottoman cities became major centers of Jewish life.
A Common Word (2007) — Modern Interfaith Dialogue▼
An open letter signed by 138 Muslim scholars addressed to Christian leaders, emphasizing love of God and love of neighbor as common ground. Vatican II (1962–65), the Amman Message (2004), and grassroots interfaith organizations have created unprecedented dialogue. While political conflicts continue, theological conversation has never been more active.

Myths vs Facts

Popular misconceptions debunked — every fact verified against primary sources and scholarly consensus.

Topic❌ Common Myth✅ Documented Fact
Same God?Myth
They worship completely different gods.		Fact
All three trace worship to the God of Abraham. While they differ on God's nature, theologians acknowledge a shared monotheistic root. Arabic-speaking Christians also call God "Allah."
Always at War?Myth
Fundamentally incompatible and always at war.		Fact
History shows extended periods of coexistence and exchange (Al-Andalus, Ottoman millets, modern dialogue). Conflict is driven by politics and identity as much as theology.
ViolenceMyth
Some scriptures promote violence; others are peaceful.		Fact
All three contain passages that in isolation appear violent — and passages of extraordinary mercy. Mainstream interpretations emphasize justice, mercy, and sanctity of life.
Monotheism OriginMyth
Judaism invented monotheism.		Fact
Judaism is the oldest surviving monotheistic tradition, but scholars debate whether early Israelite religion was strictly monotheistic from the start (possibly henotheism). Judaism made ethical monotheism permanent.
Topic❌ Common Myth✅ Documented Fact
Trinity = 3 Gods?Myth
Christians worship three gods.		Fact
The Trinity is ONE God in three persons. Formalized at Nicaea (325) and Constantinople (381). Sophisticated monotheistic theology, not tritheism.
Dec 25Myth
December 25 is Jesus's actual birthday.		Fact
The New Testament doesn't specify a date. Adopted in the 4th century — scholars debate whether it was to coincide with Sol Invictus or calculated from a March 25 conception. Orthodox celebrate Jan 7.
Anti-ScienceMyth
Christianity has always opposed science.		Fact
Monks preserved classical knowledge. Mendel (genetics) was an Augustinian friar. Georges Lemaître, the Catholic priest who first proposed the Big Bang theory, was ordained in 1923.
Catholics ≠ Christians?Myth
Catholics aren't really Christians, or they worship Mary.		Fact
Catholicism is the oldest and largest Christian denomination. Catholics venerate (honor) Mary but worship God alone — a distinction they maintain rigorously.
Topic❌ Common Myth✅ Documented Fact
JihadMyth
Jihad means "holy war" against non-Muslims.		Fact
Jihad means "struggle." The "greater jihad" is the internal struggle against ego and sin. "Lesser jihad" is defensive warfare under strict rules. Mainstream scholarship condemns terrorism.
"Allah" ≠ God?Myth
"Allah" is a different god.		Fact
"Allah" is the Arabic word for "God," cognate with Hebrew "Eloah" and Aramaic "Alaha." Arabic-speaking Christians and Jews use "Allah" too.
Spread by SwordMyth
Islam was spread entirely by the sword.		Fact
Quran: "there is no compulsion in religion" (2:256). Southeast Asia — world's largest Muslim population — converted through trade and Sufi teaching, not conquest.
Women OppressedMyth
Islam is inherently oppressive to women.		Fact
7th-century Islamic law granted women property rights, inheritance, and divorce — centuries before Western women. Khadijah was a successful businesswoman and Muhammad's employer.
MonolithicMyth
All Muslims think and practice the same way.		Fact
1.9 billion people across every continent. Sunni, Shia, Sufi, Ibadi. Four legal schools. Enormous cultural variation from Indonesia to Morocco to Detroit.
Topic❌ Common Myth✅ Documented Fact
"Chosen"Myth
Jews believe they are superior.		Fact
"Chosen" means chosen for responsibility — 613 commandments, "light unto the nations." The Talmud teaches righteous people of all nations share in the World to Come.
ControlMyth
Jews control banks/media/governments.		Fact
Oldest antisemitic conspiracy theory. Rooted in medieval job restrictions. With 0.2% of world population, "control" is absurd. The Protocols of the Elders of Zion is a proven fabrication by Russian secret police (~1903).

A supersessionist distortion. The Tanakh overflows with divine love and mercy. Psalm 103:8: "Compassionate and gracious, slow to anger, abounding in love." Rabbinic Judaism deeply emphasizes God's love.

Summary

Key Divergences

Despite shared Abrahamic roots, the three faiths have developed into profoundly different theological systems across these core dimensions.

God's Nature

Christianity's Trinitarian monotheism stands unique. Islam and Judaism both insist on strict, indivisible unity — though expressed differently.

Jesus

The single most divisive figure. Christianity: divine savior. Islam: revered prophet. Judaism: not the Messiah.

Human Nature

Original sin (Christianity) vs. fitrah/innate purity (Islam) vs. dual-inclination model (Judaism).

Salvation

Faith/grace (Christianity) vs. submission + deeds (Islam) vs. righteous living in this world (Judaism).

Afterlife

Christianity and Islam provide vivid descriptions. Judaism is notably restrained, focusing on this-world ethics.

Law

Judaism: most law-centered (613 mitzvot). Islam: comprehensive Sharia. Christianity: replaced Mosaic law with the "law of love."

Despite these divergences, the three traditions share foundational commitments to monotheism, ethical conduct, charity, prayer, and the sanctity of human life. Honest engagement with genuine differences — rather than superficial claims of sameness — offers the most productive path toward mutual understanding.
