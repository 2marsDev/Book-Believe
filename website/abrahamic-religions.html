Origins & History

Three faiths, one patriarch. All trace their lineage to Abraham — yet diverged across millennia into vastly different civilizations.

c. 2000 BCE

Abraham's Covenant

God establishes a covenant with Abraham in Mesopotamia, promising him descendants as numerous as the stars. This foundational event is the shared root of all three faiths.

c. 1300 BCE

Moses & the Torah

Moses receives the Torah at Mount Sinai — the foundational law of Judaism. The Exodus from Egypt becomes the defining narrative of the Jewish people.

c. 960 BCE

Solomon's Temple

King Solomon builds the First Temple in Jerusalem, establishing it as the spiritual center of Judaism and a site that remains sacred to all three faiths.

c. 4 BCE – 30 CE

Jesus of Nazareth

Jesus's ministry, crucifixion, and — according to Christian belief — resurrection, give rise to Christianity. His followers proclaim him the Messiah (Christ) foretold in Jewish scripture.

325 CE

Council of Nicaea

Emperor Constantine convenes the first ecumenical council, formalizing core Christian doctrines including the Nicene Creed and the nature of Christ's divinity.

610 CE

Revelation to Muhammad

The Prophet Muhammad receives the first revelation of the Quran in the cave of Hira near Mecca, marking the beginning of Islam — understood by Muslims as the final and complete revelation from God.

622 CE

The Hijra

Muhammad and his followers migrate from Mecca to Medina, establishing the first Muslim community (ummah). This event marks Year 1 of the Islamic calendar.

1096–1291 CE

The Crusades

A series of religious wars launched by European Christians to reclaim the Holy Land. This period profoundly shaped Christian-Muslim-Jewish relations for centuries to come.

Core Theology

All three affirm monotheism — belief in one God — yet their understanding of God's nature, humanity, and the path to righteousness differ profoundly.

Concept ✝ Christianity ☽ Islam ✡ Judaism
Nature of God Trinitarian monotheism — One God in three persons: Father, Son (Jesus), Holy Spirit. The Trinity is a mystery of faith, not three gods. Strict monotheism (Tawhid). God (Allah) is absolutely one, indivisible, without partner or equal. The 99 Names describe His attributes. Strict monotheism. God (YHWH/HaShem) is one, indivisible, incorporeal. The Shema declares: "Hear O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is One."
Original Sin Humanity inherited a fallen nature from Adam & Eve's disobedience. All humans are born with original sin and require salvation through grace. No concept of inherited sin. Adam repented and was forgiven. Every person is born in a pure state (fitrah) and is accountable only for their own deeds. No concept of original sin. The "evil inclination" (yetzer hara) exists in everyone, but it can be overcome through free will and adherence to the Torah.
Jesus The Son of God, the Messiah, divine and human. His death atoned for humanity's sins; his resurrection is the cornerstone of faith. A great prophet (Isa) born of the Virgin Mary, performer of miracles. Not divine, not crucified (according to the Quran), but raised to heaven. Not the Messiah. Regarded as a historical Jewish figure. The Messiah is still awaited — expected to be a human leader who will restore Israel and bring world peace.
Muhammad Not recognized as a prophet. Generally viewed as a historical religious figure. The final prophet and "Seal of the Prophets" — God's last messenger to humanity. He restored the original monotheistic message. Not recognized as a prophet. Not part of Jewish theological tradition.
Relationship with God Personal, intimate. God as loving Father. Access through prayer, sacraments, and the Holy Spirit. Jesus is the mediator. Direct relationship of submission (islam) to God. No intermediary needed. Relationship built through the five pillars and constant remembrance (dhikr). Covenantal partnership. God chose Israel; Israel accepts the commandments. Relationship maintained through study, prayer, and mitzvot (commandments).
Free Will Debated across denominations. Catholics emphasize cooperation of grace and free will; Calvinists lean toward predestination; most affirm meaningful human choice. Complex balance: God has absolute knowledge and power (qadr), yet humans have moral freedom and accountability. Actions are judged fairly. Strongly affirmed. "Everything is foreseen, yet freedom of choice is given" (Pirkei Avot 3:15). Human choice is central to moral life.

Sacred Scriptures

Each tradition preserves its revelation in texts considered authoritative — though the degree and nature of divine inspiration varies.

📖

The Bible

Structure

Old Testament (shared with Judaism, in different order) + New Testament (27 books: Gospels, Epistles, Acts, Revelation).

Inspiration

Inspired by God through human authors. Views range from literal inerrancy (fundamentalist) to inspired but contextual (mainline/liberal).

Languages

Hebrew & Aramaic (OT), Greek (NT). Key translations: Latin Vulgate, King James Version, modern translations.

Key Feature

The Gospels (Matthew, Mark, Luke, John) narrate Jesus's life and teachings — the heart of Christian faith.

📗

The Quran

Structure

114 chapters (surahs), ~6,236 verses (ayat). Arranged roughly by length, not chronology. Supplemented by Hadith (sayings of Muhammad).

Inspiration

Believed to be the literal word of God, dictated to Muhammad through the angel Gabriel (Jibril) over 23 years. Unchanged since compilation.

Languages

Arabic. Translations are considered "interpretations" — the Arabic text is the only true Quran. Recitation (tajweed) is itself an act of worship.

Key Feature

Muslims believe it is the final, uncorrupted revelation, confirming and superseding earlier scriptures given to Moses and Jesus.

📜

The Tanakh & Talmud

Structure

Tanakh (Torah, Nevi'im, Ketuvim — Law, Prophets, Writings). The Talmud (Mishnah + Gemara) is the vast oral tradition of legal and ethical commentary.

Inspiration

The Torah is considered direct divine revelation to Moses. The Prophets and Writings carry high authority. The Talmud represents centuries of rabbinic scholarship.

Languages

Hebrew (Torah, most of Tanakh), Aramaic (parts of Daniel, Ezra, and the Talmud).

Key Feature

The 613 mitzvot (commandments) derived from the Torah form the legal and ethical backbone of Jewish life. Interpretation is ongoing and dynamic.

Practices & Rituals

From daily prayers to dietary laws, each faith structures the lives of its followers through distinct — yet sometimes surprisingly similar — practices.

Practice✝ Christianity☽ Islam✡ Judaism
Prayer No fixed number; varies by denomination. Catholics may pray the Liturgy of the Hours (7 times daily). Protestants emphasize personal, spontaneous prayer. Sunday worship is central. Five obligatory daily prayers (salat) at prescribed times, facing Mecca. Friday congregational prayer (Jumu'ah) is especially important. Preceded by ritual washing (wudu). Three daily prayer services (Shacharit, Mincha, Maariv). Prayer often involves a minyan (quorum of 10). Shabbat (Saturday) services are the weekly highlight.
Fasting Varies by tradition. Catholics fast during Lent (40 days) and on specific days. Orthodox Christians observe extensive fasting seasons. Protestants less structured. Ramadan: one full lunar month of dawn-to-sunset fasting. Also recommended fasts on Mondays, Thursdays, and specific days. Fasting cultivates self-discipline and empathy. Several fast days: Yom Kippur (most solemn, 25-hour fast), Tisha B'Av, and four other minor fasts. Fasting is a form of affliction and repentance.
Pilgrimage Not obligatory, but visiting holy sites (Jerusalem, Rome, Santiago de Compostela, Lourdes) is a cherished tradition with deep spiritual significance. Hajj to Mecca — obligatory once in a lifetime for every able Muslim who can afford it. One of the Five Pillars. Umrah (lesser pilgrimage) can be performed anytime. Historically, pilgrimage to the Temple in Jerusalem three times yearly (Pesach, Shavuot, Sukkot). Today, the Western Wall is the holiest accessible site.
Dietary Laws Few formal restrictions. Catholics abstain from meat on Ash Wednesday and Lenten Fridays. Some Orthodox traditions observe extensive fasting diets. Most Protestants have no dietary laws. Halal requirements: no pork, no alcohol, meat must be slaughtered with God's name invoked (dhabihah). Seafood is generally permissible. All intoxicants are forbidden. Kashrut (kosher law): no pork or shellfish, no mixing of meat and dairy, ritual slaughter (shechita). Requires separate kitchen utensils. Detailed and all-encompassing.
Charity Tithing (10% of income) encouraged. Catholic social teaching emphasizes "preferential option for the poor." Charity is a moral duty, not a fixed legal obligation. Zakat: obligatory 2.5% annual tax on wealth — one of the Five Pillars. Sadaqah (voluntary charity) is also highly encouraged and considered a form of worship. Tzedakah: not mere charity but "justice." Maimonides outlined eight levels of giving. It is a religious obligation, often set at 10% of income.
Holy Day of Rest Sunday for most Christians (commemorating Jesus's resurrection). Seventh-Day Adventists observe Saturday. Friday (Jumu'ah) is the day of congregational prayer, but it is not a full rest day like the Sabbath. No theological prohibition on work. Shabbat — from Friday sunset to Saturday nightfall. Strict rest from 39 categories of creative work. A cornerstone of Jewish life and identity.

Afterlife, Sin & Salvation

How is a person "saved"? What happens after death? These questions sit at the heart of each tradition — and the answers are strikingly different.

✝

Christianity

Path to Salvation

Through faith in Jesus Christ and God's grace. Catholics emphasize faith + works + sacraments; Protestants often stress "faith alone" (sola fide). The atonement of Jesus on the cross is central.

Afterlife

Heaven (eternal communion with God) and Hell (eternal separation from God). Catholics add Purgatory — a state of purification. Many modern theologians debate the nature of Hell.

Judgment

Particular judgment at death + Final Judgment at Christ's Second Coming. The dead will be resurrected in body for the Last Judgment.

Sin & Repentance

Confession and repentance are essential. Catholics practice the Sacrament of Reconciliation (Confession to a priest). God's mercy is boundless for the truly repentant.

☽

Islam

Path to Salvation

Through submission to God, faith, and righteous deeds. The Five Pillars are the framework. On Judgment Day, deeds are weighed — God's mercy and justice determine the outcome.

Afterlife

Jannah (Paradise) — described vividly as gardens of bliss. Jahannam (Hellfire) — for those who rejected God. Some scholars hold that Hell may not be eternal for all sinners.

Judgment

Yawm al-Qiyamah (Day of Resurrection). Every soul is resurrected and judged. The deeds are recorded in a book; the scales (mizan) determine one's fate.

Sin & Repentance

Tawbah (repentance) is direct to God — no intermediary. God is al-Ghafur (The Forgiving) and al-Rahim (The Merciful). Sincere repentance erases sins. Shirk (associating partners with God) is the only unforgivable sin if one dies without repenting.

✡

Judaism

Path to Salvation

Judaism focuses less on "salvation" and more on living a righteous life (olam hazeh — "this world"). Observing the commandments, doing good deeds, and pursuing justice are paramount.

Afterlife

Views vary widely. Olam Ha-Ba (World to Come) is affirmed but not elaborately described. Some traditions include Gehinnom — a temporary purification process (up to 12 months), not eternal damnation.

Judgment

Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur are annual periods of divine judgment. The concept of a final messianic age exists, but emphasis is on present-life ethics rather than eschatology.

Sin & Repentance

Teshuvah (return/repentance) is central, especially during the Days of Awe. Sins against God can be forgiven by God; sins against people require making amends with that person first.

Denominations & Sects

None of these faiths is monolithic. Each has fractured into diverse branches — sometimes through theology, sometimes through politics, often both.

⛪

Christianity

  • Roman Catholicism (~1.3 billion) — Headed by the Pope. Emphasizes sacraments, apostolic tradition, the Magisterium, and the Virgin Mary. The largest single denomination.
  • Eastern Orthodoxy (~220 million) — A communion of autocephalous churches (Greek, Russian, Serbian, etc.). Rich liturgical tradition, icons, and mystical theology.
  • Protestantism (~900 million total) — Born from the 16th-century Reformation. Includes Lutherans, Calvinists, Anglicans, Baptists, Methodists, Pentecostals, and hundreds more. Emphasizes Scripture alone and individual faith.
  • Oriental Orthodoxy (~60 million) — Ancient churches (Coptic, Ethiopian, Armenian, Syriac) that split after the Council of Chalcedon (451 CE) over Christological differences.
🕌

Islam

  • Sunni (~85-90%) — The majority. Follows the Quran, Hadith, and the consensus of the community. Four major legal schools: Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali.
  • Shia (~10-13%) — Believes leadership should have passed through Ali (Muhammad's cousin and son-in-law). Subdivisions include Twelvers (Iran, Iraq), Ismailis, and Zaidis.
  • Sufism — The mystical dimension of Islam (can be Sunni or Shia). Emphasizes inner purification, love of God, and spiritual practices (dhikr, whirling, poetry).
  • Ibadi (~0.5%) — A distinct sect, neither Sunni nor Shia, predominant in Oman. Known for moderate and tolerant theological positions.
🕍

Judaism

  • Orthodox — Strict adherence to halakha (Jewish law). Includes Modern Orthodox (engaged with secular world) and Haredi/Ultra-Orthodox (more insular communities).
  • Conservative/Masorti — Middle ground: respects halakha but allows for historical evolution and change. Strong in North America.
  • Reform/Liberal — Emphasizes ethical principles over ritual law. Adapts traditions to modern life. Ordains women and LGBTQ+ rabbis. Largest movement in the US.
  • Reconstructionist & Renewal — Smaller movements. Reconstructionists view Judaism as an evolving civilization. Renewal integrates mysticism and meditation.

Women & Social Law

The role of women is one of the most debated and evolving areas within all three traditions — with significant variation across denominations and cultures.

Aspect✝ Christianity☽ Islam✡ Judaism
Religious Leadership Varies widely. Catholics and Orthodox prohibit female clergy. Many Protestant denominations ordain women as pastors, priests, and bishops. Traditionally, women do not lead mixed-gender prayers or serve as imams in mainstream Sunni/Shia Islam. Female scholars (alimat) have always existed. Modern reform movements are emerging. Reform, Conservative, and Reconstructionist Judaism ordain women as rabbis and cantors. Orthodox Judaism does not, though some Modern Orthodox have created new roles (Yoetzet Halakha).
Marriage & Family Monogamy is the standard. Marriage is a sacrament in Catholicism (indissoluble) and a covenant in Protestantism. Divorce views range from forbidden (Catholic) to permissible (Protestant). Marriage is a civil and sacred contract (nikah). Polygamy (up to 4 wives) is permitted under strict conditions of justice and equality, though it is rare and banned in some Muslim-majority countries. Divorce (talaq) is permitted but considered the "most disliked permissible thing." Monogamy has been the norm since Rabbeinu Gershom's decree (~1000 CE). Marriage is a contract (ketubah). Divorce (get) is permitted — both parties' consent is ideally required.
Dress & Modesty Historically, head coverings were required for women in church. Still practiced in some Orthodox and traditional communities. Most modern denominations have relaxed these norms. Hijab (headscarf) is widely practiced, with varying interpretations of what "modesty" requires: from simple headscarf to full niqab/burka. Modesty also applies to men. Cultural practice varies enormously. Orthodox women practice tzniut (modesty): covering elbows, knees, collarbone. Married women cover their hair (sheitel/wig or tichel/scarf). Reform and Conservative Judaism generally do not require these.
Inheritance No specific religious inheritance laws in most modern practice. Historically influenced by local civil law. Detailed Quranic inheritance law: daughters receive half the share of sons; wives and mothers have guaranteed fixed shares. Considered revolutionary for 7th-century Arabia, as women previously inherited nothing. Biblical law favored sons, but rabbinic law developed mechanisms to provide for daughters. Modern practice in most communities follows civil law.

Historical Relations

Centuries of coexistence, conflict, alliance, and mutual influence — the Abrahamic faiths have shaped each other profoundly.

The Golden Age of Coexistence — Al-Andalus (711–1492 CE) ▼
Muslim-ruled Spain (Al-Andalus) is often cited as a high point of interfaith coexistence. Jewish scholars like Maimonides flourished under Muslim rule. Arabic-speaking Jews and Christians contributed to science, philosophy, and medicine alongside Muslim scholars. The "Convivencia" (living together) was not without tensions, but it produced extraordinary intellectual cross-pollination. Jewish philosophy was deeply influenced by Islamic thought (Kalam, Falsafa), and both traditions engaged with Greek philosophy — which would later re-enter Christian Europe through Arabic translations.
The Crusades (1096–1291 CE) ▼
Pope Urban II's call to reclaim the Holy Land launched two centuries of warfare. The First Crusade captured Jerusalem in 1099, massacring both Muslim and Jewish inhabitants. The Crusades entrenched mutual hostility between Christianity and Islam, while Jewish communities in Europe suffered brutal pogroms along Crusader routes. The period also opened trade routes and cultural exchanges that would reshape Europe. Saladin's recapture of Jerusalem (1187) is remembered in Islamic tradition for his chivalry and mercy.
The Ottoman Millet System (14th–20th Century) ▼
The Ottoman Empire organized religious minorities (Christians, Jews) into semi-autonomous "millets" — communities with their own courts, schools, and leaders. Jews expelled from Spain in 1492 were welcomed by Sultan Bayezid II. While not equality in the modern sense (non-Muslims paid a special tax, the jizya), it provided a framework for pluralistic governance that was remarkable for its era. Istanbul, Thessaloniki, and other Ottoman cities became major centers of Jewish life.
Anti-Semitism & The Holocaust (19th–20th Century) ▼
Christian anti-Judaism — rooted in theological disputes about deicide and supersessionism — contributed to centuries of persecution: expulsions, ghettos, pogroms, and blood libels. This culminated in the Holocaust (1939–1945), in which six million Jews were murdered by Nazi Germany. While the Nazis' ideology was racial rather than religious, centuries of Christian anti-Semitism created the cultural preconditions. After the Holocaust, many Christian denominations undertook deep theological reckoning, most notably the Catholic Church's Nostra Aetate (1965), which repudiated collective Jewish guilt for Jesus's death and opened a new era of Jewish-Christian dialogue.
Modern Interfaith Dialogue ▼
The 20th and 21st centuries have seen unprecedented interfaith efforts. Vatican II (1962–65) transformed Catholic-Jewish relations. The Amman Message (2004) and A Common Word (2007) were major Muslim-Christian dialogue initiatives. Interfaith organizations, academic programs, and grassroots movements promote mutual understanding. While political conflicts (especially in the Middle East) continue to strain relations, theological dialogue has never been more active — with scholars from all three traditions engaging in honest, rigorous conversation about shared values and real disagreements.

Myths vs Facts

Popular misconceptions abound about all three faiths. This table separates common myths from documented realities — essential for any serious study.

Topic❌ Common Myth✅ Documented Fact
Same God? Myth
"The three religions worship completely different gods."		 Fact
All three traditions trace their worship to the God of Abraham. While they differ profoundly on God's nature (Trinity vs. Tawhid vs. YHWH's unity), theologians across traditions generally acknowledge a shared monotheistic root. The Arabic-speaking Christians also call God "Allah."
Compatibility Myth
"These religions have always been at war and are fundamentally incompatible."		 Fact
History shows extended periods of peaceful coexistence and intellectual exchange (Al-Andalus, Ottoman millets, modern interfaith movements). Conflict is real but driven by politics, resources, and identity as much as by theology. Scholars from all traditions have worked collaboratively for centuries.
Abraham's Role Myth
"Abraham is the founder of all three religions."		 Fact
Abraham is a shared patriarch, but he is not the "founder" of any of these religions in the way they exist today. Judaism crystallized with Moses and the Torah; Christianity with Jesus and the apostles; Islam with Muhammad and the Quran. Abraham is the ancestral origin point — not the institutional founder.
Violence & Scripture Myth
"Some of these scriptures promote violence while others are peaceful."		 Fact
All three scriptures contain passages that, taken in isolation, can appear violent — and passages of extraordinary mercy and compassion. Context, interpretation, and scholarly tradition matter enormously. Within each religion, scholars have debated these texts for centuries, and mainstream interpretations in all three traditions emphasize justice, mercy, and the sanctity of human life.
Monotheism Origin Myth
"Judaism invented monotheism."		 Fact
Judaism is the oldest surviving monotheistic tradition, but scholars debate whether early Israelite religion was strictly monotheistic from the start (some argue it evolved from henotheism — worshipping one God while acknowledging others' existence). Akhenaten's Egyptian solar cult (c. 1350 BCE) also approached monotheism, though it didn't survive. Judaism's contribution was making monotheism permanent and ethically grounded.
Topic❌ Common Myth✅ Documented Fact
The Trinity Myth
"Christians worship three gods."		 Fact
The Trinity is a doctrine of ONE God existing in three co-equal, co-eternal persons. It is emphatically not polytheism. This was formalized at the Councils of Nicaea (325) and Constantinople (381), and Christians who taught otherwise were considered heretical. Muslims and Jews disagree with the doctrine, but it is a sophisticated monotheistic theology, not tritheism.
The Bible Myth
"The Bible was written by one person or at one time."		 Fact
The Bible is a library of 66–73 books (depending on tradition) written by dozens of authors over approximately 1,000+ years. It includes poetry, law, history, prophecy, letters, and apocalyptic literature. The canon was debated and gradually finalized over centuries.
Christmas Myth
"December 25th is Jesus's actual birthday."		 Fact
The New Testament does not specify Jesus's birth date. December 25th was adopted in the 4th century, likely to coincide with existing Roman winter solstice festivals (Sol Invictus, Saturnalia). Eastern Orthodox churches celebrate on January 7th. The date is liturgical, not historical.
Catholicism & Protestantism Myth
"Catholics aren't really Christians" or "Catholics worship Mary."		 Fact
Catholicism is the oldest and largest Christian denomination. Catholics venerate (honor) Mary and the saints but worship God alone — a theological distinction they maintain rigorously. The claim that Catholics "aren't Christian" is a sectarian assertion, not a historical or theological fact.
Science & Faith Myth
"Christianity has always opposed science."		 Fact
While conflicts occurred (Galileo, evolution debates), the Catholic Church was a major patron of science for centuries. Monks preserved classical knowledge; Jesuits pioneered astronomy, seismology, and genetics (Mendel was an Augustinian friar). The Big Bang theory was first proposed by Georges Lemaître, a Catholic priest. Many branches actively embrace scientific inquiry.
Topic❌ Common Myth✅ Documented Fact
Jihad Myth
"Jihad means 'holy war' against non-Muslims."		 Fact
Jihad literally means "struggle" or "striving." In Islamic theology, the "greater jihad" is the internal struggle against one's own ego and sinful desires. The "lesser jihad" refers to defensive warfare under strict conditions (protecting the community, with rules prohibiting harm to civilians, destruction of crops, or forced conversion). Mainstream Islamic scholarship condemns terrorism as a violation of these principles.
"Allah" Myth
"Allah is a different God from the God of Christianity and Judaism."		 Fact
"Allah" is simply the Arabic word for "God" — cognate with the Hebrew "Eloah" and Aramaic "Alaha." Arabic-speaking Christians and Jews have used "Allah" for centuries. The Quran explicitly states it is revealing the same God worshipped by Abraham, Moses, and Jesus.
Women Myth
"Islam is inherently oppressive to women."		 Fact
Early Islamic law (7th century) granted women rights to own property, inherit wealth, seek divorce, and enter contracts — rights Western women often didn't gain until the 19th-20th centuries. Khadijah, Muhammad's first wife, was a successful businesswoman and his employer. Cultural patriarchy exists in many Muslim-majority societies, but scholars debate whether this is required by Islam or imposed by culture. There are active feminist movements within Islamic scholarship.
Forced Conversion Myth
"Islam was spread entirely by the sword."		 Fact
The Quran states "there is no compulsion in religion" (2:256). While military expansion did create Muslim-ruled empires, conversion was typically gradual — driven by trade, intermarriage, Sufi missionaries, and social incentives. Southeast Asia (the world's largest Muslim population) was converted almost entirely through trade and Sufi teaching, not conquest.
Monolithic Faith Myth
"All Muslims think and practice the same way."		 Fact
Islam spans 1.9 billion people across every continent, including Sunni, Shia, Sufi, and Ibadi traditions, four major legal schools, and enormous cultural variation from Indonesia to Morocco to Detroit. The lived experience of a Muslim woman in Istanbul differs vastly from one in rural Pakistan or Detroit. Internal diversity is massive.
Topic❌ Common Myth✅ Documented Fact
"Chosen People" Myth
"Jews believe they are superior to other people."		 Fact
"Chosen" in Jewish theology means chosen for responsibility — to bear the burden of 613 commandments and to serve as a "light unto the nations." It implies obligation, not superiority. The Talmud teaches that righteous people of all nations have a share in the World to Come. Being "chosen" is a covenant of duty, not privilege.
Race & Ethnicity Myth
"Judaism is a race" or "All Jews are white/European."		 Fact
Judaism is a religion, a civilization, and a peoplehood — but not a race. Jewish communities span the globe: Ashkenazi (European), Sephardi (Iberian/Mediterranean), Mizrahi (Middle Eastern), Ethiopian (Beta Israel), Indian (Bene Israel, Cochin Jews), Chinese (Kaifeng), and more. Conversion is open to all.
Wealth & Power Myth
"Jews control banks/media/governments."		 Fact
This is one of the oldest and most dangerous antisemitic conspiracy theories, rooted in medieval restrictions that barred Jews from most professions, channeling some into finance. With 0.2% of the world's population, the idea of Jewish "control" of global institutions is statistically absurd and historically a precursor to violence. The Protocols of the Elders of Zion, the most infamous source for this myth, is a proven fabrication (created by Russian secret police, c. 1903).
The Old Testament Myth
"Jews follow the 'Old Testament' and reject grace/love."		 Fact
The term "Old Testament" is a Christian designation. Jews call their scripture the Tanakh. The characterization of Judaism as legalistic and loveless (vs. a "graceful" Christianity) is a supersessionist distortion. The Tanakh is filled with divine love, mercy, and compassion: "The Lord is compassionate and gracious, slow to anger, abounding in love" (Psalm 103:8). Rabbinic Judaism deeply emphasizes God's love and mercy.
Israel & Judaism Myth
"All Jews support everything the state of Israel does" or "Criticizing Israeli policy is antisemitic."		 Fact
Jewish opinion on Israel is enormously diverse — from religious Zionism to anti-Zionist Orthodox communities (Satmar, Neturei Karta) to secular critics. Mainstream Jewish organizations generally distinguish between legitimate criticism of Israeli policies and antisemitism (which targets Jews as a people). Internal Israeli debate is itself vigorous and deeply divided.
