# Chapter 10: The Money Machine

In 2017, Hurricane Harvey dumped fifty-one inches of rain on Houston, Texas. People were drowning in their living rooms. Shelters filled up. The convention center ran out of space. And Joel Osteen's Lakewood Church — a 16,800-seat former NBA arena sitting on prime Houston real estate — locked its doors.

The building was dry. The parking garage had minor flooding on its lowest level. The arena itself was fine.

Osteen didn't open the doors until the internet shamed him into it. Until the photographs of locked gates went viral. Until other churches, smaller churches, churches without book deals and television contracts, had already been sheltering hundreds.

When he finally opened Lakewood, he said the church had been inaccessible due to flooding. Aerial photographs showed otherwise.

I keep coming back to that image. Sixteen thousand empty seats in a dry building. People sleeping in water two miles away.

Because it tells you something about what the money machine has become.

*     *     *

Let me give you a number. Actually, let me give you a range, because nobody knows the real number.

The total assets held by religious organizations in the United States are estimated at somewhere between $1.2 trillion and $4.8 trillion.

That range should bother you. The gap between the low estimate and the high estimate is $3.6 trillion. We don't know where in that range the truth falls, because religious organizations in America are not required to file the financial disclosures that every other nonprofit must file.

Every secular charity — the Red Cross, Doctors Without Borders, your local food bank — files a Form 990 with the IRS. This form details revenue, expenses, executive compensation, assets, and liabilities. It's public. Anyone can look it up.

Churches don't file Form 990s.

They don't have to. Under Section 508(c)(1)(A) of the Internal Revenue Code, churches are automatically exempt from federal income tax without even applying. They don't pay property tax. They don't pay sales tax in many states. They aren't required to disclose what they earn, what they spend, or what they pay their leaders.

The reasoning is the First Amendment. Government shouldn't entangle itself with religion. Requiring financial reporting, the argument goes, is a form of government oversight that infringes on religious liberty.

I understand the argument. I even respect it in principle.

But here's what it looks like in practice.

*     *     *

Kenneth Copeland is worth an estimated $300 million. He lives in a lakefront mansion valued at roughly $7 million, which is classified as a parsonage — a church-provided home — making it tax-exempt. He owns a fleet of private jets. He has his own airport. Kenneth Copeland Airport. In Newark, Texas. A man of God with his own runway.

In 2019, a reporter from Inside Edition caught Copeland outside and asked him about the jets. Why does a preacher need a private jet?

Copeland's answer, delivered with a smile that didn't reach his eyes: "If I flew commercial, I'd have to stop 65 percent of what I'm doing." In an earlier conversation with fellow prosperity preacher Jesse Duplantis, Copeland had been more candid. He said he couldn't fly commercial because he'd be stuck "in a long tube with a bunch of demons."

The demons, apparently, are you and me.

Creflo Dollar — and yes, that's his real name — stood in front of his congregation in 2015 and asked them to help him buy a $65 million Gulfstream G650 jet. He needed 200,000 people to give $300 each. He framed it as an act of faith. Giving to your pastor, he explained, is giving to God.

Dollar eventually got his jet.

Joel Osteen's net worth is estimated between $50 million and $100 million. His books have sold millions of copies. His television broadcast reaches over 100 countries. Lakewood Church takes in an estimated $90 million per year in donations.

None of these figures can be independently verified, because Lakewood doesn't have to tell anyone what it does with the money.

*     *     *

The prosperity gospel is the most financially efficient theology ever invented. Its core claim is elegant: God wants you to be rich. If you give generously to His church — and by church, we mean this specific pastor's ministry — God will multiply your investment. It's divine returns on divine capital.

The people who give are overwhelmingly not rich.

Sociological research consistently shows that prosperity gospel congregations skew lower-income. These are people who can't afford to tithe 10 percent of their income. They do it anyway, because they've been told that financial struggle is a symptom of insufficient faith, and that giving more will unlock God's abundance.

A 2016 study in the Journal for the Scientific Study of Religion found that members of prosperity-oriented churches reported higher rates of financial stress than members of non-prosperity churches, even when controlling for income. They gave more. They worried more. They had less.

The theology is unfalsifiable. If you give and get rich, God rewarded your faith. If you give and stay poor, your faith wasn't strong enough. Give more.

It's a closed loop with one exit: the pastor's bank account.

*     *     *

This isn't new. The money has always been there. What changes is the scale and the shamelessness.

Oral Roberts built a university, a hospital, and a television empire starting in the 1950s. In 1987, he went on air and told his viewers that God would "call him home" — kill him — if they didn't send $8 million by March. They sent $9.1 million. Roberts lived. God, apparently, was satisfied.

Jim Bakker and his wife Tammy Faye built the PTL Club into a broadcasting powerhouse. They had a satellite network, a theme park called Heritage USA, and an air-conditioned doghouse. In 1989, Bakker was convicted of fraud and sentenced to forty-five years in prison. He'd oversold "lifetime partnerships" at Heritage USA — basically timeshares — by 60,000 units. He took in $158 million and spent most of it on operations, personal luxury, and hush money to Jessica Hahn, a church secretary he'd sexually assaulted.

Bakker served five years. He's back on television now, selling survival food buckets.

Jimmy Swaggart was the most-watched televangelist in the world in the mid-1980s. His ministry brought in $150 million a year. In 1988, he was photographed with a prostitute at a motel in New Orleans. He gave his famous crying confession on live television — "I have sinned against you, my Lord" — and his viewership briefly dropped. Then he was caught with another prostitute in 1991. This time he told his congregation, "The Lord told me it's flat none of your business."

He kept preaching.

The pattern is always the same. Build the audience. Build the revenue. When scandal hits, cry, repent, and rebuild. The audience forgives because forgiveness is the product they've been buying all along.

*     *     *

But the prosperity preachers are the loud, obvious cases. The real money is quieter.

The Catholic Church is the largest non-governmental landowner on Earth. The Vatican's wealth is estimated at somewhere between $10 billion and $15 billion — and that's the conservative, liquid-assets estimate. It doesn't include the art.

The Vatican Museums contain works by Michelangelo, Raphael, Caravaggio, and Bernini. The Sistine Chapel alone is effectively priceless. The total value of the Vatican's art collection has been estimated in the hundreds of billions of dollars, though these estimates are speculative because the collection has never been independently appraised and never will be.

Then there's the Vatican Bank, formally called the Institute for the Works of Religion — the IOR. Its history reads like a crime novel because, in parts, it is one.

In 1982, Roberto Calvi, the chairman of Banco Ambrosiano — a bank closely tied to the Vatican — was found hanging under Blackfriars Bridge in London. His pockets were filled with bricks and cash. Banco Ambrosiano had collapsed with $1.3 billion in debt, much of it connected to loans funneled through the IOR. The Vatican denied responsibility but eventually made a $244 million payment it described as a "voluntary contribution" rather than an admission of guilt.

Calvi's death was initially ruled a suicide. It was later reclassified as murder. No one has been convicted.

The P2 Masonic lodge, which had members in the Italian government, military, and intelligence services, was deeply intertwined with both Banco Ambrosiano and the IOR. Licio Gelli, the lodge's grandmaster, was convicted of fraud in connection with the collapse. The full extent of the Vatican's involvement has never been established, in part because the IOR is not subject to Italian banking regulations.

More recently, in 2020, the Vatican put a cardinal on trial for the first time in modern history. Cardinal Angelo Becciu was charged with embezzlement, abuse of office, and witness tampering in connection with a London real estate deal that lost the Vatican an estimated $400 million. The money had come from Peter's Pence — donations from ordinary Catholics around the world who thought they were giving to charity.

Becciu was convicted in 2023 and sentenced to five and a half years.

He was one man. The system that allowed him to operate — opaque, unregulated, accountable to no external authority — remains intact.

*     *     *

Islamic finance operates on different principles but generates its own questions.

The waqf system — the Islamic endowment — is one of the oldest charitable structures in the world. A waqf is property donated for religious or charitable purposes in perpetuity. Mosques, schools, hospitals, and vast tracts of land have been held as waqf for centuries. In many Muslim-majority countries, the total waqf holdings are enormous. Turkey's Directorate General of Foundations manages assets accumulated over five hundred years. Egypt, India, and Malaysia hold waqf properties worth billions.

The system works well when it works. Waqf properties fund genuine charitable work — education, healthcare, community infrastructure.

But then there's the petrodollar pipeline.

Since the oil boom of the 1970s, Saudi Arabia has spent an estimated $100 billion exporting Wahhabism — its ultraconservative interpretation of Sunni Islam — around the world. The money has built mosques, funded madrasas, trained imams, and distributed literature on every continent.

This is not a conspiracy theory. It's documented Saudi government policy. The kingdom's own figures acknowledge spending billions on Islamic outreach. A 2006 Freedom House report examined literature distributed by the Saudi government to mosques in the United States and found material promoting hatred of Jews and Christians, rejection of democracy, and endorsement of violence against those who leave Islam.

The radicalization pipeline doesn't start with a bomb. It starts with a building. A new mosque. A free school. A charismatic imam whose salary is paid from thousands of miles away, whose theology was not chosen by the community he serves but by the state that signs his checks.

Most mosques funded by Saudi money are not breeding grounds for terrorism. That claim would be absurd. But the influence operation is real, and the money is staggering, and it has shifted the global character of Sunni Islam in a more conservative direction over fifty years.

*     *     *

Jewish institutional finance is different in structure, though not immune to questions.

Jewish organizations in America tend to operate with more financial transparency than churches, in part because most are structured as secular nonprofits that do file Form 990s. Synagogues operate on membership dues rather than tithes, and the amounts are generally disclosed.

The major Jewish philanthropic organizations — the Jewish Federations of North America, Hadassah, the American Jewish Joint Distribution Committee — are among the most financially transparent large nonprofits in the country. Their budgets are audited. Their reports are public.

But wealth exists. The real estate holdings of major Orthodox institutions in New York City alone are worth hundreds of millions. And where there is concentrated wealth in religious institutions, there are questions about accountability, about who decides how community money is spent, and about what happens when leadership is insular.

In 2019, a federal investigation into a decades-long tax fraud scheme led to charges against members of a Lakewood, New Jersey, Orthodox community who had systematically underreported income to qualify for government benefits while receiving substantial communal support. The fraud involved millions of dollars.

It was not representative. It was specific. But it was enabled by a community structure that resists outside scrutiny on religious grounds.

*     *     *

Now zoom out. Forget the scandals. Look at the basic mechanism.

Tithing. Ten percent.

The concept comes from the Hebrew Bible. Abraham gave a tenth of his spoils to Melchizedek, the priest-king of Salem. Leviticus 27:30 declares that "a tithe of everything from the land, whether grain from the soil or fruit from the trees, belongs to the Lord."

Modern churches, particularly evangelical Protestant churches, have turned this into a weekly expectation. Some churches teach that tithing is mandatory for salvation. Others frame it as an act of obedience. Either way, the social pressure is enormous. In many congregations, giving is tracked. Envelopes are labeled. Some churches publish donor lists.

Americans gave an estimated $135 billion to religious organizations in 2022, according to Giving USA. That's more than they gave to education, human services, and health organizations combined.

Where does it go?

Some of it goes to genuine charitable work — food pantries, homeless shelters, disaster relief, community programs. Religious organizations do an enormous amount of social good, and denying that would be dishonest.

But studies suggest that the majority of church spending goes to operational costs — buildings, salaries, utilities, and internal programs for existing members. A 2016 analysis by the Evangelical Council for Financial Accountability found that member churches spent an average of 82 percent of their budgets on internal operations and 18 percent on outreach and missions. Of that 18 percent, much of the "missions" spending was evangelism — not charity but recruitment.

So when someone puts $100 in the offering plate, roughly $82 goes to keeping the building open and the pastor paid. Maybe $10 goes to community service. And some portion of the rest goes to convincing more people to put money in the offering plate.

This is not fraud. It's how organizations work. Buildings cost money. Staff costs money.

But the donor thinks she's giving to God. She's giving to overhead.

*     *     *

The tax exemption question is, at its core, a math problem.

If religious organizations in the United States paid taxes at the same rate as other nonprofits — or, more dramatically, at the rate of for-profit businesses — what would the government collect?

A widely cited 2012 study by University of Tampa professor Ryan Cragun estimated that the total subsidy to religion through tax exemptions is approximately $71 billion per year. This includes property tax exemptions ($26.2 billion), the tax-deductibility of donations ($12.5 billion), and various income, investment, and parsonage exemptions.

Seventy-one billion dollars a year. That's more than the annual budget of the Department of Homeland Security.

Now, defenders of religious tax exemption make several arguments, and some of them are strong.

First, the First Amendment. Government has no business entangling itself with religious institutions. Taxation is a form of control. If you can tax a church, you can shut it down by taxing it to death. The founders, having fled religious persecution, had good reason to build a wall.

I take this seriously. I do.

Second, social services. Religious organizations provide an estimated $20 billion or more in social services annually — food banks, addiction programs, shelters, disaster relief. Remove the tax exemption, and some of that goes away.

Third, small churches. The vast majority of religious congregations in America are small. The median church has seventy-five regular attendees. The median budget is under $200,000. These aren't corporations. They're community organizations run by someone who probably has a second job.

All of this is true. And none of it addresses the problem.

The problem is that the same exemption that keeps a small Baptist church in rural Alabama solvent also shields Lakewood Church's $90 million annual revenue from any public scrutiny. The same rule that protects a modest synagogue in Brooklyn protects Kenneth Copeland's airport. The system makes no distinction between a church that spends everything on its community and a ministry that makes its leader a billionaire.

Because to make that distinction, you'd need oversight. And oversight means entanglement. And entanglement means the First Amendment argument kicks in.

It's a perfect shield. The smallest church and the largest televangelist hide behind the same constitutional principle.

*     *     *

I want to be clear about something, because it matters.

Most of the people who work in religious institutions are not getting rich. Most pastors in America earn modest salaries. Most imams are underpaid. Most rabbis balance spiritual leadership with the mundane reality of keeping a building from falling down.

The woman who runs the food pantry out of her church basement is not the problem. The imam who teaches Arabic classes after evening prayers is not the problem. The rabbi who spends three hours every week visiting elderly congregants in the hospital is not the problem.

The problem is the industry that has grown up alongside them. The television empires. The mega-churches with coffee bars and gift shops. The Vatican's investment portfolio. The Saudi export machine. The political lobbying firms that ensure the tax exemption never gets examined too closely.

The problem is that this industry — one of the largest economic systems on the planet — operates in the name of God and reports to no one.

Not to its donors. Not to the government. Not to the people who fill the seats every week and believe their money is being used for sacred purposes.

Trillions of dollars. Zero mandatory transparency.

In any other sector, we'd call that a scandal. In religion, we call it freedom.

*     *     *

I sat in a prosperity gospel church once, in a suburb of Atlanta. The building was enormous. The sound system was professional grade. The screens were high-definition. The parking lot had a traffic management team.

The pastor wore a suit that cost more than my first car. He preached about abundance. He preached about the windows of heaven opening. He preached about planting a seed — that was the word, "seed" — and watching God multiply it.

Then the offering came. The ushers passed the buckets. But first, the pastor asked everyone who wanted to give $1,000 or more to stand up.

A few people stood. The congregation applauded.

Then $500. More people stood. More applause.

Then $100. Most of the room.

I looked at the faces. I saw a woman in her sixties who had come in a car with a cracked windshield. I saw a young couple who didn't look like they had $100 to give. I saw them put money in the bucket.

I saw the pastor smile.

And I thought: this is the oldest con in the world. It's just wearing a nicer suit.

# Chapter 11: The Children

I was eight years old the first time someone told me I might go to hell.

I don't remember the exact words. I remember the feeling. A Sunday school teacher describing a lake of fire. A place where you burned and burned and never died. A place God sent people who didn't believe the right things.

I was eight. I went home and couldn't sleep.

That's where this chapter starts. Not with theology. Not with doctrine. With the face of a child who has just been told that an all-powerful being might torture them forever.

*     *     *

In 2006, Richard Dawkins published The God Delusion and made a claim that provoked outrage. He said that labeling children with their parents' religion — calling them "Catholic children" or "Muslim children" — was a form of abuse. Not metaphorical abuse. Real abuse. He argued that the systematic indoctrination of children into religious belief, particularly when accompanied by threats of eternal punishment, could cause lasting psychological harm comparable to other forms of childhood trauma.

The backlash was immediate and fierce. Critics called Dawkins arrogant, insensitive, offensive. Religious leaders said he was trivializing actual child abuse. Moderate secularists distanced themselves from the comparison.

Was Dawkins right?

Let me answer carefully, because this matters more than almost anything else in this book.

He was not entirely right. Calling all religious upbringing "abuse" is too broad, too blunt, and too dismissive of the billions of people who were raised in faith and emerged as healthy, functional, grateful adults. It flattens an enormous range of experience into a single inflammatory label.

But he was not entirely wrong either. And the evidence has only grown since he made the claim.

*     *     *

Let me tell you about hell.

Not the theological concept. The psychological reality. What it does to a developing brain.

A 2003 study published in the Journal of Anxiety Disorders examined the relationship between religious beliefs and anxiety symptoms in children and adolescents. Children who held strong beliefs in divine punishment — specifically, in hell as a real place of eternal torment — showed significantly higher levels of generalized anxiety than those who did not hold such beliefs. The effect was independent of general religiosity. It wasn't belief in God that correlated with anxiety. It was belief in hell.

A 2011 study in the journal Mental Health, Religion & Culture found that adults who had been raised with a vivid, literal doctrine of hell were more likely to report symptoms consistent with post-traumatic stress, including intrusive thoughts, hypervigilance, and avoidance behaviors. The study controlled for other childhood adversity. The hell exposure was an independent predictor.

Dr. Marlene Winell, a psychologist who has spent decades treating people recovering from authoritarian religious upbringings, coined the term "Religious Trauma Syndrome" to describe the cluster of symptoms she observed: anxiety, depression, difficulty with decision-making, problems with self-worth, and a persistent fear response triggered by religious stimuli. She's careful to note that not all religious upbringing produces this. But certain combinations of factors — an angry God, literal hell, rigid behavioral rules, and social isolation from the non-religious world — reliably produce psychological harm.

I spoke with a woman named Sarah, who grew up in a fundamentalist Baptist church in rural Tennessee. She asked me not to use her last name because her parents still don't know she's left the faith.

"I was five," she told me. "My Sunday school teacher had this flannel board with felt figures. She put a figure on the board and said, 'This is a boy who didn't accept Jesus.' Then she moved the figure down to the bottom of the board where she'd drawn red and orange flames. She said, 'This is where he goes forever.'"

Sarah paused.

"I had nightmares for years. I would wake up in the middle of the night and pray, just pray and pray, because I was terrified I hadn't believed hard enough. That maybe I'd said the salvation prayer wrong. That I'd burn."

She's thirty-six now. She left the church at twenty-two. She still has the nightmares sometimes.

"Fourteen years later," she said. "I know it's not real. My brain knows. But my body doesn't."

*     *     *

This is not about one woman. This is about a mechanism.

When you teach a child that an omniscient being is watching their every thought, you install a surveillance system inside their mind. When you teach them that wrong thoughts or wrong beliefs will result in infinite torture, you attach the most extreme possible consequence to the act of thinking freely.

A child cannot evaluate this claim. A child cannot weigh evidence, consider alternative perspectives, or assess the reliability of the authority making the claim. A child trusts the adults in their life. When those adults say hell is real, hell becomes real.

And here's what I find most disturbing: the adults often believe they're helping. The Sunday school teacher with the flannel board isn't trying to traumatize a five-year-old. She's trying to save a soul. The motivation is love — or what has been shaped to look like love by the same system that shaped her.

The harm is structural. It doesn't require bad people. It just requires a bad idea taught to someone too young to reject it.

*     *     *

Now let's talk about education.

In 2004, the school board of Dover, Pennsylvania, voted to require that a statement about "intelligent design" be read to students in ninth-grade biology class. The statement said that evolution was "a theory, not a fact," and that intelligent design was an alternative explanation students should consider.

Eleven parents sued.

The resulting case — Kitzmiller v. Dover Area School District — became one of the most important legal battles over science education in American history. The trial lasted six weeks. Expert witnesses from both sides testified. The judge, John E. Jones III, was a Republican appointee. A churchgoing Lutheran.

His ruling was devastating. He found that intelligent design was not science. It was religious doctrine dressed in scientific language. He wrote that the board members who had pushed the policy had lied in their depositions about their religious motivations. He found that the policy violated the Establishment Clause of the First Amendment.

The ruling was 139 pages long. It cost the Dover school district over $1 million in legal fees. Several board members lost their seats in the next election.

But here's what matters for this chapter: what happened to the students.

For months, these kids were caught in a national culture war. Their biology class became a political battlefield. They were taught, by their own school board, that their science textbook might be wrong — not because new evidence had emerged, but because the adults in charge had a religious objection to the content.

This is what creationism in schools does. It doesn't just give children bad information. It teaches them that authority figures will override evidence when evidence conflicts with belief. It teaches them that knowledge is negotiable. That the scientific method is just one opinion among many.

And Dover was the case that made it to court. In thousands of school districts across America, the same pressure operates quietly. Teachers self-censor. They skip the evolution unit. They teach it quickly and vaguely, afraid of parent complaints. A 2020 survey published in Evolution: Education and Outreach found that only about two-thirds of U.S. public high school biology teachers present evolution as the scientific consensus without qualification.

One-third of biology teachers either hedge on evolution, skip it, or present creationism alongside it. In biology class. In the twenty-first century.

*     *     *

Now travel six thousand miles east.

The word "madrasa" simply means "school" in Arabic. Most madrasas are normal educational institutions. In many Muslim-majority countries, they provide the only accessible education for children in poverty. They teach literacy, mathematics, history, and Islamic studies.

But some madrasas teach nothing else but the Quran.

In parts of Pakistan, Bangladesh, and West Africa, children — some as young as four — are enrolled in residential madrasas where the entire curriculum consists of memorizing the Quran in Arabic. Not understanding it. Memorizing it. Many of these children do not speak Arabic. They learn to recite a text they cannot comprehend.

A 2019 report from Human Rights Watch documented conditions in Quranic schools in Senegal, where an estimated 100,000 children known as talibés were sent to study under Quranic teachers. Many were forced to beg in the streets to fund the teacher's household. Physical abuse was common. Children were beaten with electrical cables for failing to meet begging quotas or for errors in Quran recitation.

This is an extreme. It's not representative of Islamic education globally. But it exists, it involves children, and it is justified in religious terms.

The broader question is what happens to a child who spends ten years memorizing a sacred text and emerges at age sixteen able to recite 6,236 verses from memory but unable to do multiplication. What are that child's economic prospects? What is that child's capacity for critical inquiry? What choices does that child actually have?

The memorization is a genuine achievement. The brain that can hold the entire Quran is formidable. But it has been trained for one purpose, in service of one system, before it was old enough to understand what was being chosen for it.

*     *     *

In Brooklyn, New York, there are Hasidic yeshivas where boys spend the majority of their school day studying Talmud in Yiddish. Secular subjects — English, math, science, history — receive minimal attention, sometimes as little as ninety minutes a day, sometimes nothing at all.

This has been an open secret for decades.

In 2015, a group called Young Advocates for Fair Education, or YAFFED, filed a complaint with the New York City Department of Education. The complaint alleged that dozens of Hasidic yeshivas were failing to provide the "substantially equivalent" secular education required by state law.

The city investigated. Slowly. Over the course of years.

In 2022, the New York Times published a major investigative report that examined Hasidic schools receiving more than $1 billion in government funding over the previous four years. The investigation found that in many of these schools, boys could not read English at a functional level. They could not do basic arithmetic. They had never been taught science.

Graduates described leaving the yeshiva system unable to fill out a job application in English. Unable to read a lease. Unable to navigate a world they had been deliberately kept separate from.

The city and state knew. Politicians knew. The Hasidic community represents a powerful voting bloc in New York — roughly 200,000 people who vote in high proportions and largely follow the endorsements of their rebbes. Elected officials who challenged the yeshiva system risked losing an entire community's support.

So they didn't challenge it. For years. While children graduated without the tools to survive outside their community.

And that's the point. You don't need to keep someone in a room to trap them. You just need to make sure they can't function in any room but yours.

*     *     *

I keep returning to a concept that has no satisfying name: the right to leave.

Every major religion allows conversion — in theory. In practice, the cost of leaving is often so high that the exit door might as well be welded shut.

In fundamentalist Christian communities, leaving the faith means losing your family. Not metaphorically. Literally. Parents stop speaking to adult children who deconvert. Siblings cut contact. The entire social network — which, in an insular community, is the only social network — collapses.

In Islam, apostasy — the act of leaving the faith — is a capital offense in at least twelve countries. Even in countries where it's not legally punishable, the social consequences are severe. Family rejection. Community ostracism. In some cases, violence.

Among Jehovah's Witnesses, leaving is met with "disfellowshipping" — a formal process of shunning. Your family members who remain in the faith are instructed not to speak to you. Not to eat with you. Not to acknowledge you.

In ultra-Orthodox Jewish communities, leaving — going "off the derech," as it's called — means losing your family, your community, and often your children in custody disputes where religious courts carry enormous weight.

Now imagine you were born into one of these systems.

You didn't choose it. You were an infant. The beliefs were installed before you had language to question them. The community was all you knew. Your education was controlled by the community. Your social connections exist entirely within the community. Your sense of identity — who you are, what you are, why you exist — was constructed by the community.

And then someone tells you that you're free to leave.

Are you?

Freedom requires options. Options require knowledge, skills, and social connections outside the system. If the system has made sure you don't have those things, the freedom to leave is theoretical. It exists on paper. It doesn't exist in the life of a twenty-year-old who has never been taught to read in the language of the country they live in.

*     *     *

I need to say something that complicates everything I've just written.

Many children raised in religious households turn out wonderfully. They are kind, disciplined, community-oriented, and morally serious. They have strong family bonds. They have a sense of purpose that many secular people struggle to find.

The research on this is real and I won't pretend it doesn't exist.

A 2018 study published in the American Journal of Epidemiology, based on data from the Nurses' Health Study II and the Growing Up Today Study, found that children who attended religious services at least weekly were approximately 18 percent more likely to report high happiness as young adults. They were 29 percent more likely to volunteer. They were 33 percent less likely to use illicit drugs.

Religious communities provide structure, mentorship, intergenerational connection, and a framework for navigating suffering. For children in unstable environments, a church, mosque, or synagogue may be the most stable institution in their lives. For children in poverty, religious organizations may provide the only extracurricular activities, the only adult mentors, the only sense of belonging available.

A religious upbringing can be a profound gift. I have met people for whom it was.

I spoke with a man named David, raised in a Modern Orthodox Jewish home in Chicago. He described Shabbat dinners with extended family every Friday night. He described a community that showed up when his father was sick — meals at the door every day for six weeks. He described learning Hebrew, learning to read Torah, feeling connected to a tradition that stretched back thousands of years.

"It gave me roots," he said. "In a world that feels rootless, I always knew where I came from."

David still practices. He sends his children to Jewish day school. He told me he wants them to have what he had.

I believe him. I believe it was good.

*     *     *

So how do you hold both truths?

The child who thrives in a loving religious home and the child who lies awake at night, certain she's going to burn. The boy who learns Torah alongside math and science and becomes a thoughtful, educated adult, and the boy who can recite every word of a holy text but can't read a street sign in the city where he lives.

You hold them by recognizing that "religion" is not one thing. The experience of a child in a progressive Reform synagogue and the experience of a child in a fundamentalist compound share almost nothing except the word "religious."

But here's what I can't accept: the idea that we shouldn't ask the question because the answer is complicated.

The question is simple. Does a child's mind belong to their parents' tradition before it belongs to the child?

Every religious tradition answers yes. That's what baptism is. That's what circumcision is. That's what the shahada whispered into a newborn's ear is. The claim is made before the child can speak.

And in most cases, no great harm is done. The child grows up, absorbs the tradition, and either keeps it or leaves it. The system is loose enough to permit movement. The education is broad enough to enable choice. The family is loving enough to survive disagreement.

But in some cases, the system is not loose. The education is not broad. The family's love is conditional on the child's belief. And the child discovers — at sixteen, at twenty, at thirty — that they were never given the tools to be anything other than what they were told to be.

*     *     *

I think about this more than any other question in this book.

Not the existence of God. Not the historical accuracy of scripture. Not the sociology of religious institutions.

The children.

Because adults can evaluate evidence. Adults can change their minds. Adults bear some responsibility for the beliefs they hold and the communities they join.

Children can't. Children are handed a worldview by the people they trust most in the world. They are given their metaphysics the way they are given their language — early, completely, and without consent.

I'm not arguing that parents shouldn't teach their children values. I'm not arguing that religious education should be banned. I'm not arguing that children should be raised in some sterile, belief-free environment. That wouldn't work even if it were desirable, and it's not desirable.

I'm arguing for a minimum standard.

Teach your children your faith. But also teach them that other people believe different things, and that those people are not evil.

Teach them your scripture. But also teach them to read, to reason, to question, and to doubt. Including to doubt what you've taught them.

Teach them about God. But don't teach them that an all-loving God will torture them forever if they think the wrong thoughts. Don't install a terror in a child's mind and call it salvation.

Teach them your tradition. But give them enough education to survive outside it. Give them math, science, language, history. Give them the tools to leave — even if you pray they never will.

Because a faith that can only survive by keeping its children ignorant of alternatives is not a faith. It's a cage.

*     *     *

I keep thinking about Sarah, the woman from Tennessee. Thirty-six years old. Fourteen years out of the church. Still dreaming about fire.

And I think about David, the man from Chicago. Lighting candles every Friday night with his kids. Grateful for what he was given.

Both of those are real. Both of those are religion's relationship with children.

The question is not whether children can be raised religiously and turn out fine. Of course they can. The evidence is overwhelming. Billions of people are proof.

The question is whether a child's mind belongs to their community's tradition before it belongs to the child. And whether the answer to that question should depend on which tradition we're talking about.

Because if the answer is yes — if children's minds do belong to the tradition first — then we've accepted that some children will be casualties. That some will spend their lives recovering from what was done to their minds before they were old enough to resist.

And if the answer is no — if the child's mind belongs to the child — then every tradition has some accounting to do.

Not just the extreme ones. All of them.
