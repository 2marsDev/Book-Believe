## CHAPTER 4: The Receipt

### Voice Check

The voice is consistent and strong throughout. The narrator is present, controlled, and lets the evidence build. The opening ("Every deal has a receipt") is punchy and sets the transactional framing well. The invisible dragon passage (lines 18-23) is borrowed from Carl Sagan but used effectively.

**One register slip:** Line 54, "It's also possible that I'll sprout wings tomorrow and fly to Mars" — this was in v1 and has been **removed** in v2. Good. The v2 version ("Anything is possible. But the question isn't what's possible. The question is what's probable.") is tighter and more dignified.

**Improved passage:** The Muhammad section (lines 71-86) no longer contains the v1 line about walking into a police station and being evaluated for mental health. The v2 replaces this with "People report private revelations constantly — voices that speak to them, visions that reveal cosmic truths, messages from beings no one else can see. We have clinical frameworks for understanding these experiences." This is better — it makes the same point without the sneer.

**Still slightly performative:** "You're allowed to say it now." (line 21) — this works once, but it's a mic-drop line in a section that doesn't need one yet. The evidence hasn't been presented. The punchline arrives before the joke.

### Steel-Man Check

**Present and effective.** The v2 adds a substantial new section (lines 89-101) that was absent in v1. The author engages Alvin Plantinga, William Lane Craig, the Kalam cosmological argument, fine-tuning, and the argument from religious experience. He also engages the "wrong category" objection — that empirical standards shouldn't apply to the supernatural — and gives it a serious hearing before responding with the love/MRI analogy.

The key passage: "This is the strongest counter-argument theology has, and I want to take it seriously" (line 93). This is the right register. He doesn't dismiss it; he addresses it.

**One weakness in the steel-man:** He says "Some of them are clever. None of them produce evidence." (line 101). This is true but slightly glib. The fine-tuning argument, for example, does engage with physical constants — it isn't purely philosophical. A sentence acknowledging that the fine-tuning argument at least points to physical data (even if its interpretation is contested) would strengthen the steel-man.

### Fact-Check

**Sinai excavation findings:**
- Claim: "Decades of excavation in the Sinai Peninsula have found nothing. No pottery. No bones. No campsites. No graves." — CORRECT. This matches the archaeological consensus as described by Finkelstein, Silberman, Herzog, and Dever.

- Claim: Israel Finkelstein "concluded that the Exodus as described in the Bible simply did not happen" — CORRECT. From *The Bible Unearthed* (2001).

- Claim: Ze'ev Herzog: "The Israelites were never in Egypt" — CORRECT. Published in Ha'aretz, 1999.

- Claim: "remarkably little of proven or provable historical worth in the biblical Exodus narrative" — The quote appears in scholarly literature on the Exodus and is cited on Wikipedia's Exodus article, but the **specific scholar is not attributed in the text**. The author presents it as "scholarly consensus" rather than a direct attribution, which is acceptable but imprecise. The original source appears to be William G. Dever or a similar minimalist scholar.

**Egyptian record-keeping:**
- Claim: Egyptians "mentioned the arrival of a few dozen nomads seeking grain, the escape of two runaway slaves" — CORRECT. The Papyrus Anastasi V records the pursuit of two runaway slaves. The "Admonitions of Ipuwer" and other records document small Semitic groups entering Egypt.

**Jesus historicity:**
- Claim: "Virtually all historians — including secular scholars — accept that a Jewish preacher named Jesus of Nazareth existed" — CORRECT. This is the mainstream scholarly consensus (Bart Ehrman, E.P. Sanders, Paula Fredriksen, etc.).

- Claim: Tacitus mentions Jesus — CORRECT. Annals 15.44 (c. 116 CE).

- Claim: Josephus mentions Jesus — CORRECT. Antiquities 18.3.3 (Testimonium Flavianum, with scholarly consensus that the core reference is authentic but with later Christian interpolations) and 20.9.1.

- Claim: Gospels "written decades after the events they describe, by people who were not eyewitnesses, in a language Jesus didn't speak" — CORRECT. Mainstream NT scholarship dates the Gospels c. 70-100 CE; Jesus spoke Aramaic, Gospels are in Greek; scholarly consensus holds they are not eyewitness accounts.

**STEP study (lines 47-48 in Ch7, referenced conceptually in Ch4):**
- Claim in Ch4: No specific STEP citation in Ch4 v2. The study is cited in Ch7. See Ch7 section below.

**John Jay Report:**

## CHAPTER 5: The Books Don't Agree

### Voice Check

This is one of the strongest chapters in terms of voice. The narrator is calm, methodical, and forensic. He lays out contradictions like a prosecutor presenting exhibits. The pacing is excellent — short paragraphs, each building on the last.

**Strong passage:** "A man has one set of last words. Not three." (line 51) — clean, devastating, in-voice.

**Slight overreach:** "If four witnesses gave these accounts in a courtroom, the case would be thrown out." (line 61) — this is a well-worn atheist talking point. It's not wrong, but it's the kind of line that sounds like it came from a YouTube debate rather than original reporting. The author is better than this.

**Good register throughout the Quran section.** The author is respectful but direct. "An all-knowing God would know where sperm actually comes from. A seventh-century Arab merchant relying on the medical knowledge of his time would not." (line 91) — this is the author at his best: precise, calm, letting the implication land.

### Steel-Man Check

**Present and well-executed.** Lines 99-108 engage the sophisticated Christian response (Bible as "inspired through fallible human authors") directly and fairly. The author quotes the position accurately, calls it "more intellectually honest," and then presses on it with a genuine question: "If divine inspiration couldn't prevent factual errors... what exactly did it accomplish?"

This is proper steel-manning. He doesn't caricature the position. He gives it its strongest form and then applies pressure to the internal logic.

**The closing paragraphs (lines 115-123) are also well-balanced:** "The King James Bible is one of the greatest works of English literature. The Quran's Arabic is, by all accounts, stunningly beautiful. The Talmud is one of the most sophisticated bodies of ethical reasoning ever produced." — this prevents the chapter from reading as a demolition job. It respects the texts while questioning their divine status.

### Fact-Check

**Documentary Hypothesis (P, J, E, D sources):**
- Claim: "Biblical scholars identified four separate source traditions... the P (Priestly), J (Yahwist), E (Elohist), and D (Deuteronomist) sources" — CORRECT. This is the classical Documentary Hypothesis, associated with Julius Wellhausen (1878) and still the dominant framework, though modified by subsequent scholarship.

**Genesis 1 vs Genesis 2:**
- Claim: In Gen 1, plants then animals then humans (male and female together); in Gen 2, man first, then plants, then animals, then woman from rib — CORRECT.
- Claim: Gen 1 uses "Elohim," Gen 2 uses "YHWH" — CORRECT. Gen 2 uses "YHWH Elohim" (the combined form), but the distinction is real and well-established.
- Claim: Gen 1 is from P, Gen 2 from J — CORRECT per the Documentary Hypothesis.

**Gospel birth narratives:**
- Claim: Matthew says family was from Bethlehem; Luke says they lived in Nazareth and traveled for a census — CORRECT. Matthew 2 implies Bethlehem as home; Luke 2 has them traveling from Nazareth.
- Claim: Quirinius census was 6 CE; Herod died 4 BCE; "a gap of at least ten years" — CORRECT. The Census of Quirinius is dated to 6 CE. Herod the Great died in 4 BCE (possibly 5 BCE by some reckonings). The gap is approximately 10 years.

**Last words of Jesus:**
- Mark/Matthew: "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" — CORRECT (Mark 15:34, Matt 27:46).
- Luke: "Father, into your hands I commit my spirit" — CORRECT (Luke 23:46).
- John: "It is finished" — CORRECT (John 19:30).

**Resurrection witnesses:**
- Mark: Mary Magdalene, Mary mother of James, Salome — CORRECT (Mark 16:1).
- Matthew: Mary Magdalene and "the other Mary" — CORRECT (Matt 28:1).
- Luke: Mary Magdalene, Joanna, Mary mother of James, and "other women" — CORRECT (Luke 24:10).
- John: Mary Magdalene alone — CORRECT (John 20:1).

**What they found at the tomb:**
- Mark: young man in white robe — CORRECT (Mark 16:5).
- Matthew: angel who descended in earthquake — CORRECT (Matt 28:2-3).
- Luke: two men in gleaming clothes — CORRECT (Luke 24:4).
- John: two angels — CORRECT (John 20:12).

**Genealogy (Matthew vs Luke):**
- Claim: Matthew says Joseph's father was Jacob; Luke says Heli — CORRECT (Matt 1:16; Luke 3:23).
- Claim: Both explicitly trace Joseph's line — CORRECT. Both texts name Joseph.

**Quran creation days:**
- Claim: Surah 7:54, 10:3, 11:7 say six days — CORRECT.
- Claim: Surah 41:9-12 adds up to eight (2+4+2) — CORRECT on the arithmetic. The apologetic response (that the four includes the first two) is accurately reported.

**Surah 86:5-7 (sperm from between backbone and ribs):**
- Claim: "The Quran says human beings are created from a fluid that comes from 'between the backbone and the ribs'" — CORRECT. The Arabic terms are "sulb" (backbone/loins) and "tara'ib" (ribs/breastbones).
- Claim: "consistent with the medical theories of Galen" — CORRECT. Galenic medicine, widespread in the 7th-century Middle East, held that semen originated near the kidneys, between the spine and ribs.

**Surah 19:28 (Mary as "sister of Aaron"):**
- Claim: "The Quran refers to Mary, the mother of Jesus, as 'sister of Aaron'" — CORRECT. Surah 19:28: "O sister of Aaron."
- Claim: "Aaron was the brother of Miriam, who lived some thirteen hundred years before Mary" — CORRECT. The gap is approximately 1,300 years.
- Claim: Muslim response is that "sister of Aaron" is figurative — CORRECT. This is the standard apologetic response, supported by a hadith in Sahih Muslim.
- The author's note that "the Quran does not use figurative language with other historical figures in this way" — This is a **debatable** claim. The Quran does use relational language loosely in other contexts ("people of Abraham," etc.), but the specific formulation "sister of" for a non-sibling is unusual.

**Abrogation (nasikh):**
- Claim: "later verses can override earlier ones" — CORRECT. This is the classical doctrine of naskh, though its scope and application are debated among Muslim scholars.
- Claim: Surah 2:256 ("no compulsion in religion") and 2:191/9:29 as examples — CORRECT verse references. However, note that 2:191 has important context — the preceding verse (2:190) says "Fight in the way of Allah those who fight you but do not transgress." Presenting 2:191 without this context is slightly misleading, though the broader point about internal tension stands.

**Code of Hammurabi:**
- Claim: "around 1754 BCE" — CORRECT. Scholarly dating is 1755-1750 BCE; 1754 is the standard round figure.

**Bart Ehrman quote:**
- Claim: Ehrman says Gen 1 and Gen 2 cannot be reconciled — CORRECT in substance. Ehrman has made this argument extensively in *Jesus, Interrupted* and other works. The exact quote should be verified against the source text, but it accurately represents his position.

## CHAPTER 6: Older Gods

### Voice Check

The opening is excellent: "The first time I came across the Epic of Gilgamesh, I had to put it down and walk around the room." The narrator is physically present, genuinely affected. This is the author at his best — showing the process of discovery, not just the conclusion.

**Strong throughout.** The Ponzi scheme analogy (line 69) is sharp but controlled. The grief passage at the end (lines 103-121) is emotionally honest and prevents the chapter from feeling like a triumphalist debunking.

**One concern:** The phrase "Human stories. All the way down." (line 111) echoes "turtles all the way down" — a well-known atheist/skeptic reference. It's clever, but it's the kind of in-group signaling that might alienate the exact readers this book should be reaching. Consider whether this is worth the risk.

**Register slip:** "I spent the next several weeks chasing this thread. And the further I pulled, the more unraveled." (line 21) — mixed metaphor. You chase a thread, but you pull a thread. Pick one.

### Steel-Man Check

**Present and well-done.** Lines 73-78 present the sophisticated theological response: "God deliberately chose to express divine truth through existing cultural forms." The author calls this "a more elegant argument than the devil theory" and then follows its logic to its conclusion. This is proper engagement — he doesn't dismiss it, he extends it and shows where it leads.

Lines 81-88 are also strong: "I am not claiming that Christianity was directly, mechanically copied from any single pagan myth." This is an important caveat that prevents the chapter from overstating its case.

### Fact-Check

**Epic of Gilgamesh date:**
- Claim: "Parts of it were scratched into clay tablets around 2100 BCE" — CORRECT but needs nuance. The earliest Sumerian Gilgamesh poems date to the Third Dynasty of Ur (c. 2100-2000 BCE). The "standard" Akkadian version with the flood narrative (Tablet XI) is later — Sin-leqi-unninni's version dates to c. 1300-1000 BCE. The flood story is present in earlier Sumerian versions (the "Eridu Genesis" and the story of Ziusudra), but the Utnapishtim version specifically is from the later standard Babylonian edition. The claim "2100 BCE" for the Gilgamesh flood story as told in the text is slightly misleading — the flood narrative in its Utnapishtim form is from the standard version, not the earliest poems.

**Utnapishtim flood details:**
- Claim: dove, raven, boat, animals, sacrifice, divine satisfaction — CORRECT. The Gilgamesh version has dove, swallow, and raven (not just dove and raven). The text says "He sends a swallow. It returns." (line 9) — this detail is CORRECT and often omitted.

**Sargon of Akkad:**
- Claim: "lived around 2300 BCE" — CORRECT. Sargon reigned c. 2334-2279 BCE.
- Claim: basket, rushes, bitumen, river, found by water-drawer — CORRECT. The Legend of Sargon matches these details. The quote "set me in a basket of rushes, with bitumen she sealed my lid. She cast me into the river" is an accurate rendering.
- Claim: "roughly seven hundred years before Moses" — This depends on when you date Moses. If Moses is placed in the 13th century BCE (the common scholarly dating for the Exodus tradition), the gap is about 1,000 years, not 700. If Moses is dated to the 15th century (early dating), it's about 800-900 years. "Seven hundred years" is on the low side.

**Enuma Elish:**
- Claim: "dated to around 1100 BCE" — PARTIALLY CORRECT. The date is debated. Some scholars place composition in the time of Hammurabi (c. 1792-1750 BCE); others favor c. 1100 BCE. The "around 1100 BCE" is one scholarly position but not the only one, and possibly not the dominant one. The oldest surviving tablets date to c. 1200 BCE.

**Code of Hammurabi:**
- Claim: "around 1754 BCE" — CORRECT. (Same as Ch5.)

**Dying-and-rising gods (Osiris, Tammuz, Adonis, Attis, Dionysus):**
- The author's descriptions are broadly accurate and — importantly — he includes the necessary caveats (lines 44-48). He notes that "Osiris doesn't physically rise from the dead the way Jesus supposedly does — he becomes king of the underworld, which is a different concept." This is CORRECT and important. Many popular atheist treatments get this wrong.
- "Mithras is 'born from a rock,' which is not exactly a virgin birth in any meaningful sense" — CORRECT.
- The overall framing — that the "category" of dying-and-rising gods existed in the cultural milieu — is defensible, though the scholarly consensus has moved against Frazer's original formulation. The author navigates this well.

**Justin Martyr:**
- Claim: He "listed the parallels himself — Dionysus, Hercules, Asclepius" and said Christians "propound nothing new or different from what you believe regarding those whom you call Sons of Zeus" — CORRECT. This is from the First Apology, Chapter 21. The quote is accurate.
- Claim: His explanation was that "the devil did it" — Satan planted counterfeit versions in advance — CORRECT in substance. Justin attributes the parallels to demonic imitation (diabolical mimicry). In the First Apology (Ch. 54, 69), he specifically argues that demons read the prophecies and planted counterfeits. The characterization as "the devil traveled backward in time to plant decoys" is the author's rhetorical framing — Justin's actual argument is that demons read Old Testament prophecies and created pagan imitations before Christ's arrival, not that they "traveled backward in time." This is a minor but meaningful distinction. Demons reading prophecy is different from time travel.

**Tertullian:**
- Claim: "Tertullian made the same argument" — CORRECT. Tertullian's *De Praescriptione Haereticorum* (Ch. 40) makes the diabolical mimicry argument regarding pagan rituals resembling Christian sacraments.

**Constantine and 312 CE:**
- Claim: "In 312 CE, the Roman emperor adopted Christianity" — CORRECT. The Battle of the Milvian Bridge was in 312 CE; Constantine's conversion is traditionally dated to this event (the vision of the Chi-Rho). The Edict of Milan (313 CE) followed.

### Flag Summary for Chapter 6

- Epic of Gilgamesh "2100 BCE" for the flood story specifically — misleading. The earliest Gilgamesh poems are that old, but the flood narrative in its Utnapishtim form is from the later standard version (c. 1300-1000 BCE). The flood motif in Sumerian literature (Ziusudra) does predate the Bible by centuries, but the specific "2100 BCE" date attached to the Utnapishtim story is imprecise.
- Sargon "seven hundred years before Moses" — likely too low. Should be ~1,000 years using standard chronology.
- Enuma Elish "1100 BCE" — one scholarly position, not consensus. Could be much older.
- Justin Martyr "devil traveled backward in time" — rhetorical exaggeration of Justin's actual argument (demons reading prophecy, not time travel).

## CHAPTER 7: The God-Shaped Hole

### Voice Check

Excellent opening. The Pascal misattribution note is a nice touch — it signals rigor. The daughter/curtains anecdote (lines 17-20) is strong scene-setting: physical, specific, emotionally real. This is the author doing what he does best.

**Best passage:** "Mickey Mouse is cold comfort on the cancer ward. But a Loving Sky Father who will carry me through the pain and separation of death — that helps me make it through the night." (line 73) — attributed as "one researcher." This is a great line, but **who said it?** If it's a real quote, it needs attribution. If it's the author's paraphrase, it shouldn't be in quotes.

**The closing walk past the church (lines 127-133):** Beautiful. Quiet, honest, un-showy. This is the author at his absolute best. No sarcasm, no triumph. Just understanding.

**One tonal wobble:** "Nobody prays to a cube." (line 83) — funny, and it works, but it's a comedian's line in what is otherwise a science chapter. It's fine. Just noting it.

### Steel-Man Check

**Present and strong.** The Justin Barrett passage (lines 110-123) is the key steel-man moment. The wife/oxytocin argument is presented fairly and called "a sharp point." The author then distinguishes between verifiable perceptions (color, wife's existence) and unverifiable ones (God). This is rigorous.

**Cross-cultural variation counter-argument:** Present (lines 99-103). The author addresses why, if the cognitive mechanisms are universal, the gods are so different. His answer — "the mechanisms produce the tendency, not the content" — is accurate and concise. This adequately addresses the objection.

**However:** The steel-man could go further. Barrett's argument has a stronger form: if the brain is *designed* to detect God (as Barrett argues), then the cognitive mechanisms aren't a debunking explanation — they're *how God arranged for us to find him*. The author gestures at this with "Maybe the brain is wired to detect God because God is there to be detected" (line 113), but he dismisses it quickly. One more sentence engaging with why this is a genuinely difficult philosophical problem (the "genetic fallacy" — explaining the origin of a belief doesn't determine its truth value) would strengthen this section.

### Fact-Check

**HADD (Hyperactive Agency Detection Device):**
- The description is scientifically accurate as a concept. The evolutionary logic (false positives vs. false negatives) is correctly stated.
- Claim that it was "coined" — the term is attributed to **Justin Barrett**, not stated in the text. The chapter doesn't attribute the coining, which is fine, but Barrett is the originator. The description is accurate.
- **Important caveat the text omits:** Recent scholarship (as of 2024) has challenged HADD's empirical support. A 2024 article in *The Skeptic* notes "no supportive empirical evidence in almost 30 years." The concept is widely cited but its empirical basis is contested. The text presents it as established science without this caveat.

**Theory of Mind:**
- Description is scientifically accurate. The link between Theory of Mind and social cognition is well-established.

**Terror Management Theory:**
- Claim: "developed by psychologists Sheldon Solomon, Jeff Greenberg, and Tom Pyszczynski" — CORRECT. All three names and the order are right.
- Claim about judges setting higher bail when reminded of death — CORRECT. This is from a well-known TMT study.
- Claim about students rating in-group more favorably after death primes — CORRECT. Standard TMT finding.
- The description of TMT is accurate and fair.

**Pascal Boyer — minimally counterintuitive concepts:**
- Claim: Boyer is an "anthropologist" — CORRECT. He is a cognitive anthropologist (currently at Washington University in St. Louis).
- Claim: "our brains preferentially remember and transmit ideas that are slightly unusual but mostly familiar" — CORRECT. This is an accurate summary of the MCI (minimally counterintuitive) hypothesis from *Religion Explained* (2001).
- The guardian angel example is a good illustration. The "endless cube of pure mathematics" counter-example is the author's invention but accurately illustrates the principle.

**Justin Barrett:**
- Claim: He is "a cognitive scientist who is himself a Christian" — PARTIALLY CORRECT. Barrett is formally an **experimental psychologist** (PhD, Cornell), not a cognitive scientist by training. However, he is a founding figure in the Cognitive Science of Religion field and is widely described as a cognitive scientist in that context. The description is defensible but imprecise.
- The wife/oxytocin argument: The text says Barrett puts it like this: "Suppose science produces a convincing account for why I think my wife loves me — it's oxytocin, attachment theory, evolutionary pair-bonding. Should I then stop believing that she does?" — The core argument (explaining the mechanism doesn't disprove the belief) is correctly attributed to Barrett. The specific phrasing with "oxytocin, attachment theory, evolutionary pair-bonding" may be the author's elaboration of Barrett's argument rather than a direct quote. Barrett's published versions of this argument are typically shorter. The oxytocin detail is the author's addition, not Barrett's exact words.

**STEP study (Ch7, line 47):**
- Claim: "the STEP study in 2006 at Harvard" — PARTIALLY CORRECT. The STEP study was published in April 2006 in the *American Heart Journal*. The lead investigator was Herbert Benson of the Mind/Body Medical Institute at **Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center, Harvard Medical School**. Calling it "at Harvard" is a simplification — it was a **multicenter** study involving six hospitals, led from a Harvard-affiliated institution. Not inaccurate, but imprecise.
- Claim: "1,802 patients" — CORRECT. 1,802 patients in three groups.
- Claim: "intercessory prayer had absolutely no effect on recovery from heart surgery" — CORRECT. No significant difference between prayed-for and not-prayed-for groups among those uncertain of their status.
- Claim: "Patients who knew they were being prayed for actually did worse" — CORRECT. Complications in 59% of the certainty group vs. 52% of the uncertainty-with-prayer group.
- The $2.4M cost (mentioned in task instructions) does not appear in the chapter text. This is fine — the cost isn't essential to the argument. But it is confirmed at $2.4M, funded primarily by the John Templeton Foundation.

**Consistency with Ch4:** The STEP study is only cited in Ch7, not in Ch4. There is no inconsistency because Ch4 does not reference it.

The main remaining issues are:
