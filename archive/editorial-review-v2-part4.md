## CHAPTER 12: What We Lose

### Voice Check

The voice is consistent throughout — first-person, British, journalistic, physically present. This is strong, honest writing. The narrator admits personal cost without self-pity. The register moves between personal memoir (the grandmother, the crematorium) and reportage (Margaret, Claire, Chartres) and brief analytical passages, and the transitions feel earned.

No passages feel like a different person. The closest to a risk is the paragraph beginning "These are not decorations. They are technologies" (line 70), which edges toward thesis-statement territory, but it works because it follows the concrete descriptions of shiva and Islamic burial — the reader has been shown before being told.

**One concern:** The sentence "We are dismantling those technologies and replacing them with a pamphlet and a twenty-minute crematorium slot" (line 72) is sharp and effective, but it's doing the same work as the car-park scene that opened the chapter. The chapter earns both, but a rigorous editor might ask whether the reader needs both or whether the repetition dilutes the earlier, more powerful version.

### Emotional Register

**Does it feel like genuine personal loss?** Yes. Emphatically. The opening funeral scene is devastating. The grandmother scene is the emotional core. The three-in-the-morning passage ("I have nothing to do with the fear. I lie there. I breathe.") is the most vulnerable writing in the book so far. The narrator's explicit admission — "if I write the chapter about what we lose as if the losses are all other people's losses, felt at a distance... then I'm lying" — is exactly right.

The Margaret/Wednesday Club section is powerful and specific and avoids sentimentality. The line "Nobody holds a vigil for the Wednesday Club" is excellent.

The Chartres passage works as personal experience. "I'm a tourist in someone else's transcendence" is a strong line.

## CHAPTER 13: What We Keep

### Voice Check

The voice is consistent and recognizable. This is the same narrator — present, specific, journalistic. The Glasgow setting is vivid and physical ("Glasgow rain — not dramatic, just persistent"). Margaret Campbell is rendered as a real person with real authority.

**One concern:** The passage beginning "Start with the ethics, because they're the easiest to separate" (line 35) shifts into a more essayistic, structured mode. The subheadings within the text (ethics, contemplative tradition, grammar of awe) give it a slightly more organized feel than the rest of the book. This is not wrong — the chapter is explicitly attempting to make an argument — but it does feel slightly more like a "TED talk" than the rest of the manuscript. The "grammar of awe" section (lines 69-87) is the strongest of the three essay sections because it's the most felt.

**Flagged line:** "Keep the ethics. Retire the theology that claimed to own them." (line 47) — This is good, but it's doing what the author warned himself against in Part Four: making an argument rather than thinking honestly. It reads like a bumper sticker. Consider whether the chapter earns this assertiveness or whether it would be stronger as something more tentative.

Similarly: "Keep the practice. Let go of the theology that framed it. But don't lose the depth in the process." (line 65) and "Keep the words. Redefine them if you have to." (line 87) — These three "Keep the..." formulations create a rhetorical structure that feels slightly more polished than the voice has been. This is a judgment call, not a problem.

### Emotional Register

**Does it feel like honest searching?** Mostly yes. The Glasgow scenes feel like real encounters. The Tom section (the retired electrician in Lewisham) is genuinely moving — "he went from silence to Thursday, and Thursday is something" is an excellent line.

The chapter is at its best when it's in scenes: Margaret Campbell's office, the shipyard funeral, Tom and Arthur watching football. It's slightly less convincing in its analytical passages, not because the analysis is wrong but because the narrator is more alive when he's in a room with someone than when he's at his desk making lists.

The final passage — the shipyard funeral, the crying-turning-to-laughing — is powerful. "That's not nothing. / That might be enough." is an excellent ending for the chapter.

### Key Scene: Protected?

- **Glasgow funeral (the toast, the singing, the crying-to-laughing):** Present and powerful. It opens the chapter and closes it. The shipyard worker, the wife, the big men, the toast story. **Status: Protected.**

### The Five Belief Statements

Here are the five "I believe..." statements from the Epilogue, evaluated against the standard: Would it make a religious reader stop and think? Would it make a confident atheist uncomfortable? If neither, it's too safe.

**Statement 1** (line 94):
> "I believe that the supernatural claims at the heart of Christianity, Islam, and Judaism are almost certainly not true. A man did not rise from the dead. An angel did not dictate a book in a cave. A sea did not part."

- Religious reader: Will disagree but has heard this before. Not new.
- Confident atheist: Will nod.
- **Verdict: Too safe on its own**, but it functions as the setup for the statements that follow. Acceptable as a baseline.

**Statement 2** (line 96):
> "I believe the people who believe those stories are, on average, living more connected lives than the people who don't. That sentence costs me something to write."

- Religious reader: Will stop and think. An atheist admitting this is rare and honest.
- Confident atheist: Will be uncomfortable. This concedes real ground.
- **Verdict: COSTS SOMETHING. This is strong.**

**Statement 3** (line 98):
> "I believe that the atheist movement, including the books that shaped my own thinking, has been dishonest about the cost of being right. We presented the abandonment of religion as liberation. For some, it was. For many others, it was amputation."

- Religious reader: Will feel heard, possibly for the first time by someone from "the other side."
- Confident atheist: Will be deeply uncomfortable. "Amputation" is a devastating word. "Dishonest" is an accusation against one's own tribe.
- **Verdict: COSTS SOMETHING. This is the strongest of the five. Protect it.**

**Statement 4** (line 100):
> "I believe I have been, at times in this book, unfair. Not in the facts. In the emphasis. I spent more time on the harm than the help."

- Religious reader: Will appreciate the honesty.
- Confident atheist: Will feel the ground shifting — the author is questioning his own project.
- **Verdict: COSTS SOMETHING. Honest and appropriately self-critical.**

**Statement 5** (line 102):
> "I believe that 'I don't know' is a braver position than either 'God exists' or 'God doesn't exist.' But I also believe it is the loneliest. I am tired of pretending it isn't."

- Religious reader: Will feel compassion. May think: "You could come home."
- Confident atheist: "Loneliest" and "tired of pretending" will sting. This rejects atheist triumphalism.
- **Verdict: COSTS SOMETHING. The word "tired" is doing heavy lifting. Protect it.**

**Overall assessment of the five statements:** Four out of five cost something. Statement 1 is the weakest but serves as necessary groundwork. The set as a whole is strong and honest. No changes recommended.

## OVERALL QUESTIONS

### 1. Does the book earn its ending?

**Yes.** The ending is earned. The narrator has moved from observation (early chapters) through investigation, through personal confrontation with cost, through honest searching, through uncertainty — and arrives at the beach not with answers but with honesty. The shell, the daughter, the sea: these are concrete, physical, non-rhetorical. The book ends with something happening, not with an argument being concluded. The five belief statements are the intellectual arrival; the beach scene is the emotional one. Together, they work.

### 2. Is there an unresolved continuity problem?

**Yes — two:**

1. **Margaret Campbell / Jean naming error in Chapter 13.** The Glasgow celebrant is named Margaret Campbell throughout, then referred to as "Jean" on line 178. This must be fixed.

2. **Thomas is dropped.** He appears in Chapter 3 as a significant character — the lifelong Catholic whose faith is "unremarkable and essential as breathing" — and never appears again. Sarah, Yusuf, and Miriam all return. Thomas does not. His absence is a structural omission. He represents the person for whom faith simply *works*, without drama or crisis. That perspective deserves at least a brief acknowledgment in the Epilogue.

### 3. What is the single most important fix still needed?

**The Margaret/Jean naming error in Chapter 13.** It is the only factual error that a copy editor, a reader, or a reviewer would catch on first reading and that would undermine trust in the entire manuscript. Everything else is a matter of judgment; this is a mistake.

## TOP 5 MOST IMPORTANT FIXES (Ranked)

1. **🔴 Chapter 13, line 178: "Jean in Glasgow" must be changed to "Margaret" (or vice versa).** The Glasgow celebrant is named Margaret Campbell throughout the chapter but called "Jean" in one later reference. This is a clear continuity error that would be caught by any attentive reader.

2. **🔴 Chapter 14, line 102: Sam Bankman-Fried's age.** "A twenty-nine-year-old" should be "a thirty-year-old." SBF was 30 at the time of arrest (born March 5, 1992; arrested December 12, 2022).

3. **🟡 Chapter 14: "Britain: non-religious now the majority."** This claim is not straightforwardly true by the 2021 Census (37% no religion in England and Wales). It is true by the British Social Attitudes survey and true for Scotland. Recommend specifying the source or softening: "In survey after survey, the non-religious now outnumber any single denomination" or citing the BSA data specifically.

4. **🟡 Thomas dropped after Chapter 3.** Consider adding a brief mention of Thomas in the Epilogue — even a single sentence. His absence is felt. He is the character who represents stable, undramatic, functioning faith, and the book's final section would be more honest with at least a nod to him.

5. **🟡 Chapter 12: Chartres blue "they've never been able to reproduce."** This is a romantic myth, not a fact. The cobalt technique is understood. Suggest softening: "that blue they've struggled to match" or "that blue whose exact quality has never been fully replicated."

### Additional Items (Lower Priority)

6. **Chapter 13: MBSR "prescribed by the NHS"** — More accurately "recommended by" or "available through" the NHS. "Prescribed" implies a formal clinical prescription.

7. **Disclosure question:** Multiple characters (Paul, Claire, Margaret of the Wednesday Club, Tom, Christine, Dale, Katrin) are presented as real people from real interviews. If any are composites, the author should include a note stating so. This is standard practice for narrative non-fiction.

8. **Epilogue, lines 128-131:** The furniture metaphor ("I am standing in a room where all the furniture has been removed") is slightly weaker than the beach imagery that surrounds it. Consider cutting or compressing.

9. **Chapter 14: Netherlands 1958 figure.** "Sixty percent... attended church regularly" conflates church membership (76% in 1958) with attendance (~60%). The distinction matters for a book about precision.

10. **Chapter 13: Sunday Assembly "seventy cities"** — at peak it was closer to 90; by 2019 it was 48. "Seventy" may reflect a mid-point. Consider "at its peak, nearly a hundred cities" or use a more current figure.
